# Organic Strategic Planning Model

## Definition

The Organic Strategic Planning Model is a values- and vision-centric planning approach in which strategy emerges through continuous iteration, stakeholder dialogue, and milestone testing rather than through top-down cascade. Pereira describes it as an organization "looking inward for practical solutions to stay ahead of the competition," with the model laying the foundation for long-term execution across service delivery, operations, and customer relations. It depends on three governance practices: stakeholders clarifying vision and values, stakeholders creating personal action plans, and stakeholders reporting on action-plan results. The discipline isn't speed or precision — it's continuous alignment.

## Key attributes

- **Values-first orientation** — every milestone is tested against company vision and values
- **Stakeholder-driven** — strategy lives in continuous dialogue across the team, not on a slide
- **Iterative milestone validation** — each milestone is iterated and tested before the next
- **Personal action plans** — every stakeholder owns a piece of the company's strategic delivery
- **Validated-learning discipline** — borrowed from lean; what works gets kept, what doesn't gets pruned
- **Continuous monitoring** — the model depends on consistent review, not periodic ceremony
- **Foundation-laying** — Pereira emphasizes that despite appearing "theoretical and flexible," its purpose is laying the foundation for execution

## When to use

- For mission-driven organizations where values are first-order strategic inputs
- In founder-led companies where the founder's vision is the strategic anchor
- For non-profits and social enterprises where stakeholder buy-in is the limiting factor
- When the team is mature enough to self-direct without heavy top-down cascade
- After a major restructure when shared values need to be re-established
- For organizations that have rejected Hoshin/Cascade rigidity but still need a frame

## When NOT to use

- In large hierarchical enterprises where stakeholder dialogue at scale is impractical
- When values are vague or contested — Organic depends on shared clarity
- For execution-heavy projects with hard deadlines (use Cascade or Hoshin)
- When the team lacks the discipline for continuous monitoring (slips into drift)
- As a substitute for strategic choice — Organic clarifies; it doesn't decide

## Common pitfalls

- Mistaking flexibility for the absence of structure
- Values poster syndrome — values stated but not lived
- Endless stakeholder dialogue without convergence
- Personal action plans that aren't aggregated into a coherent strategic frame
- Failing to monitor — the organic model dies quietly when continuous review lapses
- Confusing organic with emergent (Mintzberg's emergent strategy is a different concept)
- Using Organic to avoid hard strategic choices — "we'll figure it out together"

## Outputs / signals of good use

- Vision and values that every stakeholder can articulate consistently
- Personal action plans aggregated into a visible team-level strategy
- Iteration logs showing what was tested and what was learned
- Monitoring cadence with named owners
- Periodic strategic adjustments traced to validated learnings
- Team-wide accountability — stakeholders submitting reports on their action-plan sections

## Decision questions

- Do all stakeholders share the same vision and values, or do interpretations diverge?
- For each stakeholder — what's their personal action plan, and how does it ladder to company strategy?
- What's our iteration discipline — how do we test milestones before committing?
- Are we monitoring continuously, or just talking about strategy intermittently?
- When validated learning contradicts our plan, do we actually revise?
- Are we using Organic to align, or to avoid choice?

## Canonical examples

- **Pereira's stakeholder cycle** — Stakeholders clarify vision and values → create personal action plans → report results in a feedback loop that improves quality and speed of progress (pages 65-66)
- **Patagonia and other values-led firms** — culture-first strategy in practice
- **Worker-cooperatives and B-corps** — stakeholder governance fits the model naturally
- **Holacracy-adjacent organizations** — distribute strategic ownership across stakeholders
- **Early-stage founder-led startups** — the founder's vision is the values anchor; the team iterates organically around it
