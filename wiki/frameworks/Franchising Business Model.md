# Franchising Business Model

## Definition

Franchising is a model where a brand owner (franchisor) licenses its business system, brand, and ongoing support to independent operators (franchisees) in exchange for an upfront franchise fee plus ongoing royalties (typically 4-12% of revenue) and marketing contributions. Pereira distinguishes franchising from licensing by its operational rigor: franchisees must follow the franchisor's playbook on product, training, store design, supply chain, and pricing. The model enables rapid geographic expansion using franchisee capital rather than corporate cash.

## Key attributes

- **Franchisor-franchisee relationship** — brand owner + independent operator with rights to use the system
- **Upfront fee + royalties** — initial franchise fee ($10K-$50K typical for small; $1M+ for hotels) + 4-12% royalty + 1-4% marketing
- **Standardization required** — operations, branding, product, customer experience must be consistent across units
- **Capital-light expansion** — franchisor expands without funding each unit
- **Mutual obligation** — franchisor provides training, support, marketing; franchisee follows the system and pays royalties
- **Long-term contracts** — typically 10-20 years with renewal options

## When to use

- Concept proven in 3-5+ company-owned locations with strong unit economics
- Brand can be operationalized into a repeatable playbook
- Local operator advantage (people who know the community execute better than corporate)
- Capital constraints on direct expansion
- When competitor expansion creates first-mover urgency

## When NOT to use

- Concept hasn't proven repeatable across locations
- Quality requires founder-level oversight that can't be systematized
- Premium positioning that depends on consistency that franchisees might erode
- High capital concept where franchisees can't afford the unit cost
- Categories where franchise regulations are prohibitive (heavy disclosure rules in US)

## Common pitfalls

- Franchising before unit economics are proven — franchisees fail
- Brand quality degradation as franchisees cut corners
- Franchisor-franchisee conflict over royalty rates, exclusive territories, supply contracts
- Poor franchisee selection — wrong operators damage brand
- Inadequate field support causing franchisee underperformance
- Encroachment disputes (new units cannibalizing existing franchisees)

## Key metrics

- Franchise unit count and growth
- Average unit revenue (AUV)
- Royalty revenue and growth
- Franchisee satisfaction scores
- Brand consistency audit scores
- Franchisee default / closure rate
- Time-to-profitability for new units

## Decision questions

- Have we proven unit economics in 3+ company-owned locations?
- Is our operating system documented well enough to be franchised?
- What franchisee profile will succeed in our model?
- What controls maintain brand consistency at scale?
- Where does franchising vs. company-owned make more sense (geography, market type)?

## Canonical examples

- **McDonald's** — the dominant franchising case; 93%+ of locations franchised; supply chain integration (page 115, page 212 of BMC Explained)
- **Subway** — largest by unit count (40,000+); franchised model with shrinking unit economics
- **7-Eleven** — convenience-store franchising
- **Marriott / Hilton** — hotel franchising
- **Anytime Fitness** — fitness club franchising
- **UPS Store / FedEx Office** — service franchising
