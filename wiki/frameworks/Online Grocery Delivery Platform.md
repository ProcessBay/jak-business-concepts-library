# Online Grocery Delivery Platform

## Definition

Online grocery delivery platforms aggregate inventory from existing supermarkets and specialty stores onto a single app, then dispatch independent contractors (gig "shoppers") to pick, pack, and deliver orders to customers within hours. Pereira's Instacart case study (pages 63-66) shows the model's key trick: the platform owns no inventory, no warehouses, and no employee fleet — it monetizes the coordination layer (matching shoppers to orders, routing, real-time inventory sync) plus delivery fees, membership subscriptions, retailer media spend, and advertising. The model extends naturally beyond groceries to pharmacy, convenience, and specialty retail. The "Q-commerce" variant (Gorillas, Getir, Jokr) operates dark stores for sub-15-minute delivery — capital-heavier but faster.

## Key attributes

- **Asset-light aggregation** — no inventory, no stores, no fleet; the platform is the coordination layer
- **Gig-economy workforce** — independent-contractor shoppers; flexible supply scales with demand
- **Real-time inventory integration** — must sync with each retailer's POS/inventory to avoid out-of-stock fails
- **Multi-sided monetization** — customer delivery fee, membership, retailer commission, brand advertising
- **Retailer partnership-led GTM** — partner with national chains and local stores to access selection
- **Sub-2-hour delivery promise** — the core value proposition vs. weekly grocery trip
- **Last-mile density economics** — profitability depends on stacking orders per shopper per hour
- **Advertising flywheel** — brands pay for placement and promotions on the high-intent grocery surface

## When to use

- Dense urban markets where last-mile delivery is economically viable
- Geographies where grocery e-commerce penetration is below 10% and growing
- Categories where customers value convenience more than price (busy families, professionals)
- When you can credibly partner with anchor retail chains (not build inventory yourself)
- When advertising and retailer media is a credible secondary revenue stream
- Post-pandemic markets where consumer behavior has shifted toward delivery

## When NOT to use

- Low-density geographies — last-mile economics collapse without order density
- Markets where supermarkets have already built their own delivery (defensive moat)
- Categories where shoppers genuinely enjoy in-store selection (specialty foods, deli)
- When labor regulations classify gig shoppers as employees (California AB5-style risk)
- When average basket size is too small to support delivery fees customers will pay

## Common pitfalls

- Inventory inaccuracy — shoppers reach the store and find items out of stock; refund/substitution destroys NPS
- Unit-economics theatre — celebrating GMV growth while losing money per order
- Shopper churn and supply shortages during peak times — undelivered orders are the worst CX failure
- Pricing opacity — marking up retailer prices without disclosing erodes trust when caught
- Retailer-partner dependency — losing one anchor chain (e.g., Kroger going direct) can be existential
- Over-relying on subscription (Instacart Express, etc.) to mask broken transaction economics

## Key metrics

- **GMV** and order frequency per active customer
- **Average basket size** (must clear ~$35 in most markets to support fees)
- **Take rate** from retailer + delivery fee + tips
- **Shopper utilization** (orders per shopper per hour) — the operations KPI that drives unit economics
- **Membership penetration** (Instacart Express, Walmart+) — recurring revenue and frequency driver
- **Retailer advertising revenue** per active customer (the high-margin upside)
- **Repeat order rate** and 12-week retention cohorts
- **On-time delivery rate** and fill rate (orders delivered complete)

## Decision questions

- Will we own inventory (dark-store Q-commerce) or partner with retailers (Instacart model)?
- What's our advertising/retailer-media strategy — is it a meaningful second leg or wishful thinking?
- How do we handle inventory accuracy without owning the store's POS?
- Where's the path to gig-shopper sustainability if regulation reclassifies them as employees?
- Which retail anchors are we one-deal-away from losing, and what's the concentration risk?

## Canonical examples

- **Instacart** — US gig-economy grocery aggregator; partnerships + ad platform + Express membership (pages 63-66)
- **DoorDash** — extended from restaurants into grocery and convenience (DashMart dark stores)
- **Uber Eats** — added grocery via Cornershop acquisition
- **Gorillas / Getir / Jokr** — Q-commerce dark-store variant (15-min delivery); many failed post-2022
- **Gopuff** — US dark-store convenience and grocery
- **HungerStation** — Delivery Hero subsidiary serving KSA, includes grocery
- **Mrsool** — Saudi anything-on-demand delivery, includes groceries
- **Nana** (now Sary/etc.) — Saudi-native grocery delivery
- **Talabat Mart** — Delivery Hero's MENA grocery dark-store play
- **Walmart Grocery Delivery / Walmart+** — vertically integrated alternative (page 56)
