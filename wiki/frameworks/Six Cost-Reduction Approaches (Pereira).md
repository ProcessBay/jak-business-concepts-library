# Six Cost-Reduction Approaches (Pereira)

## Definition

Pereira frames every cost-reduction effort through six fundamental approaches that, individually or in combination, cover the universe of legitimate cost-saving moves. The framework's premise is that cost reduction is **not** the traditional "elimination process" most owners default to — proper cost management means choosing the right approach (or stack of approaches) for the structure of the spend you're attacking. The six approaches are: **Combination** (aggregate buying / cross-department procurement), **Adaptation** (lean / strict structuring to consumer-and-market demand), **Elimination** (identify and remove unproductive benefits, products, services, workflows, processes), **Substitution** (cheaper inputs without quality compromise), **Repurposing** (creative reuse of existing tools/processes for current pain points), and **Optimization** (restructure workflows to remove redundancies and bottlenecks). Pereira treats these as a toolkit, not a sequence: the discipline is in matching approach to spend type.

## Key attributes

- **Six distinct approaches** — Combination, Adaptation, Elimination, Substitution, Repurposing, Optimization
- **Combinable** — most real programs stack 2–4 approaches per cost line
- **Approach-to-spend matching** — wrong approach destroys value (e.g., elimination where substitution would have worked)
- **Continuous and dynamic** — not a one-off campaign; must reflect the organization's evolving position
- **Quality-preserving by design** — substitution and adaptation explicitly forbid quality compromise
- **Pre-distress preferred** — best results when adopted "from the get-go", not when financial distress forces it

## When to use

- Designing a structured cost-reduction program (not random cuts)
- Diagnosing why prior cost cuts failed or eroded value
- Training procurement, ops, or finance teams on intentional cost discipline
- Building a board-defensible narrative for cost actions
- Distinguishing cost-reduction moves from quality- or growth-eroding cuts

## When NOT to use

- Pure emergency cash crises where speed beats taxonomy (use elimination first, sort later)
- Single-line tactical decisions where the framework adds bureaucracy
- Organizations without basic cost visibility (fix "not knowing your costs" first — see anti-patterns)
- Pure pricing or revenue problems disguised as cost problems

## Common pitfalls

- Defaulting to Elimination because it's the most visible approach (Pereira's main warning)
- Treating the six as a sequence rather than a menu
- Substituting on inputs that customers actually perceive as part of the value proposition
- Repurposing tools beyond their structural fit (technical debt accumulates)
- Optimization theater — reshuffling workflows without removing real redundancies
- Treating the program as a project, not a permanent capability (Pereira: <12% of orgs sustain past 3 years)

## Key metrics

- **Cost reduction %** by line item and category
- **Operating margin delta** pre/post program
- **COGS ratio** and **overhead ratio** trends
- **Approach-mix** — % of savings from each of the six approaches (avoid Elimination-heavy mix)
- **Sustained savings rate** — fraction of year-1 savings still held at year 3

## Decision questions

- For each cost line, which of the six approaches is the right primary lever?
- Are we over-relying on Elimination because it's politically easy to announce?
- Where could Substitution or Combination produce the same savings without behavior change?
- What process or workflow is a Repurposing candidate (vs. buying new)?
- Have we converted this from a one-off program into permanent operating discipline?

## Canonical examples

- **Combination** — bundling department-level purchasing into a single procurement run; insurance volume discounts (page 33)
- **Adaptation** — restructuring operations to match leaner consumer demand (page 11)
- **Elimination** — cutting unproductive products, redundant workflows, surplus storage items (pages 11, 47)
- **Substitution** — paid office software → free alternatives where quality holds (page 45)
- **Repurposing** — applying existing tools/tech in new ways to address current pain points (page 11)
- **Optimization** — workflow restructuring; system integration to remove silos (pages 12, 37)
