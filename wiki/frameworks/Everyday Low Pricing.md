# Everyday Low Pricing

## Definition

Everyday Low Pricing (EDLP) is a retail pricing framework where the retailer commits to consistent, low baseline prices across the assortment, with promotions kept to a minimum. Pereira positions EDLP as the structural opposite of High-Low (promotional) pricing: instead of inflating prices then discounting to create traffic spikes, EDLP retailers maintain a steady, trustworthy price line so shoppers can stop comparison-shopping and simply buy. Sam Walton introduced it at Walmart in the 1960s and built the world's largest retailer on it; it has since been adopted across grocery, mass merchandise, and warehouse club formats. EDLP only works when the back-end matches the front-end: supplier negotiation leverage, lean operations, tight inventory turns, and a culture of relentless cost management. The pricing claim is downstream of operational discipline.

## Key attributes

- **Price stability over time** — same shelf price week after week, no inflation-then-discount cycles
- **Minimal promotional spend** — savings reinvested into baseline price, not into ads/coupons
- **Operational excellence as prerequisite** — supplier scale, supply-chain efficiency, low SG&A
- **Trust-based loyalty** — customers stop comparison-shopping because the price is always fair
- **Predictable demand and revenue** — flat sales curve vs. spiky Hi-Lo; cleaner forecasting and inventory planning
- **Selective "rollback" exception** — Walmart-style temporary deeper cuts without abandoning the EDLP frame
- **Volume-dependent margin model** — thin per-unit margins offset by velocity and basket size
- **Brand promise as positioning** — "always low prices" is the value proposition, not a tactic

## When to use

- Categories where price is the primary purchase driver (groceries, consumables, commodity general merchandise)
- Retailer has structural cost advantages (scale, vertical integration, lean ops) that competitors cannot match
- Target shopper is price-sensitive and shops the format weekly (stock-up trips)
- When promotional cycle costs (ad production, labor for resets, margin leakage) are eating profitability
- To deter competitors from entering price wars — EDLP signals you will not chase promotional spikes

## When NOT to use

- Luxury, fashion, or experience categories where high price signals quality and exclusivity
- When the retailer lacks the cost structure to sustain low prices profitably (no scale leverage)
- Categories with strong impulse and novelty buying (where Hi-Lo's excitement converts)
- Markets where shoppers are conditioned to expect deep discounts and read flat pricing as "no deals here"
- When unique merchandise or curated assortment is the differentiator (price isn't the lever)

## Common pitfalls

- Claiming EDLP without the cost structure — margins collapse and the strategy fails
- "EDLP-ish" — running too many promotions and confusing shoppers about the brand promise
- Letting suppliers' list-price drift erode the EDLP gap without renegotiating
- Underinvesting in shopper communication — EDLP only works if shoppers *believe* prices are low
- Mistaking EDLP for a marketing tagline rather than an operating model
- Competitor matches the price but offers a better store experience — EDLP alone isn't a moat

## Key metrics

- **Gross margin %** — must remain healthy despite low prices; tracks back-end discipline
- **Basket size / units per transaction** — EDLP wins on volume per trip
- **Sales per square foot** — efficiency of low-margin / high-velocity model
- **Sell-through rate** — predictable demand means cleaner sell-through with less markdown
- **Price perception index** — survey-based measure of whether shoppers *think* prices are lowest
- **Marketing spend as % of sales** — EDLP retailers should run materially lower than Hi-Lo peers
- **SKU turn velocity** — driver of supplier leverage and warehouse economics

## Decision questions

- Do we have a structural cost advantage that competitors can't match within 12-24 months?
- Is our target shopper price-led, or are they buying for experience / fashion / novelty?
- Can our supply chain and ops actually deliver low prices at a healthy gross margin?
- Will shoppers *believe* our EDLP claim, or has Hi-Lo conditioning damaged price credibility?
- Are we willing to give up the short-term traffic spikes that promotions deliver?

## Canonical examples

- **Walmart** — the EDLP archetype; built around "Always Low Prices, Always" since the 1960s under Sam Walton (pages 46, 124-127)
- **Walmart "Rollback"** — temporary deeper discounts within the EDLP frame (page 125)
- **Aldi** — extreme EDLP via no-frills, limited assortment, private label dominance (pages 129-131)
- **Costco** — EDLP in warehouse club format; margin cap (~14%) enforces low pricing structurally
- **Trader Joe's** — EDLP via private label and curated narrow assortment
- **Decathlon** — EDLP for sporting goods via vertical integration
