# Conversion Franchise

## Definition

A conversion franchise is a franchising structure in which an existing independent business — the "convert" — enters into a franchise agreement with a franchisor and rebrands its operations to join the franchise system. Pereira describes it as an increasingly popular expansion strategy because the convert brings an established customer base, location, staff, and revenue, while the franchisor gains a productive franchisee without the time and cost of greenfield site selection and ramp-up. Subway, Jiffy Lube, and Meineke built much of their networks through conversions. The model accelerates franchisor expansion and gives independent operators access to brand strength, marketing scale, and operational systems — at the cost of franchisee autonomy and the discipline of converting to the franchisor's standards.

## Key attributes

- **Existing business converts under franchisor brand** — not a greenfield unit
- **Brings established customer base, location, staff, revenue** — productive from day one
- **Franchisor avoids site selection and ramp-up costs** — faster expansion
- **Rebrand to franchisor standards** — signage, SOPs, product mix, training, staff
- **Initial franchise fee + ongoing royalties** — standard fee structure applies
- **Heavy adaptation cost on the convert** — equipment, technology, retraining
- **Conversion targeting industries with many independents** — auto services, sandwich shops, hair salons

## When to use

- Fragmented industry with many viable independent operators
- Franchisor wants rapid network expansion without greenfield risk
- Independent operators face declining margins from chain competition
- Franchise system has proven brand pull, marketing scale, and operating systems
- Geographic markets where greenfield site selection is slow or expensive

## When NOT to use

- Operating manual is rigid in ways incompatible with existing independent operations
- Convert population's quality is too variable to maintain brand standards
- Industry has few independents (greenfield only path)
- Franchisor lacks the resources to support a heavy conversion training/rebranding effort
- Premium brand where existing convert facilities can't meet standards

## Common pitfalls

- Convert resists giving up operational control they're used to
- Hidden conversion costs (equipment, renovation, retraining) erode franchisee economics
- Existing staff resist the new SOPs; customer experience dips during transition
- Convert's existing customer relationships don't transfer to new brand
- Brand inconsistency if conversions are approved with substandard facilities
- Legal/contractual cleanup from convert's existing supplier and lease agreements is complex

## Key metrics

- Conversion rate (independents converted ÷ targeted)
- Time-to-rebrand per unit
- Post-conversion revenue lift (vs. pre-conversion baseline)
- Franchisor acquisition cost per converted unit
- Convert satisfaction and retention through the agreement term
- Brand consistency audit scores for converted units

## Decision questions

- How many viable independent operators exist in our target market?
- What conversion economics — fees, support, lift — make the math work for both sides?
- How do we maintain brand consistency across operators who didn't start with our standards?
- Where do conversion vs. greenfield expansion make more sense?
- What rebrand timeline minimizes customer disruption?

## Canonical examples

- **Subway** — majority of 41,000+ locations were conversions from independent sandwich shops (page 72-73)
- **Jiffy Lube** — most of 1,900+ North American franchises are conversions from independent oil change shops (page 72)
- **Meineke** — 900+ North American franchises largely converted from independent auto repair shops (page 73)
- **Ace Hardware** — converts independent hardware stores under cooperative franchise model (page 241)
- **Saudi/MENA examples** — independent F&B and automotive shops converted under Americana, Herfy, or Apparel Group brands
