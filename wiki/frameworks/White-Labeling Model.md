# White-Labeling Model

## Definition

White-labeling is a revenue model where a producer manufactures or develops a product/service that another company then rebrands and sells as its own. The producer captures revenue through wholesale margins, royalties, or revenue-share, while the marketing brand captures the customer relationship and end-customer margin. Pereira distinguishes it from licensing by its full operational depth — the white-label producer handles manufacturing/development, while the marketing partner handles brand, sales, and customer experience. Common in TV sets (electronics manufactured by Foxconn/LG for many brands), mobile apps (white-label SaaS), and consumer products (store brands).

## Key attributes

- **Two-party value chain** — producer (manufacture/develop) + marketing partner (brand/sell)
- **Revenue-share or wholesale** — typical structures: wholesale price with marketing partner setting retail, or revenue share on sales
- **Producer scale advantages** — same factory makes multiple brands; amortizes fixed cost
- **Marketing brand control** — partner owns brand, customer, and pricing
- **Quality risk to brand** — producer's quality issues become marketing brand's problem
- **Lower differentiation** — multiple competitors may use same producer

## When to use

- Producer wants scale without brand investment
- Marketing company wants quick product launch without manufacturing infrastructure
- Categories with high production scale economics (electronics, software, basic consumer goods)
- Emerging markets where local brand owns customer relationship better than global producer

## When NOT to use

- When product quality requires deep manufacturer-brand integration
- When marketing brand wants vertical integration for control
- In small categories where economies of scale don't materialize
- When intellectual property boundaries can't be cleanly defined

## Common pitfalls

- Producer cuts corners that damage marketing partner's brand
- Multiple competing brands using same producer (commodity dynamic)
- Marketing partner builds no real product capability — becomes brand-only and gets disrupted
- Producer scaling so big they eventually compete with their marketing partners (Foxconn → own brand attempts)
- Quality inconsistency across partners

## Key metrics

- White-label partner count and revenue concentration
- Gross margin (producer side)
- Partner NPS / relationship health
- Brand dilution risk (number of competing brands sharing producer)
- Capacity utilization (producer side)

## Decision questions

- Are we better positioned as a producer (scale, capability) or a brand (customer, marketing)?
- What's our defensible advantage if competitors use the same white-label producer?
- For producers: what's our partner concentration risk?
- For brands: is our value-add purely brand, or do we add product capability?

## Canonical examples

- **Foxconn** — produces iPhones for Apple, electronics for many other brands (page 23 mentioned generally)
- **Costco Kirkland** — many products produced by major brands for store-brand sale
- **Trader Joe's brand** — most products are white-label from well-known producers
- **Mobile apps white-labeled** — many SaaS tools resold under partner brands
- **Social media management software** — backend resellers (page 23)
