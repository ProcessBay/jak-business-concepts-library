# Balanced Scorecard

## Definition

The Balanced Scorecard (BSC) is a strategic management and performance-measurement framework developed by Robert Kaplan (Harvard) and David Norton in their 1992 _Harvard Business Review_ article and 1996 book _The Balanced Scorecard: Translating Strategy into Action_. It corrects the historical over-reliance on lagging financial indicators by linking financial measures with three additional perspectives — Customer, Internal Business Processes, and Learning & Growth — that act as leading indicators of future financial performance. Pereira frames it through a pilot analogy: managing a business on financial dashboards alone is like flying a plane with one instrument; the BSC gives executives a multi-dimensional cockpit view.

## Key attributes

- **Four-perspective architecture** — Financial, Customer, Internal Processes, Learning & Growth, each with its own objectives and KPIs (page 21)
- **Leading + lagging indicators** — Learning & Growth, Internal Processes, and Customer are leading; Financial is lagging (page 35)
- **Cause-and-effect logic** — perspectives build on each other from the bottom (Learning & Growth) up to Financial; not four parallel silos (page 34)
- **Strategy translation, not just measurement** — converts vision/mission into testable objectives and concrete KPIs (page 16)
- **Top-leader sponsorship required** — demands CEO or top-leader participation; cannot be delegated to a working group (page 22, 60)
- **Few metrics by design** — forces focus on the handful of indicators that genuinely move strategy (page 12, 32)
- **Adaptable across sectors** — originally private-sector; now used by public sector, nonprofits, government agencies, sports teams (page 14, 18)
- **Total management overhaul, not a tool drop-in** — Pereira: cannot be "finished in a few weeks or months"; requires committing to change how you manage (page 13)

## When to use

- Translating a strategic vision into measurable execution across a multi-unit organization
- When financial reports alone are not telling the full performance story (e.g. revenue is fine but customer churn or technical debt is rising)
- During major strategic shifts (new market, M&A integration, turnaround) where alignment across units matters
- Public-sector or nonprofit contexts where mission outcomes need to coexist with financial sustainability
- When the board or investors are asking for a coherent performance-reporting story

## When NOT to use

- In small startups where the strategy is still being discovered (use OKRs or lean experiments instead)
- When leadership will not personally sponsor and engage (Pereira: BSC fails without leadership buy-in, page 60)
- When the organization lacks the data infrastructure to feed KPIs reliably (the BSC "requires a lot of data," page 61)
- When the goal is short-term tactical course-correction rather than multi-year strategy execution
- If you cannot resist the urge to track everything — BSC value collapses under metric sprawl

## Common pitfalls

- **Treating the four perspectives as equal/parallel** instead of as a cause-and-effect stack (Pereira's "fundamental" mistake, page 34)
- **Copying another company's scorecard** — Kaplan & Norton are explicit: must be tailored to your market, products, competitive pressure (page 16, 60)
- **Too many KPIs** — FranklinCovey citation: 3 goals → all achieved; 4–10 → one or two; 10+ → none (page 33)
- **All-lagging measures** because they are easiest to count, leaving nothing actionable to change (page 33)
- **Treating BSC as a measurement system** rather than a strategic management system (Kaplan & Norton's repeated warning, page 16)
- **No leadership air cover** — initiative dies in the middle layer (page 60)
- **Data fatigue** — managers resent filling out scorecards because it crowds out "real work" (page 61–62)

## Key metrics

- **Strategy execution rate** — % of strategic objectives on track quarter-over-quarter
- **Perspective balance** — distribution of objectives across the four perspectives (no perspective neglected)
- **Leading vs lagging ratio** — share of KPIs that are forward-looking (Pereira: most plans over-index on lagging, page 33)
- **KPI alignment score** — % of unit/team KPIs that ladder up to a corporate strategic objective
- **Cascading depth** — number of org levels with their own scorecard tied to the corporate scorecard

## Decision questions

- Do we have a real strategy to execute, or are we using the BSC to invent one? (BSC translates strategy; it does not create it)
- Who is the executive sponsor, and are they personally on the hook?
- For each perspective, what are the 1–3 objectives that actually move the strategy?
- Are our leading indicators things we can _change_, or just easy-to-measure noise?
- How will this scorecard cascade down — and who owns alignment at each level?

## Canonical examples

- **Telco company** (Pereira's worked example, page 44) — Financial: +5% net profit; Customer: +2.5% CSI; Internal: 90% UX score; Learning & Growth: 95% employee development plans completed
- **Mobil North American Marketing & Refining** — Kaplan & Norton's most-cited turnaround case, used throughout _The Balanced Scorecard_
- **CIGNA Property & Casualty** — cited in _The Strategy-Focused Organization_ (2000) for using BSC to climb from bottom-quartile to top-quartile performance
- **Wells Fargo Online Financial Services** — early BSC adopter in the financial services sector
- **Public-sector adoption** — government agencies, charities, sports teams; Pereira: now used worldwide (page 18)
