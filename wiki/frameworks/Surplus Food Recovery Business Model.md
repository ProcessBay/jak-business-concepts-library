# Surplus Food Recovery Business Model

## Definition

The surplus food recovery model buys foodstuffs that would otherwise be discarded — produce that fails cosmetic standards, items near expiry, oversupply from farms and packers, salmon the wrong shape for sushi grade — and resells them at a discount to consumers who don't need supermarket aesthetics. Pereira's Imperfect Foods case (pages 56-58) is the worked example: USDA estimated US food retail/consumer waste at 31% in 2010; by 2019, 35% of all US food went unsold or uneaten ($408B, ~2% of US GDP). Imperfect Foods operates as a **weekly subscription**: customers sign up, receive a suggested box, can add/remove items, and Imperfect delivers to door. About 20% of farm produce is rejected at the farm gate for cosmetic reasons — the entire harvestable supply is therefore the addressable inventory. The model layers a mission narrative (waste reduction, hunger relief, partnerships with food banks) on top of a cost-saving and convenience value prop. A B2B variant operates the same logic for foodservice: surplus reseller-to-restaurant or to charities.

## Key attributes

- **Discounted upstream supply** — buy what others reject, sell at a discount that still leaves margin
- **Subscription delivery format** — weekly box with customer-curated contents
- **Mission-driven brand narrative** — sustainability + hunger + cost savings stacked
- **Variable inventory** — what's in the box depends on what was available to rescue that week
- **Direct-to-consumer logistics** — controls own delivery or partners with regional carriers
- **Supplier-relationship moat** — locked-in relationships with farms, packers, processors who would otherwise dispose of surplus
- **Donation partnerships** — food banks as both a brand asset and a release valve for unsold inventory

## When to use

- Markets with documented high food waste at retail/farm/packer level
- Consumer segments price-sensitive AND values-driven (Millennials, Gen Z, sustainability-conscious)
- When you can lock in farm/packer relationships before competitors do
- Geographies with delivery infrastructure that can handle perishables
- When subscription mechanics are culturally accepted in grocery

## When NOT to use

- Markets where farm/packer surplus is already efficiently routed (e.g., into livestock feed, secondary processing)
- Regions with no last-mile cold-chain capability
- Cultures where "imperfect" produce carries strong stigma not addressable by marketing
- When you cannot maintain box composition variety week-to-week (drives churn fast)
- Markets where regulation prevents commercial sale of cosmetically-rejected or near-expiry food

## Common pitfalls

- Inventory variability frustrating subscribers expecting consistency (high churn)
- Building D2C logistics before supply lock-up is secure (expensive infrastructure, no goods to ship)
- Over-relying on mission marketing — customers eventually still need value and quality
- Competing too directly with full-price grocery and getting squeezed on both ends
- Letting box value creep toward parity with conventional grocery — kills the cost-saving promise
- Failing to scale donation partnerships, missing both reputational benefit and inventory release

## Key metrics

- **Pounds of food diverted from waste** (mission proof point — Imperfect donated > 1.7M pounds, page 58)
- **Weekly active subscribers**
- **Average box value** (vs. comparable grocery basket)
- **Churn rate** (cohort, weekly and monthly)
- **Supplier relationships** (farms, packers, processors under contract)
- **Gross margin per box** (after cold-chain logistics)
- **Cost per acquired subscriber** vs. lifetime box revenue

## Decision questions

- What's our defensible supply lock-up vs. follow-on competitors?
- Can we keep box variety high enough to keep subscribers from churning?
- Is our cold-chain logistics economical at our planned subscriber density?
- How do we manage week-to-week inventory volatility without disappointing subscribers?
- What's our mission story, and is it backed by audited diversion-from-waste numbers?

## Canonical examples

- **Imperfect Foods (US)** — weekly subscription, 14+ cities, > 1.7M pounds donated to date (pages 56-58)
- **Misfits Market (US)** — direct competitor in ugly produce subscription
- **Too Good To Go (Europe)** — near-expiry surplus from restaurants and bakeries via consumer app (in-store pickup variant)
- **Olio (UK)** — peer-to-peer food sharing platform
- **Saudi context: Marn, Etamna, and similar local food-rescue initiatives** — early-stage; Saudi Vision 2030 sustainability priorities and the Saudi Food Bank (Etaam) create policy tailwinds; NEOM and large-format retailers (Panda, Lulu, Tamimi) generate significant rescuable inventory not yet systematically captured
