# Go-to-Market Strategy

## Definition

A Go-to-Market (GTM) strategy is the comprehensive blueprint that defines how a company will bring an offering to market, reach its target audience, and achieve revenue and growth objectives. Pereira frames it as the binding plan that aligns market research, product positioning, pricing, distribution channels, marketing campaigns, sales tactics, and customer support into one coherent revenue motion. A GTM strategy is not a marketing plan and not a sales plan — it is the cross-functional orchestration that makes both work together, tuned to the specific industry, product complexity, target audience, and competitive landscape.

## Key attributes

- **Cross-functional by design** — aligns marketing, sales, product, and customer support around shared objectives
- **Eight interlocking components** — market research, product positioning, target segmentation, pricing strategy, distribution channels, marketing & promotion, sales strategy, customer support
- **Two top-level motions** — Sales-Led (high-touch, enterprise) vs Product-Led (self-serve, SaaS); some firms hybrid
- **Customer-centric anchor** — every decision traces back to a defined target segment and value proposition
- **Iterative, not one-shot** — designed in stages, then continuously measured and refined against data
- **Resource-allocation tool** — directs spend toward the most promising segments and channels, away from low-yield avenues
- **Bound to business goals** — revenue, growth, market share targets cascade down into GTM choices

## When to use

- Launching a new product or service into any market
- Entering a new geography or customer segment with an existing offering
- Repositioning or relaunching an underperforming product
- After a pivot — when the offering, audience, or business model has materially changed
- When functional silos (sales/marketing/product) are misaligned and impeding revenue

## When NOT to use

- For minor product updates that don't change positioning, pricing, or audience
- As a substitute for a business model — GTM presupposes you already know what you're selling and to whom
- When the team can't commit to cross-functional coordination (the strategy will fail in execution)
- As a one-time document — a static GTM plan in a dynamic market will drift out of relevance

## Common pitfalls

- Skipping market research and assuming you understand the customer
- Treating GTM as a marketing-only or sales-only exercise (siloed execution)
- Choosing the wrong motion — applying Sales-Led to a self-serve product or PLG to a complex enterprise sale
- Misalignment between value proposition, pricing, and distribution channel
- Launching before the supporting infrastructure (sales enablement, support, analytics) is in place
- Failing to define measurable KPIs upfront, then having no way to know if it worked
- See: [[Common GTM Mistakes]]

## Key metrics

- **Time to market** — speed from strategy lock to availability
- **Market share** — penetration in target market vs competitors
- **CAC** (Customer Acquisition Cost) and **LTV** (Lifetime Value); target LTV:CAC ≥ 3:1
- **Conversion rates** at each funnel stage
- **Revenue from new markets / segments / products** — direct attribution of GTM impact
- **ROI** — net profit vs total GTM investment

## Decision questions

- Who specifically are we selling to, and what proves they want this?
- Which motion (Sales-Led, Product-Led, hybrid) fits our offering and customer's buying behavior?
- What distribution channels reach this audience most efficiently?
- How are pricing, messaging, and channel selection mutually reinforcing — or fighting each other?
- Do we have the cross-functional alignment and resources to actually execute?
- What KPIs will tell us within 90 / 180 / 365 days whether the strategy is working?

## Canonical examples

- **Tesla** — disruptive technology launch, direct-to-consumer sales model, Supercharger infrastructure, brand evangelist cultivation (pages 126-128)
- **Peloton** — niche market penetration via proprietary hardware + content subscription + community (pages 128-130)
- **Airbnb** — global expansion via localized market entry, trust through reviews, government relations (pages 130-132)
