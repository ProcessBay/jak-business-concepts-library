# One-Time Purchase Pricing for Digital Products

## Definition

The one-time purchase model charges a single upfront fee in exchange for unlimited, permanent access to a digital product (software, e-book, course, music download, plugin). Pereira frames it as the classic perpetual-license approach that pre-dated the subscription economy and still suits non-recurring use cases. Unlike subscriptions, the customer pays once and owns access indefinitely; unlike physical goods, the seller incurs near-zero marginal cost per additional copy, which both enables generous pricing flexibility and creates piracy exposure (no continuous authentication tether). The model's economics live or die on average selling price, upgrade attach rate, and the company's ability to keep generating new buyer cohorts.

## Key attributes

- **Single upfront payment** — no recurring billing, no renewal
- **Permanent access** — customer "owns" the license, often perpetually
- **Simplicity and transparency** — no hidden fees or auto-renew surprises
- **Lower entry-barrier psychology** — no long-term commitment scares customers off
- **Higher upfront price-point** — customers face a single larger number vs. small recurring fee
- **Cash flow front-loaded** — revenue arrives at sale, fueling reinvestment
- **Vulnerable to piracy** — without recurring authentication, copies are easily distributed
- **Limited recurring revenue** — only repeat-buyers, upgrades, or add-ons generate additional revenue from same customer

## When to use

- Standalone digital products with finite, non-renewing value (e-book, single course, plugin)
- Products where customer prefers ownership over access (cultural expectation)
- When the firm wants to brand-signal premium quality via single-payment positioning
- When operating in markets where subscription fatigue is high
- When supporting infrastructure for recurring billing/customer success is too costly
- When upgrade paths (new versions, add-ons) can generate follow-on revenue
- For lower-touch indie/small-team digital products where ongoing relationship isn't needed

## When NOT to use

- When the product genuinely delivers ongoing value that the customer would pay recurring for
- When competitors have switched to subscription and customers expect it
- When piracy exposure would materially erode revenue and DRM is impractical
- When you need predictable MRR/ARR for forecasting or investor narrative
- When the product requires continuous content updates, support, or hosting costs
- When customer-success investment requires sustained relationship revenue
- When upgrade/upsell paths are non-existent (single sale = end of relationship)

## Common pitfalls

- Underpricing to look competitive, then unable to fund ongoing development
- Promising "free updates forever" without budgeting for the maintenance burden
- No DRM or copy protection — piracy compounds with no recurring authentication
- Failing to design an upgrade or add-on path — no way to monetize existing customers
- Treating one-time as "ship and forget" — customers expect support, bug fixes
- Pricing too high without value justification — customers wait for sales or pirate
- Mixing one-time and subscription models clumsily (Adobe's pre-Creative Cloud was this story)
- Ignoring competitive pressure as subscription rivals offer lower entry price

## Key metrics

- **Average selling price (ASP)** — anchor of the model
- **Conversion rate** — proportion of visitors who buy
- **Upgrade attach rate** — % of buyers who later buy a new version or add-on
- **Lifetime value per purchase** — includes upgrades and cross-sells
- **Piracy rate / unauthorized usage estimates** — directly erodes ASP × volume
- **Refund rate** — high refunds signal misaligned price / value
- **Cohort repeat-buyer rate** — repeat purchases from past customers
- **Cash flow per new-customer cohort** — front-loaded vs. recurring comparison

## Decision questions

- Does our product deliver discrete, finite value (one-time fits) or ongoing value (subscription fits)?
- What's our piracy exposure, and can DRM or platform distribution (Steam, App Store) absorb it?
- Can we build a viable upgrade / add-on / new-version cadence to monetize existing customers?
- Are we positioned to fund ongoing maintenance without recurring revenue?
- Does our price feel like ownership-grade or rental-grade to our target customer?
- Will we lose price-sensitive customers to subscription competitors with lower monthly entry?

## Canonical examples

- **Pre-2013 Adobe Creative Suite** — perpetual license at ~$2,500; replaced by Creative Cloud subscription (page 117)
- **Amazon Kindle e-books** — per-title one-time purchase, variable pricing (page 119-121)
- **Online courses (Udemy, Teachable)** — one-time enrollment, lifetime access
- **WordPress plugins (Gravity Forms, Yoast Premium one-time)** — one-time purchase with optional renewal for updates
- **Microsoft Office 2019/2021** — still sold as perpetual license alongside Microsoft 365 subscription
- **Steam-sold games** — one-time purchase for permanent library access
- **Stock photo single licenses** — Shutterstock, Getty per-image purchase
