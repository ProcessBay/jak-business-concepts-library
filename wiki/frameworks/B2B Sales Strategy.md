# B2B Sales Strategy

## Definition

A B2B sales strategy is the codified plan for selling to other businesses — distinct from B2C in stakes, complexity, and cycle length. Pereira frames B2B sales as fundamentally harder than B2C: higher prices, smaller addressable market (often hundreds rather than millions of buyers), longer cycles, multi-stakeholder decision-making (Shane Gibson cites six-person decision networks on the largest deals), heavier legal/procurement overhead, and higher post-sale service expectations. The strategy must communicate an unmistakable value proposition centered on how the product improves the buyer's day-to-day operations, and must be designed around modern B2B realities: heightened buyer skepticism, easy access to reviews, and acute risk awareness. The four B2B selling categories — selling to Producers, Resellers, Government, and Institutions — each demand different motions. Forrester forecasts the US B2B market at $10.7T by 2023, making strategic discipline non-optional.

## Key attributes

- **Multi-stakeholder decision-making** — average enterprise deal involves 6+ buyers
- **Long cycles** — typically 30-180+ days vs B2C's minutes to days
- **Higher ACV** — average deal sizes 10x-1000x B2C
- **Smaller TAM** — hundreds to thousands of accounts, not millions of consumers
- **Heavy post-sale expectations** — customer success, account management, renewal motion required
- **Four buyer categories** — Producers (manufacturers), Resellers (retail), Government, Institutions (nonprofit/edu/healthcare)
- **Two rep types** — Inside (phone/email/digital) and Outside (field, face-to-face)
- **Modern challenges** — buyer skepticism, online reviews, financial risk aversion
- **Inbound + Outbound + ABM** — most mature B2B strategies blend all three

## When to use

- Selling to businesses, governments, institutions, or resellers (definitionally B2B)
- ACVs justifying a dedicated sales team (typically >$5K ARR)
- Categories with buying committees rather than individual buyers
- Long-sales-cycle, considered purchases (software, services, capital equipment)
- Markets where account expansion (NRR) drives the economics
- When the product requires implementation/onboarding (B2C self-serve doesn't apply)

## When NOT to use

- Pure B2C transactional purchases (different motion entirely)
- Product-Led Growth motions where no sales team is needed below the enterprise tier
- Truly self-serve SaaS with high-volume / low-ACV (sales overhead kills unit economics)
- When the product lacks PMF — no sales strategy fixes a bad product

## Common pitfalls

- "Sell to anyone with a pulse" — the most common B2B failure (page 111)
- No defined ICP — broad messaging dilutes everything
- Treating sales and marketing as separate ("Sales and Marketing Win Together" — page 108)
- No follow-up plan post first contact (lost deals from neglect)
- No lead-nurture for not-yet-ready prospects
- Ignoring repeat-business and expansion revenue from existing accounts
- Failing to ask for referrals from satisfied customers
- No feedback loop on lost leads (page 110)
- Generic outreach instead of ABM for named accounts

## Key metrics

- **Average Contract Value (ACV)** — dollar size per deal
- **Sales cycle length** — days from first touch to close
- **Win rate** — Closed-Won / Total Opportunities
- **Pipeline coverage** — pipeline ÷ quota (healthy: 3-5x)
- **Deal velocity** — pipeline × win rate / cycle length
- **NRR** — Net Revenue Retention (expansion is the B2B compounding engine)
- **Account penetration** — % of named accounts engaged
- **Cost of acquisition by segment** — SMB vs MM vs ENT economics differ wildly

## Decision questions

- Have we defined the ICP sharply enough to disqualify, not just to qualify?
- Are sales and marketing aligned on lead definition and SLA?
- Do we have a lead-nurture process for not-ready buyers, or do we drop them?
- Are we mining existing customers for expansion (or only chasing new logos)?
- Do we have a referral motion built in, or is it accidental?
- Are we measuring win rate by segment, ACV, persona, and source?
- Have we built a feedback loop on lost deals to systematically improve?
