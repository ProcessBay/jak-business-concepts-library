# Standard Costing

## Definition

Standard Costing is a cost-allocation system — most often adopted by large-scale manufacturers — that helps a business measure the difference between the **expected cost of production** and the **actual cost of production**, a process Pereira calls **variance analysis**. The first step is to determine what production should cost under typical operating conditions (the "standard cost"), then compare that figure against what production actually costs. Negative variance (actual > expected) is "unfavorable" and signals hidden cost or wastage; positive variance (actual < expected) suggests economies of scale or scope are kicking in. Pereira identifies two primary variables driving the analysis: **rate variance** (the difference between expected and actual price of the input) and **volume variance** (the difference between expected and actual quantity consumed). Standard costing is also the natural endpoint of cost modeling — Pereira closes the guide by recommending operators build "a standard, reproducible, and flexible cost structure" as the framework for current and future cost decisions.

## Key attributes

- **Expectation-driven** — define what costs *should* be before measuring what they *are*
- **Variance analysis** — actual vs. standard, broken into rate (price) and volume (efficiency) components
- **Reproducible framework** — once built, the standard cost system flexes as the operation changes
- **Management-by-exception** — managers focus on variances, not on every transaction
- **Scale-friendly** — particularly suited to large-scale or repetitive manufacturing
- **Forecastable** — gives a planning baseline for budgets, pricing, and procurement

## When to use

- Repetitive manufacturing with stable inputs and processes
- Multi-plant or multi-line operations where a uniform reporting standard is required
- When budgets and pricing decisions need a planning baseline
- For performance management — holding teams to a defined cost target
- When a business is mature enough that "typical operating conditions" can actually be characterised

## When NOT to use

- Highly customized, one-off jobs (use job costing instead)
- Early-stage operations where production is too volatile to define a "standard"
- Industries with extreme input-price volatility, where standards become stale weekly
- Pure-services businesses where cost objects are hard to standardize
- When leadership lacks the discipline to investigate variances (the system goes ignored)

## Common pitfalls

- Setting standards too tight (chronic negative variance, demoralized teams) or too loose (variance hides waste)
- Failing to update standards as input prices, technology, or product mix shift
- Treating variances as blame instead of as diagnostic signals
- Conflating rate and volume variance — fixing the wrong driver
- Letting standard cost become "the budget" while operators run on actuals nobody reconciles

## Key metrics

- **Standard cost** — expected cost per unit under typical conditions
- **Actual cost** — realized cost per unit
- **Total variance** — actual cost − standard cost
- **Rate (price) variance** — (actual price − standard price) × actual quantity
- **Volume (efficiency) variance** — (actual quantity − standard quantity) × standard price
- **Favorable / unfavorable variance** classification
- **Variance ratio** — variance as % of standard

## Decision questions

- Are our standards based on current operating conditions, or are they two years stale?
- Is this variance driven by price (rate) or by consumption (volume)?
- What's the threshold at which a variance triggers a management investigation?
- Are favorable variances real productivity gains, or is the standard simply too loose?
- Where in our cost stack do we have no standards at all — and why?

## Canonical examples

- **Large-scale manufacturers** — Pereira's primary adopter category (page 28)
- **Process industries** — chemicals, food production, automotive assembly
- **Multi-plant operations** — comparing performance against a common cost baseline
- **Procurement teams** — using rate variance to monitor supplier price drift
