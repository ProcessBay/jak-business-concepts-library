# Porter's Five Forces

## Definition

Porter's Five Forces is an industry-structure analysis framework created by Michael Porter at Harvard Business School in 1979 (introduced in the Harvard Business Review article "How Competitive Forces Shape Strategy"). It assesses the long-run profit potential of an industry by examining five competitive forces: (1) competitive rivalry among existing firms, (2) threat of new entrants, (3) bargaining power of suppliers, (4) bargaining power of buyers, and (5) threat of substitute products. Unlike SWOT, which examines the internal/external position of a single firm, Porter's Five Forces operates at the macro-industry level — it asks "is this industry structurally attractive?" rather than "what should our firm do?"

## Key attributes

- **Industry-level lens** — analyzes the structure of the competitive arena, not a single company
- **Five distinct forces** — rivalry, new entrants, suppliers, buyers, substitutes
- **Profitability predictor** — the stronger the forces, the lower the average industry profitability
- **Static snapshot, not trajectory** — captures current structure; trends require repeated application
- **Force-rating syntax** — Pereira recommends rating each force from "++" (very favorable) to "--" (very unfavorable) with "o" for neutral
- **Strategy implication** — identifies which forces a firm must defend against or attempt to reshape
- **Complements SWOT** — Five Forces is macro/external; SWOT mixes micro/internal and external

## When to use

- Evaluating entry into a new industry or market segment
- Assessing whether an industry's profit pool is worth competing for
- During strategic planning when the industry structure is shifting (deregulation, new technology)
- Before major capacity investments or vertical-integration decisions
- When comparing industry attractiveness across multiple business units

## When NOT to use

- For firm-level strategic positioning (use Strategic Group Analysis or SWOT)
- In hyper-dynamic industries where the structure changes faster than the analysis can capture
- For platform/ecosystem businesses where complementors are a sixth force not captured here
- When the question is execution-level (operational, marketing, organizational) rather than structural
- As a one-and-done exercise — forces shift and require periodic re-assessment

## Common pitfalls

- Defining the industry too broadly or too narrowly (skews every force rating)
- Treating each force in isolation when they interact (e.g., low entry barriers + powerful buyers compound)
- Ignoring complementors and ecosystem dynamics (Porter's model predates platform thinking)
- Confusing high competitive rivalry with high overall profitability (they are usually inversely related)
- Failing to identify the *non-obvious* substitutes — they often kill industries faster than direct rivals
- Mistaking the analysis for the strategy — Five Forces describes the landscape; strategy still requires choice

## Key metrics

- **Concentration ratio** — top-4 or top-8 firms' combined market share (higher = lower rivalry)
- **Buyer concentration** — number of buyers / volume per buyer (higher concentration = stronger buyer power)
- **Supplier concentration** — same logic for upstream
- **Switching costs** — both for buyers and for suppliers, anchors stickiness
- **Capital intensity / fixed-cost ratio** — high fixed costs amplify price competition during downturns
- **Industry growth rate** — fast growth suppresses rivalry; mature/declining markets intensify it
- **Substitute price-performance ratio** — how close the next alternative is on value-per-dollar

## Decision questions

- Which force is currently the most binding constraint on industry profitability?
- Are any of the forces in motion (regulation, tech, demographics shifting them)?
- Which forces can our firm actively shape vs. only respond to?
- If we entered this industry, would we be a price-taker or price-maker?
- Where in the value chain does the profit pool actually sit, given supplier and buyer power?
- What would have to be true for a substitute to make our category obsolete in 5 years?

## Canonical examples

- **Under Armour (performance apparel)** — high competitive rivalry from Nike and Adidas (deeper resources, ability to replicate fabrics due to lack of patent protection); weak supplier power (diversified vendor base across nations); mixed buyer power (wholesale buyers like Dick's Sporting Goods can substitute, end consumers cannot); high entry barriers (brand-building cost); low substitute threat (rising demand for performance apparel) (pages 64-66)
- **Airline industry** — high startup costs and limited route slots create entry barriers, yet regulatory loosening has enabled low-cost agile entrants to break through (page 61)
- **Haulage business setup** — illustrates rivalry assessment requiring count of rivals, prices charged, resources, and entry barriers (page 60)
- **Food retail** — supermarkets face strong buyer power because shoppers can switch stores, which in turn weakens supermarkets' position against their own suppliers; Walmart/Costco bulk purchasing flips the script with extensive buying power (pages 63)
- **3D printing as substitute** — disruptive tech that lowered cost of manufacturing toys and plastic tools, increasing substitute threat for traditional manufacturers (page 63)
