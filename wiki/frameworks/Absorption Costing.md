# Absorption Costing

## Definition

Absorption Costing is a costing system in which **the full cost of manufacturing an item or providing a service is evaluated** — every fixed and variable cost associated with production is assigned to cost units, and overhead associated with operating the business is "absorbed" based on the business's activity level and distributed across departments through a process Pereira calls **apportionment**. Because it accounts for all costs involved in the operation, it is also known as **full costing**. A defining feature: the overhead apportioned per unit is fixed overhead, and overhead is allocated to a product whether or not that product was sold within the period. The mechanical consequence: more cost gets buried in ending inventory (carried forward as a balance-sheet asset), so reported expenses on the income statement are typically lower under absorption costing than under marginal costing — a quirk Pereira flags as something operators "must carefully take into account."

## Key attributes

- **Full cost recovery** — every fixed and variable cost is loaded into the unit cost
- **Apportionment** — overhead is distributed across cost units based on activity level
- **Inventory carries cost** — unsold goods carry fixed-overhead cost into the next period
- **Lower reported expense** — income-statement expenses are typically lower vs. marginal costing
- **GAAP/IFRS-friendly** — required for most external financial reporting
- **Decision distortion risk** — full-cost-per-unit can mislead short-term pricing decisions

## When to use

- External financial reporting and inventory valuation under GAAP/IFRS
- Long-run pricing where fixed costs must be recovered
- Cost-plus contracts where buyers expect full-cost recovery
- Mature manufacturing with stable overhead patterns
- Regulatory or tax filings that require full-cost inventory

## When NOT to use

- Short-term special-order or incremental-volume decisions (use marginal costing)
- Highly volatile production volumes where overhead-per-unit swings wildly
- Internal performance management where fixed costs are not controllable at unit level
- When inventory builds are large and you risk hiding losses in unsold stock
- Service businesses with minimal inventory and largely period costs

## Common pitfalls

- Building inventory to "improve" reported profit (overhead deferred to balance sheet)
- Using full unit cost to reject profitable special orders that cover marginal cost
- Picking arbitrary apportionment bases that distort product-line economics
- Failing to reconcile absorption-cost reports with cash flow reality
- Mistaking lower reported expense for better operational performance

## Key metrics

- **Unit cost** = (direct material + direct labor + variable overhead + fixed overhead absorbed) per unit
- **Overhead absorption rate** = total overhead / total activity base
- **Ending inventory value** at full cost
- **Gross margin** under absorption (vs. contribution margin under marginal)
- **Cost of goods sold (COGS)** with absorbed overhead
- **Over/under absorbed overhead** — actual vs. applied

## Decision questions

- Is reported profit moving because of operations or because of inventory build/draw?
- Are our apportionment bases still causally connected to overhead behavior?
- What does this product look like under marginal costing — would we still produce it?
- Are we under- or over-absorbing overhead, and is the variance explained?
- For external reporting we need absorption — but which internal decisions are we wrongly basing on full unit cost?

## Canonical examples

- **Manufacturers preparing audited financial statements** — required by GAAP/IFRS
- **Cost-plus government contractors** — bids based on full absorbed cost plus margin
- **Inventory-heavy retailers and consumer-goods makers** — inventory valuation at full cost
- **Vertically integrated industrials** — overhead apportioned across plants and product lines
