# On-Demand Healthcare Model

## Definition

The on-demand healthcare model rests on the principle that **"access is better than ownership"** — it provides immediate, individualised medical services without long-term contractual commitment, aiming to make healthcare "as easy as ordering a product online." Pereira frames it as the consumer-experience answer to a healthcare system bloated with friction (long waits, fragmented pricing, unclear access points). The model targets three goals — cost reduction, improved outcomes, enhanced patient experience — and operates across five touchpoints in the patient's healthcare cycle: personalized health solutions, information on request, patient experience benchmarks, care-anywhere networks, and telehealth. Pereira splits the model into two operational variants: **on-demand digital** (telehealth, virtual consults) and **on-demand on-site** (home services, medical transport, mobile diagnostics) — the differentiator being whether physical presence is delivered to the patient or the patient stays remote.

## Key attributes

- **Access over ownership** — patient buys the service event, not an ongoing relationship
- **Two delivery modalities** — fully digital (telehealth) or on-site delivered to the patient (home services, transport)
- **No long-term commitment** — no insurance enrolment, no provider lock-in, no membership required
- **Speed and convenience as the value proposition** — service time and ease are the SKUs being sold
- **Standardisation discipline** — price is standardised, service is standardised, delivery time is standardised; that's what makes "on-demand" credible
- **Cash-pay friendly** — works without insurance reimbursement; the friction of pre-authorisation kills the on-demand promise
- **Asset-light supply network** — a roster of credentialed clinicians, drivers, equipment partners; the platform doesn't own most of it
- **24/7 availability is implicit** — "on-demand" that's only available 9-5 isn't on-demand

## When to use

- Episodic, low-acuity needs — minor illness, prescription refills, urgent-care equivalent, basic diagnostics, vaccinations
- Markets with overcrowded ER/clinic infrastructure where alternatives are wanted but absent
- Affluent or middle-class consumer segments who'll cash-pay for convenience (Gulf and SEA core markets)
- Home services for elderly, post-surgery recovery, chronic disease monitoring — where transport is the actual barrier
- Mobile diagnostics (phlebotomy at home, mobile imaging) where the device is small enough to bring to the patient

## When NOT to use

- Complex chronic disease management requiring continuity — the lack of long-term relationship is the bug, not the feature
- Acute and emergency care that legally needs ER infrastructure
- Conditions requiring specialist follow-up, multi-disciplinary teams, or hospitalisation
- Markets with no consumer willingness to pay out-of-pocket and no payer parity
- Geographies without dense enough demand to keep supply utilisation viable (rural with no clusters)

## Common pitfalls

- **Supply collapse during off-peak hours** — physicians and home-service providers won't sit idle; without volume they leave the platform
- **Quality-control breakdowns** — moving care out of the clinic means losing the clinic's safety net; protocols, escalation pathways, and clinical governance must be explicit
- **Continuity gaps** — patient sees a different clinician each time, no consolidated record, errors compound
- **Regulatory mis-fits with home services** — many jurisdictions require specific licensure for home-based clinical care, controlled-substance handling outside facilities, etc.
- **Treating the model like Uber** — clinical liability is not rideshare liability; the platform cannot disclaim its way out of malpractice exposure
- **Underestimating unit economics of on-site** — fuel, vehicle, traffic, and clinician time per visit can make on-site economically unviable below a per-visit price point
- **No insurance integration** when patient willingness-to-pay caps grow — the model dead-ends without a B2B/B2B2C escape hatch

## Key metrics

- **Time to service** (book-to-arrival, book-to-consult) — the single defining metric of on-demand
- **Service completion rate** — booked → delivered
- **Repeat use rate** — without long-term contracts, repeat is the closest proxy to satisfaction and clinical fit
- **Supply utilization** — % of clinician/driver hours actively serving
- **Average cost per service** (loaded with supply, ops, platform, fixed)
- **NPS** by service type
- **CAC** by channel; **CLV** computed over 12-24 month windows given the episodic nature

## Decision questions

- What's the underlying friction we're removing — time, location, cost, embarrassment, access? (Different answers point to different product shapes.)
- Is the use case safe and effective outside a clinical facility?
- How do we maintain clinical governance when care is decentralised?
- What's our supply strategy when demand spikes (and when it dips)?
- Do we plan to layer subscription/membership on top to defend retention, or stay purely transactional?
- How do we hand off complex cases back into the traditional system without losing the patient?

## Canonical examples

- **Heal** (US) — on-demand home doctor visits
- **DispatchHealth** (US) — at-home urgent and acute care
- **Pager, CareClix** (US) — on-demand telehealth + escalation
- **Cura** (KSA) — on-demand video telehealth consult
- **Altibbi** (MENA) — on-demand Arabic telehealth
- **DocOnline** (India) — on-demand teleconsult + e-pharmacy
- **Ada Health** (Germany) — on-demand symptom checker + triage
- **Doctor Anywhere** (Singapore/SEA) — on-demand telemedicine + delivered medications
- **GoMeds, Bonyan, Vezeeta** (MENA) — on-demand home services and bookings adjacent to the core model
