# Automation-Based Business Model

## Definition

An automation-based business model relies on algorithms or automated systems to perform the operations that would otherwise require continuous human labor. Pereira positions it as one of three "rapid-expansion" model archetypes (alongside platform-based and ecosystem-based), arguing that any business in which the unit of value can be delivered without a corresponding unit of human effort can scale exponentially. The model spans a wide spectrum: from physical-but-automated businesses like ATM networks, through digital content delivery (downloads, info products), to algorithmic services like search and recommendation engines. The defining test is whether revenue can grow without proportionate growth in headcount or per-transaction human work.

## Key attributes

- **Algorithmic core** — important tasks are performed by software, not by people (page 58)
- **Near-zero marginal cost** — each additional transaction costs almost nothing to fulfill
- **Asynchronous delivery** — value is delivered without real-time human attendance
- **Minimal operational handoff** — exceptions occasionally surface to humans, but the default path is unattended
- **Up-front investment, long tail of returns** — significant build cost, then revenue without proportionate effort
- **Self-service customer journey** — the buyer transacts without assisted-sales involvement
- **Pairs with subscription or transaction pricing** — revenue mechanics that don't require renewed selling effort
- **Demands rock-solid operational reliability** — uptime, payment processing, and content delivery become the company

## When to use

- The unit of value can be encoded as software, content, or a programmable physical action
- The market is large enough that high-volume, low-touch sales clear meaningful revenue
- The cost of the automated infrastructure is recoverable within a reasonable build horizon
- The founder genuinely wants a low-headcount, low-touch operation (some founders hate this — know yourself)
- The category tolerates self-service without losing too much to assisted-sales competitors

## When NOT to use

- The value proposition genuinely requires human judgment, empathy, or bespoke configuration
- The category is dominated by relationship-driven enterprise sales — automation alone cannot win the deal
- The market is too small to make low-margin, high-volume economics work
- Regulatory requirements impose human-in-the-loop checks (e.g., medical advice, financial advice in many jurisdictions)
- Customer trust requires a visible human face — pure-automation brands can feel cold in trust-sensitive categories

## Common pitfalls

- **"Set it and forget it" delusion** — automated businesses still require monitoring, updates, fraud control, and exception handling
- **Cheap-front-end, expensive-back-end** — the automated buy flow is easy; the long tail of customer support is what kills margin
- **Under-investing in reliability** — when the system goes down, there is nobody to manually fulfill orders
- **Falling for the passive-income marketing trope** — building genuine automation is real work; "passive" is the end state, not the build state
- **Skipping the moat** — pure automation with no defensibility gets cloned by anyone with the same toolkit
- **Algorithm bias and opacity** — automated decisions at scale create regulatory and reputational risk if unchecked

## Key metrics

- **Hours of human intervention per $1k revenue** — the canonical automation ratio
- **Automation rate** — share of customer journeys completed end-to-end without human touch
- **Marginal cost per transaction** — should trend to near zero
- **Uptime / system reliability** — operational SLA proxy
- **Self-service conversion rate** — for digital products, the metric that says automation is working
- **Refund/dispute rate** — proxy for whether automation is producing acceptable quality at scale

## Decision questions

- Which operations in our value chain are mechanical and ripe for full automation?
- What is our cost per transaction today, and what does it look like if we 10x volume without adding headcount?
- Where does the human still need to be in the loop, and why?
- What is the failure mode if the automation breaks at 3am — and do we have alerting and fallback?
- Could a determined customer competitor replicate our automation in 12 months, and if so, what is our moat beyond the automation itself?

## Canonical examples

- **ATM businesses** — purchase and place machines, refill occasionally; passive income from fees (page 58)
- **Drop-shipping websites** — automated order routing to suppliers, no inventory (pages 58-59)
- **Infopreneur sites** — sell digital downloads (music, software, courses) delivered instantly on purchase (page 59)
- **Publishing platforms** — Kindle Direct Publishing, course platforms running on autopilot
- **Google Search** — algorithmic ranking, no human curates each query (page 40)
- **StitchFix recommendation engine** — algorithms drive retail selection at scale, 65+ data scientists encoding judgment once (page 40)
- **Amazon warehouses with robotics** — physical automation reducing per-order labor (page 38)
- **AWS** — cloud computing as self-service infrastructure, contributing 56% of Amazon's growth (page 39)
