# Top-Down Institutional EdTech Sales Model

## Definition

The top-down (or institutional) EdTech model sells directly to school districts, university administrations, or corporate L&D buyers — the entity that controls budget, procurement, and deployment — rather than to the individual learner or teacher. Pereira describes the playbook as: estimate the addressable market of institutions, decide on penetration targets, build a sales/marketing function to sign multi-school district contracts. The advantage is scale per deal: a single district contract grants access to dozens of schools and tens of thousands of seats. The disadvantage is the procurement cycle — long, RFP-driven, politically complex, vulnerable to budget cuts, and crowded with competitors offering similar functionality. Districts also expect substantial wraparound (user agreements, accessibility compliance, support SLAs, data privacy guarantees, professional development).

## Key attributes

- **District/institution as buyer** — not the teacher and not the student
- **Long sales cycles** — typically 6-18 months, often aligned to school-year budgeting windows
- **High ACV** — a single deal can be hundreds of thousands to millions of dollars
- **Procurement-heavy** — RFPs, security reviews, compliance attestations, accessibility (WCAG/Section 508)
- **Wraparound expectations** — implementation services, teacher training, dedicated support, integration with SIS/LMS
- **Budget seasonality** — purchase decisions cluster around fiscal year-end and back-to-school
- **Multi-stakeholder buying committee** — superintendent, curriculum director, IT, principals, sometimes school board
- **Renewal-driven economics** — winning is hard; losing the renewal is catastrophic given the CAC

## When to use

- Product solves an institutional pain (compliance reporting, standardized assessment, district-wide curriculum)
- ACV justifies a direct sales motion (>$25K/year per institution)
- The buyer is structurally an institution, not a teacher or learner (LMS, SIS, curriculum platforms)
- You can afford a 12+ month sales cycle before revenue lands
- Regulated environments (K-12 public, accredited higher ed, government training)

## When NOT to use

- Consumer-grade engagement apps where the teacher is the real adopter
- Small ACVs that can't support a direct sales rep's quota
- Pre-product-market-fit (the buy cycle eats too much runway)
- Markets where institutional purchasing has collapsed (some post-COVID public-school budgets)
- When you can credibly do bottom-up (free for teachers, monetize parents) — usually faster and cheaper

## Common pitfalls

- Underestimating the procurement cycle and burning runway before close
- Skimping on compliance/security investment; one failed district audit kills the pipeline
- Selling features the IT buyer wants while ignoring the teacher who must use it (low engagement → non-renewal)
- Insufficient wraparound (training, change management); product gets bought, never used, doesn't renew
- Competing only on price; districts buy on relationships and risk reduction as much as on price
- Ignoring data-privacy regulation (FERPA, COPPA in US; GDPR in EU; PDPL in KSA)

## Key metrics

- **ACV** — annual contract value per institution
- **Sales cycle length** — calendar days from first contact to signed PO
- **District / institution penetration** — % of addressable institutions under contract
- **Seat count** — students/teachers licensed per contract
- **Win rate** on competitive RFPs
- **Pipeline coverage** — pipeline value ÷ quota; healthy >3x
- **Renewal rate** — gross and net; expansion within district counts as net retention

## Decision questions

- Is the institution genuinely the buyer, or are we forcing top-down on a teacher/learner product?
- Can we afford a 12-month sales cycle before our first revenue lands?
- Do we have the compliance posture (FERPA / GDPR / PDPL / accessibility) institutional buyers require?
- Who's the actual executive sponsor inside the district, and what political capital does this purchase cost them?
- What's our wraparound plan to ensure adoption — not just purchase?

## Canonical examples

- **2U, Inc.** — sells SaaS platform to non-profit academic institutions to deliver their online degree programs
- **K12 Inc. (Stride)** — sells curriculum and online schooling platform to public school districts (K-12)
- **Canvas (Instructure), Blackboard, Moodle** — LMS sold to institutions
- **Schoology, PowerSchool** — SIS/LMS sold to K-12 districts
- **Tahsiel / Madrasati (Saudi Arabia)** — Ministry of Education-procured national learning platforms
- **Coursera for Government, Udemy Business** — enterprise tracks layered onto consumer platforms
