# Collaborative Consulting Model

## Definition

The collaborative consulting model is, in Pereira's framing (pages 14-15), a network of consulting firms or independent consultants who pool expertise, information technology, tools, and knowledge to meet client needs. By connecting work across firms, the network accesses broader knowledge while creating delivery flexibility. The model lowers fixed costs because it draws talent from outside the firm's payroll — but trades that for higher variable costs and demanding financial planning. Pereira distinguishes two sub-models: **closed networks** (a fixed set of firms collaborating repeatedly on client assignments, yielding deep knowledge sharing and refined value propositions through long-term relationships) and **open networks** (firms collaborate in shifting constellations, with partners entering and leaving projects mid-flight, demanding the network's capability to absorb new knowledge and adjust to demand). The EdenMcCallum case (pages 17-18, 23) is the canonical worked example: a European management consultancy that built a curated network of senior independent consultants whose expertise is offered to clients on a project-matched basis — partners sell, consultants deliver, both sides gain freedom and control.

## Key attributes

- **Network as supply** — capacity comes from outside the payroll
- **Two sub-models** — closed (fixed partner set) and open (shifting constellation per project)
- **Lower fixed cost** — no salaried bench; pay only when project lands
- **Higher variable cost** — sub-consultant fees scale with revenue
- **Client choice in matching** — clients can pick consultants, not just be staffed
- **Partners sell, network delivers** — separation of BD and delivery roles
- **Curated quality** — strong vetting and onboarding makes the network the brand
- **IP-sharing tension** — closed networks compound knowledge; open networks risk leakage
- **Flexible scale** — capacity ramps up and down without hire/fire cycles
- **Demands platform discipline** — matching, contracting, quality oversight, payment are operational lifts
- **Gig-economy fit** — taps the rising senior-freelancer trend Pereira documents

## When to use

- The firm wants senior-content density without carrying senior payroll
- Client demand is lumpy / project-based and a fixed bench would sit idle
- The brand is strong enough on the BD side to attract clients; quality of network is provable
- The talent pool of senior independents in target sectors is deep enough to source from
- The firm wants to enter new geographies or sectors without hiring there
- The operating team can run matching, contracting, and quality oversight as a real platform
- Clients value being able to choose their consultant rather than be staffed

## When NOT to use

- Engagements require long-term embedded teams with deep institutional context (closed payroll wins)
- The senior-freelancer talent pool is thin in the relevant sector / geography
- The firm can't enforce quality oversight across an external network
- Clients require single-employer accountability (regulated industries, government framework agreements with staff-vetting requirements)
- IP confidentiality demands payroll-only delivery
- The firm hasn't built the operational platform (matching, contracting, payments) — collaborative without infrastructure is chaos

## Common pitfalls

- **No quality bar across the network** — variance kills the brand
- **Underinvesting in matching** — wrong consultant on the wrong project; client churn
- **IP leakage** — open networks see methodology walk out to competitors via sub-consultants
- **Partner take-rate squeeze** — sub-consultants demand more of the fee; margin compresses
- **No standing relationships** — every project starts from zero; no compound learning
- **Mismanaged closed network** — fixed set ossifies, loses freshness
- **Mismanaged open network** — too transactional, no loyalty, no quality compound
- **Contracting drag** — slow NDAs, MSAs, SOWs per project kill responsiveness
- **Payment-cycle pain** — clients pay slow, sub-consultants want fast; working-capital strain
- **Regulatory misclassification** — sub-consultants treated as employees in jurisdictions with strict tests

## Key metrics

- **Active network size** — number of vetted consultants currently engageable
- **Match rate** — % of client briefs successfully matched within target SLA
- **Partner take-rate** — % of project fee retained by the partnership vs paid to network
- **Project margin** — gross margin per engagement after sub-consultant fees
- **Network utilization** — % of network engaged in a rolling window
- **Repeat-partner rate** — % of projects staffed with consultants used before (closed-network proxy)
- **Time-to-staff** — days from brief to consultant onboarded
- **Client satisfaction by consultant** — quality variance signal across network

## Decision questions

- Closed or open network? Closed compounds knowledge; open maximizes flexibility.
- What's our partner take-rate, and is it defensible given the value the partnership adds (BD, QA, brand)?
- Who owns the IP — the partnership, the consultant, or jointly?
- How do we onboard, vet, and offboard network members without bureaucratic drag?
- What's our quality oversight model — partner shadow, client survey, peer review?
- How do we keep top consultants from going direct to our clients?

## Canonical examples

- **EdenMcCallum** (pages 17-18, 23) — European management consultancy; curated network of senior independents; partners sell, consultants deliver, clients choose their consultant
- **Catalant (ex-HourlyNerd)** — US-based platform matching independent consultants to enterprise projects
- **Business Talent Group (BTG)** — independent-consultant marketplace acquired by Heidrick & Struggles
- **A.T. Kearney's Tomorrow** / **Bain's Expert Partners** — Big-firm experiments with external-talent networks
- **PB delivery-partner roster (Group B)** — Eendigo, BVI, AI Innovate (co-delivery capacity) — ProcessBay's open-network layer for KSA consulting engagements
- **GLG** (page 23) — adjacent variant: expert *network* sold by subscription (as-a-service) rather than as project-matched delivery

## Saudi context

In KSA the collaborative model is structurally important for boutique firms because (a) the talent pool of senior ex-Big-4 / ex-McKinsey consultants going independent is growing under Vision 2030, (b) project demand from PIF portfolio companies, MoCIT, and NTDP is lumpy and senior-heavy, and (c) Saudization quotas make fixed-bench leverage expensive for sub-scale firms. ProcessBay's existing delivery-partner taxonomy (Group B in the OneDrive structure — Eendigo, BVI, AI Innovate as co-delivery capacity) is a closed-network collaborative posture: a fixed partner set, repeated collaboration, deepening knowledge sharing. The model fits PB's pre-Aliph stance and remains useful post-merger for surge capacity on engagements where the Aliph leveraged firm doesn't have idle senior staff. Operational watch-outs in KSA: contracting and payment cycles are slower than in Western markets (government clients pay 90-180 days), so working-capital planning is non-trivial; sub-consultant misclassification under Saudi labor law is a real risk for any extended engagement; CR / GOSI obligations restrict how loosely a "network" can actually operate before it triggers staffing requirements.
