# Management Franchise

## Definition

A management franchise is a franchising variant where the franchisee invests capital and personally manages the day-to-day operations of the franchised unit, while the franchisor provides a proven business system, brand, training, and ongoing support. Pereira positions it as common in services-heavy industries (commercial cleaning, business services, certain food service categories) where the franchisee functions as an owner-operator-manager rather than a hands-on technician. The model attracts white-collar professionals from corporate backgrounds who bring management experience but lack industry-specific operational skills — the franchisor's system supplies the latter.

## Key attributes

- **Owner-manager model** — franchisee is the operating manager, not a passive investor
- **Proven business system** — franchisor provides documented operating playbook
- **Brand and marketing leverage** — franchisee operates under franchisor's recognized brand
- **Initial fee + ongoing royalties** — standard franchise fee structure
- **Heavy franchisor support** — training, field visits, marketing, IT, HR, accounting
- **SOP adherence required** — franchisee must follow franchisor's standard operating procedures
- **Franchisor retains brand and product control** — franchisee controls execution

## When to use

- Service-based business where management capability matters more than technical skill
- Targeting white-collar / corporate-refugee franchisees with management experience but no industry background
- Categories where day-to-day operations require hands-on owner oversight
- Markets where franchisees need a turnkey management system but want operational control
- Businesses with complex staffing requirements that benefit from owner-manager attention

## When NOT to use

- Categories better suited to absentee multi-unit ownership (use Area Development)
- Operations too simple to justify a full management layer
- Industries where technical mastery matters more than management ability
- Franchisees expect passive investment returns (mismatch in expectations)
- When franchisor can't deliver the heavy support the model promises

## Common pitfalls

- Finding franchisees with the right management profile is difficult
- Heavy franchisor support promised but underdelivered as the system scales
- High franchise fees impact franchisee profitability
- Dependence on franchisor creates fragility if franchisor falters
- Franchisees feel constrained by SOPs when local conditions demand adaptation
- Manager burnout — owner-manager role is demanding

## Key metrics

- Franchisee retention / renewal rate
- Royalty revenue per unit
- AUV (average unit volume)
- Franchisor support utilization (training hours, field visit count)
- SOP compliance audit scores
- New franchisee time-to-profitability

## Decision questions

- Does our concept need an owner-manager, or can it run with hired managers?
- What franchisee profile (industry experience, management experience, capital) succeeds?
- Can we sustain the heavy ongoing support the model promises?
- How do we measure franchisee performance without micromanaging?
- Where does management franchising vs. business format vs. area development fit best?

## Canonical examples

- **Jan-Pro** — commercial cleaning; 10,000+ franchisees in 15+ countries; owner-managers running cleaning crews under proven system (page 238)
- **Anytime Fitness** — owner-manager fitness clubs with strong franchisor support (page 237)
- **Sport Clips** — owner-managed haircut salons under detailed operational system (page 245)
- **The UPS Store** — owner-manager retail shipping/printing/business services franchise (page 240)
- **H&R Block** — tax preparation; owner-managers run local offices with franchisor systems (page 243)
- **Saudi/MENA examples** — Apparel Group's franchised retail concepts run by owner-managers
