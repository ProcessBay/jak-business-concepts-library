# Penetration Pricing

## Definition

Penetration pricing launches a new product or service at an intentionally low price to capture market share quickly, with the plan to raise prices once a customer base is established. Pereira positions it as the canonical strategy for entering markets with intense competition or when winning user base matters more than near-term margin — common among internet companies that "often fail to make a profit until they gain monopolistic power."

## Key attributes

- **Low introductory price** — set below competitive norm or below cost
- **Market share is the goal** — not profit, not margin
- **Time-bound by design** — meant to convert to standard pricing later
- **Creates barriers to entry** — competitors can't make the math work
- **Drives economies of scale** — volume reduces per-unit cost over time
- **Risks customer expectation lock-in** — raising prices later feels unfair
- **Distinct from predatory pricing** — penetration is legal market entry; predatory is below-cost intent to kill competitors

## When to use

- Launching into a market with established competitors
- Network-effect or two-sided platform businesses needing critical mass
- Categories where switching costs grow with usage (subscription, ecosystem)
- When unit economics improve dramatically with scale
- When you have capital runway to absorb thin or negative margins

## When NOT to use

- Premium-positioned products (low price destroys perceived quality)
- Commodity categories where you can't recover later (race-to-the-bottom)
- Markets where competitors can match your price indefinitely
- When churn risk is high (acquired customers don't stick)
- When the value proposition can't justify a price increase later

## Common pitfalls

- Triggering a price war with deeper-pocketed incumbents
- Setting customer expectation that the low price is permanent
- Acquiring price-sensitive customers who churn at first price hike
- Confusing penetration with predatory (legal exposure if below-cost intent)
- Forgetting to plan and execute the price normalization
- Burning cash longer than the business can sustain

## Key metrics

- **Market share** (the primary scoreboard)
- **CAC** (acquisition cost; penetration is a CAC subsidy)
- **Churn rate** (test of whether acquired users stick)
- **LTV** (long-term recovery of the upfront price discount)
- **Customer base growth rate** (week-over-week, month-over-month)
- **Path to standard pricing** (what % of base accepts the increase)

## Decision questions

- Can we sustain low-margin or negative-margin pricing long enough to win?
- Will the customers we acquire at this price be the ones we want at the future price?
- Do we have a credible mechanism for raising prices later (lock-in, switching costs, value proof)?
- Will competitors match us, escalating into a war we can't win?
- Are we structurally a penetration play, or are we using cheap pricing to mask a weak value prop?

## Canonical examples

- **Netflix** — low introductory streaming rates built the base that funded content + price increases (page 114)
- **Spotify** — free + low-priced premium drove rapid user-base growth (page 115)
- **Fast-food chains** — promotional launch pricing on new menu items (page 115)
- **Hospitality** — Diamond Lodging-style reduced first-night rates to capture repeat travelers (page 113)
