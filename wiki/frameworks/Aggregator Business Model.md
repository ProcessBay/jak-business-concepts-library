# Aggregator Business Model

## Definition

An aggregator business model creates value by collecting information, goods, or services from many providers and presenting them in one place for end customers — typically without owning the underlying supply. Pereira identifies several variants (data aggregator, news aggregator, video aggregator, smart-grid aggregator, affiliate e-commerce aggregator). Distinct from a marketplace (which facilitates transactions between buyer and seller), an aggregator often consolidates demand and uses that scale to gain leverage over suppliers — Ben Thompson's "Aggregator Theory" explains how aggregators can become winner-take-most: superior user experience attracts demand; concentrated demand makes suppliers compete for inclusion; this further improves the experience and entrenches the aggregator.

## Key attributes

- **Demand-side aggregation** — single point of access for many supply options
- **Zero marginal cost to add supply** — supply is digital/listed, not owned
- **User experience moat** — superior UX is the competitive advantage
- **Network/scale effects** — more users attract more supply; more supply attracts more users
- **Supplier competition** — suppliers compete to be visible on the aggregator's platform
- **Variant types** — data, news, video, affiliate, smart-grid, travel, food delivery
- **Take rate vs. ads** — aggregators monetize via ads (Google), commission (Booking, Expedia), or affiliate fees

## When to use

- Categories where supply is fragmented and discovery costs are high
- Markets where consumers want comparison across many providers (travel, insurance, mortgages)
- Digital categories where supply can be listed at zero marginal cost
- When superior search/filter/UX would meaningfully improve customer experience
- Where consumer brand power can be built on top of fragmented supplier brands

## When NOT to use

- Categories with concentrated supply (few suppliers to aggregate)
- When suppliers have strong direct relationships customers prefer
- When network effects are weak (no winner-take-most dynamic)
- When the aggregator can't sustain UX investment to stay ahead

## Common pitfalls

- Letting one supplier dominate platform supply (reduces aggregator leverage)
- Underinvesting in UX — competitors with better experiences peel users
- Failing to manage supplier-side disintermediation (suppliers try to convert users to direct relationships)
- Take-rate creep that pushes suppliers off-platform
- Confusing aggregator with pure marketplace (different supplier dynamics)

## Key metrics

- Supplier count
- Active user count
- Take rate or revenue per session
- User engagement (session length, return rate)
- Switching cost (do users use multiple aggregators or one?)
- Supplier dependency on aggregator (% of supplier revenue via your platform)

## Decision questions

- Are we genuinely aggregating supply, or just listing it?
- What's our UX advantage vs. alternatives, and is it widening or narrowing?
- Are suppliers becoming dependent on us, or are we becoming dependent on them?
- Where could a competitor with better UX displace us?

## Canonical examples

- **Google Search** — aggregates the world's information; ad-funded; classic aggregator (page 22)
- **News aggregators** — Flipboard, Feedly, Apple News+ (page 23)
- **Travel** — Kayak, Booking.com, Expedia (aggregate hotels, flights)
- **Food delivery** — DoorDash, Uber Eats (aggregate restaurants)
- **Insurance** — Policygenius, NerdWallet (aggregate insurance providers)
- **Shopping** — Amazon, even AllTop (deals aggregation)
