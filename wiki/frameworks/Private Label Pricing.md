# Private Label Pricing

## Definition

Private Label Pricing is the framework retailers use to price their own-brand (store-brand) merchandise relative to national brands on the same shelf. Pereira documents six pricing approaches: cost-plus, price parity, discount, premium, tiered, promotional, and dynamic. The strategic core: by removing the brand manufacturer's margin layer, retailers can either undercut the national brand on price (capturing share from price-sensitive shoppers) or match the national brand's price and pocket a structurally higher gross margin. Aldi takes this to the extreme — most of its assortment is private label — and the model is one of the chief reasons Aldi can offer 20-40% below traditional supermarket prices. The pricing decision is downstream of the *positioning* decision: is private label the budget option, the parity alternative, or the premium tier?

## Key attributes

- **Structurally higher margin** — retailer captures the brand-manufacturer margin layer
- **Pricing flexibility across tiers** — same store can run good/better/best private label tiers
- **Anchor-against-national-brand mechanic** — shelf placement next to branded SKU makes the value gap visible
- **Quality perception risk** — shoppers historically associate private label with lower quality (eroding)
- **Negotiation leverage on national brands** — credible private label option strengthens supplier negotiation
- **Loyalty hook** — strong private label (Trader Joe's, Costco Kirkland) creates store-exclusive demand
- **Six pricing approaches** — cost-plus, price parity, discount, premium, tiered, promotional/dynamic
- **Category-by-category strategy** — not all categories suit private label (luxury, fashion, tech harder)

## When to use

- Categories where shoppers care about function more than brand (paper goods, basics, commodities)
- Retailer has scale to commit minimum-order volumes to a contract manufacturer
- When national-brand category leaders are extracting unsustainable margins (room to undercut)
- Format positioned around value (warehouse club, discount grocer, mass merchant)
- When the retailer wants to deepen shopper loyalty through exclusive merchandise

## When NOT to use

- Categories where brand *is* the value proposition (luxury, prestige beauty, premium spirits)
- Without quality control infrastructure (one product failure damages the entire store brand)
- When private label volume can't justify the minimum manufacturing run
- When national brand suppliers will retaliate by withholding allocation or exclusive SKUs
- For categories where shoppers will only buy a name they trust (infant formula, prescription)

## Common pitfalls

- Pricing private label too close to the national brand — no incentive for shoppers to switch
- Pricing too far below — shoppers infer poor quality even if the product is identical
- Inconsistent quality across the private-label assortment damaging the umbrella brand
- Over-extending into categories where shoppers reject store-brand (specialty, premium-perception)
- Treating private label as a margin lever only — ignoring the loyalty / differentiation potential
- Letting national-brand suppliers reformulate to widen the gap with the private label copycat

## Key metrics

- **Private label penetration %** — share of total revenue from private label (Aldi >70%; US grocery avg ~18%)
- **Private label gross margin %** — typically 5-15 points higher than equivalent national brand
- **Price gap vs. national brand** — typically 15-30% below for discount tier; 0-5% for premium private label
- **Category attach rate** — share of shoppers buying at least one private label item per trip
- **Basket margin contribution** — total margin uplift from private label inclusion in mix
- **Quality complaint rate** — leading indicator of brand-erosion risk

## Decision questions

- Is our shopper buying the function or the brand in this category?
- Can we hold quality consistently enough to protect the umbrella store brand?
- What tier are we positioning — budget undercut, parity alternative, or premium store brand?
- Do we have the supplier relationships and minimum-volume capacity to make it work?
- How will the national brand respond — price cut, allocation pressure, new SKU strategy?

## Canonical examples

- **Aldi** — most of assortment is private label; structural foundation of low-cost model (pages 80, 129-131)
- **Costco Kirkland Signature** — premium private label tier; rivals national brand quality
- **Trader Joe's** — ~80% private label; curated narrow assortment; loyalty engine
- **Walmart Great Value, Equate, Mainstays** — tiered private label across categories
- **Target Good & Gather, Up & Up, Cat & Jack** — multi-tier private label with strong brand identity
- **Sainsbury's** Taste the Difference / By Sainsbury's / Basics — classic good/better/best private label
