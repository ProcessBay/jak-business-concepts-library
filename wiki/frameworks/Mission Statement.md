# Mission Statement

## Definition

A mission statement is a brief, official expression of the goals and principles guiding a company's operations. Pereira defines it as the document that "outlines what the company wants to accomplish and acts as a guide for decision-making, illustrating the path the organization and its employees will follow" (page 11). It is detailed, comprehensive, and memorable; it conveys the company's one-of-a-kind value to customers, employees, and stakeholders; and it reflects the company's culture and identity. Unlike the vision statement — which paints the future the company is trying to create — the mission statement describes the *current* nature of operations, the methods used to execute them, and the value the company delivers today. Pereira positions it as both a strategic instrument (focus, consistency, decision-filtering) and a cultural one (recruitment, employee alignment, public messaging).

## Key attributes

- **Brief and focused** — ideally one or two sentences, up to a short paragraph (page 22)
- **Three structural parts** — brand purpose (why we exist), brand values (what we stand for), brand goals (what we deliver to customers) (pages 20-21)
- **Operational, not aspirational** — describes daily operations vs. the vision's long-term future state (page 13)
- **Adaptable** — can evolve with shifting priorities while remaining consistent with the vision (page 14)
- **Customer-, employee-, and stakeholder-facing** — addresses all three audiences at once (page 11)
- **Identity-defining** — communicates culture, governance posture, and brand character (pages 17-18)
- **Decision-filtering** — provides a touchstone for everyday choices (page 25)

## When to use

- Founding a venture and codifying purpose before scaling
- Stress-testing strategic decisions against a stated purpose
- Recruiting talent whose values must align with the company
- Building brand differentiation in a crowded category
- Driving cultural consistency across geographies or business units
- Communicating purpose to investors, partners, and the public

## When NOT to use

- As a substitute for actual strategy — the mission orients strategy, it does not replace it
- As marketing copy disconnected from operational reality (the gap will be visible)
- To paper over internal disagreement about purpose — write it only when leadership genuinely shares the answer
- When the organization is too early to know what it is for — premature mission statements ossify wrong commitments

## Common pitfalls

- **Over-engineering length** — mission statements that try to be exhaustive become unmemorable (page 22)
- **Generic phrasing** — statements that any competitor could adopt fail the uniqueness test (page 28)
- **Buzzwords and jargon** — corporate-speak signals lack of conviction (page 80)
- **Treating it as a one-time exercise** — missions should be reviewed and adapted (page 14)
- **Drafting in isolation** — narrow ownership produces statements that don't reflect the team
- **Confusing mission and vision** — describing future aspirations under the mission label dilutes both documents

## Key metrics

- **Employee mission recall** — % of employees who can recite or paraphrase the mission
- **Decision alignment** — % of major decisions explicitly tested against the mission
- **Recruitment yield** — % of A-list candidates who cite the mission as a decisive factor
- **Brand recall** — does the public describe the brand using the mission's language?
- **Customer affinity** — purchasing decisions and loyalty correlated with mission resonance (page 10)

## Decision questions

- Can every employee recite our mission in their own words?
- Could a competitor adopt our mission unchanged? If yes, it is too generic.
- Does our mission actually shape decisions, or does it sit unused on a wall?
- Does it describe what we *do* (mission) or what we hope to *become* (vision)?
- When was it last reviewed against our current strategy?
- Does it speak to all three audiences — customers, employees, stakeholders?

## Canonical examples

- **Microsoft** — "Empower every person and organization on the planet to achieve more." (page 46)
- **Google** — "To organize the world's information and make it universally accessible and useful." (page 40)
- **Tesla** — "To accelerate the world's transition to renewable energy." (page 62)
- **Nike** — "Bring inspiration and innovation to every athlete in the world. (*If you have a body, you are an athlete.*)" (page 50)
- **Walmart** — "Save money, live better." (page 68)
- **Patagonia** — "To save our home planet." (page 52)
- **Starbucks** — "To inspire and nurture the human spirit — one person, one cup, and one neighborhood at a time." (page 57)
- **Life Is Good** — "To spread the power of optimism." (page 44)
- **TED** — "Spread ideas." (page 63)
- **Yeti** — "Build the cooler we'd use every day if it existed." (page 71)
- **LinkedIn** — "Connect the world's professionals to make them more productive and successful." (page 45)
- **Asana** — "To help humanity thrive by enabling all teams to work together effortlessly." (page 35)
