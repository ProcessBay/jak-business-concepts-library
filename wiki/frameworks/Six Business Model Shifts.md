# Six Business Model Shifts

## Definition

A taxonomy of six structural shifts modern business models are undergoing — patterns of evolution that successful firms are riding while laggards risk obsolescence. Pereira's framework (drawn from Business Models Inc. and HBR research): (1) **Pipeline → Platform** — value shifts from transaction to interaction; (2) **Analogue → Digital** — value shifts to digitally-mediated delivery; (3) **Products → Services** — value shifts from owning a product to subscribing to its outcome; (4) **Incremental → Exponential** — value shifts from linear growth to self-sustaining scaling; (5) **Shareholder → Stakeholder** — value broadens beyond financial returns; (6) **Linear → Circular** — value created in reusing and reducing waste.

## Key attributes

- **Six concurrent shifts** — most modern businesses are partially through several at once
- **Direction matters** — most successful firms are moving with the shifts, not against them
- **Not all-or-nothing** — partial shifts are common (hybrid pipeline-platform, services-product mix)
- **Industry-dependent timing** — software is far along all six; manufacturing earlier on most
- **Drivers** — technology disruption, commoditization, competition

## The six shifts

1. **Pipeline → Platform** (Uber vs taxis): transaction-based to interaction-based; value from network
2. **Analogue → Digital** (Picnic online supermarket): physical operations digitized; 70% cheaper cost structure
3. **Products → Services** (Netflix DVD-by-mail before streaming): outcome over ownership; recurring revenue
4. **Incremental → Exponential** (SpaceX): self-sustainable scaling vs. linear growth
5. **Shareholder → Stakeholder** (Starbucks crowdsourcing ideas in 2008): broader value beyond profit
6. **Linear → Circular** (Patagonia repair/resale): reduce-reuse-recycle as value creation

## When to use

- Strategic planning to identify which shifts your firm is undergoing
- Diagnosing why a business model is losing share (often against one of these shifts)
- Pre-investment screening — laggard businesses on multiple shifts are at risk
- Innovation portfolio: which shifts to ride first

## When NOT to use

- As a rigid roadmap (shifts unfold differently by industry)
- For pure operational decisions disconnected from strategy
- In categories where these shifts haven't reached yet (some B2B services lag)

## Common pitfalls

- Trying to ride all six shifts at once (usually impossible)
- Treating shifts as fads vs. structural changes
- Going through the motions (e.g., "we're a platform now") without actual model change
- Letting incumbents define your shift pace (often too slow)

## Key metrics

- # of shifts the business is actively pursuing
- Revenue share from "shifted" parts of the model
- Customer NPS on shifted offerings
- Time-to-market for shift-related initiatives

## Decision questions

- Which of the six shifts most threatens our current model if we don't ride it?
- Where are we partially through a shift — and stuck?
- Which shifts are competitors riding faster than us?
- What's our 3-year shift plan, and is it credible?

## Canonical examples

- **Pipeline → Platform**: Uber, Airbnb, app stores (page 14)
- **Analogue → Digital**: Picnic (online supermarket, no physical stores, 70% cheaper) (page 15)
- **Products → Services**: Netflix DVD subscription before streaming; SaaS vs. licensed software (page 15)
- **Incremental → Exponential**: SpaceX (page 15-16)
- **Shareholder → Stakeholder**: Starbucks crowdsourced ideas during 2008 financial crisis (page 16)
- **Linear → Circular**: Patagonia self-repair tools + resale + recycling (page 16-17)
