# Continuous Consulting Model

## Definition

The continuous consulting model centers on data analytics and subscriptions to deliver permanent solutions that clients use every day — benchmarking, diagnostics, pricing analytics, organizational design tooling, lean operations dashboards, corporate performance management — rather than discrete one-off engagements (Pereira, page 15). The consulting firm packages its expertise and strategic insights into tools and processes, migrating from one-time project income to an evergreen subscription stream that generates ongoing client value and recurring firm revenue. Pereira pairs this directly with the McKinsey Solutions case (pages 19-20, 23) — productized data, analytics, software, and advice combinations sold via licensing — and the GLG case (pages 23-24), where on-demand expert access is sold as a subscription that ensures network access. The shift, in Pereira's language, is from "consulting to solutions" and from "fixed fees to ongoing access." It rewards established brands (whose name carries subscription credibility) and is structurally harder for new firms to sell into cold.

## Key attributes

- **Recurring revenue** — monthly, quarterly, or annual subscription billing
- **Productized tools and dashboards** — the deliverable is software / data, not a deck
- **Daily-use value proposition** — clients deploy the tool in operations, not just consume a report
- **Repeated-use stickiness** — tools that help navigate regular problems generate retention
- **Expertise embedded in software** — consultant insight encoded in templates, models, algorithms
- **Brand-dependent sales** — established firms sell subscriptions easier than newcomers
- **Lower deal size, higher repeatability** — trades large project fees for steady recurring stream
- **Data flywheel potential** — usage data improves the tool, deepens client lock-in
- **Customer-success motion required** — like SaaS, retention is the game; CSM function is non-optional
- **Hybrid often** — subscription line coexists with bespoke advisory on the same accounts

## When to use

- The firm has codified insight across many engagements into a repeatable tool or methodology
- Client problems recur regularly — benchmarking, pricing refresh, performance reviews
- The brand is strong enough to be trusted with recurring access (not a first-engagement default)
- The firm wants to smooth project-revenue volatility with predictable recurring billings
- Tools or data the firm already owns can be packaged for license rather than rebuilt per client
- Building enterprise valuation premium via recurring-revenue line
- Existing engagement clients are good candidates to upsell into continuous offerings

## When NOT to use

- The firm hasn't codified its IP into a deployable tool / dashboard / dataset
- Client problems are genuinely bespoke and don't recur in patterns
- The brand is unestablished — cold subscription sales for unknown firms are very hard
- The firm lacks the engineering / data capability to build and maintain software-grade deliverables
- Customer-success capacity is unavailable — churn will compound
- The market sees the offer as commoditized data, not differentiated insight

## Common pitfalls

- **Building software the firm can't maintain** — consultant-built tools rot without engineering
- **Underpricing the subscription** — anchored on hourly rates rather than ongoing value delivered
- **Selling once, abandoning** — no CSM, no usage support, churn happens at year-one renewal
- **Mistaking retainer for subscription** — retainer is buy-time; subscription is buy-access; different motions
- **Cannibalizing bespoke margin** — clients buy subscription instead of full engagement
- **Data quality drift** — benchmark tools depend on fresh data; stale data destroys the value prop
- **Newcomer trying cold** — Pereira (page 15) flags this explicitly: harder for new firms to sell
- **No usage instrumentation** — can't see who's using what; can't preempt churn
- **Compliance / data residency** — KSA and EU clients need local data residency; ignored at first, painful at scale

## Key metrics

- **MRR / ARR** — monthly / annual recurring revenue
- **Churn rate** — logo and revenue; target <10% annual for B2B subscriptions
- **NRR (Net Revenue Retention)** — >100% means expansion exceeds churn
- **ARPU** — average revenue per subscribing account
- **Subscription revenue share** — % of firm revenue that's recurring (valuation premium driver)
- **Tool adoption rate** — % of seats / functions actively used; leading churn indicator
- **Renewal rate** — % of subscriptions renewed at term end
- **Onboarding time-to-value** — days from contract to first usage; correlates with retention
- **CAC payback** — months of subscription revenue to recover acquisition cost

## Decision questions

- What's the daily-use value proposition for the tool, and who in the client uses it?
- Is our subscription priced on the value delivered or on hourly equivalents?
- Do we have CSM capacity to drive adoption and renewals?
- Is the offer differentiated insight, or is it commodity data the client can source elsewhere?
- What's the data pipeline that keeps the tool fresh, and who owns it?
- Does our brand carry enough trust to sell recurring access cold, or do we need to wedge in via bespoke first?

## Canonical examples

- **McKinsey Solutions** (pages 19-20, 23) — 20+ productized data/analytics tools sold via licensing; "from consulting to solutions" shift
- **GLG (Gerson Lehrman Group)** (pages 23-24) — on-demand expert network sold as subscription; "as-a-service consulting"
- **Bloomberg Terminal + advisory** (page 22) — data subscription with consulting overlay
- **Gartner / Forrester research subscriptions** — recurring access to analyst research with advisory add-ons
- **PSFK** (page 19) — trend research reports sold to clients; productized expertise as recurring offering
- **Benchmarking subscriptions** (compensation, pricing, ops) — Mercer, Willis Towers Watson, Hay Group
- **Utopian platform (ProcessBay, 2026-)** — the recurring-revenue line PB is building alongside its bespoke consulting; structurally a continuous consulting offer to the founder ecosystem

## Saudi context

In KSA the continuous consulting model is nascent but accelerating: Vision 2030 has created sustained government demand for benchmarking (PMO performance dashboards), sector intelligence (MoCIT-CODE digital adoption), and recurring data products (NTDP grant-eligibility scoring). Big-4 KSA practices have started bundling subscription dashboards with their advisory contracts. The challenge for ProcessBay is the one Pereira names directly (page 15): subscription consulting is much easier to sell when the brand is established. PB's path is to lead with productized consulting and bespoke wedge engagements, then upsell continuous offerings (Utopian platform access, NTDP grant intelligence, benchmarking dashboards) into clients already in delivery. The Utopian platform — already live as jak-business-library.vercel.app for internal use — is the most defensible continuous offering PB has in flight, positioned for founder ecosystem distribution post-Aliph. Government clients (PIF, MoCIT, NTDP) typically need data residency in KSA, so any continuous offering needs Saudi-hosted infrastructure; this is a real procurement gate. The model rewards patience: subscription credibility compounds over years, not quarters.
