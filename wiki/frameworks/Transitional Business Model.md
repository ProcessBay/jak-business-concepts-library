# Transitional Business Model

## Definition

A transitional business model is a deliberately temporary format a company runs in order to enter a new market or niche (usually small or non-scalable at first), test traction, and check whether the venture is sustainable and profitable. Pereira frames it as a phase, not a destination: management knows from day one that the model will end up somewhere else. The transitional phase lets the firm secure capital while validating the venture; based on early results the company then consolidates, iterates, or abandons the idea — avoiding large losses. Canonical cases include Tesla (luxury Roadster funding the mass-market EV roadmap), Netflix (DVD-by-mail to streaming), Facebook (Harvard-only social network to global platform), Uber (car-sharing to multi-modal mobility platform), and Amazon (online bookstore to everything-store to AWS). Pereira's frame: every business is transitional at some point — the difference is whether the phases are planned or forced.

## Key attributes

- **Temporary by design** — known from the outset to be a bridge, not the end state
- **Phased** — eases the start process and builds a solid foundation for the target model
- **Customer-driven design** — built in response to market demand, easier for new entrants than incumbents
- **Regulation-contesting** — often violates existing rules (Uber, Airbnb), forcing legal evolution
- **Brand-building** — can seed demand for a future product before the market asks for it (Amazon drones)
- **Adaptable** — exists to absorb shifts in customer behavior, technology, and regulation
- **Three exit decisions** — consolidate, iterate, or abandon based on validation results
- **Capital-efficient** — funds the next phase from the current phase's revenue (Tesla pattern)

## When to use

- Entering a new market where demand, regulation, or technology is still uncertain
- When the target business model is too capital-intensive to launch directly
- When a smaller, premium, or niche segment can fund the path to mass-market
- During industry-wide shifts (e.g., perpetual-license software to SaaS) where customers won't accept an overnight switch
- When the brand needs market presence before the full offering is ready
- For incumbents whose current revenue stream is structurally declining

## When NOT to use

- When the target model is already proven and capital is available — go direct
- When the bridge phase risks burning more capital than it raises (no clear traction path)
- When the transitional offering damages brand positioning for the target model
- When customers and team members can't tolerate the ambiguity of a known-temporary model
- When regulation makes the transitional phase commercially impossible (not just contested)

## Common pitfalls

- Treating the transitional model as permanent — losing sight of the target state
- Rushing the transition, breaking cash flow before the new model gains traction
- Switching the whole team rather than mixing old know-how with new expertise
- Failing to communicate the transition to customers (they think the value proposition changed, not the model)
- Letting the transitional model's economics define what's possible at the target state
- Underestimating how long the shift takes — usually multi-year, not multi-quarter

## Key metrics

- **Traction signals** in the current phase (sign-ups, retention, repeat purchase)
- **Unit economics** that fund the next phase (Tesla's per-Roadster margin → R&D for Model S)
- **Segment penetration** of the minimum viable segment before expansion
- **Conversion-to-target-model** rate (e.g., perpetual-license customers migrated to SaaS)
- **Runway** through the transition (cash burn vs. new revenue ramp)
- **Brand association** with the target offering before full launch

## Decision questions

- What is the target business model, and what specifically does the transitional phase de-risk?
- Which customer segment can we serve well now that funds the path to where we want to go?
- What are the consolidate / iterate / abandon criteria, and when do we evaluate them?
- How long can we sustain the transitional phase if traction is slower than planned?
- How will we communicate the shift to existing customers without destroying their trust?
- Which current revenue streams will phase out, and what bridges cash flow until the new one scales?

## Canonical examples

- **Tesla** — Roadster (2008) high-priced luxury EV funded R&D for Model S/X/3/Y mass-market vehicles (pages 10-12)
- **Netflix** — DVD-by-mail (1998) → DVD subscription (1999) → streaming → Netflix Originals (pages 13-15)
- **Facebook** — Harvard-only "The Facebook" (2004) → Ivy League → US universities → global social network → advertising platform (pages 16-19)
- **Uber** — ride-hailing (2009) → bikes, boats, helicopters → mobility platform replacing personal car ownership (pages 20-21)
- **Amazon** — online bookstore (1994) → "everything store" → marketplace → AWS, Prime, Kindle, Advertising (pages 22-25)
- **Software industry** — perpetual-license vendors transitioning to SaaS subscription, often running both models concurrently (pages 26-28)
