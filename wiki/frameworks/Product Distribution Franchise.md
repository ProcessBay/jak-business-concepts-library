# Product Distribution Franchise

## Definition

A product distribution franchise (also called trade-name franchising) is the original franchising form, in which the franchisor — typically a manufacturer — grants the franchisee the right to sell its branded products in a defined territory using the franchisor's trademark and logo, but without supplying a complete operating system. Pereira traces it to the 1862 Singer Sewing Machine Company network. The franchisee functions primarily as a distributor or retailer, purchasing products from the franchisor at wholesale and reselling at retail. It dominates in food and beverage (Coca-Cola, Pepsi), automotive parts, gasoline stations, and beverage distribution. Distinct from a business format franchise: the franchisor licenses brand and product, not the full operational playbook.

## Key attributes

- **Product is the core deliverable** — franchisor's branded goods sold by franchisee
- **Wholesale-retail margin** — franchisee buys at wholesale, sells at markup as primary revenue
- **Manufacturer-distributor relationship** — franchisor owns production; franchisee owns local sales
- **Less operational rigor than business format** — no full SOP playbook required
- **Territory exclusivity** — franchisee usually granted defined geographic area
- **Minimum purchase requirements** — franchisor often mandates volume commitments
- **Supply chain control** — franchisor benefits from guaranteed downstream channel

## When to use

- Manufacturer wants distribution scale without building owned retail
- Product is the brand (consumers come for the product, not the store experience)
- Local distributors have better last-mile knowledge than corporate
- Capital constraints prevent direct retail expansion
- Industries with established wholesale-retail dynamics (beverages, fuel, auto parts)

## When NOT to use

- Customer experience matters more than product (use business format instead)
- Product margin is too thin to support two-tier markup
- Brand requires tight service standards a distributor can't enforce
- E-commerce direct-to-consumer model offers better economics
- Categories where vertical integration outperforms distribution

## Common pitfalls

- Markup squeeze — franchisee margins crushed between wholesale price and retail competition
- Minimum purchase requirements create cash flow strain for franchisees
- Lack of operational support causes inconsistent customer experience
- Channel conflict when franchisor opens direct-to-consumer channels
- Territory disputes when franchisor adds new distributors
- Inventory risk falls on franchisee — overstocking erodes profit

## Key metrics

- Wholesale markup and gross margin
- Sales volume per territory
- Inventory turnover rate
- Minimum purchase compliance
- Customer reach within territory
- Cost per unit delivered

## Decision questions

- Is the product itself the value proposition, or is the experience the value?
- Can our franchisees survive the wholesale-retail markup math?
- What minimum purchase volume keeps franchisees solvent?
- Where would direct-to-consumer cannibalize our distributors?
- How exclusive can territories be without slowing geographic coverage?

## Canonical examples

- **Coca-Cola, Pepsi** — bottler/distributor networks worldwide (page 20)
- **Singer Sewing Machine Company** — the 1862 original (page 18)
- **Gasoline retail** — branded fuel stations (Shell, Exxon, ARAMCO/Saudi Aramco)
- **Auto parts distributors** — branded parts retailers
- **Saudi/MENA: Saudi Aramco fuel stations** — Aramco-branded retail across the Kingdom
- **Apparel Group** — Gulf distributor for global apparel brands (Tommy Hilfiger, Aldo)
