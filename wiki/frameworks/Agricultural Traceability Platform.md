# Agricultural Traceability Platform

## Definition

An Agricultural Traceability Platform uses IoT sensors, inventory software, databases, and blockchain to track agricultural products from the farm of origin through the supply chain to the final consumer. Pereira frames it as a primarily B2B model: the buyer is not the consumer but the brand, processor, or retailer who must prove provenance, organic certification, cruelty-free practice, GMO-free status, or absence of forced labor — because end-consumer expectations now make these proofs a competitive necessity. Two structural forms exist: (1) a **white-list model** that certifies and lists qualifying farms from which buyers may source, and (2) the more complex **batch traceability model** that assigns unique identifiers to product batches and tracks them across the supply chain end-to-end. Monetization combines certification survey fees, B2B data subscriptions, and (increasingly) sale of aggregated farmer data to agri-allied firms and lenders.

## Key attributes

- **B2B-first model** — brands/processors/retailers pay; consumers only benefit
- **Two structural forms** — white-list certification vs. batch unique-ID traceability
- **Tech stack** — IoT + database + (typically) blockchain for tamper-evidence
- **Multi-stakeholder** — farmers, certification bodies, brands, field surveyors, regulators
- **Three monetization paths** — certification fees, data subscriptions, farmer-data commercialization
- **Trust and recall infrastructure** — enables rapid product withdrawal in safety events
- **ESG / regulatory tailwind** — tightening rules on supply-chain disclosure pull demand
- **Low CAC, but modest LTV per Pereira** — few clients needed, but the deal size is moderate

## When to use

- Premium / specialty agricultural categories where provenance commands a price premium (organic, single-origin coffee, halal-certified, Saudi Made)
- Categories with strong recall/safety risk (meat, dairy, produce)
- Markets where regulators are tightening supply-chain disclosure (EU CSRD, FSMA 204)
- When you have access to certification bodies whose standards are in demand
- Export-oriented agricultural sectors needing buyer-side proof for foreign markets
- Cross-border halal certification (high MENA relevance)

## When NOT to use

- Commodity categories where the marginal consumer won't pay for provenance
- Markets where no regulator and no buyer demands traceability (no pull)
- Brands that already operate vertically-integrated supply chains (they own the data)
- Markets where farmer literacy / connectivity is too low to capture origin data at scale
- When blockchain is the answer to a problem the customer doesn't have (don't over-engineer)

## Common pitfalls

- Building blockchain-for-its-own-sake when a simple database would do
- Over-indexing on consumer-scan QR codes — consumers rarely scan; B2B is where the value is
- Treating onboarding as a tech problem when it's a field-officer problem
- Certifying farms without ongoing audit (one fraud event collapses platform trust)
- Underpricing the data product (the farmer-data B2B side is often the largest line item)
- Failing to design for recall scenarios (the value moment is the bad day)

## Key metrics

- **Certified farms onboarded** (supply-side network)
- **Traceable SKUs / volume covered** (% of buyer's procurement traced)
- **Audit cycle time** (cost-to-certify)
- **Recall response time** (the demonstrable ROI moment)
- **Certification revenue** vs. **data subscription revenue** vs. **farmer-data sales** (mix)
- **Scan rate** (consumer engagement, mostly a marketing metric)
- **Renewal / multi-year contract rate** (B2B stickiness)

## Decision questions

- Who actually pays — the brand, the processor, the retailer, the regulator?
- Is the buyer paying for marketing (provenance story) or risk management (recall)?
- Do we need blockchain, or is the demand actually for a trusted database?
- What is our certification authority — own brand, partner standards body, regulator-recognized?
- Can we monetize farmer data as a third revenue line, or is that a privacy minefield in our jurisdiction?
- For MENA: do we have a halal/Saudi-Made angle that compounds the standard provenance pitch?
