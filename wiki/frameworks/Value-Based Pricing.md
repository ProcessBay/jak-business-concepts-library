# Value-Based Pricing

## Definition

Value-based pricing sets price according to the value the customer perceives or receives from the product — not the cost to produce it. Pereira's formula: `Price = Cost + Value (perceived benefit to customer)`. The classic example: a software CD costs the same to press regardless of program, but Photoshop and a free utility command vastly different prices because the value delivered differs. It is the structurally highest-margin pricing approach when perceived value materially exceeds production cost, and the default for SaaS, professional services, luxury, and any differentiated B2B offering.

## Key attributes

- **Customer-perceived value drives price** — not cost, not competitor
- **Requires deep customer research** — interviews, willingness-to-pay studies
- **Highest margin potential** — captures consumer surplus competitors leave
- **Defensible via differentiation** — works best with unique or superior products
- **Quantified value formula** — translate benefit into monetary terms (hours saved × hourly rate, revenue lift, cost avoided)
- **Segment-sensitive** — different segments perceive different value, can support price tiers
- **Vulnerable to brand-image collapse** — Pereira notes fashion reverts to cost-plus when brand value deteriorates

## When to use

- SaaS and professional services where outcomes vary wildly by customer
- Premium and luxury brands with strong perceived quality / status signals
- Differentiated B2B products where ROI can be quantified
- Categories where customer is buying outcome, not feature parity
- When you have research insight into willingness-to-pay across segments

## When NOT to use

- Commoditized products where customers price-shop
- New product with no customer data on perceived value yet
- Heavily regulated industries where prices are set by rule
- When you can't articulate or evidence the value claim
- When sales cycle is too short to do value discovery

## Common pitfalls

- Asserting value without quantifying it — customer dismisses the claim
- Setting one price across segments with very different willingness-to-pay
- Confusing your view of value with the customer's view
- Forgetting to refresh value research as the market matures
- Letting cost creep dilute the value-capture ratio
- Misjudging — pricing too high alienates, too low signals low quality

## Key metrics

- **Perceived value** (monetized benefit per customer or segment)
- **Willingness-to-pay** (max each segment will spend)
- **Value capture ratio** = price / perceived value (lower = more customer surplus)
- **Gross margin** (typically 70%+ in SaaS value-based pricing)
- **Win rate by price tier** (helps locate the perceived value ceiling)

## Decision questions

- Can we quantify the value our product delivers, in dollar terms, for at least our top segment?
- What's the customer's next-best alternative, and what does it cost (the value baseline)?
- Are we capturing 10-30% of the value we deliver, or leaving most on the table?
- Do our different segments perceive different value? Should pricing reflect that?
- What evidence will we show in the sales conversation to make the value claim stick?

## Canonical examples

- **Apple** — premium pricing on iPhones, Macs justified by perceived design/UX value (page 178)
- **Tesla** — sustainability + tech + experience justify EV premium (page 178)
- **Gucci, Rolex** — luxury brands charge on exclusivity and craftsmanship (page 178)
- **Project management SaaS** — $50 cost + $300 perceived monthly time savings → $350/seat (page 178-179)
- **Specialty coffee shops** — 2× competitor price justified by quality and experience perception (page 177)
