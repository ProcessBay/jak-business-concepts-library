# Three Horizons of Growth

## Definition

McKinsey's Three Horizons of Growth is a portfolio framework that forces an organization to allocate strategic attention and capital across **three time-horizon buckets simultaneously**: **H1 — Core Business Optimization** (defending and improving today's revenue engine), **H2 — Emerging Opportunities** (medium-term new growth bets that are visible but unproven), and **H3 — Future Growth and Transformation** (long-term breakthrough innovations and disruptive business models that may not pay back inside the current planning cycle). Pereira positions it as a digital transformation tool because DX programs reliably fail when they over-concentrate in one horizon — pure H1 (process automation only) produces efficiency without growth; pure H3 (moonshot innovation only) starves the core; balance is the discipline. The framework treats portfolio risk as the unit of analysis, not individual projects.

## Key attributes

- **Three time horizons** with different risk profiles, time-to-payback, and management cadence
- **H1 generates the cash** that funds H2 and H3
- **H2 has medium uncertainty** — validated market signal, unvalidated execution
- **H3 is option-value** — bets where outcome distribution is wide and right-skewed
- **Dynamic, not static** — H3 wins migrate into H2 and eventually H1 as they mature
- **Resource allocation discipline** — explicit % of capital, headcount, leadership attention per horizon
- **Different governance per horizon** — H1 uses NPV/payback, H3 uses option-value and learning milestones

## When to use

- Setting enterprise digital transformation strategy across a multi-year horizon
- Quarterly portfolio reviews where leadership must decide where the next dollar goes
- M&A pipeline strategy — classifying acquisition targets by horizon
- Conglomerates with mature businesses needing growth optionality
- Sovereign-portfolio contexts (PIF, KSA Vision 2030 verticals) — natural fit for horizon thinking
- When the board pressure is short-term and H3 needs explicit protection

## When NOT to use

- Single-product startups — almost everything is H2/H3; the framework adds little
- Distressed organizations — survival requires H1 focus, not portfolio balance
- Organizations with no governance discipline — the framework becomes a slide, not a tool
- Very short planning horizons (under 18 months)

## Common pitfalls

- **H1 dominates capital allocation** — the loudest stakeholders (operators) always win the budget
- **H3 becomes a vanity portfolio** — under-resourced "innovation theater" with no real path to scale
- **No graduation mechanism** — H2 wins never get promoted to H1; H3 wins die in pilot
- **Same governance across horizons** — applying NPV to H3 kills option-value bets prematurely
- **Static allocation** — set 70/20/10 once and never revisit as horizons evolve
- **Confusing horizons with maturity** — H3 is about disruption potential, not company age
- **Letting H3 be the CEO's pet project** — without organizational fit, it fails on succession

## Key metrics

- **Revenue mix by horizon** (target: ~70/20/10 for stable enterprises, but context-dependent)
- **Capital allocation by horizon** (often diverges from revenue mix — H3 underfunded)
- **H1 efficiency gains** (cost-out, margin lift, NPV)
- **H2 pipeline value** (risk-adjusted)
- **H3 option value** (qualitative or real-options math; learning velocity)
- **Horizon graduation rate** — how often H2 bets become H1, H3 bets become H2
- **Leadership attention share** by horizon (often the strongest leading indicator)

## Decision questions

- What % of capital, leadership time, and headcount sits in each horizon today vs target?
- Which H1 bets are extending the core vs masking decline?
- Which H2 bets have validated demand and need execution capital?
- Which H3 bets have we kept alive for more than 24 months without a graduation path?
- What is our graduation rate from H3→H2→H1? If zero, the framework is decorative.
- Are we measuring H3 with H1 metrics? (NPV applied to moonshots = killing them early)

## Canonical examples

- **Amazon** — AWS started as H3 (cloud was non-core), graduated to H2 (proven market, scaling), then H1 (dominant cash engine); Kindle and Alexa followed similar arcs (page 102)
- **General Electric** — Predix (Industrial IoT) was an H3 bet; partial graduation, painful retrenchment (page 109)
- **Goldman Sachs** — Marcus consumer banking was H2/H3; deliberately separated from H1 investment banking (page 108)
- **Walmart** — Jet.com / Flipkart acquisitions were H2 acceleration moves to compete with Amazon (page 103)
- **PIF (KSA Public Investment Fund)** — operates an explicit horizon-balanced portfolio: Aramco/SABIC = H1, Lucid/STC = H2, NEOM/Tonomus = H3
- **NEOM / Tonomus** — almost entirely H3 (sovereign-funded option on a cognitive city); risk profile is the textbook H3 case
- **STC Group (KSA)** — telco core (H1) + STC Pay (H2, now matured to H1) + Center3/data + AI ventures (H3)
- **Aramco Digital** — Industrial AI ventures function as H2/H3 for Aramco's core energy H1
