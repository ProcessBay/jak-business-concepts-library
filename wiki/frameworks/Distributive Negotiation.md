# Distributive Negotiation

## Definition

Distributive negotiation — also called competitive or win-lose negotiation — treats the negotiation as a contest over a fixed pie of value, where any gain for one party is a loss for the other. Pereira frames it as adversarial bargaining: each side claims as much value as possible, often at the other's expense. Negotiators take rigid positions, restrict information sharing, deploy competitive tactics (bluffing, extreme anchoring, ultimatums), and prioritize immediate gains over long-term relationships. The classic example is a one-shot price negotiation over a commodity: there is nothing to "expand," only to divide. Distributive negotiation is not inherently unethical — it is the rational approach when the pie genuinely cannot be expanded and the relationship has no future value. It becomes destructive when applied to situations where integrative gains were possible but missed.

## Key attributes

- **Fixed-pie assumption** — value is finite and zero-sum
- **Positional bargaining** — parties anchor on demands rather than interests
- **Limited information sharing** — disclosure is treated as weakness
- **Competitive tactics** — bluffing, anchoring, extreme initial offers, ultimatums
- **Short-term orientation** — relationship continuity is not a priority
- **Concession-driven** — progress measured by who moves first and how much

## When to use

- One-off transactions with no future relationship (commodity purchase, asset sale)
- Pure price negotiations where no other dimensions matter
- When the counterparty insists on distributive play and integrative attempts fail
- When you have a strong BATNA and want to extract maximum value
- Closing-stage haggling on a single residual issue

## When NOT to use

- Long-term partnerships, joint ventures, strategic alliances
- Internal negotiations (with employees, between divisions)
- Complex multi-issue deals where trades create joint value
- Situations where reputation effects spread beyond this counterparty
- When the relationship itself is part of the value being negotiated

## Common pitfalls

- Defaulting to distributive when integrative gains were available
- Anchoring so aggressively that the counterparty walks away
- Damaging long-term relationships for short-term gains
- Confusing toughness with effectiveness — distributive players often leave value on the table
- Underestimating reputational spillover ("this is how they negotiate")
- Falling for the other side's distributive frame when you intended integrative play

## Key metrics

- **Share of value captured** — your slice of the disputed pie
- **Distance from opening to close** — concession magnitude
- **Number of concessions made** — frequency of movement
- **Final terms vs. BATNA** — surplus extracted above walk-away
- **Time to close** — speed (distributive often closes faster)

## Decision questions

- Is the pie really fixed, or am I missing integrative opportunities?
- Will I ever deal with this counterparty again?
- What is my BATNA, and is theirs weaker?
- Am I willing to risk walk-away to extract more value?
- Are reputational spillovers acceptable?

## Canonical examples

- **Single-issue price haggling** — commodity or used-asset sale (pages 51-52)
- **Procurement RFP closing** — final price negotiation after specs locked
- **Salary negotiation** at hiring without future relationship variables
- **Hard bargaining** in supplier contracts where buyer leverages competition (page 60)
