# Growth Hacking

## Definition

Growth hacking is a mindset and methodology for driving rapid, scalable business growth through low-cost, high-impact, data-driven tactics. Pereira frames it as a deliberate departure from traditional marketing: rather than promoting an existing product, growth hackers treat the entire business — product, acquisition, activation, retention, monetization, virality — as a system of testable hypotheses. Originating in resource-constrained startups, the discipline has crossed into established companies seeking to revitalize growth. Five principles define the approach: agile and data-driven execution, cost-effectiveness, scalability, customer-centricity, and creative experimentation. The growth hacker's job is to find the unconventional lever — Airbnb on Craigslist, Dropbox's storage-for-referrals, Hotmail's email footer — that traditional marketing budgets cannot buy.

## Key attributes

- **Mindset, not a function** — cuts across product, marketing, engineering, data
- **Data-driven by default** — every tactic instrumented and measured
- **Iterative experimentation** — A/B test, learn, kill, scale
- **Low-cost / high-leverage** — favors organic, viral, and product-driven channels over paid spend
- **Customer-centric** — built on deep understanding of behavior and pain points
- **Funnel-aware** — operates across the full AARRR pipeline (Acquisition → Activation → Retention → Revenue → Referral)
- **Creative and unconventional** — channel innovation matters more than channel volume

## When to use

- Early-stage startups with limited acquisition budget
- Established firms whose paid-marketing growth has plateaued
- Product categories where word-of-mouth and virality are achievable
- When the team has — or can build — strong analytics and experimentation infrastructure
- Businesses pursuing rapid scale (venture-funded, market-window-driven)

## When NOT to use

- Categories where customer acquisition is structurally offline (heavy enterprise sales, regulated medical, government)
- When the product is not yet at product-market fit (growth hacks amplify problems as well as wins)
- Teams without analytics literacy or A/B testing infrastructure
- Brand-led categories where the experimentation mindset clashes with brand consistency
- When the firm cannot tolerate the risk of public-facing experiments going wrong

## Common pitfalls

- Hacking growth on top of a leaky product (retention dies before acquisition matters)
- Chasing vanity metrics (sign-ups, downloads) instead of revenue/retention
- Copying tactics without understanding why they worked for the original company
- Treating growth hacking as a marketing-only function (excludes product/engineering levers)
- Over-rotating on virality in categories where it is structurally impossible
- Skipping foundational steps (audience, objectives, KPIs) and jumping straight to tactics

## Key metrics

- **AARRR funnel metrics** — acquisition, activation, retention, referral, revenue
- **CAC** (Customer Acquisition Cost)
- **LTV** (Customer Lifetime Value)
- **Conversion rates** at each funnel stage
- **Viral coefficient (K)** — referrals per user
- **Cohort retention curves**

## Decision questions

- Have we defined our target audience, objectives, and KPIs before picking tactics?
- Is our product retained well enough that pouring more users in will compound, not leak?
- Which AARRR stage is currently bottlenecking growth?
- What is our most underused low-cost channel?
- Do we have the analytics and experimentation infrastructure to learn from what we ship?
