# Angel Investment

## Definition

Angel investors are high-net-worth individuals — often successful entrepreneurs or industry operators — who invest personal capital in early-stage startups in exchange for equity, typically before institutional venture capital is accessible. Pereira positions angels as the bridge between bootstrapping/F&F money and Series A: they accept higher risk, write smaller checks (a few thousand to several million dollars), and bring something institutional money cannot — operator-level mentorship, industry relationships, and credibility signal. An angel's involvement validates the startup for the next round; their networks open doors to customers, partners, and follow-on investors. Some angels invest solo, others through syndicates or angel groups that pool capital and diligence.

## Key attributes

- **Personal capital, not institutional** — angels invest their own money
- **Early-stage focus** — pre-seed and seed, occasionally Series A
- **Equity in exchange** — typical dilution varies by stage, valuation, and check size
- **Mentorship and networks** — operator angels add value beyond cash
- **Validation signal** — credible angels attract follow-on VC interest
- **Variable involvement** — some active board-style, others passive
- **Syndication common** — angel groups (Keiretsu, ACA) pool deals
- **Exit-oriented** — angels expect acquisition or IPO to realize returns

## When to use

- Pre-seed or seed stage with a working prototype or early traction
- Founder lacks industry connections that a domain-expert angel provides
- Round size is too small for institutional VC (sub-$1M)
- Need to validate the market signal before approaching VCs
- Want operator mentorship, not just capital
- Geographic markets where VC is thin (regional angels fill the gap)

## When NOT to use

- Capital need exceeds what angels comfortably write (move to seed VC)
- Cap table risk — accumulating too many small angels creates governance friction
- Mismatched angels (passive money where operator expertise was needed, or vice versa)
- When equity dilution at low valuation is permanently expensive
- Highly regulated industries where angel diligence is insufficient

## Common pitfalls

- Stacking too many small angels — unwieldy cap table that scares Series A investors
- Accepting "dumb money" — angels who add no expertise, just dilute the founder
- Misaligned exit timelines (angel wants 3-year flip; founder wants 10-year build)
- Underestimating angel involvement expectations (board seat surprises)
- Skipping legal docs — handshake angel deals become disputes later
- Confusing the "validation" signal — wrong angel can actually hurt VC perception

## Key metrics

- **Check size** — typical angel: $25K-$250K solo; syndicates $250K-$2M
- **Pre-money / post-money valuation** — frames dilution
- **Dilution per round** — angels typically take 5-25% combined
- **Runway extension** — months added by the round
- **Time to close** — angel rounds can close in weeks vs months for VC
- **Investor NPS / activity** — how much the angel actually engages

## Decision questions

- What does this angel bring beyond capital — networks, expertise, credibility?
- Will their involvement help or hurt our chances at Series A?
- Are we aligned on time horizon, exit expectations, and involvement level?
- Is the valuation reasonable for the risk-stage we're at?
- Can we close this round fast enough to keep momentum?
