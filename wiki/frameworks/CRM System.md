# CRM System

## Definition

A Customer Relationship Management (CRM) system is the centralized platform that stores, organizes, and surfaces customer data across the full lifecycle — contact information, interaction history, deal status, communication logs, and behavioral signals. Pereira calls it the "backbone" of sales automation: every automated workflow (scoring, nurturing, forecasting, reporting) reads from and writes to the CRM. Modern CRMs evolved from 1980s contact databases into cloud-based, AI-augmented platforms that integrate with email, marketing automation, support, and ERP systems. The CRM's job is to give the entire sales team — and increasingly marketing and support — a single, real-time view of every customer so that interactions are informed, consistent, and personalized.

## Key attributes

- **Centralized customer repository** — single source of truth for contact, history, and deal data
- **Interaction tracking** — emails, calls, meetings, web visits logged automatically
- **Pipeline and deal management** — visual stages, forecasting, win/loss tracking
- **Workflow automation** — triggers, assignments, follow-up reminders, approvals
- **Reporting and analytics** — KPI dashboards, cohort and funnel analysis
- **Integration hub** — connects to email, marketing automation, support, billing, ERP via APIs
- **Real-time accessibility** — cloud-based, mobile, available to whole team simultaneously
- **AI-augmented insights** — lead scoring, next-best-action, churn prediction layered on top

## When to use

- Any sales team with more than a handful of active deals or contacts
- Multi-rep teams where deal handoffs and consistency matter
- Long sales cycles needing memory across many touchpoints over months
- Organizations where marketing-to-sales lead handoff is friction-heavy
- Businesses scaling beyond what spreadsheets and inboxes can handle
- B2B contexts where account-based selling requires deep client context

## When NOT to use

- Solo founder or pre-revenue stage where a spreadsheet still works
- When the team will refuse to enter data — an empty CRM is worse than no CRM
- Cases where simpler tools (shared inbox, lightweight pipeline tracker) suffice
- When the chosen platform is over-engineered for the actual workflow (Salesforce for a 3-person team)

## Common pitfalls

- Poor data hygiene — duplicates, missing fields, stale records degrade every downstream automation
- Low rep adoption because data entry is treated as overhead, not a tool that helps them
- Over-customizing the platform until upgrades break and admins are mandatory
- Treating the CRM as just a contact list rather than the operating system of sales
- No integration with email/calendar/marketing — reps double-enter data
- Choosing tools by feature checklist rather than fit for the team's actual workflow
- Skipping training; reps revert to old habits within weeks

## Key metrics

- **Data Completeness** — percentage of required fields populated per record
- **User Adoption Rate** — percentage of reps actively using the CRM weekly
- **Pipeline Velocity** — value moving through pipeline per unit time
- **Win Rate** — deals won / deals worked
- **Customer Retention Rate** — measurable through logged interactions
- **Time-to-Data-Entry** — lag between activity and CRM update (lower is better)
- **Integration Count** — number of connected systems sharing data with CRM

## Decision questions

- What is the smallest set of data fields reps must update for the CRM to be useful?
- Will our reps actually use this, and what will make data entry feel like leverage not overhead?
- Which existing systems must the CRM integrate with on day one?
- Who owns CRM admin and data quality going forward?
- Are we choosing a platform we will grow into, or one that fits today's reality?
- What's our migration plan if our current data lives in spreadsheets or another CRM?
