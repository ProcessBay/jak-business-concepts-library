# Distribution Channel

## Definition

Distribution Channels is the BMC block that defines how a firm reaches customers — across awareness, evaluation, purchase, delivery, and after-sales phases. Channels deliver value propositions to segments and serve as the firm's touchpoints with customers. Pereira distinguishes three structural forms (direct, indirect, hybrid) and multiple instantiations (personal sale, internet, telephone, mail, retailers, representatives, distributors), and frames channel selection as a strategic choice driven by company goals, practicality, natural partnership opportunities, and conflict minimization.

## Key attributes

- **Five phases** — Awareness → Evaluation → Purchase → Delivery → After-sales; channels can span all or specialize in some
- **Three structural forms** — Direct (own salesforce, e-commerce), Indirect (retailers, distributors), Hybrid (mix)
- **Multiple instantiations** — personal sale, internet, telephone, mail, retailers, representatives, distributors
- **Each phase has different economics** — awareness is high-volume low-conversion; delivery is operational
- **Channel mix shapes margin** — direct = higher margin, harder to scale; indirect = lower margin, more reach
- **Conflict risk** — multi-channel strategies create channel conflict (own e-commerce vs retail partners)

## When to use

- Designing GTM for a new product or market entry
- Diagnosing why CAC is rising (a channel is degrading)
- Considering channel expansion (new geographies, new partner types)
- Evaluating channel conflict (your direct e-commerce vs your retail partners)
- Optimizing channel mix to match changing customer behaviors

## When NOT to use

- For purely B2B enterprise sales where "channels" usually means salesforce structure, not retail
- When the customer relationship is entirely automated and the term "channel" is moot
- For pure digital products with single-channel delivery (it's still a channel, but the analysis is thin)

## Common pitfalls

- Over-investing in one channel and ignoring others (concentration risk)
- Treating channels as fixed when customer buying behavior shifts (e.g., DTC trend reshaping retail)
- Ignoring after-sales channels — most firms underinvest in service after purchase
- Channel conflict left unaddressed (cannibalizing your own retail partners)
- Confusing channel (how customer reaches you) with relationship (what kind of interaction they have with you)

## Key metrics

- Revenue per channel
- CAC by channel
- Channel margin (after channel cost / commission)
- Time-to-market via channel
- Customer NPS by acquisition channel (often varies dramatically)
- Channel concentration (top channel as % of total)

## Decision questions

- For each customer touchpoint: which phase does it serve and what's its economics?
- Where is our channel mix concentrated and is that risk acceptable?
- Are direct and indirect channels in conflict or complementary?
- Which channel is the customer's preferred — and are we strong there?
- What channels are emerging that we should pilot?

## Canonical examples

- **Apple** — hybrid: own retail stores, online direct, Apple authorized resellers, App Store as distribution (page 180)
- **Amazon** — direct e-commerce + marketplace; later AWS as a channel for cloud services (page 184)
- **Netflix** — direct only; streaming via apps on consumer hardware (page 191)
- **Nike** — hybrid: own retail (Nike Town), wholesale (Foot Locker), direct e-commerce, Nike app (page 195)
- **IKEA** — predominantly direct via own stores; supplemented by catalog and online (page 204)
- **Coca-Cola** — heavily indirect: franchise bottlers, retailers, restaurants, vending (page 224)
