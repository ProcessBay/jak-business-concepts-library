# Empathy Map Canvas

## Definition

The Empathy Map Canvas is the updated version of David Gray's empathy map. It replaces the traditional four quadrants (Says/Thinks/Does/Feels) with a goal-anchored, seven-section structure that expands the range of inputs the customer receives. The canvas starts with **Who** (the persona) and **Do** (the goal), then works clockwise through See, Say, Do, Hear, and ends with Think and Feel — including a dedicated Gains and Pains pass at the centre. The structural shift is meaningful: by starting with the goal, the canvas forces the team to anchor every observation against what the customer is trying to achieve, not just what they're doing or saying.

## Key attributes

- **Goal-first sequencing** — section #1 is "Who are we empathising with?" and section #2 is "What do they intend to DO?". Every downstream observation is read against this goal (pages 30-31).
- **Clockwise discipline** — Goal → See → Say → Do → Hear → Think & Feel. The order is intentional: external observable inputs first, internal mental state last (page 30).
- **SEE adds environment** — section #3 captures the marketplace, the immediate environment, the design trends and competitor products the customer is exposed to (pages 32-33).
- **HEAR adds auditory inputs** — section #6 covers podcasts, media, friends, family, hearsay. Often the silent driver of brand perception. Note what's first-hand vs. hearsay so you can spot misconceptions to correct (pages 34-35).
- **Think & Feel separated from Say & Do** — section #7 explicitly distinguishes inner monologue (conscious) from feelings (subconscious). Customers can rate a "good outcome" device negatively if the experience felt difficult (page 36).
- **Gains and Pains** — closing pass inside Think & Feel: Gains = what they hope to achieve (wants, needs, hopes); Pains = challenges, fears, anxieties — real or imagined, often inherited from past brand experiences (page 36).
- **Per-persona** — one canvas per user persona; never composite (page 31).

## When to use

- When the traditional four-quadrant map feels too thin — particularly when environmental and auditory context matter (consumer goods, B2C, lifestyle products).
- When the team needs to anchor research against a specific goal or job — the goal-first structure is what makes this version more rigorous than the original.
- As a feeder for a Value Proposition Canvas — Gains and Pains map directly to Gain Creators and Pain Relievers.
- When integrating empathy work with a Business Model Canvas — the Customer Segment block becomes much richer when fed by a goal-anchored canvas.
- B2B settings where you need to track what stakeholders see in trade media, hear from peers, and the political/cultural inputs around their decision.

## When NOT to use

- When you don't have the time or research depth to fill seven sections rigorously — a thin seven-section canvas is worse than a sharp four-quadrant one.
- When the team is new to empathy mapping — start with the traditional [[Empathy Map]] to build the muscle, then graduate to the canvas.
- When the goal is unstable or unclear — the canvas falls apart without a real anchor in section #2.
- For lightweight interview prep — the original four quadrants do the job faster.

## Common pitfalls

- **Skipping the Goal section** — teams jump to See/Say/Do because they feel productive, but every downstream box is incoherent without the anchor (pages 30-31).
- **Filling Think & Feel from imagination** — the hardest section, the easiest to fake. Pereira flags this explicitly as "most difficult" (page 35); if you can't cite a source for an entry, it's a hypothesis, not data.
- **Confusing Think with Say** — they are different on purpose. Words often fail to convey true intent (page 35).
- **Ignoring Hear** — most teams treat marketing as a Say/See problem and miss the podcast/peer/family channel where perception actually forms (pages 34-35).
- **Treating Pains as only product pains** — Pereira explicitly extends Pains to fears inherited from past experiences with other brands (page 36). This is gold for differentiation.
- **One canvas, many personas** — averaging kills the tool. One canvas per persona, period (page 31).

## Signals of good use

- Section #2 (Do) reads as a concrete goal, not "use the product"
- Each Think & Feel entry has a citation (interview, observation, recording)
- Gains and Pains are specific enough to be tested
- The canvas changes the next Value Proposition or roadmap conversation

## Decision questions

- What is this persona actually trying to do — name the job, not the feature interaction.
- For each section, what evidence backs each entry — observation, quote, survey, or assumption?
- Which Pains are inherited from prior brand experiences vs. genuinely caused by our category?
- Where does this persona hear about brands like ours — and are we present in those channels?
- Have we separated conscious thinking from subconscious feeling?

## Canonical examples

- **Quick-cleaning vacuum cleaner for middle-to-upper class housewives** — Pereira's worked B2C example: persona defined (Who), goal = reduce house-chore burden (Do), steps mapped (research → purchase → delivery → setup → use), with each step tied to a business lever (advertising, financing, delivery chain, support) (pages 30-32).
- **Orthopaedic bed brand** — surface motivation = "good night's sleep"; deeper investigation via Think & Feel revealed most buyers had lower back pain, which became a stronger marketing point. Classic case for the Think & Feel separation and the Pains pass (page 36).
