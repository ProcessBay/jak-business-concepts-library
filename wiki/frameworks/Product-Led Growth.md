# Product-Led Growth (PLG)

## Definition

Product-Led Growth is a go-to-market strategy where the product itself is the primary driver of customer acquisition, conversion, and expansion — rather than traditional sales and marketing. In PLG, users discover, try, and buy the product with minimal human interaction. Pereira: "A successful PLG strategy gets the SaaS products in the hands of customers as quickly as possible, hence, solving their problems almost immediately. If a customer gets unique value from a product or service right away, it can then attract other new customers."

PLG is the opposite of sales-led growth, where SDRs cold-call prospects, AEs demo the product, and account managers close deals. In PLG, the product IS the salesperson.

## Key attributes

- **Self-serve onboarding** — users can sign up and get value without talking to anyone
- **Time-to-value < 5 minutes** — core value must be delivered almost instantly
- **Freemium or free trial** — low/no barrier to first use
- **Viral loop potential** — product gets better with more users (collaboration, network effects)
- **Bottom-up adoption** — individual users adopt first, then spread within organization
- **Product-Qualified Leads (PQLs)** — users who have demonstrated product engagement become leads for expansion/upsell
- **Data-driven optimization** — every step of the user journey is measured and optimized

## PLG vs. Sales-Led vs. Hybrid

| Dimension | Product-Led | Sales-Led | Hybrid (most common) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Primary driver** | Product | Sales team | Both, sequenced |
| **CAC** | Lower (product does the work) | Higher (salaries + commissions) | Medium |
| **Deal size** | Smaller, high volume | Larger, low volume | Both |
| **Time to close** | Minutes/days | Weeks/months | Mixed |
| **Customer segment** | SMB, prosumer, developer | Enterprise, complex B2B | All |
| **Onboarding** | Self-serve | White-glove | Tiered |
| **Examples** | Slack, Dropbox, Calendly | Salesforce, Workday, Oracle | HubSpot, Notion, Zoom |

## The PLG flywheel

```
Free Signup → Quick Time-to-Value → Active Usage 
     ↑                                      ↓
  Viral Loop ← Network Effects ← Delighted User
```

1. **Acquisition:** Free signup or trial removes friction
2. **Activation:** User experiences core value within first session
3. **Engagement:** Habit formation through repeated use
4. **Expansion:** User hits limits, upgrades to paid tier
5. **Virality:** User invites teammates (collaborative products) or shares (content products)
6. **Retention:** Product becomes embedded in user's workflow

## When to use

- Product can deliver core value without human onboarding
- Target users are comfortable self-discovering software
- Network effects or collaboration features exist (more users = more value)
- Viral coefficient > 0.5 (each user brings at least 0.5 new users)
- CAC via traditional sales is prohibitive for target price point
- Product has "aha moment" that can be reached in <5 minutes
- Target market is large but deal sizes are small (SMB/prosumer)

## When NOT to use

- Product requires complex implementation or integration
- Target customers are enterprises with procurement processes
- Regulatory/compliance requirements mandate human review
- Product has no viral loop or network effects
- Average deal size >$50K (sales-led is more efficient)
- Users need extensive training before getting value
- "Aha moment" requires significant data input or setup time

## Common pitfalls

- **Confusing PLG with "no sales"** — PLG companies still have sales teams, but they focus on PQLs (product-qualified leads) not cold prospects
- **Making free tier too generous** — users never hit limits, never upgrade
- **Ignoring enterprise needs** — PLG creates bottom-up demand, but enterprise deals still need sales to close
- **Poor onboarding** — if users don't reach value in first session, PLG fails
- **No expansion path** — free users love the product but never have a reason to pay
- **Measuring vanity metrics** — signups mean nothing if activation rate is <20%

## Key metrics

| Metric | Definition | Healthy benchmark |
|---|---|---|
| **Time-to-Value** | Minutes from signup to first "aha moment" | <5 minutes |
| **Activation Rate** | % of signups who reach core value | >30% |
| **Free-to-Paid Conversion** | % of free/trial users who convert to paid | 2-5% freemium; 15-30% free trial |
| **PQL Conversion Rate** | % of product-qualified leads who buy | >20% |
| **Virality Coefficient (K)** | New users generated per existing user | >0.5 |
| **NPS** | Net Promoter Score | >40 |
| **DAU/MAU Ratio** | Daily active users / Monthly active users | >20% |

## Decision questions

- Can a new user experience our core value without talking to anyone?
- What's our "aha moment," and how long does it take to reach?
- Do we have a natural viral loop (invites, collaboration, sharing)?
- What's our free-to-paid conversion rate, and what's the industry benchmark?
- If we go PLG, what's the role of sales — PQL closers, or pure support?
- Should we start PLG-first and add sales later, or vice versa?
- What's our expansion revenue mechanism — seat growth, feature gates, or usage limits?

## Canonical examples

- **Slack** — team invites team members; product gets better with more users
- **Dropbox** — free storage + referral program = viral growth
- **Calendly** — every meeting invite is an advertisement for the product
- **Zoom** — free 40-minute meetings; easy to try, natural to upgrade
- **Notion** — individual users adopt, then spread to entire teams
- **Figma** — design collaboration = every shared link is a user acquisition event
