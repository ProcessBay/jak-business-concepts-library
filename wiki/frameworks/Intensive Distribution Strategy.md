# Intensive Distribution Strategy

## Definition

Intensive distribution places a product in the maximum number of retail outlets possible — convenience stores, vending machines, gas stations, supermarkets, pharmacies, big-box stores — so that the consumer encounters it everywhere a purchase decision might be made. Pereira documents it as the standard play for convenience-goods producers (Pepsi cans, instant noodles, toothpaste, soaps, Doritos, Budweiser) where impulse buying, brand recall, and physical availability are the primary drivers of sales. The logic is intuitive: revenue is a function of territory covered, and category leaders defend share by being the brand consumers see first when they cannot find their usual choice. The trade-off is cost (it is expensive to be everywhere), thin margins (intensive distribution products are usually low-priced), and limited control over how hundreds or thousands of retailers handle the brand.

## Key attributes

- **Maximum retail coverage** — every relevant outlet, with no exclusion
- **Convenience-goods focus** — works best for low-consideration, frequently purchased items
- **Impulse-driven sales mechanic** — physical availability triggers purchase
- **Brand recall amplified** — repeated exposure across many touchpoints
- **Low margin per unit** — economics depend on volume, not premium pricing
- **Expensive to maintain** — logistics, merchandising, and distributor management at scale
- **Limited retailer control** — too many accounts to police merchandising standards tightly
- **Numeric and weighted distribution metrics** govern execution discipline

## When to use

- Convenience goods, FMCG, and low-priced consumer staples
- Categories where consumers buy whatever is on the shelf if their preferred brand is absent
- Mature brands with the marketing budget to defend ubiquitous availability
- When competitors are intensively distributed (matching them is table stakes)
- New product launches in categories where trial volume matters more than margin

## When NOT to use

- Premium or luxury products (intensive distribution destroys scarcity)
- High-consideration purchases where customers research before buying
- Products requiring expertise to sell or install
- Small firms lacking the capital, sales force, or logistics to cover so many doors
- Categories with thick margins that can support more selective approaches

## Common pitfalls

- Underestimating the cost of merchandising and replenishment across thousands of outlets
- Out-of-stocks at high-velocity doors (lost sales, brand frustration)
- Losing control over how products are displayed, priced, or promoted in store
- Being squeezed by retailers who exploit the manufacturer's dependence on shelf presence
- Cannibalizing higher-margin direct channels with low-margin retail
- Confusing "intensive" with "extensive" — intensive maximizes outlets within a channel; extensive uses many channel types simultaneously
- Failing to fund the marketing required to translate availability into preference

## Key metrics

- **Numeric distribution** — % of all outlets in category carrying the SKU
- **Weighted distribution** — % of category sales accounted for by outlets carrying the SKU
- **Out-of-stock rate** at point-of-sale (target <5% in priority outlets)
- **Shelf share / facings** vs. competitors
- **Retail doors covered** by region/channel
- **Impulse conversion rate** (uplift when prominently displayed)
- **Cost-to-serve per outlet** (the channel must remain economic)

## Decision questions

- Is our category genuinely an impulse / convenience purchase, or are we forcing intensity on a considered category?
- Can our margin structure absorb the cost of merchandising and replenishment at scale?
- Do we have the sales force and distributor relationships to police execution?
- Will intensive availability strengthen or dilute our brand?
- What's our out-of-stock rate today — and do we have the supply chain to keep up?

## Canonical examples

- **Pepsi, Coca-Cola** — beverage cans available in every conceivable outlet (pages 30, 34)
- **Newspapers** — daily intensive distribution at kiosks, gas stations, cafes (page 30)
- **Toothpaste manufacturers** (Colgate, P&G) — convenience stores, big-box, online (pages 31, 49)
- **Doritos, Budweiser** — snacks and beer with maximum retail presence (page 31)
- **Saudi context — Almarai dairy** — chilled trucks reach every supermarket, convenience store, and corner grocery (baqala) across the Kingdom; one of the most intensive cold-chain distribution networks in the GCC
- **Saudi context — Salla and Zid merchant networks** — enable Saudi SMEs to achieve "digital intensive distribution" by listing simultaneously across multiple online channels, marketplaces, and social commerce
- **Saudi context — Tamimi Markets and Panda hypermarket distribution** — high-velocity FMCG availability across Saudi metros
- **Saudi context — Sadafco (Saudia milk, ice cream)** — direct-store-delivery across thousands of outlets
