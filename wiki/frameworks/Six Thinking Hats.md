# Six Thinking Hats

## Definition

Six Thinking Hats is Edward de Bono's parallel-thinking framework (introduced in his 1985 book of the same name) for examining a decision or idea from six distinct perspectives, one at a time. Each "hat" represents a mode of thought: **Logic** (facts and details), **Optimism** (worth and advantages), **Devil's Advocate** (risks, obstacles, limitations), **Emotion** (feeling, intuition, gut instincts), **Creativity** (opportunities, possibilities, fresh concepts), and **Management** (process and rule-following). Pereira places it among the "lateral thinking" brainstorming methods. The discipline is that the whole group wears the same hat at the same time (parallel thinking) — collapsing argument into structured exploration. It is the canonical decision-review and idea-stress-testing meta-framework: most teams default to mixed-hat chaos; the Six Hats force structure on the conversation.

## Key attributes

- **Six modes of thinking** — Logic, Optimism, Devil's Advocate, Emotion, Creativity, Management
- **Parallel thinking** — whole group wears the same hat together (not adversarial roles)
- **Sequenced** — one hat at a time, in order, prevents argumentative debate
- **Inclusive** — even introverts can contribute under the structured prompt
- **Decision-friendly** — designed for evaluating ideas, not generating them
- **Surfaces blind spots** — most teams over-index on one hat without knowing it
- **Time-boxed per hat** — typically 5-15 min per perspective

## When to use

- Evaluating a major decision before committing
- Reviewing a strategy, plan, or proposal
- Post-brainstorming convergence (test top ideas through all six lenses)
- Team conflicts where competing perspectives are masquerading as facts
- Board-level decisions where each member has implicit hat preferences
- Workshops where you need structured discussion, not free chaos

## When NOT to use

- Pure divergent ideation (use Classic Brainstorming or SCAMPER)
- Time-pressured operational decisions (overhead doesn't pay back)
- For root-cause analysis (use 5 Whys or Fishbone)
- When the team has no facilitator (the hat discipline is hard to self-enforce)
- For execution problems (this is a thinking framework, not a doing framework)

## Common pitfalls

- People wearing their preferred hat regardless of the assigned one
- Allowing mixed-hat debate to creep back in
- Skipping the Emotion hat ("we don't do feelings here" — it surfaces real signals)
- Treating Devil's Advocate as the "no" person (it's everyone's job in that round)
- No timer per hat — the early hats consume all the time
- Confusing it with assigning fixed roles per person (it's parallel, not divided)

## Key metrics

- **Perspectives covered** (did each hat get genuine airtime?)
- **Decision confidence delta** (pre vs post)
- **New risks/opportunities surfaced** (vs what was on the table going in)
- **Time per hat** (process discipline)

## Decision questions

- What is the specific decision/idea we're hat-ing?
- What order will we wear the hats? (Logic first is common; Creativity often last)
- How do we handle senior people who refuse the discipline?
- Will we capture per-hat outputs separately, then synthesize?

## Canonical examples

- **Strategy review** — board evaluates a market-entry plan through all six hats
- **Product launch decision** — team stress-tests the launch from each angle
- **Conflict resolution** — competing views reframed as different hats, not opposed sides
- **Post-brainstorming filter** — top 3 ideas evaluated through six-hat sequence
- **Decision retrospective** — what hats did we wear too much / too little when we decided X?
