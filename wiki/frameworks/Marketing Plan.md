# Marketing Plan

## Definition

A marketing plan is a meticulously crafted roadmap that delineates a company's marketing goals, strategies, and tactics aimed at reaching and captivating its intended audience. Pereira frames it as a blueprint that facilitates organization, implementation, and assessment of marketing initiatives — but emphasizes it is **not a document to be drafted and archived**. It is a dynamic, flexible entity that should be continually updated in response to market shifts. A complete marketing plan integrates one or more marketing strategies into a unified framework, links them to broader business goals, details specific tactics and campaigns, and defines how success will be evaluated. It typically spans 1-3 years and is refined yearly as part of the budgeting process.

## Key attributes

- **Roadmap, not archive** — dynamic document, continually updated to reflect market and business shifts
- **Eleven elements** — objectives, current positioning, market research, target market, activities calendar, KPIs, marketing mix, competition/USP, strategies, budget, monitoring mechanism
- **SMART objectives** — specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, time-bound goals aligned with the brand
- **Customer-centric** — built around target market definition and buyer personas
- **Aligned with business strategy** — marketing objectives derive from and reinforce business plan goals
- **Budgeted and resourced** — every dollar mapped to an initiative with expected ROI
- **Measured continuously** — KPIs and monitoring mechanism baked in from the start
- **1-3 year horizon** — shorter than business plan (5 years), longer than tactical campaigns

## When to use

- Founding or relaunching a venture and need to align marketing effort with business objectives
- Annual budgeting cycle — refine the plan yearly as part of resource allocation
- Launching a new product, entering a new market, or executing a major repositioning
- Securing internal alignment across marketing, sales, product, and customer service
- Demonstrating to leadership / board / investors how marketing budget produces business results
- When marketing spend has been ad-hoc and ROI has been opaque

## When NOT to use

- As a one-time document filed away — Pereira is explicit: an archived plan is worthless
- When the team treats it as theater for executives rather than an operating document
- For purely tactical, single-campaign decisions — use a campaign brief instead
- When market conditions are so volatile that quarterly re-planning is more honest
- As a substitute for a business plan (it covers marketing only, not financing/ops/legal)

## Common pitfalls

- **Drafting and archiving** — the most common failure mode; plan becomes shelfware
- **Vague targets** — "increase return rate" or "get more followers" replace measurable goals
- **Ignoring the full funnel** — focusing only on bottom-of-funnel conversion at the expense of awareness and interest
- **Distribution-only thinking** — obsessing over channels while neglecting message and differentiation
- **No internal feedback loop** — written by marketing leadership without input from sales, product, customer service
- **Rigid plans with high fixed costs** — no flexibility to respond to new competitors or market shifts
- **Skipping the executive summary** — losing stakeholder attention in the first page
- **No omissions section** — failing to declare what the plan will *not* address (creates scope ambiguity)

## Key metrics

- **Sales objectives** — revenue, units, deal count
- **Acquisition** — new customers, lead volume, CAC
- **Retention** — existing customer retention rate, churn
- **Brand awareness** — share of voice, brand recall, mention sentiment
- **Website traffic** — sessions, organic vs. paid, conversion rate
- **Funnel metrics** — top-of-funnel awareness, mid-funnel engagement, bottom-funnel conversion
- **Marketing ROI** — return on every dollar of marketing spend
- **80/20 rule application** — the 20% of products/customers that drive 80% of revenue

## Decision questions

- Do our marketing objectives derive from and reinforce the business plan's SMART goals?
- Have we defined a target market specifically enough that messaging and channel choice flow from it?
- Does each marketing activity have a budget, an owner, a deadline, and a KPI?
- Is the plan flexible enough to absorb a new competitor or market shift without rewriting from scratch?
- Have we gathered feedback from sales, product, and customer service before finalizing?
- Are we covering the full funnel — awareness, interest, consideration, conversion — or only the bottom?
- What is the plan *not* addressing, and have we declared that explicitly?

## Canonical examples

- **Visit Oxnard CVB** — comprehensive plan with target demographics, performance metrics, USPs, customer personas, and color-coded sections (page 35)
- **Safe Haven Family Shelter** — non-profit plan as paradigm for SMART objectives + timelines + action steps + target profiles + KPIs (page 36)
