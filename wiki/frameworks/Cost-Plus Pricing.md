# Cost-Plus Pricing

## Definition

Cost-plus pricing sets the selling price by calculating the total cost of producing a product (direct materials, direct labor, overhead) and adding a fixed percentage markup for profit. The formula is `Price = Cost + (Cost × Markup %)`. Pereira positions it as the simplest pricing method to implement — easy to explain to finance, easy to defend, but structurally blind to what customers are actually willing to pay.

## Key attributes

- **Cost-driven formula** — price = cost × (1 + markup); no demand input
- **Guaranteed margin per unit** — if costs are accurate, profit is locked in
- **Easy to implement** — no market research required
- **Stable prices** — facilitates forecasting and budgeting for both seller and buyer
- **Ignores willingness-to-pay** — leaves money on the table when value exceeds cost
- **Encourages cost inflation** — guaranteed margin removes incentive to control costs
- **Competitively blind** — internal cost focus may price you out vs competitors

## When to use

- Government contracts and regulated industries (cost-plus is often mandated)
- Professional services billing on hours plus markup (law, accounting)
- Manufacturing with stable, well-understood input costs
- Low-differentiation commodities where price discovery is mature
- Internal transfer pricing within multi-division companies

## When NOT to use

- Premium or differentiated products where perceived value far exceeds cost
- Dynamic markets where prices must respond to competition or demand
- Digital goods with near-zero marginal cost (cost-plus would underprice)
- Categories where customers anchor on competitor prices, not your costs
- When you want to capture customer willingness-to-pay

## Common pitfalls

- Underpricing a product whose perceived value greatly exceeds production cost
- Inflating cost estimates to inflate price (death spiral when competitors don't)
- Treating allocated overhead as if it were marginal cost
- Ignoring elasticity — raising price proportionally when costs rise loses volume
- Using cost-plus on software where copies are free (results in absurd pricing)

## Key metrics

- **Unit cost** (direct + allocated indirect)
- **Markup percentage** (industry-typical 20-50%; retail keystone is 100%)
- **Gross margin** = (Price − COGS) / Price
- **Contribution margin** (price minus variable cost; covers fixed cost + profit)

## Decision questions

- Do we actually know our true unit cost, including allocated overhead?
- Is our markup defensible, or is it just "what we've always done"?
- What would the market bear if we priced on perceived value instead?
- Are competitors undercutting us because their cost structure is genuinely lower, or because they're pricing differently?
- Does our markup hold up under volume changes (semi-fixed costs)?

## Canonical examples

- **Furniture manufacturers** — timber + labor + overhead + markup (page 47)
- **Government contracts** — cost reimbursement plus fixed % profit (page 47)
- **Law and accounting firms** — billable hours × rate, with the rate built on cost + markup (page 47)
- **Retail keystone pricing** — wholesale × 2 (a specific cost-plus markup convention, page 81)
