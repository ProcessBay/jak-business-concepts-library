# Five Stages of Subscription Maturity

## Definition

A capability-maturity framework for organizations adopting or operating subscription business models. Pereira's five stages: (1) **Ad Hoc** — subscription as side experiment; manual billing; few customers; no clear strategy; (2) **Opportunistic** — recognizing subscription's value; pilots in specific segments; basic recurring billing; (3) **Repeatable** — subscription is a real revenue line; consistent pricing, billing, retention practices; (4) **Managed** — subscription is core to strategy; sophisticated analytics; segmentation; cross-sell/upsell motions; (5) **Optimized** — subscription is the operating model; data-driven pricing, predictive churn, AI-augmented customer success.

## Key attributes

- **Five sequential stages** — Ad Hoc → Opportunistic → Repeatable → Managed → Optimized
- **Capability accumulates** — each stage adds operational sophistication
- **Different metrics matter per stage** — early stages count subscribers; mature stages optimize cohort economics
- **Tooling differs** — Ad Hoc uses spreadsheets; Optimized uses Zuora/Recurly/Stripe Billing with custom analytics
- **Cultural shift required** — moving from product-sale culture to ongoing-service culture
- **Reverse possible** — orgs can regress when execution slips

## The five stages

1. **Ad Hoc**: subscription as side experiment; manual processes; no formal pricing strategy; revenue is small fraction of business
2. **Opportunistic**: leadership sees subscription's value; pilots in specific segments; basic recurring billing infrastructure
3. **Repeatable**: subscription is a real revenue line; consistent pricing; standard billing system; retention practices documented
4. **Managed**: subscription is core to strategy; analytics on MRR/ARR/churn; segmented offerings; upsell/cross-sell motions; customer success function
5. **Optimized**: subscription is the operating model; predictive churn modeling; data-driven pricing; AI-augmented success motions; world-class NRR

## When to use

- Strategic planning to identify current maturity and gaps
- Investment prioritization (which capabilities to build next)
- Comparing your subscription maturity to competitors
- Pre-IPO / pre-acquisition diligence on subscription operations

## When NOT to use

- For non-subscription businesses (no fit)
- As a rigid roadmap (companies don't always progress linearly)

## Common pitfalls

- Pretending to be at Stage 4-5 while operating at 2-3
- Skipping stages (Ad Hoc → Managed without Repeatable infrastructure)
- Investing in optimization tooling before achieving repeatability
- Letting maturity slip (acquired teams often regress)
- Misjudging which stage you're at (most orgs overestimate)

## Key metrics

- Revenue predictability (forecast accuracy)
- Billing infrastructure score (manual vs. automated)
- Churn management sophistication (reactive vs. predictive)
- Customer Success function maturity (none → reactive → proactive → predictive)

## Decision questions

- Honestly assessed, what stage are we at?
- What would moving up one stage require (people, tools, process)?
- Where are we operating below our claimed stage?
- Which competitor is operating one stage above us, and what does that look like?

## Canonical examples

- **Stage 5 Optimized**: Salesforce, Adobe, Netflix — fully data-driven subscription operations
- **Stage 4 Managed**: most mature SaaS companies; have analytics + customer success
- **Stage 3 Repeatable**: subscription lines that work but lack sophisticated optimization
- **Stage 2 Opportunistic**: traditional companies (manufacturers, retailers) piloting subscription
- **Stage 1 Ad Hoc**: most small businesses' first subscription attempts
