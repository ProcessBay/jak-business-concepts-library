# Theory of Change

## Definition

Theory of Change (ToC) is a strategic-planning technique that works backwards from long-term outcomes to identify the intermediate changes, early-term shifts, assumptions, and interventions required to make those outcomes plausible. Originating in evaluation and social-impact contexts in the 1990s, ToC has spread to corporate strategy as a tool for surfacing the causal logic behind ambitious goals. Pereira describes it as planning "in backwards steps" — starting from long-term targets, moving to intermediate targets, and finally to the early-term changes essential to instigate the preferred modification. Its discipline is forcing the team to articulate not just *what* outcome they want, but *why* the chain of causes will actually produce it.

## Key attributes

- **Backwards mapping** — start from the long-term outcome, work backwards through intermediate milestones to early changes
- **Six-element process** — Long-term goals → Backward map → Identify assumptions → Identify interventions → Indicators → Reasoning
- **Surfaces assumptions explicitly** — a ToC is only as strong as its underlying beliefs
- **Interventions define the work** — what specific actions are required to trigger each link in the chain?
- **Indicators measure progress** — at each level of the chain, not just the final outcome
- **Articulates causal reasoning** — produces a defensible story of how change happens
- **Originated in non-profit evaluation** — now used in foundations, governments, and increasingly corporate strategy

## When to use

- For ambitious long-term outcomes where the path is non-obvious
- In social-impact and grant-funded programs (often required by funders)
- When the strategy depends on chains of behavioural or systemic change
- When leadership disagrees on *why* a strategy will work — ToC forces explicit reasoning
- For program design where outcomes are several causal steps removed from activities
- During strategic resets when the team needs to defend a multi-year bet

## When NOT to use

- For short-term, tactical decisions
- When the causal chain is well-understood and short (a direct sale, a feature launch)
- When the team isn't willing to surface assumptions honestly
- As a substitute for execution discipline — ToC is the map, not the journey
- For pure financial-return decisions where standard ROI logic is sufficient

## Common pitfalls

- Building the chain forwards instead of backwards (defeats the purpose)
- Leaving assumptions implicit — the most fragile links go untested
- Defining outcomes too vaguely to measure
- Picking indicators that measure activity, not change
- Treating the ToC as a static document rather than a living hypothesis
- Confusing ToC with a logic model (related but distinct — ToC emphasises assumptions and causal reasoning)
- Using ToC without a feedback loop — assumptions that prove wrong should trigger revisions

## Outputs / signals of good use

- A diagrammatic chain from long-term outcome → intermediate outcomes → early changes → interventions
- An explicit list of assumptions, each rated for confidence
- Indicators defined at each level of the chain, with monitoring assigned
- A clear narrative explaining why the chain will produce the outcome
- Periodic review — assumptions revisited, chain updated as evidence accumulates
- Funders, partners, and team members can recount the same causal story

## Decision questions

- What's the long-term outcome, stated concretely enough to measure?
- Working backwards — what must be true at the intermediate level for that outcome to occur?
- What assumptions does each link depend on? Which are weakest?
- What specific interventions will trigger each change?
- What indicators tell us each link is firing?
- Where in the chain are we most exposed, and how would we know?

## Canonical examples

- **Pereira's HR payroll software example** — organization aims to increase market share; identifies that targeting small businesses (while continuing mid-scale) is the path; identifies the need to subsidize prices to make the product affordable; defines new-user acquisition and retention as the indicators (pages 58-59)
- **Foundations and impact investors** — ToC is standard for grant applications; Rockefeller, Gates, and Hewlett foundations all require versions
- **Social enterprises** — common tool for proving impact to investors
- **Corporate sustainability strategies** — used to articulate why net-zero commitments will actually materialize
- **Vaccination campaigns and public-health programs** — classic ToC use case
