# 4/5 Threshold Lead Qualification

## Definition

The 4/5 Threshold is Shopify's BANT-derived qualification framework, popularized by Loren Padelford (VP Sales, Shopify Plus). A lead is considered qualified only if the salesperson can give definitive answers to at least four of five criteria: Pain, Power, Money, Process, and Timeline. Pereira documents it as the screening mechanism that powered Shopify's growth to a $20B+ valuation and the fastest-ever SaaS path to $1B in revenue. The framework upgrades BANT in three ways: it replaces the vague "Need" with the sharper "Pain" (forcing reps to articulate the specific business problem), it adds "Process" (how the prospect actually makes purchases — a known killer of deals that pass BANT but die in procurement), and it permits qualification on 4 of 5 (acknowledging real-world ambiguity). The model treats sales as a science, not an art — qualification is data-driven gatekeeping, not gut feel.

## Key attributes

- **Five criteria** — Pain, Power, Money, Process, Timeline
- **4-of-5 threshold** — qualified if at least four are confirmed (not all five)
- **Pain replaces Need** — forces specificity about the problem
- **Adds Process** — surfaces procurement/approval workflow before it kills the deal
- **Data-driven gatekeeping** — built to be measured and enforced systematically
- **Hard disqualification** — leads under 4/5 are dropped, not nurtured
- **Pairs with CRM enforcement** — reps cannot advance opps without filling the fields
- **Built for velocity** — designed for high-volume SaaS inside sales

## When to use

- High-velocity SaaS inside sales (50+ opps per rep)
- Growth-stage companies needing forecast accuracy at scale
- Inbound-heavy motions where lead volume requires ruthless filtering
- When BANT alone is letting too many bad deals through to AEs
- Teams adopting a "sales as science" culture
- After implementing a CRM mature enough to enforce field hygiene

## When NOT to use

- Enterprise / strategic-account selling (use MEDDIC/MEDDPICC instead)
- Early-stage companies with too few leads to disqualify
- Long-cycle deals where Process is genuinely unknowable early
- Founder-led sales where the founder makes the qualification call by intuition
- Markets where Pain isn't yet articulated by the buyer (consultative discovery first)

## Common pitfalls

- Treating 4/5 as a checklist rather than a conversation — feels like an interrogation
- Reps filling CRM fields without real data, just to advance opps
- Disqualifying on Process when the real answer is "we'll figure it out"
- Confusing Power (economic buyer) with a sympathetic champion
- Letting the threshold be enforced inconsistently across reps
- Not retraining reps when the buyer profile shifts (criteria stale)
- Skipping the "Pain" diagnosis and just asking for a budget number

## Key metrics

- **Qualification rate** — % of leads that hit 4/5
- **Disqualification rate** — healthy: 50-70% of inbound disqualified
- **Win rate on qualified opps** — should be 2-3x unqualified
- **Forecast accuracy** — 4/5-qualified pipe should forecast tightly
- **Avg deal size by qualification level** — 5/5 typically larger than 4/5
- **Sales cycle on qualified vs unqualified** — qualified should close faster

## Decision questions

- Have we defined what counts as a definitive "yes" on each criterion?
- Are reps disqualifying enough, or hoarding bad pipeline?
- Is our CRM enforcing the threshold, or just collecting fields?
- Is Process being asked about early enough — before legal/procurement kills it?
- Are we measuring win rate by qualification level to validate the framework?
- Has our buyer changed in a way that demands new criteria?

## Canonical examples

- **Shopify Plus** (pages 26-27) — Padelford's 4/5 Threshold drove the $1B SaaS milestone in record time
- **Modern SaaS sales orgs** — many velocity-stage SaaS teams use Pain-Power-Money-Process-Timeline variants
- **CRM enforcement at scale** — Salesforce, HubSpot custom fields configured to require 4/5
- Adopters typically extend or replace BANT after hitting ~$20-50M ARR
