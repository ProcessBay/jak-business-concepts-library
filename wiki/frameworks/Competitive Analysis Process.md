# Competitive Analysis Process

## Definition

Competitive analysis is the structured evaluation of major players in an industry to understand their products, sales tactics, pricing, marketing, positioning, and engagement — and use that understanding to inform a firm's own strategy. Pereira frames it as a *recurring* process triggered at key moments (starting a business, launching a product, pivoting, stagnation, organic traffic drops) rather than a one-time exercise. The process spans competitor identification, multi-dimensional information gathering (often via the 7Ps), sales-tactic analysis, content/technology/social-media audits, SWOT on competitors, and synthesis into a competitive advantage statement. The output is not a report but a continuously refreshed view that informs product, pricing, promotion, and positioning decisions.

## Key attributes

- **Recurring, not one-time** — competitor landscape shifts; analysis must refresh on a cadence
- **Multiple framework deployment** — uses SWOT, Porter's Five Forces, Strategic Group Analysis, Perceptual Mapping, BCG Matrix, and 7Ps as complementary lenses
- **Trigger-driven** — Pereira identifies five triggers: starting a business, launching a product/service, considering a pivot, stagnating business, drop in organic traffic
- **Competitor typology** — direct, indirect/secondary, and substitute competitors all matter
- **Multi-dimensional data collection** — products, pricing, place, promotion, positioning, reputation, people, partnerships
- **Action-oriented** — analysis must terminate in specific moves (positioning shift, pricing change, content investment, product feature add)
- **Customer-centric** — incorporates customer reviews and feedback as primary signal alongside competitor observation
- **Combines qualitative and quantitative** — feature matrices, market share %, pricing data alongside reviews, brand sentiment, content audits

## When to use

- **Starting a business** — understand the field before launching
- **Launching a new product or service** — identify differentiation and messaging
- **Considering a pivot** — assess feasibility and barriers in the new space
- **Stagnating business** — identify what competitors are doing differently
- **Drop in organic traffic** — investigate whether competitors are taking your SEO position
- **Designated intervals** — quarterly or annual strategic planning cycles
- **Major industry events** — new regulation, new entrant, technological disruption

## When NOT to use

- As a substitute for customer discovery (competitors aren't customers; their choices may be wrong)
- When the team will copy competitors rather than learn from them (Pereira: "it should never become a copycat game")
- For very early-stage ideation when the value proposition itself isn't yet defined
- As a procrastination device when the action needed is execution, not more research
- When you don't have time or capability to act on findings — analysis without action is theater

## Common pitfalls

- **Copycat trap** — treating competitor moves as a playbook rather than a baseline to exceed
- **Direct-competitor tunnel vision** — missing indirect and substitute threats that often disrupt categories
- **Static report syndrome** — producing a one-time deck instead of a living view
- **No clear competitive advantage statement** — analysis runs but doesn't terminate in differentiation
- **Ignoring smaller/newer entrants** — they often disrupt fastest
- **Internal-only data sources** — missing customer-side perception data from reviews and social media
- **Failing to act on findings** — Pereira closes with: "constantly measure how well you're doing against your chosen goals"
- **Over-reliance on a single framework** — each framework illuminates one dimension; layered use is stronger

## Key metrics

- **Number of direct/indirect/substitute competitors tracked**
- **Refresh cadence** — quarterly minimum for fast-moving industries
- **Depth of data per competitor** — coverage across 7Ps + sales + content + tech stack
- **% of findings converted to action items**
- **Time from finding to action**
- **Competitive advantage clarity score** — can the team state the firm's differentiation in one sentence?

## Decision questions

- Who are our top 5-10 direct competitors, and who are our top 3-5 indirect/substitute competitors?
- What is our competitive advantage, stated in a single sentence?
- Which competitor is most likely to take share from us in the next 12 months, and why?
- What is the most underutilized signal in our current process (sales-lost reasons, customer reviews, social media)?
- Have we acted on our last analysis, or is it sitting in a slide deck?
- Which trigger has just occurred that should prompt a fresh analysis?

## Canonical examples

- **Sony vs. Nintendo (gaming consoles)** — Pereira's headline example walks through direct competitor identification, product/pricing/marketing/engagement analysis: Nintendo Switch (14.3M units sold) vs. PS5 (7.8M, semiconductor-constrained); Sony at $499 vs. Nintendo at $349; Sony PlayStation Twitter at 29.5M followers vs. Nintendo at 12M (pages 15-18)
- **Google (Katie Miller, Cloud Developer Relations)** — uses competitive analysis to monitor reach, engagement, sentiment beyond their own channels; leverages NLP automation to feed insights into internal dashboards (page 19)
- **McDonald's competitor set** — Wendy's and Burger King as direct competitors; frozen meals and grocery takeout as substitute competitors satisfying the same convenience-meal need (pages 113-115)
