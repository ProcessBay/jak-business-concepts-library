# Cloud Kitchen Business Model

## Definition

A cloud kitchen — also called ghost kitchen, dark kitchen, or virtual restaurant — is a delivery-only food production facility with no dine-in space, no front-of-house, and often no street-visible brand. Orders arrive exclusively through aggregator apps or the operator's own digital channel, and food leaves only via delivery riders. Pereira documents the model as the operational backbone of the on-demand foodtech wave: Maple, Sprig, SpoonRocket, and Munchery all operated some variant of it. The model strips out the most expensive real-estate and labor lines of a traditional restaurant (prime-frontage rent, waitstaff, dining room) to chase delivery-only unit economics. Multiple brands can be produced from the same physical kitchen ("multi-brand" or "virtual brand" plays), letting one operator A/B-test concepts without opening new locations.

## Key attributes

- **Delivery-only, no dine-in** — no front-of-house, no foot-traffic dependency
- **Off-prime real estate** — industrial parks, commissary zones, cheap urban back-streets
- **Aggregator-channel dependency** — discoverability lives inside the marketplace app, not the street
- **Menu engineered for transport** — items chosen for travel resilience, not plate presentation
- **Multi-brand from one kitchen** — same production line ships multiple consumer-facing brands
- **Throughput-driven economics** — profit is orders × contribution margin per kitchen-hour, not table-turn
- **Capex-light vs. full restaurant** — typically 20-40% of a comparable dine-in build-out

## When to use

- Dense urban delivery zones with 30-minute reach to a large daytime/evening population
- When the operator has a strong demand-gen channel (own app, parent marketplace, viral brand)
- For testing a new food concept before committing to a physical restaurant
- When labor and prime rent in the local market make traditional restaurant economics unworkable
- For chain expansion into a city before opening flagship physical locations

## When NOT to use

- Cuisines that don't travel (anything that goes soggy, melts, or loses crunch in 20 minutes)
- Low-density delivery zones where you can't hit the orders-per-hour breakeven
- Concepts whose value proposition is the in-restaurant experience (ambience, social, occasion)
- Markets where aggregator commissions (25-35%) leave no room for contribution margin
- When you lack a discovery channel and assume "build it on the app and they will come" — see Maple, Sprig (page 94-96)

## Common pitfalls

- **Subsidizing each order** to chase growth and never reaching contribution-positive (Maple lost money on every meal — page 94)
- Underestimating aggregator commission's bite on a delivery-only P&L (no dine-in cushion)
- Building one virtual brand instead of stacking many — wasting kitchen utilization
- Ignoring delivery radius math: outside ~5km, food quality and unit economics both collapse
- Treating cloud kitchens as cheap restaurants instead of a different operating model with its own playbook
- Over-indexing on technology hardware (smart fryers, robot lines) before nailing throughput basics

## Key metrics

- **Orders per kitchen per day** (utilization)
- **Average order value (AOV)** and items per order
- **Contribution margin per order** (after aggregator commission, packaging, rider, ingredients)
- **Kitchen utilization rate** (peak-hour orders vs. theoretical max throughput)
- **Delivery radius density** (orders within profitable 4-6km zone)
- **Repeat order rate / cohort retention** (if running your own brand)
- **CAC per new ordering customer** (especially if not pure aggregator-fed)

## Decision questions

- Do we have a real-estate advantage that makes the kitchen footprint genuinely cheap?
- Is our cuisine engineered for 20-30 minute transport without quality loss?
- Can we hit ≥ 100 orders/day per kitchen within 6 months at our locations?
- What's our path to contribution-positive economics after aggregator commission?
- Are we running 1 brand or stacking 3-5 brands per kitchen to maximize utilization?
- Do we own demand or is the aggregator our landlord?

## Canonical examples

- **Kitopi, Kitchen United, REEF Technology** — global cloud-kitchen platform operators
- **Maple (NYC)** — direct-to-consumer cloud kitchen; closed 2017 after losing money per meal (page 94)
- **Sprig, SpoonRocket, Munchery** — US delivery-only kitchens that all failed on unit economics (pages 94-96)
- **Faasos / Rebel Foods (India)** — multi-brand cloud kitchen pioneer running 10+ brands per facility (page 101)
- **Saudi context: The Chefz, Jahez** — both operate or partner with cloud kitchens to serve dense Riyadh/Jeddah delivery zones; local operators like Kitch and PIF-backed cloud-kitchen plays have emerged 2022 onward
- **6D Bytes** — automated cloud-kitchen vending machines (page 104)
