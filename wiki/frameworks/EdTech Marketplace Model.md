# EdTech Marketplace Model

## Definition

The EdTech marketplace model is a two-sided digital storefront where independent instructors publish courses and learners browse, purchase, or subscribe to them, with the platform taking a transaction cut. Pereira describes it as "a kind of digital marketplace that functions as an online store or like a digital library where students will be able to browse and purchase courses." Teachers can post courses for free or charge a fee, and the platform earns from rev-share on every transaction. The archetype is Udemy: instructors retain ownership of their content and earn money from student purchases while the platform handles discovery, payments, delivery infrastructure, and trust signals (reviews, completion stats). Some marketplaces operate a dual model — open marketplace for individual instructors plus a separate enterprise track (e.g., Udemy Business) where companies license branded versions for internal training.

## Key attributes

- **Two-sided platform** — instructors on one side, learners on the other; platform earns from take-rate, not from teaching
- **Rev-share economics** — typical splits range from 50/50 to 97/3 depending on who acquired the learner (organic vs platform-promoted)
- **User-generated catalog** — content quality varies; ratings/reviews and refund policies are critical trust mechanisms
- **Discovery is the product** — search, categorization, recommendations, and bundling drive learner conversion more than course delivery itself
- **Long-tail catalog dynamics** — head courses generate most revenue, but the long tail makes the catalog defensible
- **Aggressive promotional discounting** — common pattern of permanent "sale" pricing (Udemy's $9.99 sales) to drive volume
- **Dual B2C / B2B2C track** — open marketplace for individuals plus enterprise license for corporate L&D (Udemy Business archetype)

## When to use

- A category with abundant willing creators/teachers and fragmented demand
- Subjects where peer-rated quality signals work (most professional skills)
- When you can build supply faster than competitors and create a discovery moat
- Markets where institutional gatekeepers are absent or slow (skilled trades, software, design, business skills)
- Geographies where credentialing is less formally regulated

## When NOT to use

- Regulated credentials (K-12 schooling, accredited degrees, medical licensure) — these need vertically-integrated institutions
- High-stakes assessments where quality must be guaranteed
- Categories where the supply of expert teachers is too thin to populate a marketplace
- Where the buyer expects a single-curator brand (MasterClass model) rather than a UGC catalog
- Markets dominated by a single subsidized free incumbent (e.g., Khan Academy in core K-12 math)

## Common pitfalls

- Treating courses as commodities and racing to the bottom on price (Udemy's permanent-discount trap)
- Under-investing in instructor success — high churn on the supply side starves the catalog
- Ignoring completion rates — courses sold are not courses delivered; low completion erodes word-of-mouth
- Failing to verify instructor credentials, leading to brand-damaging low-quality content
- Confusing course volume with quality; over-time, learners trust narrower curated brands more than infinite catalogs
- Building only B2C and missing the higher-ACV enterprise opportunity

## Key metrics

- **GMV** — gross merchandise value (total course sales)
- **Take rate** — platform's share of GMV (typically 30-50% on platform-acquired learners)
- **Instructor count** and **course count** — supply-side scale
- **Active learners** and **enrollments per learner** — demand-side engagement
- **Course completion rate** — leading indicator of repeat purchase
- **Instructor ARPU** — health of supply-side incentives
- **CAC** vs **learner LTV** — economics of paid acquisition vs lifetime spend

## Decision questions

- Do we have a credible supply side, or are we trying to bootstrap with too few teachers?
- What's our take-rate philosophy, and how do we keep top instructors from going direct?
- How do we prevent quality collapse as the catalog scales?
- Is there a B2B/enterprise channel that converts our marketplace into something with stickier ACVs?
- Will credentialing matter in this category in five years, and can we build it?

## Canonical examples

- **Udemy** — the archetype; instructors publish, learners purchase one-off, platform takes 37-63% depending on attribution
- **Coursera** — hybrid marketplace; universities/companies publish courses, platform monetizes via paid certificates and subscriptions
- **Skillshare** — subscription-based creative-skills marketplace
- **Teachable, Thinkific, Kajabi** — white-label tools that let instructors run their own marketplace (the "unbundle Udemy" play)
- **Rwaq (Saudi Arabia)** — Arabic-language MOOC marketplace partnered with universities and corporates
- **Noon Academy (Saudi Arabia)** — live-tutoring marketplace popular across MENA; teachers run interactive group classes
- **Madrasa (UAE)** — Arabic-language free academic marketplace (Sheikh Mohammed bin Rashid foundation-funded — a sponsored variant)
