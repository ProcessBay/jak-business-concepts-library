# Wholetailing (Real Estate)

## Definition

Wholetailing is a portmanteau of wholesale + retail — and operationally, it sits between the two. The investor takes title to a distressed property (unlike wholesaling), performs only the minimum repairs needed to make the home listable on the MLS (unlike full flipping), and sells at slightly below market value to attract both retail buyers and investors. Pereira positions it as the "best of both" risk-return: lower capital and timeline than a full flip, higher margin than a wholesale assignment. The typical wholetail addresses health-and-safety issues (plumbing, code violations) plus cosmetic refresh (carpet, paint, landscaping) — but skips the kitchen/bath full gut that defines a true fix-and-flip. Pereira cites typical wholetail spreads of $40k–$80k per deal versus $9k for a comparable wholesale or $27k for a full flip on the same property, with significantly less risk and capital exposure than the flip path.

## Key attributes

- **Takes title** — unlike wholesaling, the investor owns the asset
- **Minimum-viable rehab** — fix health/safety issues + light cosmetics; skip the full gut
- **Slightly-below-market pricing** — speed and broader buyer pool over peak price
- **Sells to anyone** — retail homebuyers OR investors, not just investors (as wholesale)
- **Shorter cycle than flipping** — typically 30–60 days vs. 90–180 for full flip
- **Lower risk than flipping** — smaller rehab budget exposed, less chance of cost overruns
- **Higher margin than wholesaling** — typically 3–5x the assignment fee

## When to use

- Property needs only modest repairs (not a gut job) to be MLS-ready
- Investor has capital for purchase but wants faster turn than a full flip
- Market has both active retail buyers and investor buyers (broadens exit options)
- Investor lacks contractor depth for a full renovation
- Distressed acquisition at deep discount makes light-rehab math work

## When NOT to use

- Property requires structural / major-system work to be insurable or financeable
- Market favors fully renovated homes (buyers won't pay near-ARV for partial)
- Investor has zero capital — wholesale assignment may be the only option
- Investor has full rehab capability and wants the larger flip margin
- Property in a price band where retail buyers expect turnkey condition

## Common pitfalls

- Misjudging which repairs are skippable — leaving a deal-killer untouched
- Pricing too close to full-ARV — losing the speed advantage
- Underestimating MLS-readiness requirements (lender appraisal, inspection)
- Letting "minor cosmetic" creep into a full rehab budget
- Holding cost surprises when the property sits longer than expected
- Failing to disclose known issues — lawsuit risk same as full flipping

## Key metrics

- **ARV (light-rehab variant)** — typically 80–90% of full-rehab ARV
- **Rehab budget** — target ≤25% of full-rehab equivalent
- **Cycle time** — target 30–60 days from purchase to resale close
- **Gross profit per deal** — typical $40k–$80k per Pereira
- **Holding cost** — minimized by short timeline
- **Buyer pool breadth** — both retail and investor; broader = faster

## Decision questions

- Which repairs are *required* for MLS listing vs. *nice-to-have*?
- What's the lender appraisal threshold for the asset class — will partial rehab pass?
- Will the local market accept a partially renovated home, or do buyers expect turnkey?
- Am I capitalized to hold longer if the partial-rehab discount doesn't move fast?
- Does my disclosure language protect me from undisclosed-defect claims?

## Canonical examples

- **Pereira's $60k → $130k case study** — distressed home bought at $60k, $15k light rehab (plumbing + carpet + paint), sold at $130k for ~$38.7k gross vs. $32k on full flip with $35k rehab (page 50-52)
- **Pereira's $50k → $110k case** — wholetail nets $36k vs. wholesale $9k or full flip $27k (page 53)
- **HomeVestors franchise system** — many "We Buy Houses" operators wholetail rather than full-flip
- **BiggerPockets community** — popularized the term and playbook in US investor culture
