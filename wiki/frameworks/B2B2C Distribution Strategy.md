# B2B2C Distribution Strategy

## Definition

A B2B2C distribution strategy reaches end consumers through a business partner that holds the consumer relationship — the firm sells to a business (B2B) whose offering, in turn, reaches consumers (2C). Pereira frames it as a market-entry option where the brand piggybacks on partners' established distribution and consumer trust. Critically, B2B2C is differentiated from pure indirect distribution by **brand visibility to the end consumer**: B2B2C end consumers know the brand whose product they're using, not just the partner's. This enables eventual direct relationship (data, communication, retention) while accepting near-term scale constraints. Examples in the present economy: Intel Inside (chips sold to PC makers, branded to consumers), Stripe Connect (payments embedded in marketplaces, with Stripe-aware merchants), insurance white-labeled by retailers (with the carrier disclosed), and SaaS sold to enterprises whose employees recognize the tool by name (Slack, Zoom).

## Key attributes

- **Two-step distribution** — firm → business partner → consumer
- **Brand visibility at end-consumer level** — distinguishes B2B2C from pure B2B
- **Partner owns initial customer relationship** — firm builds relationship over time
- **Leverages partner's existing distribution and trust**
- **Faster market entry** than building direct consumer presence from scratch
- **Constrained short-term scalability** — limited by partner adoption pace
- **Long-term direct-relationship potential** — partner-mediated → direct over time
- **Often paired with embedded technology** — APIs, white-label SDKs, co-branded UX
- **Revenue share or per-transaction economics** typical

## When to use

- New entrants without consumer brand or distribution
- Categories where consumer trust is hard to build cold (finance, insurance, health)
- Products that work as features inside larger products (chips, payments, AI APIs)
- Markets where regulated entities (banks, carriers, hospitals) gate consumer access
- Geographic expansion via local distribution partners
- When the firm wants telemetry on consumer behavior but lacks consumer-acquisition capability
- Platform plays where partner network effects compound

## When NOT to use

- Categories where brand intimacy with the consumer is the entire value proposition
- When end-consumer disclosure is impossible (true white-label, no brand visibility)
- Categories where the partner can quickly displace or replicate the firm's offer
- When margin structure cannot support a revenue share with a powerful intermediary
- When the firm has direct consumer distribution capability already at scale

## Common pitfalls

- Partner relegating the firm's brand to invisibility (collapses to pure B2B)
- Partner using the firm's product to learn the category, then disintermediating
- Inadequate end-consumer support standards (firm gets blamed for partner-side problems)
- Misaligned incentives — partner's roadmap conflicts with firm's
- Underestimating the time to convert B2B2C end consumers to direct relationships
- Concentration risk — too much revenue through a single partner
- Channel conflict if the firm later opens direct consumer distribution
- Failing to negotiate brand-visibility clauses upfront (logo, attribution, co-marketing)

## Key metrics

- **Active business partner count** by tier/region
- **End-consumer reach via partner** (DAUs/MAUs served through partners)
- **Brand recognition at end-consumer level** (unaided / aided recall surveys)
- **Partner revenue share %** (firm's economics)
- **Partner acquisition cost** (sales-led cost to land a B2B partner)
- **Partner concentration** (% of revenue from top N partners — risk indicator)
- **End-user NPS** of consumers served through partners
- **Direct-relationship conversion rate** (B2B2C end consumers who later engage directly)

## Decision questions

- Will end consumers actually know our brand, or will partners absorb us?
- Do we have brand-visibility clauses, or are we accepting silent white-label?
- What is our partner concentration risk, and how do we diversify?
- Can our economics survive a meaningful revenue share to powerful partners?
- What's our path from B2B2C to direct relationship — or are we okay being permanently intermediated?
- Will partners eventually compete with us, and how do we defend?

## Canonical examples

- **Intel Inside** — chips sold to PC OEMs but branded to end consumers (classic B2B2C)
- **Stripe Connect** — payments embedded in marketplaces with Stripe attribution
- **Visa, Mastercard** — issued by banks, branded to consumers
- **Insurance carriers white-labeled by retailers** — carrier disclosed for trust
- **Slack, Zoom adopted enterprise-wide** — employees recognize the brand
- **Tesla via Hertz** — Tesla-branded vehicles in Hertz's consumer rental fleet
- **Netflix on smart-TV remote controls** — Netflix button on partner-manufactured remotes; Netflix paid hardware partners ~$1/subscriber and $17.4M total in 2011 to seed (Pereira pages 14-15)
- **Saudi context — STC Pay embedded in third-party apps** — fintech reach via partner consumer products
- **Saudi context — Tabby and Tamara BNPL embedded in retailer checkouts** — consumer sees the BNPL brand, retailer sees the integration
- **Saudi context — Lean Technologies' open-banking APIs** — embedded in third-party fintech apps used by Saudi consumers
- **Saudi context — Foodics POS used by restaurants** — diners experience Foodics-powered ordering kiosks and digital menus
