# Distribution Partnership

## Definition

A distribution partnership enables companies to extend their market reach by sharing distribution channels, networks, or routes-to-market with another organization. Pereira frames these as essential in industries where a multichannel approach is necessary — a clothing brand partnering with a popular online marketplace to access a wider audience; a restaurant brand layered on top of a delivery platform; a coffee maker reaching supermarket shelves through a packaged-goods giant. The structure is typically a win-win: one party benefits from the other's customer base, while the host benefits from exclusive product or category enrichment. Unlike supply chain partnerships (upstream/inbound), distribution partnerships focus on downstream/outbound flow to the end customer.

## Key attributes

- **Channel access** — partner's customer base, geography, or platform is the asset
- **Multichannel strategy** — distribution partnerships layer onto direct channels, not replace them
- **Win-win mechanics** — partner gains exclusive product; brand gains audience
- **Revenue share or take-rate economics** — host typically captures a percentage of GMV
- **Brand-presence trade-off** — partner channels often dilute direct brand control
- **Geographic or segment specialization** — partners often unlock specific regions or buyer types
- **Scalable without owning infrastructure** — distribution costs become variable, not fixed

## When to use

- Direct channel build-out is too slow, expensive, or geographically impractical
- The partner has loyalty or routine in a customer segment the firm can't easily access
- Local market entry requires a partner familiar with regulations and logistics
- A platform or marketplace already aggregates the firm's target demand
- The product needs context (curation, bundling) that the partner channel provides

## When NOT to use

- When direct economics are materially better and the firm can fund the buildout
- When the partner could disintermediate or replicate the product after observing demand
- When brand control is critical and the partner channel dilutes positioning
- When partner take-rate erodes margin below sustainable levels
- When the partner already carries close competitors and won't differentiate the firm's product

## Common pitfalls

- Becoming dependent on one channel partner — leverage shifts to them at renewal time
- Loss of customer data — the partner owns the relationship, not the firm
- Channel conflict with direct sales — sales teams fight over the same accounts
- Take-rate creep — partner economics worsen over time as power consolidates
- Underinvesting in partner enablement — partner reps don't know how to sell the product
- No exit plan — if the channel turns hostile or shifts strategy, the firm has nothing

## Key metrics

- Channel reach (audience or geographic footprint added)
- Channel revenue share and contribution to total revenue
- Market penetration in the partner's region or segment
- GMV through the partner channel
- Partner take-rate or commission percentage
- Channel-attributed CAC vs. direct CAC

## Decision questions

- Do we own the customer relationship after the sale, or does the partner?
- What percentage of our revenue can safely come from any single channel?
- Is the take-rate stable, or will it ratchet up as we depend more on the partner?
- What's our channel-exit plan if the partner is acquired or changes terms?
- Are we investing enough in partner enablement to actually sell our product?

## Canonical examples

- **Starbucks + Nestlé** — Nestlé's distribution network placed Starbucks branded coffee in supermarkets and retail outlets across 90+ countries (pages 98-100)
- **McDonald's + Uber Eats** — Uber Eats as a delivery distribution channel extending McDonald's reach beyond physical restaurants (pages 108-110)
- **Clothing brand + online marketplace** — Pereira's archetype for fashion (page 22)
