# Flywheel Business Model

## Definition

The flywheel business model is a strategic frame that replaces the linear marketing funnel (attract → convert → close) with a self-reinforcing cycle centered on the existing customer. Pereira describes a three-stage cycle in which each satisfied customer becomes a source of new customers — via referrals, organic content, or the sheer attractive force of a growing network. The defining property is compounding: spin energy that goes into the wheel is conserved and accumulates, so each turn of the cycle requires less push than the last. The model is closely related to network effects but operates at the level of business choreography, not just product mechanics: it asks the organization to organize around customer delight as the primary growth lever rather than acquisition spend.

## Key attributes

- **Customer at the center of the cycle** — every stage is designed around the existing customer's experience (page 86)
- **Three-stage compounding loop** — satisfy → retain → attract via referrals (page 86)
- **Self-reinforcing energy** — past spin reduces the energy required for future spin (page 86)
- **Pre-internet roots** — telephone networks and Yellow Pages were proto-flywheels (page 86)
- **Internet-age archetypes** — Amazon (more shoppers → more vendors → more variety → more shoppers); Facebook (friends invite friends); Uber (drivers attract riders attract drivers); Venmo (friends already have it) (page 87)
- **Community is the seed** — Pereira's prescription for implementation: start by creating the community (page 87)
- **Multiple loops, not one** — mature businesses operate several reinforcing flywheels in parallel
- **Asymmetric to launch** — flywheels are slow to start and dangerous to stop once moving

## When to use

- Customer retention and referrals can plausibly drive a meaningful share of new acquisition
- Your category benefits from word-of-mouth, social proof, or network density
- You have or can build a community around the product
- You are willing to invest in customer satisfaction as a strategic lever, not a customer-service cost line
- The product has high enough quality that customers actually want to refer it

## When NOT to use

- The product is one-shot or low-frequency (customers won't be around to refer)
- Customers buy in stealth or do not talk about the category socially
- The market is so small that referral compounding cannot scale
- Quality is too low to survive word-of-mouth (negative loops are just as compounding)
- Acquisition economics work fine without flywheel mechanics — adding flywheel ceremony just slows decisions

## Common pitfalls

- **Calling a funnel a flywheel** — renaming a linear acquisition machine "a flywheel" without changing the architecture
- **No mechanism for referral** — relying on customers being "delighted" without giving them tools or incentives to share
- **Underinvesting in customer success** — flywheels fail at the satisfaction stage long before the referral stage
- **Stopping spin during downturns** — cutting the satisfaction investments that produce future free acquisition is the classic mistake
- **Single-flywheel fragility** — relying on one loop that a competitor can break (e.g., supply concentration)
- **Confusing flywheel with viral coefficient** — virality is one mechanism; the flywheel encompasses all compounding loops

## Key metrics

- **Net Promoter Score (NPS)** — leading indicator of referral propensity
- **Referral rate** — % of new customers attributable to existing customer behavior
- **Repeat purchase rate** — proxy for satisfaction stage
- **Cycle velocity** — time from first purchase to first referral
- **Compounding-advantage index** — declining CAC over time as flywheel matures
- **Community size and engagement** — for community-seeded flywheels

## Decision questions

- What are our top three flywheels — and which are actually compounding vs. stalled?
- Where in the cycle do we lose energy (satisfaction gap? referral mechanism missing? onboarding friction for new customers from referrals?)
- What is the smallest investment that would speed the cycle by one full turn?
- If we cut customer-success spend by 30%, which flywheel breaks first — and how long until we feel it?
- Do we have a community, and if so, is it producing the referral lift we assume?
- Which competitor flywheels are accelerating against us, and where can we intervene?

## Canonical examples

- **Telephone** — utility rose with every new subscriber on the network (page 86)
- **Yellow Pages** — more businesses listed → more readers → more businesses listed → more ad revenue (page 86)
- **Amazon** — more shoppers → more vendors → more variety → competitive pricing → more shoppers (pages 47, 87)
- **Facebook** — joining members invite friends, growing the graph (page 87)
- **Uber** — more drivers → more riders → more drivers (page 87)
- **Venmo** — your friends already have it, so you get it, so your friends' friends get it (page 87)
- **Google** — more searches → more data → better results → more searches (page 45)
