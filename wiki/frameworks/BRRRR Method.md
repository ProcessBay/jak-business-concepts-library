# BRRRR Method

## Definition

BRRRR stands for **Buy, Rehab, Rent, Refinance, Repeat** — a real-estate investing strategy where the investor (1) buys a distressed or undervalued property, often with short-term/hard-money financing; (2) rehabs it to lift its market value and meet rental code; (3) rents it to a quality tenant for recurring cash flow; (4) refinances at the new, higher appraised value via cash-out (typically 75–80% LTV) to recover the original capital plus rehab costs; (5) repeats the cycle on the next property using the recycled equity. Pereira frames BRRRR as the doctrine for scaling a rental portfolio without continuously raising new equity — the same dollar of investor cash compounds across multiple properties as long as forced appreciation through rehab consistently exceeds the LTV gap. Best suited to off-market, distressed, or auctioned single-family homes priced below market in middle-class neighborhoods.

## Key attributes

- **Five sequential steps** — Buy → Rehab → Rent → Refinance → Repeat, each gating the next
- **Capital recycling engine** — cash-out refinance recovers the original equity for the next deal
- **Forced appreciation focus** — value-add through rehab is the wealth-creation mechanism, not market appreciation
- **Two-loan structure** — short-term/hard-money for acquisition + rehab, long-term term loan after refinance
- **Seasoning period** — most lenders require 3–12 months of ownership before allowing a cash-out refi
- **LTV ceiling (typically 75–80%)** — determines how much of the rehabilitated value can be recovered
- **Targets distressed / off-market deals** — turnkey properties don't generate enough lift to recover capital

## When to use

- Investor building a long-term rental portfolio with limited starting capital
- Markets with a healthy gap between distressed-acquisition price and post-rehab market value
- Investor has rehab expertise (or a reliable contractor network) and patience for an 8–12 month cycle
- Lender ecosystem available that supports both hard-money acquisition and conventional/commercial cash-out refis
- Rental demand strong enough to fill the unit quickly with creditworthy tenants

## When NOT to use

- Turnkey, A-class properties (no forced-appreciation headroom)
- Markets where appraisals are softening (refinance risk kills the recycle)
- Investor lacks the time / skill / network for a multi-month rehab
- Local rental laws or rates make holding economics unworkable
- Investor needs a quick exit — BRRRR is structurally illiquid

## Common pitfalls

- Underestimating rehab cost and timeline; over-leveraging during the hold
- Buying at the wrong price — the entire model depends on Buy step discipline (use the 70% rule as a guardrail)
- Appraisal risk — the post-rehab valuation comes in below expectations and traps capital
- Ignoring vacancy, maintenance, and management drag when modeling rent coverage
- Skipping seasoning planning and getting stuck with expensive short-term debt
- Scaling too fast before the operational playbook is repeatable

## Key metrics

- **ARV** (After Repair Value) — projected post-rehab market price
- **LTV** (Loan-to-Value) — typically 75–80% on the refi
- **DSCR** (Debt Service Coverage Ratio) — rent must cover the new mortgage + reserves
- **Cash-on-cash return** — annual cash flow / capital still in the deal (post-refi this can be very high)
- **Cap rate** — NOI / property value
- **All-in cost vs ARV** — purchase + rehab + holding costs should sit well below ARV × LTV
- **Cycle time** — months from buy to refi; shorter = faster portfolio growth

## Decision questions

- Can I source distressed deals at a deep enough discount that ARV × LTV recovers my all-in cost?
- Do I have a contractor / construction team I trust to hit budget and timeline?
- What's the local seasoning rule, and how will I service the short-term loan in the meantime?
- Is rental demand strong enough that vacancy won't kill cash flow during the refi process?
- What's my fallback if the appraisal comes in low — hold longer, or sell?
- How many cycles do I need before this becomes the primary income driver?

## Canonical examples

- **Pereira's Johnny Crushit case (Austin, TX)** — $220k purchase, $15k rehab, ARV $290k, refinanced at 75% LTV ($217.5k) — recovered capital and $65.5k surplus toward the next deal (page 105)
- **BiggerPockets community** — popularized BRRRR as the default playbook for the US single-family rental class
- **Roofstock** — marketplace built around remote BRRRR investors (page 92)
- **Saudi/MENA parallel**: BRRRR is uncommon in KSA due to (a) thinner distressed-property market, (b) limited cash-out refi infrastructure, (c) Sharia-compliant financing structures that complicate the refinance leg. Investors using **Ejar**-registered rentals and **Sakani**-supported acquisitions tend toward straight buy-and-hold instead. Could become viable as Saudi mortgage market deepens post-Vision 2030
