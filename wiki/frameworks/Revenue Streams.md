# Revenue Streams

## Definition

Revenue Streams is the BMC block that defines how a firm makes money from each customer segment — the specific monetization mechanisms (sale, subscription, usage fee, advertising, etc.) and the pricing systems behind them. Unlike a single price point, revenue streams capture the structure: one-time vs. recurring, fixed vs. dynamic, who pays vs. who uses. Pereira enumerates nine distinct revenue mechanisms (Direct Sales, Freemium, Asset Sale, Usage Fee, Subscription Fee, Lending/Renting/Leasing, Licensing, Brokerage Fee, Advertising) and two pricing systems (fixed-price and dynamic), each suited to different VPs and customer relationships.

## Key attributes

- **Nine revenue mechanisms** — Direct Sale, Freemium, Asset Sale, Usage Fee, Subscription Fee, Lending/Renting/Leasing, Licensing, Brokerage Fee, Advertising
- **Two pricing systems** — Fixed-price (list, feature-dependent, segment-dependent, volume-dependent) and Dynamic (negotiation, yield management, real-time market, auctioning)
- **Multiple streams per business** — most businesses have several streams (Amazon: product, marketplace fees, Prime subs, AWS)
- **Recurring vs. transactional** — recurring streams (subscription, usage fee) compound; transactional streams require new sales constantly
- **Who pays differs from who uses** — advertising models, multi-sided platforms (Google: advertiser pays, user uses)
- **Stream-specific economics** — gross margin varies wildly by stream type

## When to use

- Designing a new business or product monetization
- Diagnosing margin compression (stream mix may be deteriorating)
- Evaluating pricing change implications across streams
- Considering new monetization (adding subscription tier, adding advertising, etc.)
- Investor pitch — revenue stream story is core to valuation

## When NOT to use

- Before validating customer demand — monetization without validated demand is theoretical
- As a substitute for pricing strategy — streams describe mechanism; pricing sets level
- For pure non-profit contexts where "revenue" is funding (different dynamics)

## Common pitfalls

- Single revenue stream dependency — vulnerable to single shock
- Adding streams that don't reinforce each other (or worse, cannibalize)
- Choosing the wrong stream for the customer relationship (subscription where customers want transactional, vice versa)
- Fixed pricing in markets that have shifted dynamic, missing margin
- Underpricing at launch; hard to raise prices later
- Ignoring volume-dependent or segment-dependent pricing opportunities

## Key metrics

- Revenue per stream (absolute and %)
- Gross margin per stream
- Growth rate per stream
- Revenue concentration (top stream as % of total)
- ARPU (Average Revenue Per User) by stream
- MRR / ARR for subscription streams
- Contribution margin per stream

## Decision questions

- For each customer segment: what revenue stream(s) capture the most value?
- Where do we have pricing power that we're not exercising?
- What recurring stream could we add to smooth revenue?
- Which stream is most vulnerable to commoditization?
- Which pricing mechanism best fits our category — fixed or dynamic?

## Canonical examples

- **Apple** — asset sale (hardware) + usage fee (App Store) + subscription (Apple Music, iCloud) + advertising (Apple Search Ads) (page 181)
- **Amazon** — direct sales + marketplace brokerage fees + subscription (Prime) + usage (AWS) + advertising (page 185)
- **Netflix** — subscription fee only (page 192)
- **Spotify** — freemium + subscription + advertising (free tier) (page 219)
- **Google** — primarily advertising; some subscription (Google Workspace, YouTube Premium) and licensing (Android partners) (page 172)
- **Uber** — brokerage fee (commission on rides + delivery) + dynamic pricing (surge) (page 188)
