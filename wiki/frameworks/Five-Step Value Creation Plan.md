# Five-Step Value Creation Plan

## Definition

Pereira's Five-Step Value Creation Plan is a meta-framework that sequences the major moves a firm must make to deliberately create value: (1) Define Value Proposition, (2) Identify Target Customers, (3) Conduct Value Chain Analysis, (4) Implement Innovation Strategies, (5) Create Positive Social/Environmental Impact. Unlike piecemeal application of these concepts, the plan integrates them into a coherent sequence — each step depending on the prior. The source uses Tesla, Nike, Amazon, Apple, and Patagonia as anchor examples across the steps.

## Key attributes

- **Sequential dependency** — steps build on each other; skipping creates fragility
- **Five distinct moves** — each step is a known concept (VP, personas, VCA, innovation, sustainability) integrated into a flow
- **Integrative** — combines customer-facing, operational, and external-impact dimensions
- **Strategic plan output** — the deliverable is a coordinated strategic plan, not a list of unrelated initiatives
- **Tested by example** — each step has a canonical company demonstration in the source
- **Repeatable** — runs through the plan periodically as conditions shift

## When to use

- Annual strategic planning when leadership wants a coordinated frame
- After a significant disruption or market shift requires re-baselining strategy
- When building a new business unit or entering a new market
- During a turnaround where the current strategy isn't coherent
- As a checklist to audit an existing strategy for missing dimensions

## When NOT to use

- As a one-time exercise — the plan must be re-run as conditions evolve
- When the firm needs a deep dive into a single dimension (use the specific framework instead)
- When time pressure demands a faster framework — this is comprehensive but slow

## Common pitfalls

- Treating the steps as a checklist to fill in rather than a strategy to act on
- Skipping Step 2 (target customers) and writing a generic VP — most common failure
- Doing Steps 1-4 financially and treating Step 5 (impact) as PR garnish
- Letting different teams own different steps without integration
- Not revisiting earlier steps when later analysis reveals contradictions

## Key metrics

- Plan completion: each step has explicit, documented output
- Inter-step alignment score (does Step 3 actually serve Step 1?)
- Leadership-team alignment post-plan (shared mental model of strategy)
- Initiatives launched from the plan with assigned owners and metrics

## Decision questions

- Have we actually defined our VP, or do we just have a tagline?
- Are our target customers the same as our actual customers? — if not, why?
- What did our value chain analysis change in our operations vs. before it?
- What innovation are we shipping this quarter that ties to the plan?
- What positive social/environmental impact do we measurably create, and is it credible?

## Canonical examples

- **Step 1 — Tesla** — value proposition defined as innovative, high-quality electric vehicles that are environmentally friendly and technologically advanced (page 32)
- **Step 2 — Nike** — identified target customers as athletes and fitness enthusiasts; designed innovative footwear and apparel for their specific needs (page 33)
- **Step 3 — Amazon** — used value chain analysis to engineer a highly efficient supply chain and seamless customer experience, becoming the e-commerce leader (page 33)
- **Step 4 — Apple** — implemented innovation strategy via iPhone, iPad, Apple Watch, disrupting markets and creating growth (page 34)
- **Step 5 — Patagonia** — created positive societal and environmental impact through sustainable practices and environmental protection initiatives (page 34)
