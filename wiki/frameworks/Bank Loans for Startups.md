# Bank Loans for Startups

## Definition

Bank loans are debt financing provided by commercial banks, government-backed lenders (SBA in the US), credit unions, microlenders, and peer-to-peer lending platforms. Pereira treats them as the traditional non-dilutive funding path: capital exchanged for repayment obligation with interest, no equity given up. Bank loans favor businesses with creditworthiness, collateral (real estate, equipment, personal guarantee), and predictable cash flow to service the debt. They typically offer lower cost of capital than venture capital, but startups face structural disadvantages — limited operating history, intangible-asset base, and risk-averse bank underwriting often exclude them from conventional facilities. SBA-guaranteed loans, microloans, and P2P lending exist precisely to address these gaps for startups.

## Key attributes

- **Debt, not equity** — no dilution, but mandatory repayment
- **Lower interest rates** — typically cheaper than VC's implicit cost of capital
- **Collateral-secured** — assets pledged (business or personal)
- **Creditworthiness-gated** — credit score, financial history matter
- **Personal guarantees common** — founder's personal credit at risk
- **Flexible use of funds** — working capital, equipment, inventory, expansion
- **SBA/government-backed variants** — guarantee reduces lender risk, opens access
- **Microloan and P2P alternatives** — for startups unbankable by conventional lenders

## When to use

- Asset-backed businesses (equipment, real estate, inventory) where collateral exists
- Predictable cash flow models (services, established SMB) that can service debt
- Founders with strong personal credit and willingness to personally guarantee
- Working-capital and equipment financing where the loan funds revenue-generating assets
- SBA-eligible startups in the US
- When equity dilution at current valuation is unattractive

## When NOT to use

- Pre-revenue startups with no cash flow to service debt
- Founders unwilling to provide personal guarantees on personal assets
- High-burn, growth-stage tech startups (debt would accelerate failure)
- Industries with volatile cash flow where covenant breaches are likely
- When the loan size is too small to justify the underwriting effort

## Common pitfalls

- Personal guarantees that wipe out the founder if the business fails
- Covenant breaches (debt-service ratios, revenue minimums) triggering acceleration
- Mismatched loan term vs asset useful life
- Underestimating the cash-flow drag of monthly repayment on growth investment
- Predatory P2P or microloan terms (effective APR much higher than headline)
- Layering debt on top of debt — cumulative service burden becomes unsustainable

## Key metrics

- **Interest rate** — APR; varies by credit, collateral, loan type
- **Loan amount** — typical SMB loans $50K-$5M; SBA 7(a) up to $5M
- **Repayment term** — months to years; SBA 7(a) up to 25 years for real estate, 10 for working capital
- **Collateral ratio** — value of pledged assets / loan amount
- **Debt-service coverage ratio (DSCR)** — operating cash flow / debt service; >1.25x typical bank minimum
- **Credit score** — personal and business; banks typically require 680+ personal

## Decision questions

- Can our cash flow comfortably service the monthly payment in a downside scenario?
- Are we comfortable with personal guarantees and the personal risk they create?
- Is the use of funds going to generate enough return to justify the interest expense?
- What happens if we miss a covenant — acceleration, default, asset seizure?
- Have we compared SBA, microloan, P2P, and conventional terms?
