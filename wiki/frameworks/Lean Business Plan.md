# Lean Business Plan

## Definition

A lean business plan is a condensed, often one-page version of a traditional [[Business Plan]] that captures only the essential elements — core competencies, value proposition, target market, key processes, financials at a glance, and milestones — and is built to be updated rapidly. Pereira frames it as "a snapshot of a traditional business plan, on a smaller scale" (p. 17): it strips out the long-form narrative and supporting prose, keeping only what teams actually use to make decisions. Candice Lu of OnPrem Solution Partners credits Lean Startup's one-page business model canvas with replacing the traditional 50-page plan in her nine years of practice (p. 53-54). The Lean Plan is not a fundraising document for institutional VCs; it is an **operating** document for the team.

## Key attributes

- **Compact** — typically one page; covers core competencies, value prop, target, key activities, financials at a glance, milestones
- **Speed-optimized** — drafted in hours, not weeks; revised continuously
- **Strips redundancy** — eliminates prose that doesn't drive decisions
- **Built to update** — designed for rapid iteration as the market is tested
- **Focus on core competencies** — forces explicit identification of competitive advantage
- **Visual / scannable** — readable by anyone regardless of business background
- **Pairs with experimentation** — assumes [[Build-Measure-Learn Loop]] iteration
- **Internal-first** — primary audience is the team, not investors

## When to use

- Early-stage startups still testing product-market fit
- Established companies that need a working strategy document (not a fundraising deck)
- Rapidly changing markets where a 50-page plan would be outdated before it's finished
- Onboarding new employees to the core strategy
- Quarterly planning and OKR-setting sessions
- When the team is going to actually use the plan rather than file it
- As the starting point before graduating to a Traditional plan for VC/bank purposes

## When NOT to use

- Raising a Series A or later round — VCs typically want the full Traditional plan
- Bank loans requiring detailed financial schedules and collateral analysis
- Government grant applications with prescribed long-form requirements
- Highly regulated industries (medical, financial services) where regulators expect detail
- When the team uses "lean" as an excuse to skip difficult financial or market analysis

## Common pitfalls

- **Confusing lean with shallow** — short ≠ poorly thought through; depth must still be present in the underlying analysis
- **Skipping financial discipline** — even a one-page plan needs honest cash-flow thinking
- **Treating it as one-and-done** — the whole point is that it gets updated; abandoned lean plans are worse than no plan
- **Using it for the wrong audience** — handing a one-pager to a Series B VC will read as unserious
- **No supporting evidence** — claims still need data behind them, even if the data lives in a separate appendix
- **Skipping competitive analysis** — "lean" is not an excuse to ignore competitors

## Key metrics

- **Plan length** — target one page (excluding appendix)
- **Update frequency** — monthly minimum; weekly during active pivots
- **Time to first draft** — should be hours, not weeks
- **Plan-vs-actual review cadence** — built into ops rhythm
- **Pivots per year** — Lean Plans should accommodate 2-4 pivots without rewrite
- **Core competencies named** — explicit list, not implied

## Decision questions

- Will this plan be revised at least quarterly? If not, why not?
- Have we identified our core competencies, or are we listing features?
- Does our team actually open this document, or does it sit unused?
- When will we know we've outgrown the Lean format and need Traditional?
- Are we using "lean" to avoid hard thinking, or to enable fast iteration?

## Canonical examples

- **OnPrem Solution Partners (Candice Lu)** — nine years of Lean Plan practice; recommends to all clients (p. 53-54)
- **Early-stage startup pre-Seed** — Lean Plan + Business Model Canvas
- **Internal product-launch teams** — Lean Plan for cross-functional alignment
- **Agile transformation** — Lean Plan replacing annual 50-page strategic plan
