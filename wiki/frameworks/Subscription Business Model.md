# Subscription Business Model

## Definition

The subscription business model charges customers a recurring fee (weekly, monthly, or annual) for ongoing access to a product or service. Pereira documents two structural types: (1) **Access** — premium content/software for dedicated subscribers (SVOD, written content, SaaS); (2) **Repeat Services** — a new or restocked product every month (subscription boxes, meal kits). Origins date to 17th-century book clubs and 19th-century opera; the contemporary subscription economy has grown >100% during the 2020 pandemic alone, fueled by payment infrastructure (Stripe, PayPal), analytics (Zuora, Recurly), and the consumer preference shift from ownership to access. The model trades higher upfront customer acquisition cost for predictable long-term recurring revenue.

## Key attributes

- **Recurring billing** — weekly, monthly, or annual cycles
- **Two structural types** — Access (content/software) and Repeat Services (boxes/meal kits)
- **Predictable revenue** — MRR/ARR is forecastable; valuation rewards predictability
- **Customer-relationship depth** — sustained relationship vs. transactional sale
- **Lower upfront barrier** — small recurring price feels lighter than full upfront
- **Churn-sensitive** — small churn changes compound dramatically over time
- **Five maturity stages** — Ad Hoc → Opportunistic → Repeatable → Managed → Optimized

## When to use

- Products/services that deliver ongoing (not one-time) value
- Categories where customer relationship matters and grows over time
- When pricing power exists (or can be built via differentiation)
- B2B contexts where customers prefer opex over capex
- Markets where competitors are subscription-based (table stakes)

## When NOT to use

- One-time purchase categories where customers feel manipulated by forced subscription
- Commoditized categories with no perceived ongoing value
- When churn risk is structurally high (no defensible value proposition)
- Markets with strong cultural preference for ownership
- When the firm can't sustain consistent quality / service delivery

## Common pitfalls

- Underpricing initially (very hard to raise later)
- Ignoring churn until it compounds out of control
- Confusing subscription with loyalty program (different mechanics)
- Failing to invest in customer success (high churn follows)
- Operating across product-sale and subscription cultures inefficiently
- Subscription fatigue (consumers cancel many at once when budget tightens)

## Key metrics

- **MRR** (Monthly Recurring Revenue), **ARR** (Annual)
- **ARPU** (Average Revenue Per User)
- **CLV / LTV** (Customer Lifetime Value)
- **CAC** (Customer Acquisition Cost); target LTV:CAC ≥ 3:1
- **Churn Rate** (logo and revenue); world-class B2B SaaS <5% annual; consumer <40% annual
- **NRR** (Net Revenue Retention); >100% means expansion exceeds churn
- **Retention Rate** (cohort retention curves)

## Decision questions

- Is our value proposition genuinely ongoing, or are we forcing subscription on a transactional offer?
- What's our churn rate, and what drives it (pricing, value, competition)?
- Can we sustain CAC long enough to recover via recurring revenue?
- Are we measuring AND managing both acquisition and retention?
- Where would subscription fatigue affect us?
