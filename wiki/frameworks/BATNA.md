# BATNA

## Definition

BATNA — Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement — is the most favorable course of action a party can take if the current negotiation fails to produce an agreement. Coined by Roger Fisher and William Ury in *Getting to Yes*, it represents the fallback or "Plan B" against which any proposed deal must be measured. A negotiator's BATNA defines their walk-away point: if the offered terms are worse than the BATNA, they should reject the deal and pursue the alternative. The stronger the BATNA, the greater the leverage; the weaker the BATNA, the more vulnerable the negotiator is to pressure and unfavorable terms. Pereira treats BATNA as foundational to negotiation planning — without it, a negotiator has no objective benchmark for evaluating offers and no measure of their own bargaining power.

## Key attributes

- **Fallback option** — the best path forward outside this negotiation
- **Leverage indicator** — stronger BATNA = more bargaining power
- **Walk-away threshold** — anything worse than BATNA should be rejected
- **Objective benchmark** — provides rational basis for accept/reject decisions
- **Dynamic** — must be re-evaluated as market and circumstances change
- **Improvable** — preparation, relationship-building, and resource investment all strengthen it
- **Asymmetric information** — what the counterparty *believes* about your BATNA matters as much as the actual BATNA

## When to use

- Before entering any consequential negotiation
- When deciding whether to accept, reject, or counter an offer
- When you need to assess your true leverage vs. perceived leverage
- When considering whether to invest more time in a stalled deal
- When weighing multiple deals or partner options simultaneously

## When NOT to use

- Trivial transactions where the cost of BATNA analysis exceeds the value at stake
- Pure relationship-driven contexts where forcing alternatives signals bad faith
- When alternatives are genuinely indistinguishable (no real choice exists)
- As a bluffing tool — fake BATNAs collapse under scrutiny

## Common pitfalls

- Confusing wishful thinking with a real alternative (overstating BATNA strength)
- Failing to develop or invest in a BATNA before negotiating
- Anchoring on the current deal and ignoring better alternatives
- Disclosing BATNA prematurely or unnecessarily
- Forgetting to estimate the *counterparty's* BATNA, which is equally important
- Letting BATNA decay during long negotiations without refreshing it

## Key metrics

- **BATNA value** — quantified expected outcome of the alternative
- **Reservation price** — minimum acceptable terms derived from BATNA
- **Leverage ratio** — your BATNA strength vs. counterparty's BATNA
- **Time-to-execute BATNA** — how quickly the alternative can be triggered
- **Confidence interval** — probability the BATNA materializes as expected

## Decision questions

- What will I actually do if this deal does not close?
- Is my BATNA real, or am I telling myself a story?
- How can I strengthen my BATNA before the next session?
- What is the counterparty's BATNA, and how does it compare to mine?
- At what offer do I walk away — and have I committed to that line?

## Canonical examples

- **Job offer negotiation** — a competing offer is the candidate's BATNA; without one, leverage collapses (page 63)
- **Supplier contract** — alternative suppliers form the buyer's BATNA, justifying competitive bidding (page 64)
- **M&A** — standalone growth plan is the seller's BATNA vs. acquirer's offer (page 64)
- **Procurement** — Pereira's Company B leveraging multiple supplier options as BATNA during contract negotiation (page 118)
