# Marginal Costing

## Definition

Marginal Costing is a cost-allocation system used to measure the cost of **variable expenses** — specifically, the effect that changes in production volume have on variable costs and therefore on overall profitability. Pereira anchors it in the underlying concept of marginal cost: "any expense incurred by a company if it produces one extra unit of output," also called incremental or differential cost. The system is "quite important because it gives a more accurate depiction of the state of profitability of a business in the short term," since total fixed costs are relatively stable over short periods and most significant short-term margin changes come from variable cost movements. **Break-even analysis is essential to this method**, used to determine the production level at which the business turns profitable. Marginal cost is also the basis for most price-fluctuation and price-determination decisions, since fixed costs don't move much.

## Key attributes

- **Variable cost focus** — fixed costs are treated as period costs, not unitized
- **Short-term lens** — designed for decisions where fixed costs are sunk
- **Break-even native** — naturally produces contribution margin and break-even point
- **Pricing-friendly** — supports floor pricing, special orders, and incremental-volume decisions
- **Volume-sensitive** — output every time-unit profitability moves the answer
- **Prevents over/under allocation** of overhead since overhead isn't unitized

## When to use

- Short-term pricing decisions, including special-order quotes and floor pricing
- Break-even and contribution-margin analysis
- Make-or-buy and product-mix decisions where fixed costs are sunk
- Capacity-utilization decisions (should we accept incremental volume?)
- Volatile environments where fixed-cost allocation would mislead

## When NOT to use

- Inventory valuation for external financial reporting (most standards require absorption)
- Long-run pricing decisions where fixed costs must eventually be recovered
- Businesses where fixed costs dominate and variable costs are trivial
- Strategic capital-allocation decisions (where total cost matters)
- When management would confuse "marginally profitable" with "structurally profitable"

## Common pitfalls

- Confusing marginal cost with marginal profit — Pereira's red flag is "once marginal cost exceeds marginal profit, the model is heading towards unsustainability"
- Pricing at marginal cost long-term and never recovering fixed costs
- Ignoring stepped fixed costs that jump at certain volumes
- Treating short-term marginal-cost decisions as evergreen pricing policy
- Forgetting opportunity cost on capacity used for incremental orders

## Key metrics

- **Marginal cost** = change in total cost / change in output
- **Contribution margin** = revenue − variable cost
- **Contribution margin ratio** = contribution margin / revenue
- **Break-even point** = fixed costs / contribution margin per unit
- **Variable cost per unit**
- **Total variable cost** = total quantity of output × variable cost per unit (Pereira's formula, p. 12)

## Decision questions

- At what volume do we break even on this product?
- Will this incremental order's revenue exceed its marginal cost?
- Is our marginal cost trending up or down, and what's driving the change?
- Are we close to a stepped fixed cost (a new shift, a new plant)?
- If we accept this special order at marginal cost, what's the opportunity cost?

## Canonical examples

- **Manufacturers evaluating special orders** — accept if price > marginal cost and capacity exists
- **Airlines pricing last-minute seats** — marginal cost of one more passenger is tiny
- **SaaS companies pricing usage-based tiers** — marginal cost per additional API call
- **Restaurants** — break-even analysis on covers per night
- **Energy utilities** — marginal cost of an additional kilowatt-hour at peak
