# Customer Segments

## Definition

Customer Segments is the BMC block that defines the distinct groups of people or organizations a business aims to serve. It's the starting point of the canvas — without identified segments, all other blocks are speculative. Customers are grouped by shared characteristics (demographics, geography, behaviors, needs, value brought to the business) so the firm can design tailored value propositions, channels, and relationships for each. Pereira positions this as the highest-leverage block: get segments wrong and everything downstream is mis-targeted.

## Key attributes

- **Distinct grouping** — segments must be distinct enough to design around differently
- **Multi-dimensional** — defined by demographics, geography, psychographics, technographics, behavior, needs, OR value
- **Tied to value proposition** — each segment may need a different VP, or the firm may need to consolidate to one
- **Validates downstream blocks** — channels, relationships, and revenue are designed PER segment
- **Five canonical types** — Mass Market, Niche Market, Segmented, Diversified, Multilateral Platform
- **Customer != consumer** — the customer is who pays; the consumer is who uses (often the same, but not always)

## When to use

- Defining who you actually serve (vs. who you wish you served)
- Repositioning when current strategy is underperforming
- Deciding which segments to invest in vs. which to deprioritize
- Building targeted GTM motions (different segments need different sales playbooks)
- Designing pricing strategies that vary by segment willingness-to-pay

## When NOT to use

- As a substitute for talking to actual customers — segments are abstractions from real research
- Without testing — segments defined internally without market validation are guesses
- When you're trying to serve "everyone" — that's a failure to segment, not a strategy
- For very early product validation (use individual customer interviews first, segment later)

## Common pitfalls

- Confusing customer segment with customer persona — segment is a group; persona is a specific archetype
- Over-segmenting — too many segments dilutes focus and resources
- Under-segmenting — treating "the market" as one segment misses real differentiation opportunities
- Segmenting by demographics alone when behavior or needs would be more predictive
- Failing to evaluate each segment against criteria: depth of pain, budget, reach, market size, value

## Key metrics

- Size and growth rate per segment
- Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) by segment
- Lifetime Value (LTV) by segment
- LTV:CAC ratio by segment
- Conversion rate at each funnel stage by segment
- Share of segment (your customers / total addressable segment)

## Decision questions

- For each segment we serve: what's the depth of pain, budget, and market size?
- Is our offering tailored to each segment, or are we using one VP for all?
- Which segment generates the highest LTV:CAC ratio?
- Are we serving any segment unprofitably out of habit?
- Which segment is growing fastest, and are we positioned to capture it?

## Canonical examples

- **Spotify** — three distinct segments (users, content creators, advertisers), each with its own VP (pages 215-217)
- **Airbnb** — two-sided platform serving hosts and travelers with different VPs (page 164)
- **LinkedIn** — three segments: job seekers, recruiters, advertisers (page 168)
- **Coca-Cola** — mass market segmentation, broad demographic reach (page 223)
- **Nike** — athletes and fitness enthusiasts as defined segment (page 194)
