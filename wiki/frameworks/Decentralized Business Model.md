# Decentralized Business Model

## Definition

The decentralized business model distributes decision-making authority away from top management and toward middle, lower, and operational layers — the people closest to customers, stakeholders, and the actual work. Pereira frames it as the systematic allocation of authority across an organization's layers, in deliberate contrast to centralization where a single headquarters or executive cohort controls all decisions. The model has a deep corporate history (Pierre du Pont and Alfred Sloan engineered it at General Motors in the 1920s, followed by DuPont 1921, Westinghouse 1934, International Harvester 1943, GE 1950) and a present-day "Decentralization 2.0" revival enabled by blockchain, peer-to-peer networks, and distributed infrastructure. Modern decentralized business models trade headquarters control for speed, local relevance, employee engagement, and resilience — at the cost of consistency, coordination, and the risk of duplication.

## Key attributes

- **Distributed authority** — decisions made at the level closest to the work and customer
- **Top management freed for strategy** — operational decisions devolve, executives focus on long-term direction
- **Faster decision-making** — fewer escalation layers, problems resolved in real time
- **Higher employee engagement** — autonomy and ownership drive morale and innovation
- **Regional / contextual responsiveness** — local managers know their market better than HQ
- **Resilience to single-point failure** — no central node whose collapse halts the system
- **Spectrum, not binary** — most companies sit somewhere between fully centralized and fully decentralized (franchises, MNCs, holacracies are intermediate forms)

## When to use

- Multinational or geographically dispersed operations where local context matters
- Knowledge-intensive industries (consulting, software, R&D) where innovation depends on autonomy
- Markets that change quickly and reward speed over consistency
- Organizations large enough that centralized decision-making becomes a bottleneck
- When customer proximity is competitively decisive (service businesses, retail, B2B field sales)
- When the firm wants to attract and retain high-agency talent

## When NOT to use

- Very small companies — limited workforce makes centralization more efficient
- Categories requiring strict uniformity (regulated industries, brand-consistent retail chains)
- When the firm's competitive advantage is economies of scale (centralized manufacturing, ~3% unit-cost advantage per APQC)
- Industries with high coordination costs across units (where duplication outweighs autonomy gains)
- When the leadership team isn't ready to genuinely cede control (decentralization in name only breeds cynicism)
- Early-stage startups that need a single coherent vision and lack resources for distributed structure

## Common pitfalls

- Decentralizing without strong values and reporting systems — teams drift and lose coherence
- Duplicated functions across units (e.g., parallel customer service teams in every department)
- Lower-level decisions on corporate social responsibility issues that backfire on the whole firm
- Slow change-implementation because there's no single protocol for rolling out new policies
- Treating decentralization as a binary switch rather than a deliberate spectrum decision
- Failing to invest in training, culture, and teamwork that make distributed authority actually work
- Mistaking the absence of hierarchy for the absence of accountability (Zappos's holacracy struggles)

## Key metrics

- **Decision velocity** — time from problem identification to action taken
- **Employee engagement / autonomy index** — survey measures of perceived ownership
- **Regional responsiveness** — local market share growth, time-to-market in new geographies
- **Duplication cost** — overlap of functions across decentralized units
- **Manager retention at middle layers** — proxy for whether autonomy is real
- **Innovation rate** — new products / processes initiated outside HQ

## Decision questions

- Where in our value chain does local context matter more than global consistency?
- Are we decentralizing decisions or just delegating tasks? (Real authority vs. errands)
- What's our reporting and coordination layer that prevents drift?
- Do middle managers have the skills and confidence to exercise authority well?
- Where would decentralization create unacceptable duplication or risk?
- How does our scale dictate the right point on the centralization spectrum?

## Canonical examples

- **Johnson & Johnson** — 130,000+ employees across 100+ countries; local management runs subsidiaries because they know the customer, the market, and the regulatory context better than US HQ (page 40)
- **Eventbrite** — event organizers double as managers; regional managers run global development offices because they're closer to the customer base (page 41)
- **General Motors (1920s)** — Sloan/du Pont's pioneering decentralization that let GM surpass Ford (pages 21-22)
- **Unilever** — decentralized after expansion so global branches operate as independent businesses adapting to local markets (page 60)
- **Zappos** — holacracy experiment removed managers entirely; employees lost progression paths and quit en masse via the buyout option (page 59) — cautionary tale
- **Franchise chains** — hybrid centralized/decentralized: HQ owns product and brand, franchisees own hiring and operations (page 42)
- **Airbnb, Uber** — quasi-decentralized P2P platforms, though Pereira notes they remain centrally controlled in ways that contradict the "sharing" framing (page 29)
