# Eisenhower Matrix

## Definition

The Eisenhower Matrix is a 2x2 prioritization framework that sorts tasks along two axes — Urgency and Importance — to clarify what to do, schedule, delegate, or eliminate. Pereira describes it as "a straightforward 2x2 matrix that aids in sorting jobs according to significance and urgency. It is intended to sharpen focus and hasten decision-making. Decide which of the four quadrants your choices belong in, and then act accordingly." Attributed to U.S. President Dwight D. Eisenhower (who reportedly said, "What is important is seldom urgent, and what is urgent is seldom important"), the matrix's discipline is treating urgency and importance as distinct variables — most poor time use comes from confusing the two.

## Key attributes

- **Two orthogonal axes** — Urgency (time pressure) and Importance (value contribution to goals)
- **Four quadrants with four actions** — do now / schedule / delegate / eliminate
- **Q1 (Urgent + Important)** — do immediately; crises, deadline-driven critical work
- **Q2 (Important, Not Urgent)** — schedule a specific time; strategic, preventive, relationship-building work — the high-leverage quadrant
- **Q3 (Urgent, Not Important)** — delegate; interruptions, some meetings, others' priorities
- **Q4 (Not Urgent, Not Important)** — eliminate; busywork, time-wasters, low-value habits
- **Discipline target** — minimize Q1 (often a symptom of Q2 neglect), maximize Q2, ruthlessly cut Q4
- **Urgency-importance confusion** — the chronic failure mode; urgent things crowd out important ones unless deliberately scheduled

## When to use

- Weekly or daily planning — sort tomorrow's list before starting
- Crisis-mode diagnosis (too much Q1 is a Q2-deficit symptom)
- Calendar audit — what percentage of your time goes to each quadrant?
- Coaching/delegation decisions — Q3 is delegation territory
- Strategic planning — Q2 is where compounding leverage lives (strategy, prevention, skills, relationships)
- Personal productivity reviews

## When NOT to use

- Genuinely simple to-do lists where the framework adds overhead, not signal
- Highly collaborative work where prioritization is a team-level decision
- Crisis moments when sorting takes time the situation doesn't allow
- When the binary urgency/importance call is contested and needs deeper analysis

## Common pitfalls

- **Living in Q1** — most "busy" knowledge workers are stuck there because they haven't invested in Q2
- **Confusing urgency with importance** — the urgent feels important precisely because of the time pressure
- **Skipping the delegation step on Q3** — doing Q3 yourself drains the time you need for Q2
- **Q4 in disguise** — endless email, social media, low-value meetings often masquerade as Q3
- **No protected Q2 time** — without explicit calendar blocks, Q2 work never happens
- **Treating it as static** — what's in Q2 today (strategic planning, training) becomes Q1 if neglected long enough
- **Over-categorizing** — spending more time sorting than doing

## Key metrics

- **% time in Q2** — high-performing knowledge workers protect 30%+ in Q2
- **Q1 volume trend** — chronic Q1 overload signals Q2 neglect
- **Q4 audit** — what fraction of weekly hours is genuinely Q4 and could be cut?
- **Delegated Q3 ratio** — of Q3 tasks, what percentage actually moved to someone else?

## Decision questions

- Is this task urgent because of consequence, or just because someone is asking now?
- Is this important to my goals, or just important to someone else's goals?
- If I don't do this Q2 work today, what Q1 crisis will it create next month?
- Of my last week's hours, what percentage was Q2? Q4?
- What in my Q3 list can I delegate or batch?
- What in my Q1 list could have been prevented by better Q2 investment last quarter?

## Canonical examples

- **Pereira's four-action sort (p. 58)** — "If something is both urgent and crucial, act right away; Give it to someone else if it is urgent but not crucial; If something is significant but not urgent, set a time in the near future to do it; Eliminate it if it's not crucial or urgent"
- **Stephen Covey, 7 Habits of Highly Effective People** — popularized the matrix in modern management literature with the "Habit 3: Put First Things First" framing
- **Dwight D. Eisenhower** — the namesake; famously said "What is important is seldom urgent, and what is urgent is seldom important"
- **Founder time allocation** — strategy, recruiting, and product judgment are nearly always Q2 — easily crowded out by Q3 operational interruptions unless explicitly protected
