# As-a-Service Business Model

## Definition

The As-a-Service (aaS, also Anything-as-a-Service / XaaS) business model shifts from product ownership to a fluid customer-merchant relationship where the consumer rents or temporarily uses the infrastructure or service rather than owning it. The provider takes the role of "value-facilitator" rather than just "value-producer," maintaining and upgrading the infrastructure while charging recurring fees. Pereira's catalog of XaaS variants: SaaS, CaaS (Communications), NaaS (Network), PaaS (Platform), DBaaS (Database), IaaS (Infrastructure), StaaS (Storage), DRaaS (Disaster Recovery), MaaS (Mobility), BaaS (Blockchain), IoTaaS (Internet of Things). Driven by consumer preference for less ownership (57% of surveyed want to own less stuff per Harris Group), pay-as-you-go convenience, recession resilience, scalability, and reduced consumer liability.

## Key attributes

- **Non-ownership** — customer rents capability instead of buying infrastructure
- **Recurring revenue** — typically subscription or consumption-based pricing
- **Value-facilitator role** — provider maintains, upgrades, secures the infrastructure
- **Pay-as-you-go option** — many aaS models use consumption-based pricing
- **Resilience against downturns** — service contracts more stable than product sales (50% vs 23% downturn in 2020)
- **Scalability** — easily scale up/down with customer demand
- **XaaS pattern** — applicable to almost any infrastructure/capability
- **Closely tied to cloud computing** — most aaS models are cloud-delivered

## When to use

- Infrastructure/software with high marginal value but high upfront cost to own
- Categories where customer needs to scale up/down (B2B SaaS, cloud computing)
- When ongoing innovation matters and customers can't keep up with self-managed upgrades
- B2B markets where TCO matters more than upfront price
- When the firm has expertise/economies-of-scale advantage in operating the infrastructure

## When NOT to use

- Products where ownership is fundamental to customer value (luxury, status, identity goods)
- Categories with strict regulatory requirements for on-premises infrastructure
- When the customer requires deep customization that aaS can't deliver
- Markets where consumer preference favors ownership (in some regions, cultures)
- When the firm lacks operational excellence for ongoing service delivery

## Common pitfalls

- Pivoting to aaS without rebuilding cost structure (high operational costs)
- Confusing aaS with subscription (subscription is pricing; aaS is structural model)
- Underestimating customer engagement requirements (aaS requires ongoing customer success)
- Increased product complexity (aaS often requires more sophisticated tech)
- Security challenges (handling customer infrastructure raises security stakes)
- Difficult shift from product-sale culture to ongoing-service culture

## Key metrics

- MRR / ARR (Monthly / Annual Recurring Revenue)
- CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost)
- LTV (Lifetime Value); target LTV:CAC ≥ 3:1
- Churn rate (logo and revenue)
- Gross margin (typically 60-80% for aaS)
- NRR (Net Revenue Retention); target >100%

## Decision questions

- Is our infrastructure better operated by us at scale or by each customer themselves?
- Can we sustain the operational excellence required for aaS delivery?
- What's our customer's TCO calculation — ownership vs. aaS?
- Do we have the financial runway for the cash-flow pattern shift (large customer-acquisition spend, slow recurring recovery)?

## Canonical examples

- **AWS / Azure / GCP** — IaaS (page 17)
- **Salesforce, Microsoft 365, Google Workspace, Slack** — SaaS
- **Heroku, Google App Engine** — PaaS (page 24)
- **Rolls-Royce** — engines "rented" by airlines, charged per hour used (IoTaaS) (page 20)
- **Michelin** — tires by usage rather than purchase
- **John Deere** — tractor/equipment with IoT enabling outcome-based pricing
