# Empathy Map

## Definition

The empathy map is a pictorial framework that visualises what a target customer says, thinks, does, and feels — turning scattered research into a shared, decision-ready view of the user. Originally designed by David Gray of XPLANE as a four-quadrant canvas, its purpose is to surface both spoken and unspoken behaviour so that product, marketing, and CX teams can stop guessing about the buyer and start designing for them. Pereira positions it as the antidote to the common failure mode of "placing company goals before consumer values" — a structured way to immerse the team in the customer's experience before resources are committed.

## Key attributes

- **Says quadrant** — verbatim verbal information from the customer: direct quotes from interviews, usability sessions, reviews, social media, surveys. Keep quotes raw and unfiltered (page 24).
- **Thinks quadrant** — the customer's inner monologue — what they believe but didn't verbalise. Surfaced through guided probing because users hide things out of self-consciousness, politeness, or guilt (pages 25-26).
- **Does quadrant** — observed actions before, during, and after product use. Best captured by direct observation or session recording because user self-reports are unreliable (pages 26-27).
- **Feels quadrant** — the emotional state during the interaction (frustrated, anxious, happy, confident). Filled last because it ties the other three together (page 27).
- **Visual and shared** — designed as a poster so a cross-functional team can argue and converge on a single view, not a private document.
- **One map per persona** — if you have multiple customer segments, build one empathy map per persona, not a composite (page 31).
- **Living document** — must be refreshed as the target demographic shifts; the market is not static (page 38).

## When to use

- Building or sharpening a customer segment block in a Business Model Canvas (page 20).
- Elaborating a user persona or user story before product spec writing (pages 20-21).
- Framing customer interviews — the four quadrants double as an interview guide (page 21).
- Planning a marketing campaign that needs to land on the target's actual cultural and emotional context (page 22).
- Designing up-sell or cross-sell motions where you need to understand the buyer's deeper need (pages 21-22).
- Onboarding a customer-success or CRM team into a customer segment they haven't met.

## When NOT to use

- When you have no real customer data — an empathy map built from assumptions is a confident hallucination, worse than no map.
- When the decision in front of you is a pricing/financial trade-off — use sizing and unit economics, not qualitative empathy.
- As a substitute for [[Customer Journey Mapping]] — empathy maps capture a snapshot of a persona; journey maps capture the experience over time. They complement each other, they don't replace each other.
- Late in execution when the team needs ship-ready specs, not exploratory research.

## Common pitfalls

- **Confusing what they say with what they think** — customers self-edit. Pereira's worked example: "the product was great but took too long" → real thought was "the login process is much longer" (pages 25-26).
- **Bias contamination** — letting team assumptions fill in the gaps. Counter with multiple reviewers, factual data, root-cause analysis (page 53).
- **Treating it as static** — a 12-month-old empathy map is decoration. Refresh as the segment evolves (page 38).
- **Building a composite "average" customer** — kills decisiveness. Build one map per persona.
- **Stopping at the map** — the map is the artefact, not the goal. The goal is a sharper persona and changed decisions (page 41).

## Signals of good use

- Surfaces at least one non-obvious insight the team didn't already hold
- Changes a real product, marketing, or pricing decision (not just gets pinned to a wall)
- Reproducible — two teams researching the same segment land on similar maps
- Used by CRM/CX team to brief new hires on who they're serving

## Decision questions

- Who exactly is this map for — name the persona, not a vague segment.
- Where did each Says/Thinks/Does/Feels item come from — quote, observation, or assumption?
- What decision will this map change in the next 30 days? If none, why are we making it?
- Are the Thinks items genuinely probed, or are they our projections?
- Have we built one map per persona, or are we averaging?

## Canonical examples

- **Jane, 29, scheduling team manager** — Pereira's worked example using a beta SaaS scheduling app: Says "experience was nice but took longer to use", Thinks "loading pages too slow, prefer the old software", Does "clicks loading pages repeatedly", Feels "frustrated by slow pages but impressed by new tools" (pages 44-45).
- **Ink! Coffee gentrification campaign** — failure case: no empathy map of the local Denver community, marketing campaign read as gentrification-celebratory, protests and vandalism followed (pages 15-16).
- **RJ Reynolds smokeless cigarette (1988)** — ~$1B sunk on a product no consumer asked for; classic "placing company goals before consumer values" failure that an empathy map would have prevented (pages 14-15).
