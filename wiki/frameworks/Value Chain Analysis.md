# Value Chain Analysis

## Definition

Value chain analysis (originally Porter, 1985) is the systematic decomposition of a company's operations into discrete activities — primary (inbound logistics, operations, outbound logistics, marketing & sales, service) and support (procurement, technology, HR, infrastructure) — to identify where value is created and where cost or inefficiency lives. The goal is twofold: find opportunities for cost reduction and process improvement, and identify activities where the firm can build a differentiation advantage. Pereira's primary example is Amazon, which used VCA to engineer a supply chain so efficient and a customer experience so seamless that it became the dominant e-commerce player.

## Key attributes

- **Activity-level decomposition** — analysis happens at the activity, not the function or department, level
- **Primary vs. support activities** — distinguishes value-creating operations from enabling functions
- **Cost AND differentiation lens** — every activity is examined for both cost reduction and differentiation potential
- **Linkages matter** — activities don't exist in isolation; the interfaces between them often hide the biggest opportunities
- **Comparable to competitors** — most powerful when the firm's chain is compared activity-by-activity against rivals
- **Strategic input** — outputs feed both cost leadership and differentiation strategies

## When to use

- Diagnosing where cost is genuinely created vs. just allocated
- Identifying which activities to outsource (cost) vs. own (differentiation)
- Building a cost-leadership strategy operationally
- Identifying which activities to invest in to amplify a differentiation strategy
- Comparing operational footprint against a benchmarked competitor
- Pre-acquisition due diligence on a target's operational structure

## When NOT to use

- Pure service or knowledge work where activities don't decompose cleanly
- When the firm is too small for activity-level granularity to add insight
- As a substitute for customer-centric analysis (VCA tells you internal mechanics, not customer value)
- For pure brand strategy questions where the chain is downstream of identity

## Common pitfalls

- Mapping the chain accurately but stopping there — analysis without recommended changes is paperwork
- Confusing activity cost with activity value — high-cost activities can be high-value
- Forgetting linkages — optimizing individual activities can break the system
- Treating VCA as one-time when supply chains evolve rapidly
- Failing to update the chain when the firm pivots its business model

## Key metrics

- Cost per unit at each activity step
- Cycle time per activity
- % value-add vs. total cost per activity
- Bottleneck capacity utilization
- Linkage cost (cost of handoffs between activities)
- Margin contribution per activity

## Decision questions

- Which 2-3 activities create the most customer-perceived value?
- Which activities are necessary but uneconomic to own — should they be outsourced?
- Where are the linkage points and how much friction lives in handoffs?
- If we changed our strategy from cost leadership to differentiation, which activities would have to change?
- Are we measuring each activity, or just departmental P&Ls?

## Canonical examples

- **Amazon** — used VCA to create a highly efficient supply chain and seamless customer experience; engineered fulfillment-center automation and logistics scale that made them dominant in e-commerce (pages 17, 29, 33)
- **Toyota** — production system as a value chain optimization story; high-quality cars at lower cost via process discipline (page 40)
- **Walmart** — supply chain scale and efficiency as the foundation of its cost leadership (pages 15, 37)
