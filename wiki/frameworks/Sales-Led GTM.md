# Sales-Led GTM

## Definition

A Sales-Led Go-to-Market strategy makes the sales team the primary engine of customer acquisition. The sales team drives engagement, generates leads, and converts prospects into paying customers through direct, personalized interactions. Pereira frames this as the appropriate motion for complex, high-touch, or enterprise-level solutions where deep customer relationships and consultative selling are essential to closing — the opposite of a self-serve product motion. In Sales-Led GTM, marketing supports sales (with content, lead gen, ABM), product supports sales (with demos, custom configurations), and the sales rep is the relationship owner from first touch to renewal.

## Key attributes

- **Sales team as primary driver** — reps own the customer relationship, not the product UX
- **High-touch, personalized engagement** — tailored conversations, not standardized funnels
- **Consultative selling** — solution-shaped to each buyer's pain, often custom-quoted
- **Long sales cycles** — weeks to months for SMB, quarters to year+ for enterprise
- **Built around relationship depth** — multi-threading across the buying committee
- **CRM-centric operations** — pipeline, forecasting, and rep performance run through CRM
- **Continuous feedback loop** — field intel flows back to product and marketing
- **Comprehensive enablement** — training on product, competitive, objection-handling, communication

## When to use

- Complex, high-ACV solutions where buyer needs guided evaluation
- Enterprise deals with multiple stakeholders and procurement processes
- Customizable or configurable offerings that don't fit self-serve
- Markets where buyers expect a sales relationship (regulated industries, large IT, services)
- New categories where buyer education is required before they know what to ask for
- When LTV is high enough to amortize a costly direct sales motion

## When NOT to use

- Low-ACV products where unit economics can't support a sales rep
- Self-serve, SMB-friendly products where buyers prefer to try-before-buy
- High-volume / high-velocity markets where speed of acquisition matters more than depth
- When the product is simple enough to sell itself (use [[Product-Led Growth]])
- Early-stage products without product-market fit — adding sales hides the PMF gap, doesn't close it

## Common pitfalls

- Hiring senior reps before the playbook exists (they need a system, not just talent)
- Treating sales as the only function and starving marketing of investment
- Over-dependence on a few star reps — bus factor risk
- Long cycles disguising poor qualification — pipeline that never closes
- Scaling rep headcount linearly with revenue instead of investing in tools and enablement
- Ignoring product feedback the sales team gathers (the feedback loop atrophies)
- Confusing relationships with revenue — friendly buyers who never close

## Key metrics

- **Pipeline value and coverage** — typically 3x quota for healthy coverage
- **Win rate** by stage and by source
- **Average sales cycle length** — and movement quarter-over-quarter
- **ACV** (Average Contract Value) and **deal size distribution**
- **Quota attainment** — percentage of reps hitting plan
- **CAC and CAC payback period** — sales-loaded CAC tends to be high
- **Pipeline-to-close conversion** at each stage

## Decision questions

- Is our deal size large enough to support a $200K+ loaded rep?
- Does our buyer expect or prefer human guidance, or do they prefer self-discovery?
- Can we describe the buying committee, their roles, and the decision criteria?
- Do we have a documented sales process and qualified pipeline, or just relationships?
- Is our CRM the source of truth, or do reps run from spreadsheets and memory?
- How quickly does product feedback from sales reach the roadmap?

## Canonical examples

- **Enterprise SaaS** — Salesforce, Workday, ServiceNow at the enterprise tier (page 15)
- **Consulting and professional services** — high-touch, relationship-driven (page 15)
- **Industrial / B2B equipment** — large capex requires consultative sales
- **Complex platforms** — Snowflake's enterprise motion, Databricks
- **Tesla's early direct-to-consumer model** — though B2C, it was sales-led at flagship stores (page 127)
