# Product Development Lifecycle (9 Stages)

## Definition

Pereira frames new product development as a 9-stage progression from idea to sustained growth, each stage demanding distinct skills, deliverables, and decision criteria. The stages are: (1) Idea Generation and Conceptualization, (2) Feasibility Assessment, (3) Product Design and Development, (4) Testing and Validation, (5) Manufacturing and Production, (6) Marketing and Positioning, (7) Launch and Distribution, (8) Post-Launch Evaluation and Optimization, (9) Sustenance and Growth. Unlike a simple waterfall, the lifecycle is described as cross-functional and iterative within stages — design and testing loop repeatedly, post-launch feedback shapes ongoing iteration. The framework's value is as a stage-gate scaffold: it forces explicit go/no-go decisions and resource reallocation between stages rather than letting projects drift.

## Key attributes

- **Nine sequential stages with gates** — each ends in a go/no-go decision and resource recommit
- **Cross-functional from the start** — research, marketing, engineering, design collaborate from Stage 1
- **Two non-development bookends** — Stage 1 (ideation) and Stage 9 (sustenance) are often skipped or under-resourced, with predictable failure
- **Feasibility assessment is the cheapest filter** — Stage 2 catches the most expensive mistakes early
- **Iterative within stages, sequential between** — design-test loops inside Stage 3-4, but you don't manufacture before validation
- **Marketing starts early** — Stage 6 (Marketing and Positioning) precedes launch, not follows it
- **Launch is not the finish line** — Stages 8 and 9 carry the actual revenue and retention work

## When to use

- New product launches where structure prevents costly skipped steps
- Cross-functional teams that need explicit handoffs and shared vocabulary
- Hardware or regulated products where stage gates are required (medical, automotive, aerospace)
- Organizations migrating from ad-hoc product work to a repeatable NPD process
- Portfolio management — comparing multiple projects at the same stage

## When NOT to use

- Pure software iteration after PMF — agile / continuous deployment beats stage-gate
- Lean startup experimentation — MVP loops collapse multiple stages
- Pivots and pivots-of-pivots — the framework assumes linear progress
- Tiny teams that can't staff cross-functional gates without burning velocity
- When the framework becomes process for process's sake (the "stage-gate theater" anti-pattern)

## Common pitfalls

- Skipping Stage 1 idea generation and starting from a half-baked concept
- Underinvesting in Stage 2 feasibility (technical, financial, market) — leads to costly Stage 5 surprises
- Treating Stage 4 testing as a formality — finding defects in Stage 5 manufacturing is 10-100x more expensive
- Starting marketing (Stage 6) only after launch — no anticipation, no demand
- Declaring victory at Stage 7 launch — most products fail in Stages 8-9 from neglect
- Sequential rigidity — refusing to loop back when validation data demands a redesign
- Stage-gate theater — going through motions without genuine decision authority at each gate

## Key metrics

- **Time to market** — total elapsed time from Stage 1 to Stage 7
- **Stage-gate conversion rate** — % of projects passing each gate
- **Time per stage** — identifies bottlenecks (often Stages 2, 4)
- **Post-launch NPS** — Stage 8 quality indicator
- **Defect rate / quality cost** — caught in testing vs caught in production
- **Feature adoption** — Stage 9 leading indicator of sustenance success
- **Cost per stage** — actual vs budgeted

## Decision questions

- At each gate: do we have evidence this product still deserves the next stage's investment?
- Have we resourced Stages 6, 8, and 9 (the post-development stages) or just Stages 1-5?
- Are cross-functional teams genuinely working together, or is it sequential handoffs in disguise?
- Where in the lifecycle do our products consistently fail or stall? Why?
- Is the gate process adding rigor or just slowing things down?
- For software: would a continuous-iteration model serve us better than this stage-gate?

## Canonical examples

- **Tesla Roadster** — Stages 1-9 visible: concept (eco-luxury sports car), feasibility (battery tech), design (Lotus chassis), validation (early adopters), production (initial scale), positioning (premium EV), launch, post-launch iteration via OTA updates, sustained growth into Model S/3/Y (page 127-128)
- **Peloton bike** — proprietary hardware + content ecosystem developed in parallel through the stages (page 128-129)
- **Airbnb** — non-physical product, but the same lifecycle: ideation (sharing economy), feasibility (host supply), validation (early hosts), launch, iteration (reviews, ID verification), sustenance (Experiences platform) (page 130-132)
