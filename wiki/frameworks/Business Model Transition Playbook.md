# Business Model Transition Playbook

## Definition

Pereira's six-step practitioner guide for running a business model transition. It assumes the target model is chosen and the company is starting the shift. The playbook is operational and behavioral, not strategic — it answers "how do we actually do this without breaking the company" rather than "which model should we move to." Pereira's underlying claim: every business will face a transition at some point, and the difference between success and failure is usually execution discipline, not strategic insight. The six steps are: (1) start with a smaller customer segment, (2) mix and balance old and new teams, (3) allow re-invention, (4) communicate with the team, (5) never forget the customer, (6) take your time.

## Key attributes

- **Start with a smaller segment** — the minimum viable segment; easier to understand customers, adjust the offer, prepare to scale (Facebook = Harvard, Tesla = ultra-rich, Amazon = books)
- **Mix old and new teams** — keep the existing team's know-how and add new members with the target-model expertise; don't replace the team wholesale
- **Allow re-invention** — let long-tenured employees develop new abilities for the new model; they're motivated and add value; remove only those who actively sabotage
- **Communicate with the team** — change is scary; answer the five questions repeatedly: why the transition, why the old model fails, what changed in the market, what it means for stakeholders, why it makes the company stronger
- **Never forget the customer** — bring customers along with explanations and benefits; if they think the product changed (not the model), trust erodes
- **Take your time** — transitions are multi-year, not multi-quarter; gradual change protects cash flow when the old revenue stream phases out before the new one scales
- **Cash flow is the silent killer** — even when the new model is more profitable, the gap while it gains traction can break the business

## When to use

- When a business model transition has been decided and execution starts
- When an incumbent is moving to a new revenue model (e.g., perpetual license to SaaS)
- When a startup is graduating from its transitional model to its target model
- When change management needs a concrete, sequenced playbook rather than abstract principles

## When NOT to use

- As a substitute for the strategic decision of which model to move to
- When the transition needs to be sudden for survival reasons (regulatory shutdown, acquisition mandate)
- For minor product or pricing changes — this is for full business-model shifts

## Common pitfalls

- Skipping the minimum viable segment and trying to transition the whole customer base at once
- Firing the existing team for "lack of new-model experience" and losing institutional knowledge
- Communicating once at kickoff and assuming the team understands — leaders must repeat the whys, hows, and whats throughout
- Going dark with customers, who then assume the value proposition changed rather than the model
- Rushing to "finish" the transition and breaking cash flow before the new model is self-sustaining
- Failing to plan the cash-flow bridge between the phasing-out and phasing-in revenue streams

## Key metrics

- **Segment penetration** of the minimum viable segment before expansion
- **Team retention** through the transition (especially of bridging hires)
- **Customer NPS or churn** during the transition window
- **Cash runway** vs. new-model revenue ramp curve
- **Transition duration** vs. plan
- **Old-model revenue decay rate** vs. new-model growth rate (the crossover gap)

## Decision questions

- What is our minimum viable segment for the new model, and how do we measure traction in it?
- Who on the existing team should bridge into the new model, and what new expertise must we hire?
- Have we answered the five team-communication questions in writing, and are leaders repeating them?
- How are we explaining the change to customers — and what benefits, not features, are we leading with?
- What's our cash-flow bridge if the old revenue stream phases out faster than the new one ramps?
- What's our realistic transition timeline, and what triggers a course correction?

## Canonical examples

- **Facebook** — minimum viable segment was Harvard students; 1,200 sign-ups in 24 hours, half the Harvard population in a month, then Boston, Ivy League, US, world (pages 16-17, 29)
- **Tesla** — minimum viable segment was the ultra-rich Roadster buyers funding the path to mass-market (pages 10-12, 29)
- **Amazon** — minimum viable segment was book buyers, then music and video, then everything (pages 22-24, 29)
- **Software vendors moving to SaaS** — many run perpetual licenses and SaaS concurrently to keep an engaged base satisfied while migrating (pages 27-28)
