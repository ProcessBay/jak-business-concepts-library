# Buyer's Journey Three-Stage Model

## Definition

The Buyer's Journey is the prospect-side counterpart to the seller's sales process — a three-stage progression from problem recognition to purchase decision: Awareness → Consideration → Decision. Pereira positions it as the foundational construct of inbound sales: instead of designing a sales process around what the seller wants to do (qualify, demo, close), inbound teams first map what the buyer is doing at each stage and then build the sales process to support it. In Awareness, the buyer recognizes and prioritizes a problem. In Consideration, they research solution categories. In Decision, they pick a vendor from a shortlist. Each stage calls for different content (educational → comparative → proof) and different sales motion (insight → exploration → advisement). The model corrects the traditional sales failure of pitching solutions to buyers still in awareness.

## Key attributes

- **Three stages** — Awareness (problem recognition), Consideration (solution research), Decision (vendor selection)
- **Buyer-perspective, not seller-perspective** — describes what the customer is doing, not the rep
- **Content type changes per stage** — educational blog posts → comparison guides → case studies/demos
- **Sales motion changes per stage** — insight-led outreach → exploratory conversation → tailored advisement
- **Stage misalignment is the #1 sales failure** — pitching to awareness-stage buyers feels coercive
- **Diagnostic questions per stage** — Pereira provides 3 per stage to assess where the buyer is
- **Pairs with the seller's ICEA process** — Identify-Connect-Explore-Advise mirrors the buyer's journey

## When to use

- Designing or redesigning a sales process (use the journey as the input)
- Building a content marketing plan (map content type to stage)
- Diagnosing why deals stall (often: rep is one stage ahead of buyer)
- Onboarding new SDRs/AEs (teach the journey before teaching scripts)
- Auditing why MQL-to-SQL conversion is poor (likely awareness-vs-consideration mismatch)
- Aligning sales and marketing on a shared model of the buyer

## When NOT to use

- Transactional impulse purchases (B2C, low-consideration goods — the journey collapses)
- When your team mistakes the model for a rigid script — buyers skip stages, loop back
- As a substitute for actual customer research (model is a frame, not the truth)

## Common pitfalls

- Treating the three stages as sequential and atomic — real buyers oscillate
- Designing for Decision-stage buyers only (missing top-of-funnel content)
- Confusing buyer's journey with sales process (they're related, not identical)
- Building generic personas instead of stage-specific journey maps
- Failing to ask diagnostic questions to assess which stage a prospect is actually in
- Forcing every prospect through every stage at the same pace
- Letting marketing own the journey and sales ignore it (or vice versa)

## Key metrics

- **Stage conversion rate** — Awareness → Consideration → Decision drop-off
- **Time-in-stage** — average days per stage; long Consideration suggests poor enablement
- **Content engagement by stage** — which assets pull buyers forward
- **MQL-to-SQL conversion** — proxy for Consideration-to-Decision movement
- **Win rate by entry stage** — Decision-stage entries usually close higher

## Decision questions

- Can our reps tell which stage a prospect is in within the first call?
- Do we have content for each stage, or is everything bottom-of-funnel?
- Where in the journey do most prospects drop off — and why?
- Are we pitching solutions to Awareness-stage buyers (pushing too hard, too early)?
- Have we mapped the journey from buyer research, or from our own assumptions?
- Are sales and marketing operating from the same journey map?

## Canonical examples

- **HubSpot's inbound methodology** — codified the three-stage model into practice
