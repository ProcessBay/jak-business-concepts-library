# E-Commerce Platform Business Model

## Definition

An e-commerce platform sells the storefront-and-operations layer that lets a merchant run an online shop — website hosting, templates, checkout, payments, inventory, order management, and a third-party app ecosystem — typically as a subscription. Pereira splits the category into four structural types (page 33): **Hosted** (Shopify, BigCommerce, Squarespace — full-stack SaaS); **Open-Source** (WooCommerce, Magento, OpenCart — free software, self-hosted, customizable); **Marketplaces** (Amazon, eBay, Alibaba — multi-vendor venues, commission-based); **Social Commerce** (Facebook Shops, Instagram Shopping, Pinterest — selling inside social feeds). Shopify exemplifies the modern Hosted model: e-commerce-as-a-service plus a vibrant app store, integrated payments (Shopify Payments), merchant financing (Shopify Capital), and POS extensions that bridge online and offline (page 57-60).

## Key attributes

- **Subscription-based core revenue** — monthly/annual fee scaled by store size, features, or sales volume
- **Layered revenue streams** — payments take-rate, app-store revenue share, capital/lending, transaction fees
- **App ecosystem flywheel** — third-party developers extend the platform; platform earns rev-share and gets stickier
- **Hosted vs Open-Source trade-off** — Hosted is fast/managed; Open-Source is flexible/customizable but ops-heavy
- **Templates + customization** — non-technical merchants get a store live in hours
- **Payments attach** — the platform's most lucrative add-on; payments alone often exceed subscription revenue
- **Multichannel by default** — modern platforms ship POS, mobile, social, and marketplace integrations together
- **Low merchant CAC, high merchant LTV** — once a store is set up, switching costs are extreme (catalog, integrations, SEO)

## When to use

- Targeting SMB/mid-market merchants who can't or won't build commerce infrastructure themselves
- Markets where commerce infrastructure (payments, fulfillment) is underdeveloped or expensive
- When you can credibly build an app/extensions ecosystem (or partner into one)
- When payments take-rate alone can justify the unit economics
- Vertical-specific commerce (e.g. food, B2B wholesale, services bookings) where horizontal platforms underserve

## When NOT to use

- Enterprise retailers who need deep customization — they pick open-source or build in-house
- Categories dominated by marketplaces where the merchant doesn't need a standalone store
- When you can't offer payments (either by license or partnership) — too much margin lost
- Geographies where local platforms (Salla/Zid in KSA, Mercado Libre in LATAM) have entrenched merchant relationships
- When you cannot sustain platform reliability — downtime on Black Friday is existential

## Common pitfalls

- Building features merchants ask for but don't use; ignoring app-store quality
- Underpricing the base subscription and over-relying on payments (regulatory or competitive disruption to payments breaks the model)
- Letting open-source forks fragment the ecosystem (Magento's history)
- Failing to invest in international payments, taxes, and compliance — blocks geographic expansion
- Marketplace conflict of interest — platform competing with its own merchants (Amazon-style criticism)

## Key metrics

- **GMV** (Gross Merchandise Volume processed through merchant stores)
- **Take rate** (payments + transaction fees as % of GMV)
- **MRR / ARR** (subscription revenue)
- **Merchant count** (active stores) and net adds
- **Payment attach rate** (% of merchants using platform-native payments)
- **App-store revenue share**
- **Logo and revenue churn** (merchant retention; SMB churn is structurally high)

## Decision questions

- Hosted or open-source positioning — and can we credibly do both (Shopify Plus + Shopify open-source headless)?
- Can we own payments, or are we dependent on third-party gateways?
- What's our app-ecosystem strategy — open vs curated, rev-share terms, marketplace governance?
- Which vertical(s) earn us defensibility against horizontal incumbents?
- Where is our merchant-funnel CAC payback — paid acquisition, partner agencies, or organic?

## Canonical examples

- **Shopify** — global hosted leader; PaaS + app store + Shopify Payments + Shopify Capital + POS (pages 57-60)
- **BigCommerce** — hosted, more enterprise-leaning than Shopify
- **Squarespace** — hosted, design-led, smaller merchants
- **WooCommerce** — open-source WordPress plugin; massive long-tail share (page 34)
- **Magento** (Adobe Commerce) — open-source/enterprise hybrid (page 34)
- **Salla** — Saudi Arabia's leading hosted e-commerce platform for SMB merchants
- **Zid** — Saudi competitor to Salla, also hosted
- **Noon** — Saudi/UAE marketplace (marketplace variant)
- **Jarir** — Saudi retailer with own e-commerce, not a platform play
