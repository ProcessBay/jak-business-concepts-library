# Back Stage Vs Front Stage (BMC Sides)

## Definition

A theater metaphor for reading the Business Model Canvas at a macro level before filling individual blocks. The **right side** of the canvas — Customer Segments, Customer Relationships, Channels, Revenue Streams — is the **front stage**: everything customers can see, the value they perceive, the reason they pay. The **left side** — Key Partners, Key Activities, Key Resources, Cost Structure — is the **back stage**: the machinery behind the curtain that makes the front-stage performance possible but stays invisible to the audience. A sound, sustainable business model is one where revenue generated by the front stage exceeds the costs of the back stage, producing profit. Pereira recommends filling the canvas right-to-left (front stage first, since that is where revenue originates), starting with Customer Segments and Value Proposition.

## Key attributes

- **Front stage = right side** — visible to customers, where value is perceived and paid for
- **Back stage = left side** — invisible to customers, where cost is incurred to deliver the show
- **Profit equation** — front-stage revenue > back-stage cost
- **Fill right-to-left** — begin with Customer Segments and Value Proposition
- **Disruption can attack either side** — front-stage (DTC) or back-stage (user-as-producer)
- **Theatre lens is intuitive** — helps non-strategists grasp the canvas quickly

## When to use

- Early business-model design, before block-by-block canvas work
- Coaching founders or operators new to the BMC
- Diagnosing where revenue or cost imbalance is killing margin
- Mapping disruption opportunities (which side is incumbents' weak spot)
- Communicating business-model logic to non-strategy audiences

## When NOT to use

- Detailed financial modeling (the metaphor is directional, not quantitative)
- Multi-sided platforms where "customer" and "producer" are the same actor (the front/back split breaks down — see Waze pattern)
- When the team already operates fluently in canvas vocabulary (the metaphor adds nothing)

## Common pitfalls

- Treating the metaphor as the model rather than as an entry ramp
- Filling the canvas left-to-right (starting with resources/activities), which inverts the logic
- Forgetting that some businesses fuse front-stage and back-stage (UGC, marketplaces)
- Skipping the profit-equation check (revenue side must outweigh cost side)

## Key metrics

- **Revenue from right-side blocks** vs **cost from left-side blocks**
- **Profit margin** as the gap between the two
- (Directional, not formal — this is a mental model, not a measurement framework)

## Decision questions

- Have we filled Customer Segments and Value Proposition before anything else?
- Does our front-stage value justify what we charge?
- Is back-stage cost growing faster than front-stage revenue?
- Where could a disruptor attack our back stage (efficiency play) or front stage (DTC play)?
- Are we accidentally exposing back-stage cost to customers (price transparency without value justification)?

## Canonical examples

- **Dollar Shave Club** — front-stage disruptor: dismissed retailers, merged marketing + distribution, direct access to consumers (page 11)
- **Waze** — backstage disruptor: users themselves provide the navigation data, collapsing the producer-user distinction (page 12)
- **Theater itself** — the original metaphor: audience pays for what they see, but the show depends on invisible crew
