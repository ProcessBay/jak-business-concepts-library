# Direct-to-Consumer Model

## Definition

The Direct-to-Consumer (D2C) model has the manufacturer or brand sell directly to end customers, bypassing wholesalers, distributors, and retailers. Pereira positions D2C as a sub-type of B2C (page 32), but distinguishes its strategic significance: by owning the customer relationship and removing intermediary margins, D2C brands capture higher gross margins (often 40-60% vs. 15-30% wholesale) AND own customer data for personalization, retention, and product iteration.

## Key attributes

- **Disintermediation** — no wholesaler, distributor, or retailer between brand and customer
- **Margin capture** — eliminates retailer markup (15-30%) and distributor margin (5-15%)
- **Customer relationship ownership** — direct data, direct communication, no intermediary
- **Higher CAC** — without retail traffic, brand must drive customer acquisition itself
- **Lower CAC dilution risk** — but better LTV economics with retention
- **Often digital-first** — DTC brands typically launch online with social/digital marketing
- **Product iteration loop** — direct feedback enables faster product evolution

## When to use

- New brand with strong product differentiation that retailers wouldn't prioritize
- Categories where customer experience matters and intermediaries dilute it
- Categories where margin compression at retail prevents brand investment
- When the firm has digital marketing capability to drive direct demand
- For high-LTV products that justify investment in customer acquisition

## When NOT to use

- High-bulk low-margin commodity products (retail efficiency wins)
- Products requiring in-person trial/fit/demo that the brand can't replicate
- When the firm lacks digital marketing capability or logistics infrastructure
- Mature markets dominated by established retail brands
- Categories where impulse purchase at retail is the dominant buying behavior

## Common pitfalls

- Underestimating CAC — DTC marketing has gotten expensive as competition rose
- Treating DTC as "just a website" — it requires full GTM (acquisition, fulfillment, returns, support)
- Ignoring fulfillment economics (shipping cost can break unit economics)
- Inability to scale beyond the digitally-native first cohort
- Pivoting to wholesale too late, missing scale opportunities
- Forgetting that retail still drives discovery (DTC + selective retail often beats pure-DTC long-term)

## Key metrics

- CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost)
- LTV (Customer Lifetime Value)
- LTV:CAC ratio (target 3:1+)
- Gross margin (DTC should be 40-60%+)
- Retention rate / repeat purchase rate
- Direct revenue share (% of revenue from owned channels)
- Average Order Value (AOV)

## Decision questions

- Is our LTV high enough to support the CAC needed to acquire customers directly?
- What digital channels can we sustain economically as we scale?
- Where would selective retail partnerships accelerate brand awareness?
- Are we measuring AND managing both CAC and retention, or just chasing top-of-funnel?
- Is our customer experience genuinely better D2C than retail?

## Canonical examples

- **Warby Parker** — eyewear DTC; later added retail stores (D2C + selective retail) (page 86)
- **Dollar Shave Club** — subscription DTC razors; acquired by Unilever
- **Casper** — mattresses DTC
- **Glossier** — beauty DTC; community-driven product development
- **Allbirds** — sustainable footwear DTC
- **Apple** — partial DTC via Apple Stores + apple.com alongside retail partners
