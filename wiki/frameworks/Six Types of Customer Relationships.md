# Six Types of Customer Relationships

## Definition

A framework cataloguing the six structural modes a company can use to relate to its customers, drawn from the Business Model Canvas tradition and elaborated by Pereira. The six modes — Self-Service, Automated Services, Communities, Personal Assistance, Dedicated Personal Assistance, and Co-Creation — sit on a spectrum from purely transactional and impersonal (kiosk-style) to deeply collaborative (customer-as-product-developer). They are not mutually exclusive: a single firm typically operates several in parallel, matched to different customer segments and journey stages. The choice of mix is a deliberate cost/intimacy tradeoff that shapes pricing, channel design, hiring profile, and tooling investments.

## Key attributes

- **Spectrum, not a menu** — modes range from impersonal/cheap (self-service) to high-touch/expensive (dedicated personal assistance) to participatory (co-creation)
- **Combinable** — most firms run 2-4 modes concurrently across segments and journey stages (e.g., self-service for free tier, dedicated assistance for enterprise)
- **Cost-to-serve trade-off** — each step up the intimacy ladder raises unit cost; pricing and segment value must justify the mode
- **Mode shapes economics** — self-service enables low prices; dedicated assistance enables premium pricing
- **Segment-driven** — different customer subgroups expect different relationship types (BMC question: "what relationship does each segment anticipate?")
- **Technology shifts the frontier** — automated services and communities now deliver near-personal experience at scale via personalization and social tooling
- **Co-creation flips the seller-buyer dynamic** — customers help build the product and become brand champions

## When to use

- During Business Model Canvas design or refresh, to map the relationship block deliberately
- When segmenting customers and pricing tiers (different modes per tier)
- When evaluating cost-to-serve versus customer LTV across segments
- When a single mode is failing a segment (e.g., enterprise on self-service)
- When designing onboarding, support, and success motions
- When considering community or co-creation as a moat against transactional competitors

## When NOT to use

- As a one-time decision — relationship mix should be revisited as segments mature
- When forcing a single mode across all segments regardless of value tier
- When mode choice is driven by founder preference rather than segment expectation
- When the firm cannot operationally sustain the chosen mode (e.g., promising dedicated assistance without the staff)

## Common pitfalls

- Defaulting to "personal assistance" because it feels caring, even when self-service would delight customers and cut cost
- Layering modes without integration, so customers feel inconsistent treatment between channels
- Using dedicated assistance as a sales crutch that doesn't scale post-purchase
- Confusing automated services with self-service — automation personalizes; self-service is bare-bones
- Launching a community with no editorial or moderation muscle behind it
- Treating co-creation as a marketing slogan rather than a real product process

## Key metrics

- **CSAT/NPS** per relationship mode and segment
- **Cost-to-serve** per mode
- **Retention rate and CLV** by mode (does the high-touch tier actually retain better?)
- **Engagement rate** for communities (active users, posts, replies)
- **Self-service deflection rate** (% of issues resolved without a human)
- **Time-to-resolution** by mode

## Decision questions

- What kind of relationship does each customer segment expect from us?
- Which relationships have we already formed, and which are aspirational?
- What is the cost of each mode, and does segment value justify it?
- How well does each mode fit our operational reality (staff, tooling, culture)?
- Where could a higher-intimacy mode unlock pricing power, and where would a lower-intimacy mode let us scale?
- Is our community a real community, or a marketing claim?

## Canonical examples

- **Airport self-service kiosk** — pure transactional self-service (page 20)
- **Amazon recommendations** — automated services that personalize via behavioral data (page 21)
- **GlaxoSmithKline weight-loss forum** — community where customers shared experiences and informed product understanding (page 21)
- **Retail sales floor / call center** — personal assistance pre- and post-purchase (page 22)
- **Private banking relationship manager** — dedicated personal assistance with a single named contact for high-value clients (page 22)
- **Open-source contributors, LEGO Ideas, Threadless** — co-creation, where customers participate in product creation (page 22)
