# Direct Costing

## Definition

Direct Costing is a costing-system in which the business pays key attention to the **direct costs** associated with the production of a specific good or service. Once direct costs are calculated, all indirect costs are written off in the profit-and-loss account. Pereira frames it as a deliberately simple approach — "favorable in certain situations and easier to do since direct costs are more easily identifiable and quantifiable than indirect costs." A critical distinction from absorption costing: direct costing usually does not include fixed costs, because they don't change with output and remain stable over time. Only **direct variable costs** get measured, which makes the method "better suited for short-term decision-making." Pereira's underlying definition of direct cost is any expense that can be directly attributed to a cost object (typically a good or service) — also called specific cost — with examples including direct labor wages, raw materials, manufacturing supplies, shipping fees, and energy consumption.

## Key attributes

- **Direct costs only** — indirect costs are written off, not unitized
- **Usually variable** — most direct costs are also variable (though some fixed direct costs exist)
- **Attribution-based** — every cost loaded onto a unit must be traceable to it
- **Easier to compute** — direct costs are easier to identify than indirect overhead
- **Short-term decision-grade** — built for product-level, near-term decisions
- **P&L treatment** — indirect costs hit the period P&L, not the unit cost

## When to use

- Product-mix decisions where indirect cost is largely uncontrollable at unit level
- Pricing decisions where you need a clean variable-cost floor
- Quick-turnaround analyses where overhead allocation would slow the decision
- Service businesses where direct labor is the dominant cost
- Job-costing environments where direct material and labor are easily traced

## When NOT to use

- External financial reporting requiring absorption-style inventory valuation
- Long-run pricing where indirect costs must be recovered
- Businesses where indirect cost dominates (high-overhead service operations)
- Strategic capacity decisions (where fixed/indirect cost matters)
- When directness of a cost is genuinely ambiguous (you'll force calls and mislead yourself)

## Common pitfalls

- Treating direct cost as the full economic cost of the product
- Misclassifying shared/indirect costs as direct to hit a price target
- Forgetting that some direct costs are fixed (e.g., dedicated machine maintenance)
- Pricing at direct cost long-term, never covering indirect overhead
- Confusing direct costing with marginal costing — they overlap but aren't identical

## Key metrics

- **Direct cost per unit** = direct material + direct labor + other direct costs
- **Direct material cost**
- **Direct labor cost**
- **Contribution margin** (revenue − direct cost)
- **Gross profit** (with indirect cost written off as period expense)
- **Direct cost ratio** = direct cost / revenue

## Decision questions

- Is this cost genuinely traceable to this unit, or are we forcing it?
- For this short-term decision, do indirect costs even matter?
- Are we pricing high enough to cover indirect costs across the portfolio?
- Which of our "direct" costs are actually fixed direct costs we're treating as variable?
- Would absorption costing tell a different enough story to change the decision?

## Canonical examples

- **Construction and contracting** — direct material and direct labor per job, indirect overhead written off
- **Custom manufacturing** — small-batch or made-to-order production
- **Professional services** — billable hours as direct labor; firm overhead as period cost
- **Print and fabrication shops** — direct material + machine time per job
