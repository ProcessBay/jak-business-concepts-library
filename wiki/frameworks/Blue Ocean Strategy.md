# Blue Ocean Strategy

## Definition

Blue Ocean Strategy (originally Kim & Mauborgne, 2005) is the deliberate creation of uncontested market space — a "blue ocean" — either by creating an entirely new market or by disrupting an existing one in a way that makes competition irrelevant. The framework contrasts with "red oceans" where firms fight over the same customers with similar offerings, eroding margins. The strategy works by combining cost reduction in some dimensions with differentiation in others, producing a "value innovation" that opens new demand. Pereira's example is Cirque du Soleil, which created a new market for circus performances by blending theater, dance, and acrobatics — sidestepping competition with traditional circuses entirely.

## Key attributes

- **Uncontested market space** — winning by avoiding the fight, not by winning it
- **Value innovation** — simultaneously reducing some dimensions of competition while raising others, breaking the cost-differentiation tradeoff
- **New demand creation** — attracts non-customers of the existing category, not just steals share
- **Time-limited advantage** — once successful, competitors flood in; the blue ocean turns red
- **Strategy canvas** — visual tool comparing offering's competitive dimensions against industry baseline
- **Eliminate-Reduce-Raise-Create grid** — practical tool for designing the value innovation

## When to use

- When the industry is mature, commoditized, and margin-eroding
- When current customers are paying for features they don't actually value (eliminate opportunity)
- When non-customers exist who would buy a different offering altogether
- When the firm has the conviction and resources to create a category, not just enter one
- When competing head-to-head against incumbents is uneconomic

## When NOT to use

- In emerging markets where the category itself is still being defined
- When the firm lacks the brand, distribution, or marketing reach to educate a new category
- When the "blue ocean" is actually too small to be a viable business
- When the cost of category creation exceeds the value of the eventual win
- When customers in the existing category are highly satisfied (no defection signal)

## Common pitfalls

- Declaring blue ocean while still competing on existing-category dimensions
- Underestimating the marketing investment needed to teach customers about a new category
- Confusing differentiation within a red ocean with true blue ocean
- Creating a blue ocean that's small enough to be ignored by incumbents (and never grows)
- Failing to defend the blue ocean once others copy — first-mover doesn't guarantee winner

## Key metrics

- Revenue from customers new to the category (not just new to the firm)
- Time before first credible competitor enters
- Gross margin in the new space vs. parent industry
- Strategy canvas divergence (how different is our profile from incumbents')
- Customer awareness of the new category

## Decision questions

- Are we competing in a red ocean disguised as a blue one? (Apply the strategy canvas.)
- Which competitive dimensions can we ELIMINATE that the industry takes for granted?
- Which can we REDUCE below the industry standard?
- Which should we RAISE well above it?
- Which CREATE that the industry has never offered?
- Are there non-customers we could attract by changing the offering's shape?

## Canonical examples

- **Cirque du Soleil** — created a new market for circus performances by combining elements of theater, dance, and acrobatics, sidestepping traditional circus competition entirely (page 18)
- **Airbnb** — created the home-sharing category, attracting travelers who wouldn't have stayed in hotels at all (page 41)
- **Uber** — created an alternative to traditional taxi-hailing with on-demand ride sharing (page 44)
- **Netflix** — streaming subscriptions opened a new market structure beyond traditional TV and movie rental (page 42)
- **Tesla** — premium electric vehicles redefined the boundary between automotive and tech (pages 25, 43)
