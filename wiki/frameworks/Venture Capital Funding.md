# Venture Capital Funding

## Definition

Venture capital is institutional private equity invested in startups with outsized growth potential in exchange for equity ownership and active strategic involvement. Pereira documents VC across the full stage progression: seed (initial validation), Series A (early scale and unit-economics proof), expansion/growth-stage (Series B, market penetration), and late-stage (Series C and beyond, pre-exit). VCs deploy fund capital, not personal money, so they answer to LPs and need power-law returns — they invest with the explicit expectation of acquisition or IPO exits within roughly 5-10 years. Beyond capital, VCs contribute strategic guidance, board oversight, portfolio-level networks, and the credibility signal that unlocks subsequent rounds, partnerships, and senior hires.

## Key attributes

- **Institutional fund capital** — LP-backed, return-obligated
- **Stage-segmented** — Seed → Series A → Series B → Series C+ → Pre-IPO
- **Equity in exchange** — significant dilution per round (15-25% typical Series A)
- **Active strategic involvement** — board seats, governance rights, milestone gates
- **High-growth bias** — VCs need 10x+ returns on winners to make the math work
- **Due diligence rigor** — multi-week evaluation of business model, market, team, IP
- **Exit-required** — acquisition or IPO is the assumed liquidity event
- **Portfolio management** — fund diversifies across 20-40 bets per vehicle

## When to use

- Capital-intensive scaling phase where speed beats dilution
- Winner-take-all markets where the funded competitor wins
- Proven unit economics + clear path to $100M+ revenue
- Founder mission tolerates board oversight and exit pressure
- Geographic markets with mature VC infrastructure
- Categories where VC pattern-matching (SaaS, marketplaces, fintech) is favorable

## When NOT to use

- Business unsuited to 10x outcomes — VC math kills it
- Founder unwilling to accept governance, board seats, or exit timeline
- Pre-product or pre-market-signal — too early for institutional capital
- Categories VCs don't fund (lifestyle businesses, low-margin services)
- When dilution would leave founders too thin to stay motivated
- Local/national markets where global scale isn't possible

## Common pitfalls

- Raising too much too early — burn rate inflates, expectations distort
- Misaligned VC partners (firm wants fast exit; founder wants long build)
- Underestimating board governance burden after Series A
- Optimizing valuation over partner quality — bad partners with good price hurt
- Failing diligence due to weak metrics, traction, or storytelling
- Ignoring exit-strategy expectations until they collide with founder vision
- Down rounds — raising at lower valuation triggers anti-dilution, demoralizes team

## Key metrics

- **Pre-money / post-money valuation** — frames dilution
- **Round size and dilution** — Series A typically 15-25% for $5-15M
- **ARR or revenue run-rate** — institutional benchmark for stage
- **Growth rate (T2D3: triple, triple, double, double, double)** — Series A+ standard
- **Burn multiple** — net new ARR / net burn
- **Runway** — months until next raise required
- **LTV:CAC, gross margin, net retention** — unit-economics stack

## Decision questions

- Is our market truly venture-scale (path to $100M+ ARR)?
- Are we aligned with our VC on exit timeline and growth strategy?
- Have we benchmarked terms (valuation, board, liquidation preferences) against market?
- Can we execute against the milestones the round implies?
- What's our plan if the next round doesn't materialize on time?
