# Selective Distribution Strategy

## Definition

Selective distribution sits between intensive (everywhere) and exclusive (one partner per territory): the manufacturer chooses a curated set of retailers per market based on fit, service capability, and brand alignment. Pereira positions it as the natural strategy for **quality-oriented firms** — Dolce & Gabbana sells through Neiman Marcus but not JC Penney or Walmart; branded televisions, home appliances, and furniture move through chosen specialty retailers, not every storefront. The logic is to maximize relevant market coverage while keeping the brand experience controlled and the channel base small enough to manage relationships actively. Big-city McDonald's locations are selective (eight to ten per major city) — present but not saturated. The strategy gives manufacturers stronger control over how their goods are merchandised, what service standards apply, and how disputes are resolved, in exchange for lower raw reach than intensive distribution.

## Key attributes

- **Curated retailer set** — manufacturer chooses partners that fit brand standards
- **Middle ground** — more reach than exclusive, more control than intensive
- **Quality-oriented positioning** — supports premium pricing without scarcity-extreme positioning
- **Stronger channel relationships** — manageable number of partners enables real partnership
- **Better service standards** — selected partners trained to handle and represent the brand
- **Outlet criteria explicit** — store format, location, sales staff competence, service capacity
- **Easier dispute resolution** than intensive (fewer partners, deeper relationships)
- **Pricing discipline** — fewer outlets reduce price erosion from channel competition

## When to use

- Premium-but-not-luxury categories (mid-to-high-end electronics, appliances, fashion)
- Products requiring informed sales conversation or post-sale service
- Brands seeking better margins than intensive distribution allows
- When channel partnership quality affects consumer experience materially
- Markets where retailers vary widely in brand fit and service capability
- Categories where geographic spread matters but not saturation

## When NOT to use

- Commodity or impulse-purchase categories
- Mass-market products where reach drives volume economics
- Categories where exclusive scarcity premium is the entire value proposition
- When the firm lacks the sophistication to qualify and manage chosen partners
- Markets too small to support multiple qualified outlets

## Common pitfalls

- Choosing partners based on capacity rather than brand fit
- Letting selected partners become complacent (no competitive pressure)
- Costly disputes with chosen retailers when there are too few alternatives
- Decreased market penetration if "selective" is interpreted too narrowly
- Failing to enforce the brand standards that justified selection in the first place
- Allowing channel conflict between selected partners (overlapping territories)
- Mistaking selective distribution for exclusive (different contract structure, different enforcement)

## Key metrics

- **Channel partner quality score** (composite: brand fit, service, training, compliance)
- **Sell-through per outlet** (selected partners should outperform per-door averages)
- **Channel NPS** (do partners advocate for the brand?)
- **Average transaction value** (selective should support higher AOV than intensive)
- **Repeat purchase rate** (curated experience should drive retention)
- **Retailer brand compliance** (visual merchandising, pricing, service standards audits)
- **Coverage of priority customer segments** (the right partners in the right places)

## Decision questions

- Have we defined explicit, measurable criteria for which partners qualify?
- Are our selected partners actually delivering on the standards that justified selection?
- Are we missing legitimate demand because our footprint is too sparse?
- How well do we resolve disputes when our partner network is small?
- Are we balancing geographic coverage with brand quality across markets?

## Canonical examples

- **Dolce & Gabbana** — sold at Neiman Marcus and similar premium retailers; not at JC Penney or Walmart (page 37)
- **Branded televisions, appliances, furniture** — distributed via specialty retailers, not every storefront (page 37)
- **McDonald's in major cities** — eight to ten locations per metro, not on every block (page 36)
- **Versace, mid-premium fashion** — selective boutiques and department stores (page 36)
- **Luxury watch brands' selective tier** — extending exclusive flagships with select luxury department stores (page 50)
- **Saudi context — Centrepoint (Landmark Group)** — selective distribution of mid-premium brands (Splash, Babyshop) across chosen mall locations
- **Saudi context — Jarir Bookstore for premium electronics** — curated authorized-retailer status for Apple, Samsung premium SKUs
- **Saudi context — eXtra Stores** — selective distribution of mid-to-premium appliances and electronics across Saudi metros
- **Saudi context — Almarai's foodservice channel** — selective B2B distribution to hotels, restaurants, airline catering — separate from intensive retail lane
