# Business Plan

## Definition

A business plan is a written document that outlines a company's goals, operational model, financial projections, and the strategies for reaching its objectives over a three-or-more-year horizon. Pereira frames it as a "roadmap" with two simultaneous purposes: (1) **external** — securing financing from investors or banks, recruiting employees, attracting partners; (2) **internal** — crystallizing strategy, prioritizing resources, managing change, and creating accountability. A traditional business plan is the lengthy, detailed variant (vs. the [[Lean Business Plan]] one-pager and the Nonprofit variant which adds an impact section). It is most useful as a **living document** that gets revised against actuals, not a one-time fundraising artifact.

## Key attributes

- **Roadmap function** — outlines goals, operational model, financials, and the path between them
- **Three variants** — Traditional (long, VC/bank-ready), Lean (one-page, agile), Nonprofit (adds impact)
- **Dual audience** — external (investors, lenders, partners, hires) and internal (founders, teams)
- **Living document** — meant to be reviewed, adjusted against actuals, and updated as the business evolves
- **Standardized structure** — title page, executive summary, company description, market analysis, competitive analysis, management, products/services, customer segmentation, marketing plan, operations plan, financial plan, supporting documents
- **Three-to-five year horizon** — typical strategic window
- **Accountability mechanism** — plan-vs-actual comparison drives reviews, celebrations, troubleshooting

## When to use

- Raising venture capital or bank financing — investors and lenders expect it
- Entering a business plan competition (mentorship, grants, investment prizes)
- Recruiting senior hires who need to evaluate the company's trajectory
- Bringing on partners, board members, or major suppliers
- Strategic planning sessions at established companies (annual or major-pivot)
- Nonprofit fundraising (donors request impact + financial plans)
- When the founder needs to crystallize a fuzzy idea into a testable strategy

## When NOT to use

- Earliest-stage exploration where a one-page [[Business Model Canvas]] or [[Lean Canvas]] is faster
- When the team uses it as procrastination from launching ("perfect plan" trap, p. 54)
- Highly volatile markets where a 50-page plan is outdated before it's finished (Candice Lu, p. 53)
- When the audience is unclear — different audiences (VC vs. bank vs. employees) need different framings (p. 56)
- As a substitute for actually testing the market with real customers

## Common pitfalls

- **Long-winded** — 50-page plans get ignored; concise beats comprehensive (p. 53, 64)
- **Underestimating user acquisition** — building product ≠ getting customers (p. 54)
- **Striving for the perfect plan** — perfectionism becomes a roadblock to starting (p. 54)
- **Failing to research the target market** — guts rather than data leads to costly errors (p. 55)
- **Forgetting cash flow** — profitable companies still die from cash mismanagement (p. 55)
- **Misunderstanding the plan's purpose** — wrong content for the actual audience (p. 56)
- **Underestimating competition** — assuming uniqueness without checking alternatives (p. 57)
- **Leaving little room to pivot** — rigid plans break against real market feedback (p. 58)
- **Treating it as a one-time deliverable** — plan unused after writing defeats its purpose (p. 69)

## Key metrics

- **Plan-vs-actual variance** — by section (revenue, expenses, milestones)
- **Milestones hit rate** — % of planned milestones achieved on time
- **Cash runway (months)** — derived from cash-flow statement
- **Investor response rate** — % of plans sent that lead to meetings
- **Plan review frequency** — monthly is the recommended cadence (p. 25)
- **Number of revisions per quarter** — proxy for plan-as-living-document

## Decision questions

- Who is the primary audience for this plan, and what do they need to see?
- Are we writing this to raise capital, align the team, or both?
- Do we have the data (market, competitive, financial) to back our claims?
- Is our financial plan realistic, or is it an aspirational sales pitch to ourselves?
- How will we review and update this plan — monthly, quarterly, never?
- Where does our plan leave room to pivot if market feedback contradicts us?
- Should we start with a Lean variant and graduate to Traditional only when needed?

## Canonical examples

- **VC pitch plan** — Traditional, 20-40 pages, heavy on financials and team (p. 16-17)
- **Bank loan plan** — Traditional, emphasizes cash flow, collateral, repayment (p. 56-57)
- **Internal alignment plan** — Lean one-page, focuses on priorities and metrics (p. 17-19)
- **Nonprofit donor plan** — Traditional + impact section, emphasizes mission outcomes (p. 19-20)
- **Mejuri, Endy** — cost-leadership-positioned plans (p. 39)
- **Knix, Qalo** — differentiation-positioned plans (p. 39)
- **TomboyX, Heyday Footwear** — segmentation/niche-focused plans (p. 40)
