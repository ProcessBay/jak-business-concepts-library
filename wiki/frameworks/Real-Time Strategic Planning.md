# Real-Time Strategic Planning

## Definition

Real-Time Strategic Planning is a flexible, fast-cycle strategic-planning model designed for organizations operating in rapidly changing environments where detailed long-term plans become obsolete within 3-5 years. Rather than committing to a static plan, the organization runs strategic planning continuously at three levels — Organizational, Programmatic, and Operational — adapting its strategy as conditions shift. Pereira frames it as the model of choice when "well-detailed plans with a long-term outlook" no longer survive contact with reality, and when the firm must respond to swift environmental changes faster than conventional planning cycles allow.

## Key attributes

- **Three-level structure** — Organizational (vision, market position, competitors), Programmatic (external opportunities/threats), Operational (internal processes, personnel, systems)
- **Continuous, not cyclical** — strategic adjustments happen as conditions warrant, not on a quarterly clock
- **Externally reactive at the Programmatic level** — only external research drives programmatic shifts
- **Internally reactive at the Operational level** — focuses on strengthening workforce and inner processes
- **Lighter governance** — no monthly Cascade reports or Hoshin catchball; decisions made as needed
- **Built for VUCA environments** — volatile, uncertain, complex, ambiguous markets
- **Risk** — without structure, can devolve into reactive firefighting

## When to use

- For startups and scaleups in fast-moving sectors (AI, crypto, consumer trends)
- During crises or major environmental shifts where long-range plans are stale
- When the firm has prior experience of "well-detailed plans becoming obsolete within three to five years" (Pereira's diagnostic)
- For organizations facing regulatory whiplash, rapid technological change, or competitive turbulence
- When pairing with Scenario Planning — scenarios define the futures; Real-Time selects among them as signals emerge
- As a transitional model between Issue-Based (short, narrow) and Hoshin/Cascade (long, structured)

## When NOT to use

- For mature firms in stable industries — overhead and instability exceed benefit
- When leadership conflates "real-time" with "no plan" — discipline is still required
- For organizations that need long capital cycles or multi-year R&D commitments
- When the team lacks the senior judgment to make rapid strategic calls
- As a permanent posture for firms that should be settling into longer cycles

## Common pitfalls

- Reactive firefighting masquerading as strategy
- No durable strategic frame — every quarter is a new direction
- Confusing tactical agility with strategic adaptability
- Failing to define the three levels — operational decisions made without programmatic context
- Burnout — continuous strategic adjustment exhausts leadership
- No retrospective discipline — the firm learns nothing from its adjustments
- Using Real-Time to dodge the harder work of making strategic commitments

## Outputs / signals of good use

- A standing organizational frame (vision, value proposition, competitive landscape) refreshed periodically
- Programmatic strategy that visibly tracks external opportunities and threats
- Operational strategy with clear internal-process improvements in flight
- Strategic decisions documented with their triggering signals
- Faster competitive response than peers using conventional planning
- Leadership team in agreement on which level a given question lives at

## Decision questions

- Is our environment genuinely fast-moving enough to justify abandoning conventional cycles?
- For the latest strategic shift — which level (Organizational, Programmatic, Operational) triggered it?
- Do we have the senior judgment to make real-time calls, or are we delegating to firefighting?
- What's our retrospective cadence — when do we learn from our adjustments?
- Are we adapting strategically, or just reacting tactically?
- When should we graduate to a longer-cycle model?

## Canonical examples

- **Pereira's triggers** — regulatory policy changes, rising prices, supply chain disruptions, new market players — all reasons that "well-detailed plans" go stale within 3-5 years (pages 66-68)
- **AI-native startups** — model and tooling shifts every few months invalidate longer-cycle plans
- **Crypto and DeFi firms** — regulatory uncertainty + rapid market structure changes
- **Consumer brands chasing TikTok trends** — opportunity windows measured in weeks
- **Crisis-era retail (COVID-19)** — firms that pivoted to e-commerce real-time outperformed those waiting for the annual plan
