# Freemium Business Model

## Definition

Freemium offers products or services in basic form free of charge while requiring a fee to access locked-off advanced features. The combination of "free" + "premium" creates a customer acquisition engine: users get exposed to value at zero cost, then convert to paid as they hit feature/usage/support limits. Pereira documents 95% of Google Play and 92% of Apple App Store apps use freemium. Three primary limitation tactics: feature limitations, usage quotas (storage, credits, processing), and support limitations (response time, channels). Five distinct variants exist: Traditional Freemium (basic free, advanced paid), Land and Expand (B2B SaaS), Freeware 2.0 (full product + ads/add-ons), Ecosystem (free base + paid marketplace), Alternative Product Strategy (free product as cross-sell hook).

## Key attributes

- **Free forever, not free trial** — no time limit on the basic tier
- **Three limitation types** — features, usage quotas, support
- **Five variants** — Traditional, Land & Expand, Freeware 2.0, Ecosystem, Alternative Product
- **High user acquisition, lower conversion** — orders of magnitude more free users than paid
- **Penny gap matters** — the gap between $0 and first paid tier is psychologically large
- **Mobile/SaaS dominant** — 92-95% of major app stores
- **Network effects friendly** — free tier grows network value for paid tier

## When to use

- Categories with low marginal cost of serving a free user (digital, software)
- When product-market fit benefits from large free user base for feedback
- When network effects compound user value
- SaaS B2B where bottom-up adoption is feasible (Land & Expand)
- When competitor analysis shows freemium is table stakes in your category

## When NOT to use

- Categories with high marginal cost per user (would destroy unit economics)
- When the target customer is enterprise procurement (they prefer evaluation contracts)
- When free users won't generate any value (referrals, ads, future paid conversion)
- When the product is mature and brand differentiation is around premium positioning

## Common pitfalls

- Giving too many features in free — no reason to upgrade
- Giving too few features in free — no users to convert from
- Treating freemium as acquisition only (it's a revenue strategy, requires ongoing innovation)
- Not nurturing free users (assuming auto-conversion)
- Underestimating cost of supporting free users
- Failing to communicate value gap between free and paid

## Key metrics

- Free-to-paid conversion rate (typical SaaS: 2-5%; consumer apps: <1%)
- Free vs. paid user ratio
- ARPU (paid users only)
- Time-to-conversion
- Engagement (free users) — must be high enough to indicate value
- Churn (paid users)

## Decision questions

- Are we giving away too much (no incentive to upgrade) or too little (no users)?
- What's our conversion rate vs. category benchmark?
- Are we extracting value from free users (ads, referrals, feedback)?
- Is the gap between free and first paid tier psychologically reasonable?
- What value would a paid user lose if downgraded back to free?

## Canonical examples

- **Dropbox** — limited storage free; pay for more space + features (pages 16, 21)
- **Spotify** — free with ads + skip limits + no offline; paid removes all (page 21)
- **Slack** — free workspaces with message history limit; paid unlimited + more (page 20)
- **LinkedIn** — fully functional free; premium for recruiters, sales reps, individual professionals (page 20)
- **Skype** — free Skype-to-Skype; paid for landline/mobile/SMS (page 19)
- **YouTube** — ad-supported free; Premium removes ads (page 22)
- **Mailchimp** — generous free tier (2,000 subscribers); paid for scale (page 23)
- **Zoom** — 40-min group call limit on free (page 62 in HCMM source)
