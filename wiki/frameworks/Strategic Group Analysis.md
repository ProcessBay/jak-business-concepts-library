# Strategic Group Analysis

## Definition

Strategic Group Analysis identifies clusters of firms within an industry that share similar competitive strategies — cost structure, product range, distribution channels, pricing policies, R&D intensity, market segments served. Companies inside the same group typically compete for the same customers with similar resources. The concept was introduced by Harvard Professor Michael S. Hunt in his 1972 doctoral thesis, defining a strategic group as "a cluster of firms that share the same or similar strategies regarding production technology, distribution channels, market segments served, pricing policies, types of products offered, labor use policies, advertising activities, and research and development efforts." The analysis is visualized as a two-axis map where firms are plotted on dimensions like price vs. inventory breadth, with circle size representing market share. Rivalry is most intense within a group, not across the whole industry.

## Key attributes

- **Cluster-level analysis** — sits between firm-level (SWOT) and industry-level (Porter's Five Forces)
- **Two-axis visualization** — typically a strategic group map with price on one axis, breadth/specialization on the other
- **Circle sizing** — firms drawn as circles sized by market share or revenue
- **Mobility barriers** — structural reasons firms cannot easily switch between groups (capability gaps, brand positioning, capital requirements)
- **Intra-group rivalry highest** — firms within a strategic group compete most directly because they pursue overlapping customers with overlapping strategies
- **Same industry, different competitive sets** — fast-food chains and fine-dining restaurants share the food-service industry but belong to different strategic groups with different customers, economics, and rivals
- **Reveals white space** — empty quadrants on the map may indicate unserved segments or strategically unviable positions

## When to use

- Mapping a complex industry to clarify *who actually competes with whom*
- Identifying which competitors are direct (same strategic group) vs. peripheral
- Before pricing or positioning decisions to understand the cluster you're already in
- When evaluating whether to reposition into a different strategic group (and the mobility barriers to crossing)
- Spotting under-served strategic positions where a new entrant could establish a defensible niche
- Combining with Porter's Five Forces to recognize that force intensity often differs sharply between groups in the same industry

## When NOT to use

- For industries with few firms (groups collapse to individual firms)
- When the relevant dimensions of competition are not yet stable enough to define axes
- As a substitute for customer-level analysis (the map shows competitor positions, not customer preferences)
- For platform/multi-sided businesses where strategic groups are harder to define across sides
- When the analysis devolves into arguing over axis selection rather than acting on insight

## Common pitfalls

- Choosing axes that conflate or correlate (e.g., price and quality often correlate, collapsing the map)
- Ignoring small or new entrants who don't fit cleanly into existing groups but may be early disruptors
- Confusing strategic groups with market segments (groups are firms; segments are customers)
- Forgetting mobility barriers — companies sometimes assume they can switch groups easily, but mobility barriers are real costs (brand, capability, customer trust)
- Treating the map as static when competitive positions evolve

## Key metrics

- **Number of strategic groups in the industry** — diagnostic of industry maturity and fragmentation
- **Market share by group** — concentrates strategic attention on dominant clusters
- **Average profitability by group** — reveals which positions actually pay
- **Mobility-barrier height** — qualitative score for how hard it is to enter/exit a group
- **Intra-group concentration** — how close together firms sit within their group
- **Distance to nearest group** — how isolated a strategic position is

## Decision questions

- Which group are we currently in, and is that the group we want to be in?
- If we wanted to move groups, what mobility barriers would we face?
- Are there empty positions on the map — and if so, why are they empty (unviable, or just unexploited)?
- Which group has the most favorable Five Forces dynamics?
- Are any new entrants forming a *new* strategic group that doesn't yet exist?
- Which group's mistakes can we learn from cheapest?

## Canonical examples

- **Food service industry** — fast-food chains (Pereira's primary example) form one strategic group based on affordability, speed, broad menu, and competitive pricing; fine-dining, cafés, and family restaurants form distinct other groups with different value propositions (pages 51-52)
- **Athletic footwear retail** — Nike, Adidas, Puma, New Balance, Reebok all share a strategic group, competing for overlapping customers with similar product types (page 52)
- **Technology company spotting an untapped retiree segment** — used as illustration of how strategic group analysis can surface adjacent groups where first-mover advantage is available (page 55)
