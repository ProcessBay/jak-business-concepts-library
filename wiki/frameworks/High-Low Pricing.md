# High-Low Pricing

## Definition

High-Low (Hi-Lo) Pricing is a retail framework where the retailer cycles between a high "regular" or list price and significantly lower promotional prices on a rotating basis across the assortment. Pereira describes it as the structural counterpart to EDLP: instead of one flat, trustworthy price, the retailer manufactures urgency and event-driven traffic by repeatedly running sales, doorbusters, BOGO offers, and clearance pushes. Hi-Lo dominates department-store retail (Macy's, Kohl's), much of apparel, and event-driven categories like back-to-school, Black Friday, and holiday. It works by tapping consumer "deal psychology" — shoppers feel they win when they buy at the discount and may pay full price on adjacent items. The model demands high merchandising and inventory-planning capability, because every cycle creates margin risk if the promo doesn't lift volume enough to offset the discount.

## Key attributes

- **Price variability by design** — list price set high specifically so promotions feel meaningful
- **Event-driven traffic** — Black Friday, BOGO weekends, end-of-season clearance drive store visits
- **Anchor + discount mechanic** — original price displayed next to sale price to make savings legible
- **Blended margin management** — full-price and promo-price units balance to a target gross margin
- **Excitement and urgency** — limited-time framing converts deal-sensitive shoppers
- **Seasonal cadence** — promotional calendar mapped to category seasonality
- **Inventory churn lever** — markdowns clear aging stock to make room for new
- **Promo dependency risk** — shoppers may stop buying at full price if cycles are too predictable

## When to use

- Department stores, fashion/apparel, and categories where novelty and impulse drive purchase
- When inventory has limited shelf life (seasonal, fashion, fresh) and clearance is structural
- When merchandise mix is broad enough that loss-leader items can pull traffic to higher-margin adjacent buys
- In markets where competitors run promotional calendars (you must match the cadence)
- When the brand can afford an event/marketing budget to advertise sales

## When NOT to use

- Commodity categories where shoppers know the "real" price and resist the anchor (groceries staples)
- When shopper trust matters more than excitement (warehouse clubs, Aldi-style operators)
- When operational capacity can't handle promo execution (resets, signage, staff)
- When promotions have trained shoppers never to buy at full price (margin collapses)
- For luxury brands where discounting damages exclusivity and brand equity

## Common pitfalls

- Over-promoting until shoppers refuse to pay full price ("promotional addiction")
- Inflating list prices unrealistically — regulators and class actions punish fake "was" prices
- Failing to model the blended margin — deep discounts on hero items don't lift adjacent sales
- Stockouts on the doorbuster within the first hour (bait-and-switch perception)
- Cadence becomes predictable — shoppers wait for the next sale instead of buying now
- Promotional fatigue erodes brand image, especially for mid-tier and aspirational retailers

## Key metrics

- **Full-price sell-through %** — share of units sold at regular price (Hi-Lo health indicator)
- **Markdown rate** — discount depth relative to original price
- **Blended gross margin** — full-price + promo-price weighted margin
- **Promotional lift** — incremental units sold vs. baseline forecast
- **Promo dependency ratio** — share of revenue from promo periods (high ratio = addiction)
- **Foot traffic during promo events** — Black Friday, doorbuster effectiveness
- **Basket size on promo vs. non-promo days** — does the deal pull additional purchases?

## Decision questions

- Is our category novelty-driven (apparel, electronics) or staple-driven (groceries)?
- What share of revenue currently comes from promo periods — and is it climbing year over year?
- Are our list prices defensible if regulators or media examine the "was / now" claim?
- Do we have the merchandising calendar, signage capacity, and staff to execute a Hi-Lo cadence?
- Is our shopper deal-motivated, or have we trained them to wait for the sale?

## Canonical examples

- **Macy's** — classic Hi-Lo department store; runs "One Day Sale", coupon stacking, doorbusters (page 49)
- **Kohl's** — Kohl's Cash, percent-off coupons, layered promotions
- **JCPenney's failed 2012 EDLP pivot** — Ron Johnson removed coupons and discounts; sales collapsed 25%, proving Hi-Lo customers cannot be switched to EDLP overnight
- **Best Buy** — Black Friday and holiday-driven Hi-Lo in consumer electronics
- **Bed Bath & Beyond's 20%-off coupon** — became the brand identity; removal accelerated demise
- **Black Friday, Cyber Monday, Boxing Day** — industry-wide Hi-Lo events (page 50)
