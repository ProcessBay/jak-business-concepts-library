# Integrative Negotiation

## Definition

Integrative negotiation — also called collaborative or win-win negotiation — treats the negotiation as a joint problem-solving exercise where both parties cooperate to expand the pie of value before dividing it. Pereira contrasts it sharply with distributive bargaining: instead of fighting over a fixed sum, integrative negotiators surface underlying interests, share relevant information, and engineer trades across multiple issues so both sides gain more than positional haggling could deliver. The shift is from positions ("I want X") to interests ("I want X because of Y"). Once interests are visible, creative trades become possible: each party gives on issues they value less and receives on issues they value more. The approach prioritizes long-term relationships, sustained trust, and outcomes that exceed what either party could have claimed in a zero-sum frame.

## Key attributes

- **Expanding-pie assumption** — joint value can be created before division
- **Interests over positions** — surface why each party wants what they want
- **Open information sharing** — disclosure unlocks trades
- **Joint problem-solving** — both sides own the search for solutions
- **Multi-issue trades** — exchange concessions across dimensions of unequal value
- **Long-term orientation** — relationship continuity is part of the value
- **Creative options** — brainstorm before evaluating

## When to use

- Long-term partnerships, alliances, joint ventures
- Multi-issue deals (M&A, complex contracts, employment packages)
- Internal negotiations across departments or with employees
- Repeat-game contexts where reputation compounds
- When trust exists or can be built during the process
- When both parties have asymmetric preferences across issues (trades available)

## When NOT to use

- Pure single-issue price negotiations (no trades possible)
- One-shot transactions with no future relationship
- When the counterparty refuses to share information or play in good faith
- Time-pressured contexts that don't allow exploration
- When power asymmetry is so extreme that "collaboration" masks coercion

## Common pitfalls

- Sharing information unilaterally without reciprocity (gets exploited)
- Confusing being nice with being integrative (substance, not style)
- Failing to claim value at the end — integrative players must still close
- Treating every deal as integrative when distributive is appropriate
- Inability to detect when the counterparty is using integrative talk to disguise distributive play
- Generating creative options but never converging on a decision

## Key metrics

- **Joint value created** — sum of value to both parties vs. distributive baseline
- **Trades executed** — count and asymmetry of concession exchanges
- **Bilateral satisfaction** — both sides' post-deal sentiment
- **Relationship durability** — repeat-deal rate, referral rate
- **Issues resolved** — coverage vs. deferred-to-later

## Decision questions

- What does the counterparty actually need (vs. what they're demanding)?
- What can I give cheaply that they value highly — and vice versa?
- Have we surfaced all the issues, or are we negotiating only on price?
- Is the counterparty genuinely cooperative, or simulating cooperation?
- How will we capture value at close, not just create it during exploration?
