# VC Sector Specialization

## Definition

VC firms cluster on a spectrum from pure generalist ("we invest in great founders building category-defining companies, full stop") to deep vertical specialist ("we only do FDA-regulated therapeutics" or "we only do silicon and space"). Pereira's catalog shows both extremes are viable at scale: Andreessen Horowitz ($32.4B, generalist with sector practices) and Vivo Capital ($5.4B, pure healthcare/biotech) both rank among the top firms. The strategic claim of a sector specialist is that *domain expertise creates an information edge* — they read deal flow faster, diligence biology or chip-design or regulated-fintech faster, and operate the company better post-investment via in-house experts. The strategic claim of a generalist is that *the next category-defining company doesn't fit existing sector buckets*, and that pattern-matching across sectors yields a sharper investment instinct. Most mid-and-large firms hybridize — generalist brand at the top, sector practices underneath, partner-level specialization in practice.

## Key attributes

- **Sector concentration spectrum** — pure-play (Vivo, ARCH, Third Rock = healthcare/life sciences only) → multi-sector specialist (a16z's fintech/crypto/bio/games practices) → pure generalist (Sequoia's "great founders, any sector")
- **Domain-partner team** — sector firms staff partners with PhDs, ex-operators, ex-regulators
- **Information edge claim** — earlier deal-flow visibility, faster diligence, better post-investment ops
- **Sector-specific operating playbooks** — life-sci firms run clinical-trial design support; deep-tech firms run gov-grant capture; fintech firms run banking-license navigation
- **Exit channels differ by sector** — biotech often IPO/acquire-by-pharma; SaaS often strategic acquisition or IPO; deep tech often gov/defense procurement
- **Concentration risk** — sector-specialists boom and bust with sector cycles (biotech 2020-21 vs 2022-23)
- **Saudi specialization angles** — Sanabil broad with Vision-aligned tilt (gigaprojects, sports, gaming); Wa'ed Ventures Aramco-adjacent (energy-tech, industrial); KAUST Innovation Ventures deep-tech / IP commercialization

## When to use (as a founder)

- Your company sits in a regulated, technical, or capital-intensive sector where domain knowledge is load-bearing (FDA, FERC, FDIC, FAA, fab capacity)
- You need an investor who can sit on the scientific advisory board, not just the board
- You want sector-specific introductions (pharma BD, defense procurement, banking partners) more than generalist network
- Your sector has long capital-intensive cycles (biotech, fusion, fabs) — match with a firm that has patient sector-specific LPs

## When NOT to use

- You're building a category that doesn't yet exist (no specialist firm covers it — generalist will move faster)
- Your business is software-defined and operates the same as any SaaS — generalist multi-sector pattern-matching helps more than narrow domain expertise
- The sector specialist has a competing portfolio company (sector firms concentrate competitive risk)
- You want speed over signal — sector specialists' deeper diligence is slower

## Common pitfalls

- Confusing brand-level sector claim with partner-level reality (a16z brands "bio" but most partners are software-trained)
- Choosing a healthcare specialist for a healthcare-adjacent software play (overpriced for the actual risk)
- Underestimating portfolio conflict — sector firms WILL invest in your future competitor if it fits their thesis
- Sector firms ride sector hype cycles harder; valuation discipline weakens at the peak (crypto 2021, biotech 2020)
- Sector specialization is often partner-level, not firm-level; the partner leaves and the expertise leaves with them

## Key metrics

- **Sector concentration** — % of fund deployed in your sector
- **Sector portfolio count** — how many active investments do they have in your space
- **Domain-partner ratio** — % of partners with operating/scientific background in your sector
- **Sector exit track record** — multiples and IRR specifically within your sector
- **Sector cycle exposure** — what's their oldest active vintage in this sector
- **Conflict density** — how many competitors are already in their portfolio

## Decision questions

- Does this firm have a real sector team or just sector marketing?
- Who at the firm has actually operated in our specific sub-vertical?
- What's their portfolio conflict map — direct competitors, adjacent threats?
- How will this sector specialization help us at the next stage (Series B and beyond)?
- Will their sector cycle exposure hurt our valuation if the sector turns?

## Canonical examples

- **Pure healthcare/life sciences** — Vivo Capital ($5.4B, biotech/pharma/medical devices/health services, p.36); Third Rock Ventures ($3.4B, biotech, p.50); Versant Ventures ($3.3B, life sciences, p.51); ARCH Venture Partners ($7.7B, life sciences and physical sciences, p.31)
- **Deep tech / hard sciences** — Lux Capital ($5.1B, AI/robotics/energy/space, p.37); Oxford Sciences Innovation ($325.8M, university-spinout deep-tech, p.96); Cottonwood Technology Fund ($153M, p.100)
- **Climate / clean tech** — Khosla Ventures ($7.2B, clean tech focus alongside generalist, p.34)
- **Fintech-leaning** — Ribbit Capital (not in catalog but archetype), Lightbank ($387M, consumer/internet/fintech tilt, p.94)
- **Marketplaces** — FJ Labs ($5.5M operating capital, marketplace specialist, p.112)
- **Multi-sector "platform" generalist** — Andreessen Horowitz ($32.4B, with named practices in bio/crypto/games/fintech, p.15); Sequoia Capital ($29.1B, multi-sector by partner, p.16); Insight Partners ($47.6B, software-only generalist, p.12)
- **Diversity-thesis** — Gaingels ($9.6M, LGBTQ+ founders, p.110)
- **Saudi specialization** — Wa'ed Ventures (Aramco — energy/industrial tech); KAUST Innovation Ventures (deep-tech / IP commercialization); MCIT-CODE backed funds (digital infra); Hala Ventures (early Saudi tech); Impact46 (Saudi tech focus)
