# Hotel Franchise Model

## Definition

The hotel franchise model is how most global hotel brands (Marriott, Hilton, IHG, Accor, Wyndham, Choice) actually scale: the brand owner (franchisor) licenses brand, standards, reservation system, loyalty program, and operating playbook to an independent property owner (franchisee), who funds and operates the hotel and pays an upfront franchise fee plus ongoing royalty (typically a % of gross room revenue) and marketing contribution. Pereira distinguishes **soft franchise** (more design and operational flexibility for the franchisee, lighter brand standards) from **hard franchise** (stricter design, operational, and brand-standard requirements with closer franchisor monitoring). The model lets the brand grow geographically using franchisee capital while the franchisee gets brand recognition, central reservations, loyalty-program demand, and group purchasing power they could not build alone — but loses a meaningful slice of revenue and operating autonomy.

## Key attributes

- **Soft vs. hard franchise** — flexibility/rigor spectrum on design, ops, brand standards
- **Upfront franchise fee** — substantial one-time payment ($50K-$500K+ typical for major brands)
- **Royalty on gross room revenue** — typically 4-6% (some brands higher); paid regardless of profitability
- **Marketing/system contribution** — additional 1-4% for brand marketing and tech systems
- **Loyalty-program demand pipeline** — franchisee gets access to Marriott Bonvoy, Hilton Honors, World of Hyatt member bookings
- **Central reservation system access** — chain distribution + GDS connectivity
- **Brand-standard enforcement** — design, service, operating procedures must comply; failures = penalties or loss of flag
- **Long contracts** — typically 10-20 years with renewal options
- **Property-improvement-plan (PIP)** obligations — franchisor can require capital investment to maintain standards

## When to use

- Owner wants brand recognition and loyalty-program demand they can't generate independently
- Owner has capital and real estate skill but lacks operating depth in hospitality
- New market entry where local owner needs international brand to attract corporate and OTA bookings
- Scaling a brand globally without raising owner capital for each property
- Conversion of an independent property struggling for distribution into a chain flag

## When NOT to use

- Property has strong independent brand equity already (boutique/lifestyle hotels often lose by franchising)
- Owner unwilling to comply with brand standards or fund PIP obligations
- Markets where the brand's distribution and loyalty advantage is weak
- When franchise economics (fees + royalty + marketing) erase GOPPAR below independent baseline
- When franchisor's brand reputation is volatile — bad incidents elsewhere affect this property

## Common pitfalls

- Underestimating total cost of franchise (fee + royalty + marketing + system fees + loyalty redemption costs + PIP capex)
- Choosing a flag for brand prestige without modeling whether the brand actually drives demand in this market
- Friction between franchisor standards and local market reality
- PIP shocks at renewal — franchisor mandates millions in capex to keep the flag
- Brand-reputation damage from incidents at other franchisees affecting this property
- Loss of customer-data ownership to the franchisor loyalty program
- Channel conflict — franchisor-direct bookings competing with property's own marketing

## Key metrics

- **Effective royalty rate** (all fees + marketing combined as % of revenue) — true cost of the flag
- **Brand RevPAR Index** — property RevPAR vs. competitive set; the test of whether the flag earns its cost
- **GOPPAR after franchise fees** — independent vs. franchised comparison
- **Loyalty-program booking share** — measures the brand-demand benefit
- **PIP capex obligation cycle** — modeled as recurring capital cost

## Decision questions

- Does the brand actually drive incremental RevPAR in our market, net of all fees?
- Have we modeled total cost of the flag, including PIP and loyalty-redemption economics?
- Can we live with the operational and design constraints for 10-20 years?
- What happens at renewal — what's the realistic PIP scenario?
- If the franchisor's brand suffers a reputational hit, what's our exposure?

## Canonical examples

- **Marriott International** — runs ~70%+ of its system through franchise/management agreements rather than ownership
- **Hilton, IHG, Hyatt, Accor, Wyndham, Choice Hotels** — major global franchisors with brand families spanning luxury to economy
- **Holiday Inn / Holiday Inn Express** (IHG) — classic franchise-driven brand
- **OYO Rooms** — asset-light variant that standardizes independent budget properties under a single brand without classic franchise structure (page 93)
- **Rotana** — MENA-origin operator with franchise-management agreements across Saudi Arabia and GCC
- **Saudi developments under Marriott/Hilton/IHG flags** — much of NEOM, Red Sea Global, AlUla, and Riyadh expansion is being built by Saudi owners under international franchise/management agreements
