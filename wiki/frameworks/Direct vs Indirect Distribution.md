# Direct vs Indirect Distribution

## Definition

Distribution channels are the pathways through which a product moves from producer to customer. Pereira frames the channel choice as a binary architectural decision with downstream implications for control, margin, reach, and customer relationship. **Direct distribution** sells straight to the end customer without intermediaries (D2C website, own retail stores, direct sales force). **Indirect distribution** uses intermediaries — wholesalers, retailers, resellers, agents, distributors, VARs — who hold the end-customer relationship. Most firms operate hybrids, but the dominant channel shapes the company's economics, brand control, and ability to learn from customers. The decision turns on five trade-offs: control vs reach, margin vs scale, customer intimacy vs intermediary expertise, capex vs opex, and brand consistency vs market access.

## Key attributes

- **Direct gives control** — full ownership of customer experience, brand presentation, pricing, and data
- **Indirect gives reach** — leverages partner networks, existing customer bases, and local expertise
- **Direct concentrates margin** — no intermediary takes a cut, but distribution cost is yours to bear
- **Indirect spreads margin** — partners earn a share but absorb logistics, inventory, and local sales cost
- **Direct enables faster feedback loops** — customer data flows back unfiltered
- **Indirect enables faster geographic expansion** — partners already operate in target markets
- **The choice is rarely pure** — most firms run hybrid (D2C + retail, direct sales + channel partners)
- **Online complicates the binary** — e-commerce and marketplaces blur direct/indirect lines

## When to use

**Use Direct when:**
- Product is complex and requires education / consultative selling
- Brand experience and customer intimacy are competitive differentiators
- Customer data is itself a strategic asset (recommendation engines, personalization)
- Margins won't survive sharing with intermediaries
- Target audience is concentrated or niche (one D2C site can reach them all)

**Use Indirect when:**
- Mass-market reach is the priority and your category is commoditized
- Geographic expansion requires local presence you can't build fast
- Customers expect to buy through established retailers (CPG, hardware)
- Logistics, warehousing, and last-mile would consume capital you don't have
- Partners bring established customer relationships you can't replicate

## When NOT to use

**Don't use Direct when:**
- You lack the capital for distribution infrastructure
- Your customer base is too dispersed for direct economics to work
- Speed-to-market matters more than long-term control
- Your team has no operational expertise in fulfillment / customer service

**Don't use Indirect when:**
- Brand experience is your moat (Apple, Tesla, luxury brands)
- Partner channels would dilute your value proposition or pricing
- You need real-time customer feedback for product iteration
- Margins are already thin (intermediary share kills unit economics)

## Common pitfalls

- Choosing direct for the romance ("we want to own the customer") without the operational chops
- Choosing indirect for the speed without building partner enablement (partners then underperform)
- Channel conflict — direct and indirect channels competing for the same customer, often on price
- Underestimating the cost of D2C infrastructure (logistics, support, returns)
- Over-relying on a single dominant partner (channel concentration risk)
- Failing to design for omnichannel — customers research online and buy offline (or vice versa)
- Ceding brand presentation entirely to partners and losing the narrative

## Key metrics

- **Channel margin** — gross margin by channel; partner channels typically 15-40% lower
- **Channel revenue mix** — % from direct vs indirect; track shift over time
- **CAC by channel** — direct CAC is often higher upfront but yields better data
- **Time to market by channel** — partner channels usually faster to launch
- **Geographic coverage** — % of target market reachable per channel
- **Brand consistency score** — qualitative assessment of message integrity across channels

## Decision questions

- What is the dominant way our target customer prefers to buy?
- Is our product complex enough to require direct education, or simple enough to sell on a shelf?
- Do we have (or can we build) the operational capability to run direct distribution?
- How quickly do we need geographic coverage, and can partners get us there faster?
- What's the LTV difference between a direct customer and an indirect one (data, retention, upsell)?
- Are we set up to manage channel conflict, or will direct and indirect cannibalize each other?
- How critical is real-time customer feedback to our product iteration speed?

## Canonical examples

- **Tesla** — direct-to-consumer, eschewed dealerships to control brand and customer experience (page 127)
- **Apple** — hybrid: Apple Stores (direct) + carriers and retailers (indirect)
- **CPG brands (P&G, Unilever)** — heavily indirect through grocery and mass retail
- **Dollar Shave Club, Warby Parker** — D2C disruption of traditional indirect categories
- **Enterprise software** — often hybrid: direct sales for enterprise, channel partners for SMB
- **Airbnb** — direct platform model, no intermediaries between host and guest (page 130)
