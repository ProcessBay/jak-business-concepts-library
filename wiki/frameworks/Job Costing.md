# Job Costing

## Definition

Job Costing is a costing method for goods or services tailored to the specific requirements of the task at hand — cost is charged per "job" or project carried out. Pereira sees it everywhere project-based work happens: construction, software design, manufacturing of small batches of unique products. The method helps a business identify which classes of projects are more profitable than others, and gives it a defensible basis for costing similar jobs in the future — "which can be quite difficult in an industry that deals majorly with tailor-made services." The mechanic is mathematically tight: pull together the cost of labor, raw materials, and operational overhead for each particular job, then apply the formula:

**Total Job Cost = Direct Materials + Direct Labor + Applied Overhead**

Contract costing (also called terminal costing) is the close cousin used for large-scale, multi-period projects.

## Key attributes

- **Per-job aggregation** — every cost is traced or applied to a specific job number
- **Built for unique work** — designed when every job has different specifications
- **Three components** — direct materials, direct labor, applied overhead
- **Profitability per job** — surfaces which classes of work make or lose money
- **Future estimation basis** — historical job costs feed quotes for similar future jobs
- **Document-heavy** — job cost sheets, time tickets, material requisitions per job

## When to use

- Construction, contracting, and infrastructure projects
- Software development with billable engagements
- Custom manufacturing and small-batch production
- Professional services (legal, accounting, consulting) with per-engagement billing
- Any business pricing tailored work where the next quote needs cost evidence

## When NOT to use

- Continuous-flow mass production (use process costing)
- Identical-product batch manufacturing (use batch costing)
- Pure subscription / utility-style services (use service costing)
- When jobs are too small or short to justify per-job tracking overhead
- When job definitions are unstable and costs can't be cleanly assigned

## Common pitfalls

- Picking a sloppy overhead application rate that distorts every job
- Not capturing all direct labor (under-billed hours = under-costed job)
- Treating change orders as freebies — original job cost balloons silently
- Confusing job cost with job price (cost discipline is upstream of pricing decisions)
- Failing to roll job-level data into estimating standards for future quotes

## Key metrics

- **Total job cost** = direct materials + direct labor + applied overhead
- **Direct material cost** per job
- **Direct labor cost** per job (hours × rate)
- **Applied overhead** = overhead rate × allocation base (hours, labor cost, etc.)
- **Job margin** = job revenue − total job cost
- **Cost per project** by class of work
- **Estimating accuracy** = actual vs. quoted cost variance

## Decision questions

- Which classes of jobs consistently lose money — and should we stop bidding them?
- Is our overhead application rate causal, or are we over/under-burdening certain jobs?
- Are change orders being costed and billed, or absorbed?
- How accurate are our quotes — do post-mortems feed back into estimating?
- Which jobs have the highest margin — and can we win more of them?

## Canonical examples

- **Construction firms** — every building/site/contract is a job
- **Custom manufacturers** — small-batch unique products (page 32)
- **Software consultancies** — per-client engagements with hours-based labor
- **Legal and accounting firms** — per-matter billing with time tracking
- **Printers and signmakers** — per-order costing with materials and machine time
