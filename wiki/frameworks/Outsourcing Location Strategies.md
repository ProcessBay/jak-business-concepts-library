# Outsourcing Location Strategies

## Definition

Outsourcing strategies are classified by the proximity of the vendor to the buyer along three tiers: **onshore** (same country — also called local or domestic outsourcing), **nearshore** (different country, geographically close — e.g., U.S. and Canada, Eastern Europe and Western Europe), and **offshore** (significant geographic distance — e.g., Brazil and Italy, U.S. and India). Pereira frames the three as a single trade-off curve: each step further from the buyer typically widens the talent pool, deepens the cost arbitrage, and reduces regulatory entanglement — at the price of communication friction, oversight cost, legal-recourse complexity, and quality-control distance. The choice is orthogonal to *what* function is being outsourced (BPO, KPO, ITO, etc.) — the same function can be delivered onshore, nearshore, or offshore.

## Key attributes

- **Three discrete tiers** — onshore, nearshore, offshore (page 24)
- **Distance is the variable** — language, culture, time zone, regulation, and oversight all correlate with distance
- **Nearshore is a hybrid** — combines benefits and shortcomings of the other two (page 27)
- **Cost arbitrage strengthens with distance** — particularly when contracting from developed to developing economies (page 12, 26)
- **Talent pool widens with distance** — especially in specialized fields (page 26)
- **Oversight and recourse weaken with distance** — physical meetings harder, legal systems differ (pages 25, 26)
- **Distance ≠ country** — coast-to-coast U.S. partnerships face many of the same frictions as cross-border (page 26)

## When to use

- **Onshore** — when oversight, language, time-zone alignment, and legal-recourse simplicity outweigh cost savings; for tasks needing physical proximity or local market literacy
- **Nearshore** — when the firm wants cost savings and a wider talent pool but cannot tolerate full offshore friction; useful for live collaboration with limited time-zone overlap
- **Offshore** — for large-scale cost optimization, deep specialist talent unavailable locally, or 24-hour follow-the-sun coverage; favored by large enterprises pursuing aggressive cost or talent arbitrage (page 26)

## When NOT to use

- **Onshore** — when limited to a single country's expertise pool would forfeit major cost or capability advantages (page 26); when domestic wage structure makes the work uneconomic
- **Nearshore** — when the cost or talent advantage is too small to justify cross-border complexity; when neither party speaks the other's language well
- **Offshore** — when data sovereignty, IP risk, or real-time collaboration needs are decisive; when the firm lacks the governance maturity to manage at distance

## Common pitfalls

- Assuming onshore eliminates communication and oversight problems — distance within a country can mimic offshore frictions (page 26)
- Choosing offshore on cost alone, then absorbing more cost in oversight, rework, and exit than was saved
- Underestimating regulatory differences (data residency, employment law, IP enforcement) in offshore deals
- Treating nearshore as "offshore lite" without specifying which benefits and which frictions actually apply
- Concentrating all outsourcing in one country and inheriting that country's geopolitical and macro risk

## Key metrics

- **Labor cost delta** vs in-house — gross and net of oversight
- **Time-zone overlap hours** with key vendor team
- **Communication friction score** — language proficiency, escalation latency, missed handoffs
- **Regulatory risk score** — data residency, employment law, IP regime
- **Oversight cost** — travel, governance, liaison FTE
- **Talent pool size** — candidates meeting required spec in that geography
- **Legal recourse complexity** — single jurisdiction vs treaty-dependent enforcement

## Decision questions

- How much real-time collaboration does this work require?
- Is the cost arbitrage large enough to absorb the governance overhead?
- Can our team manage a vendor across this distance and time zone?
- What's our exposure if the legal regime around this vendor changes?
- Could a nearshore option capture most of the cost and most of the oversight?

## Canonical examples

- **Onshore** — outsourcing IT or delivery to a local company in the same country (page 24)
- **Nearshore** — U.S. ↔ Canada; Western Europe ↔ Eastern Europe; U.S. ↔ Mexico (page 27)
- **Offshore** — Brazilian firm contracting an IT company in Italy (page 26); U.S. firms using Indian software dev centers
- **Coast-to-coast U.S.** — Pereira's reminder that distance matters more than borders; opposite U.S. coasts face frictions similar to UK ↔ Germany (page 26)
- **Saudi context** — onshore Riyadh-to-Riyadh vs nearshore GCC vs offshore South Asia is the typical decision space
