# Dynamic Pricing

## Definition

Dynamic pricing changes price in response to real-time conditions — demand, supply, time, customer segment, competitor price, even individual browsing behavior. Pereira lists five sub-types (segmented, peak, changing-market, time-based, penetration). The canonical archetypes are airlines flexing fares minute-to-minute, Uber surging at concert end, and Amazon adjusting prices algorithmically across millions of SKUs. Dynamic pricing is the structural opposite of static pricing — it trades predictability for revenue optimization.

## Key attributes

- **Price changes in real time** — driven by algorithms, data, or rules
- **Demand-elastic** — price moves to balance supply and willingness-to-pay
- **Five sub-types** — segmented, peak, changing-market, time-based, penetration (page 164)
- **Captures consumer surplus** — extracts more from less-elastic buyers
- **Inventory and yield optimizing** — clears low-demand, premium-prices high-demand
- **Tech-intensive** — needs data, analytics, optimization infrastructure
- **Customer-trust sensitive** — frequent changes erode perceived fairness

## When to use

- Perishable inventory (seats, rooms, event tickets, ad impressions)
- Categories with predictable demand spikes (rush hour, season, events)
- Markets where willingness-to-pay varies measurably by segment or time
- E-commerce at scale where algorithms can monitor competitors
- Industries with sophisticated yield management traditions

## When NOT to use

- Brands built on price fairness and trust (frequent changes feel exploitative)
- Categories with low data volume or thin signal
- Regulated markets where price discrimination is constrained
- Small businesses without analytics infrastructure to manage it
- B2B contracts where customers expect price stability for budgeting

## Common pitfalls

- Surge pricing during emergencies — reputational disaster (Uber post-storm)
- Customer learns the system and waits out the algorithm
- Triggering price wars when all competitors match algorithmically
- Regulatory scrutiny on price discrimination (page 167)
- Implementation complexity outruns operational maturity
- Brand devaluation when customers see fluctuating prices as arbitrary

## Key metrics

- **Revenue per unit** (vs. static-pricing counterfactual)
- **Price elasticity coefficient** — `E_d = %ΔQuantity / %ΔPrice` (page 168)
- **Occupancy / utilization rate** (capacity industries)
- **Demand volatility** (how much there is to optimize against)
- **Inventory turnover** (clear-rate at low-demand prices)
- **Customer trust score / NPS** (downside watchlist)

## Decision questions

- Is our inventory genuinely perishable or capacity-constrained?
- Do we have the data and analytics infrastructure to optimize, not just react?
- Will customers perceive our dynamic prices as fair, or as gouging?
- What's our regulatory exposure on price discrimination in our jurisdictions?
- What revenue uplift would justify the implementation and brand risk?

## Canonical examples

- **Airlines** — fares fluctuate by booking timing, seat availability, demand (page 167)
- **Uber, Lyft surge pricing** — fares spike during high-demand events (page 167)
- **Amazon** — algorithmic pricing across millions of SKUs based on competitor and demand signals (page 167)
- **Ticketmaster** — event ticket prices adjust to demand and remaining inventory (page 168)
- **Hotels, ride-hailing, utilities peak pricing** — common operational pattern
