# Strategic Partnership Lifecycle (Pereira)

## Definition

Pereira's Strategic Partnership Lifecycle is a comprehensive, eight-phase framework spanning the full arc of a strategic partnership: (1) objective setting (SMART goals aligned with both parties), (2) ideal-partner identification, (3) value-proposition crafting, (4) legal-and-regulatory structuring (contracts, antitrust, IP, data protection / GDPR/CCPA), (5) due diligence and partner selection, (6) negotiation and partnership structuring (JV, alliance, equity, licensing), (7) ongoing management and maintenance (communication, conflict resolution, performance assessment), and (8) leveraging, measurement, adaptation, and exit. The framework's distinctive contribution is treating the lifecycle as one continuous, iterative process — partnerships that succeed are managed end-to-end, not just signed.

## Key attributes

- **Eight-phase end-to-end arc** — from objective-setting through exit, not just deal close
- **SMART objectives upfront** — specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, time-bound
- **Legal-and-regulatory layer** — contracts, antitrust, IP, GDPR/CCPA woven throughout
- **DD-then-negotiate sequencing** — never structure before validating
- **Multiple structural options** — JV, alliance, equity, licensing — chosen based on fit
- **Active management discipline** — communication frameworks, conflict-resolution protocols, KPIs
- **Adaptation and iteration** — partnerships are not static; periodic review and adjustment built in
- **Designed-for-exit** — clear exit clauses, data/asset-division plans, customer-transition plans

## When to use

- For any strategic partnership material enough to justify formal process discipline
- When operating across multiple partnership types and a unified playbook is needed
- For partnerships in regulated industries where compliance is woven through the lifecycle
- When the firm's partnership track record is weak and a stronger methodology is needed
- For board-reported partnerships requiring documented rigor and governance

## When NOT to use

- For low-stakes transactional or one-off relationships
- When applied as bureaucratic theater rather than living discipline
- When the firm lacks the bandwidth to actually run all eight phases — partial execution is worse than tailored alternative
- For partnerships small enough that the framework's overhead exceeds the value at risk

## Common pitfalls

- Going straight to negotiation, skipping objective-setting and DD
- Treating contracts as the partnership instead of the framework around it
- Underinvesting in the management phase — most partnerships fail in execution, not formation
- Failing to schedule periodic adaptation reviews — partnerships drift without forcing function
- No exit plan at signing — termination becomes painful, expensive, and reputation-damaging
- Skipping cross-cultural and communication design — international partnerships fail here repeatedly
- Performance reviews focused only on financial KPIs, missing qualitative relationship health

## Key metrics

- Partnership ROI (financial)
- Time-to-value (months from signing to first measurable benefit)
- KPI attainment rate (SMART objectives met)
- Partner NPS / relationship-health score
- Renewal or extension rate at term end
- Dispute rate and escalation count
- Exit smoothness (when partnerships do end)

## Decision questions

- Are our SMART objectives genuinely shared with the partner, or just internally drafted?
- Have we executed all eight phases, or skipped some "to save time"?
- Who owns each phase internally — partnership lead, legal, ops, finance?
- Are our governance and conflict-resolution protocols documented and active?
- When did we last formally review and adapt the partnership against changed conditions?
- Are exit clauses written and would we still want them today?

## Canonical examples

- **Apple + IBM (MobileFirst for iOS)** — Multi-year lifecycle: clear enterprise-market objectives, due-diligence-informed structuring, joint product development, ongoing management, evolution into broader integration (pages 93-97)
- **Microsoft + LinkedIn** — Acquisition-as-partnership lifecycle: $26.2B deal followed by phased integration of services into Outlook, Dynamics 365, Microsoft 365 (pages 106-107)
- **Starbucks + Nestlé** — Objective-aligned global distribution partnership with sustainability commitments, product co-development, e-commerce expansion (pages 98-100)
- **McDonald's + Uber Eats** — Lifecycle from launch through expansion to McDelivery integration with continued evolution (pages 108-110)
