# BANT Lead Qualification

## Definition

BANT is the original lead-qualification framework — Budget, Authority, Need, Timeline — used to determine whether a prospect is worth a salesperson's continued time. Originating at IBM, BANT instructs reps to confirm during early discovery that the prospect has (B) the money to buy, (A) the decision-making authority or access to it, (N) a real and articulated need, and (T) a defined purchase timeline. Pereira presents BANT as the canonical lightweight qualifier — a few questions early in a Connect/Discovery call that quickly separate suspects from prospects. The framework is criticized for being seller-centric (it asks what the seller needs to know to close, not what the buyer needs to decide), but remains the default starting point for SDR training and CRM qualification fields. More modern frameworks (MEDDIC, CHAMP, GPCT, Shopify's 4/5 Threshold) extend or invert BANT but rarely replace it entirely.

## Key attributes

- **Four dimensions** — Budget, Authority, Need, Timeline
- **Pass/fail logic** — most teams require all four; some require three of four
- **Early-discovery placement** — qualified before the demo or proposal
- **Seller-centric** — asks what helps the seller forecast, not what helps the buyer decide
- **CRM-native** — virtually every CRM has BANT fields out of the box
- **Foundation framework** — most modern qualifiers (MEDDIC, CHAMP) are BANT derivatives
- **Best for transactional B2B** — fits short-cycle, single-stakeholder deals
- **Weak fit for complex enterprise** — modern enterprise deals require richer qualification (champions, metrics, paper process)

## When to use

- SDR teams qualifying inbound leads for handoff to AEs
- Short-cycle B2B sales (30-90 day cycles)
- High-volume inside sales motions
- When forecast hygiene matters more than depth
- Training new reps (BANT is simple, teachable)
- Solo founders qualifying their own pipeline

## When NOT to use

- Complex enterprise deals with buying committees (MEDDIC, MEDDPICC fit better)
- Long-cycle, multi-stakeholder transactions
- When budgets are fluid (modern SaaS often "find the budget" after seeing value)
- Solution selling where need is not yet articulated by buyer
- Strategic accounts where BANT-style interrogation damages relationship

## Common pitfalls

- Asking BANT questions like an interrogation ("So what's your budget?")
- Requiring 4/4 BANT before pursuing — kills viable early-stage opportunities
- Treating "no budget today" as disqualification when budget is reallocatable
- Confusing the economic buyer (Authority) with a champion or end-user
- Letting reps fake-qualify deals (filling CRM fields without real data) to inflate pipeline
- Sticking with BANT when the motion has evolved to need MEDDIC/CHAMP
- Skipping BANT entirely because "we do solution selling" — still need the basics

## Key metrics

- **MQL-to-SQL conversion** — % of marketing leads that pass BANT
- **Qualified-opp-to-close** — win rate on BANT-qualified opps
- **Time-to-qualify** — minutes per discovery call to BANT-qualify
- **Forecast accuracy** — BANT-qualified pipeline should forecast tightly
- **Disqualification rate** — healthy SDR teams disqualify 50-70% of leads

## Decision questions

- Are we using BANT as a hygiene check or as a deal-killer?
- Does our motion fit BANT, or have we outgrown it (toward MEDDIC)?
- Are reps qualifying for our convenience or for the buyer's clarity?
- Is "Authority" really the economic buyer, or a stand-in?
- Are we letting "no budget yet" disqualify deals that could be created?
- What's our disqualification rate — and is it too low (lax reps) or too high (overly strict)?

## Canonical examples

- **IBM** — originated BANT in the 1960s as a sales-management framework
- **HubSpot Sales Academy / Sales Hub** — teaches BANT as the default starter qualifier
- **Most modern CRMs** — Salesforce, HubSpot, Pipedrive all ship with BANT-style fields
- **Pereira's reference** (page 158) — names BANT as the straightforward qualifier for the Connect/Qualify stage of the Sales Process
