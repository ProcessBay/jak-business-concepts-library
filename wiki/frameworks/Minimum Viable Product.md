# Minimum Viable Product

## Definition

A Minimum Viable Product (MVP) is a version of a product built with just enough features to put it in front of real users and gather **validated learning** about what works — with the least possible time, effort, and cost. Pereira frames MVP not as a product but as a **process**: an iterative cycle of risky assumptions, small experiments, and feedback-driven changes. Originating in 2001 from Frank Robinson and Eric Ries, and rooted in Toyota's lean manufacturing principle of waste reduction, the MVP rejects elaborate up-front planning in favor of getting something functional in front of customers — because what people *say* they want and what they *do* with a real product are usually different.

## Key attributes

- **Functional, not a mockup** — must be a real product users can interact with, not a "technical prototype or imaginary idea"
- **Minimal but viable** — only the core features that communicate value, but fully reliable (no glitches, crashes, low-quality UX)
- **Process not artifact** — an iterative cycle; you can develop one MVP after another, each time learning more
- **Validated-learning oriented** — purpose is to gather data on real customer behavior, not to ship a finished thing
- **Constrains scope to test ONE hypothesis** — typically one or two features testing one market assumption
- **Cheap and fast** — built in weeks (~2 weeks for first iteration), not months
- **Two outcomes only** — iterate (pivot) or abandon; never "ship the MVP as the product"

## When to use

- New product development where market need is unproven (42% of startups fail due to no market need; page 37)
- Bootstrapped startups with limited capital that must validate before scaling spend
- Categories where customer behavior is hard to predict from interviews alone
- When you have a clear, testable hypothesis about user needs
- Pre-investment validation (MVPs make pitching investors easier — they show traction)
- Transitioning a business model (e.g., Intuit's shift from shrink-wrap to SaaS used MVPs, page 17-18)

## When NOT to use

- Regulated industries where "viable" requires full compliance before launch (medical, financial)
- Products where reputation damage from a stripped-down launch is irreversible
- When you actually have validated demand — at that point you need a Minimum Marketable Product (MMP), not an MVP
- Categories where the core experience is inseparable from polish (luxury, brand-led)
- When the team will confuse MVP with "MVP as final product" — a documented anti-pattern

## Common pitfalls

- **Confusing MVP with MMF/MMP** — building for revenue rather than feedback (page 26)
- **Skimping on quality** — using "minimum" as an excuse for buggy, unreliable releases (page 30, 113)
- **Building too much** — the "non-minimum MVP" trap; product grows so big it's hard to change when feedback comes (page 30)
- **Not iterating** — treating the MVP as a one-shot launch instead of a learning loop (page 29)
- **Ignoring user feedback** — defeats the entire purpose
- **MoSCoW abuse** — stakeholders cramming >60% of features into "must-have," producing an MVP that is neither minimum nor viable (page 113)
- **Not staying true to assumptions** — using the MVP to test features instead of the underlying hypothesis (page 29)

## Key metrics

- **Activation rate** — % of users reaching the "aha" moment
- **NPS** — would users recommend? (target significantly above 0)
- **Active users** — DAU/MAU depending on use case
- **CAC** — cost to acquire one paying customer
- **Retention / churn** — do users come back?
- **Conversion rate** — visitor → signup → paid
- **Validated learning per dollar** — how much hypothesis-clearing each test produces (the meta-metric)

## Decision questions

- What is the ONE risky assumption this MVP tests?
- Which type of MVP (concierge, Wizard of Oz, landing page, single-feature, etc.) best isolates that assumption?
- What signal would falsify our hypothesis — and are we willing to act on it?
- What's the smallest thing we can build that delivers genuine value?
- How will we capture data systematically, not anecdotally?
- What's our pivot trigger, and what's our persist trigger?

## Canonical examples

- **Dropbox** — explainer-video MVP; founders couldn't show the product, so a 3-minute video drew 70,000 signups in one day, validating demand before building (pages 17, 81)
- **Airbnb** — Brian Chesky and Joe Gebbia rented air mattresses in their own apartment during a SF conference when hotels sold out; validated demand for peer-to-peer rentals (pages 72, 79)
- **Zappos** — Nick Swinmurn photographed shoes at local stores and shipped manually (Wizard of Oz / concierge hybrid)
- **Buffer** — Joel Gascoigne's two-page smoke-test landing page tested willingness to pay before any product existed (page 80)
- **Foursquare** — launched 2009 as a single-feature check-in tool; added recommendations and city guides only after validation (page 79)
- **Groupon** — started as a WordPress site emailing PDF vouchers; built backend only after the model proved out (page 81)
- **Spotify** — used build-measure-learn iteration relentlessly from 2006 to reach $23B valuation (page 106)
- **Intuit** — used MVP discipline to transition from shrink-wrap to SaaS (pages 17-18)
