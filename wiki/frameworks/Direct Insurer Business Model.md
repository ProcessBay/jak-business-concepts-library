# Direct Insurer Business Model

## Definition

The direct insurer (or tied-agent) Insurtech model sells policies straight to the end customer without independent brokers or aggregators sitting in between. Pereira frames it as the model "where no intermediaries are needed. No intermediaries mean no need to pay commission, hence allowing them to charge low premiums to their customers" (page 20). The carrier owns the digital interface end-to-end — quote, bind, service, claim — and typically reinsures part of the assumed risk with a reinsurer. The direct insurer captures the brokerage margin and reinvests it into either lower premiums, better UX, or richer data collection used to refine underwriting. It is the structural opposite of the marketplace/aggregator model and the foundation on which most vertical Insurtech archetypes (UBI, on-demand, microinsurance) are built.

## Key attributes

- **No intermediary commission** — broker spread is eliminated and partially returned as lower premiums
- **Owned digital front door** — app or website is the only sales and service channel
- **Reinsurance on the back end** — risk is laid off to reinsurers; the direct insurer is the customer-facing brand
- **Vertical specialization is common** — pet, renters, auto, microinsurance — narrow risk classes that legacy carriers ignore
- **Data ownership** — every customer interaction generates first-party behavioural data
- **Either MGA-licensed or full-stack carrier** — different regulatory weight; full-stack holds the balance sheet, MGA delegates underwriting
- **Brand-trust dependent** — without an intermediary's reputation, the direct insurer must build trust from zero
- **CAC-heavy in early years** — paid acquisition replaces broker-led distribution

## When to use

- A target segment is underserved or overpriced by incumbents who can't economically reach it (gig workers, low-income, niche assets)
- The product is simple enough to quote and bind in a single digital session
- The founder has access to a reinsurance partner or carrier licence
- Real-time data (telematics, IoT, mobile) materially improves pricing accuracy
- Unit economics tolerate paid CAC because retention and cross-sell are strong

## When NOT to use

- Complex commercial lines that genuinely require broker advisory
- Markets where regulators mandate intermediated distribution
- Categories where customers explicitly trust a human advisor over an app (high-value life, complex health)
- When you can't secure reinsurance capacity or balance-sheet capital
- When your only edge is "cheaper" — incumbents can match price once threatened

## Common pitfalls

- **Regulatory underestimation** — direct insurance is a licenced activity in nearly every jurisdiction; MGA, broker, and full-stack licences are not interchangeable
- **Capital intensity surprise** — full-stack carriers face solvency capital requirements that destroy startup unit economics
- **Loss-ratio drift** — fast growth attracts adverse selection; the cheapest carrier often attracts the worst risks
- **CAC outrunning LTV** — paid digital acquisition can exceed multi-year premium contribution if retention is weak
- **Reinsurer dependency** — a single reinsurance partner withdrawing capacity can shut the business overnight
- **Claims experience gap** — winning the sale is easy; losing the claim destroys the brand

## Key metrics

- **Combined Ratio** — losses + expenses ÷ earned premium; <100% is profitable underwriting
- **Loss Ratio** — claims paid ÷ earned premium; the structural quality of underwriting
- **Expense Ratio** — operating cost ÷ earned premium; where direct distribution is supposed to win
- **CAC** vs. **first-year premium** — direct distribution's most-watched ratio
- **Renewal Rate** — retention is the only path to profitable LTV
- **Gross Written Premium (GWP)** — top-line scale signal
- **Policies-in-Force (PIF)** — operational scale signal

## Decision questions

- Do we have (or can we secure) a carrier or MGA licence in our target market?
- Is our reinsurance capacity contracted and large enough to scale into?
- Is our target segment genuinely underserved, or are we just undercutting price?
- Can our digital front door handle quote-bind-claim end-to-end without human intervention?
- What is our path to combined ratio <100%, and at what scale?

## Canonical examples

- **BIMA** — mobile-delivered health + life microinsurance for low-income Asia/Africa via telco channels (page 20)
- **Metromile** — pay-per-mile auto direct to driver (page 21)
- **Trov** — micro-duration policies on individual items, toggled in-app (page 21)
- **Root** — telematics-priced auto insurance after a multi-week driving test (page 21)
- **Cuvva** — flexible auto cover from 1 hour to monthly, app-distributed (page 22)
- **Neos** — UK direct life insurer bundled with smart-home tech (page 22)
- **Lemonade** — D2C renters/homeowners/pet (also P2P) (page 25)
- **Saudi context — Tameeni** — D2C motor and medical insurance aggregator-direct hybrid; **Bupa Arabia** — direct-to-employer health; **Walaa** and **Tawuniya** digital channels — incumbent direct arms
