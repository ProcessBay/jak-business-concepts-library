# Marketplace Business Model

## Definition

A marketplace is a platform that connects buyers and sellers of goods or services — facilitating transactions but not taking inventory. The marketplace operator monetizes by taking a percentage of each transaction (take rate) or charging listing fees, while sellers retain ownership of inventory and customer relationships. Pereira distinguishes marketplaces from pure intermediation by their persistent presence, search/discovery infrastructure, and trust mechanisms (reviews, dispute resolution, payment processing).

## Key attributes

- **Two-sided coordination** — supply (sellers) and demand (buyers) brought together via platform
- **No inventory ownership** — marketplace doesn't own goods; sellers do
- **Take rate monetization** — platform captures % of GMV transacted
- **Trust infrastructure** — reviews, ratings, escrow, dispute resolution enable transactions between strangers
- **Search and discovery** — finding the right seller for a buyer is the platform's core value
- **Liquidity sensitivity** — needs enough activity per category/geography to be useful

## When to use

- Highly fragmented supply OR demand benefiting from aggregation
- Markets with high search costs that the platform can lower
- Categories with information asymmetry (used goods, services) where reviews add value
- When the firm has enough capital to subsidize early supply-side growth
- Where trust between strangers is the buying obstacle

## When NOT to use

- When existing distribution serves the category well
- When supply and demand are concentrated (no fragmentation to aggregate)
- When the firm wants to control product quality (own inventory better)
- When you can't sustain seed-side subsidies long enough to reach liquidity

## Common pitfalls

- Insufficient liquidity — empty marketplace with no transactions
- Supply-side disintermediation — sellers move buyers off-platform to avoid take rate
- Failing to manage seller quality — bad sellers poison marketplace trust
- Race-to-the-bottom pricing as sellers compete on price alone
- Excessive take rate that drives high-volume sellers to alternatives

## Key metrics

- GMV (Gross Merchandise Value)
- Take rate (revenue / GMV)
- Supply-side count and growth
- Demand-side count and growth
- Transactions per user per period
- Repeat rate
- Liquidity score (% of buyer searches that result in transactions)

## Decision questions

- Which side (supply or demand) is hardest to attract, and how do we subsidize it?
- What take rate is sustainable without driving sellers off-platform?
- What trust mechanism is most critical in our category?
- Where are sellers disintermediating us, and how do we stop it?

## Canonical examples

- **Amazon Marketplace** — third-party sellers list; Amazon handles search, payment, fulfillment (page 136)
- **eBay** — auctions and fixed-price listings; trust via seller reviews (page 136)
- **Etsy** — handmade and vintage goods; niche marketplace (page 154)
- **Airbnb** — accommodations marketplace (also platform)
- **Uber Eats / DoorDash** — restaurant marketplaces with delivery layer
