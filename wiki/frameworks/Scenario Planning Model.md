# Scenario Planning Model

## Definition

Scenario Planning is a contingency-based strategic-planning model in which an organization develops several plausible alternative futures and tests its strategy against each. Rather than betting on a single forecast, the model evaluates external factors — regulatory shifts, price movements, supply-chain disruptions, new market entrants — for likelihood and impact, then asks which strategic moves are robust across the most consequential scenarios. Pereira pairs it with SWOT and PEST as natural feeders, and identifies it as the framework of choice for organizations that have "experienced unforeseen circumstances" and need to build adaptive capacity into their planning.

## Key attributes

- **Multiple-futures methodology** — typically 3-4 plausible scenarios spanning the credible range of outcomes
- **External-factor driven** — scenarios are built from environmental forces (regulatory, economic, technological)
- **Tests strategy under each scenario** — which decisions hold up across most? Which are scenario-specific bets?
- **Surfaces robust strategies** — moves that win in most futures get prioritized
- **Identifies early-warning indicators** — signals that tell leadership which scenario is materializing
- **Complement to PESTLE** — PESTLE generates the raw factors; scenario planning combines them into coherent futures
- **Best for long-term horizons** — typically 5-10+ years where forecasting is least reliable

## When to use

- For long-range strategic planning where single-point forecasts are unreliable
- After a major surprise (regulatory, geopolitical, technological) has invalidated prior plans
- In industries facing structural shifts — energy transition, AI disruption, demographic change
- When the leadership team disagrees on the future and needs a shared framework for debate
- For investment-heavy decisions (capex, M&A, market entry) that must hold across futures
- When pairing with PESTLE outputs that produce too many factors to act on directly

## When NOT to use

- For short-term tactical decisions (use Real-Time or Issue-Based planning)
- When the future is genuinely well-understood and stable
- As a substitute for execution — scenarios inform strategy, they don't deliver it
- When leadership won't actually consider scenarios outside their preferred one
- For small startups with no resources for multi-scenario analysis (one good scenario is hard enough)

## Common pitfalls

- Developing too many scenarios — the team can't reason about more than ~4
- Scenarios that aren't genuinely distinct — variations of the same future
- Optimistic/pessimistic/middle — lazy framing that obscures real uncertainty axes
- Building scenarios but not deriving decisions from them
- Failing to define early-warning indicators that signal which scenario is unfolding
- Treating scenarios as forecasts (probability assignments) rather than strategic test cases
- One-off exercise — scenarios age; they need refresh as the environment shifts

## Outputs / signals of good use

- 3-4 distinct, plausible scenarios with clear narrative descriptions
- A robustness map showing which strategic moves win in which scenarios
- A short list of "regret-free" moves (winners in most futures)
- Identified early-warning indicators with monitoring assigned to owners
- Periodic refresh — scenarios reviewed annually or after major environmental shifts
- Leadership decisions explicitly traced to scenario reasoning

## Decision questions

- What are the 2-3 axes of greatest uncertainty in our environment?
- Have we built genuinely distinct scenarios, or variations on one?
- Which moves are regret-free? Which are scenario-specific bets?
- What early-warning indicators would tell us which scenario is materializing?
- Are we monitoring those indicators, with named owners?
- When do we re-run the scenario exercise?

## Canonical examples

- **Pereira's regulatory/price/supply-chain triggers** — scenarios best apply when companies face "regulatory policy changes, rising prices, supply chain disruptions, new market players" (pages 68-69)
- **Royal Dutch Shell (1970s)** — the canonical example; Pierre Wack's scenario planning let Shell anticipate the 1973 oil crisis
- **Pandemic-era retailers** — those with pre-2020 scenarios including pandemic shocks pivoted faster
- **Energy companies on the transition** — modeling 1.5°C, 2°C, and BAU pathways
- **Defense and intelligence agencies** — long-standing users for geopolitical planning
