# OKR Cadence

## Definition

**OKR Cadence** refers to the frequency at which OKRs are set, tracked, reviewed, and refreshed within an organization. Pereira identifies four common cadences — **quarterly** (the dominant default), **annual** (strategic, slower-moving orgs), **flexible/rolling** (continuous adjustment, agile cultures), and **customized** (mixed cadences across business units; pages 39-40). Most organizations layer cadences: annual Objectives at the company level set direction, quarterly OKRs at team level drive execution, and weekly or bi-weekly check-ins drive accountability (page 62). The choice is not just operational — it shapes how often the org can course-correct, how heavy the planning overhead is, and whether short-termism dominates long-term thinking.

## Key attributes

- **Quarterly is the default** — popularized by Google; balances agility with planning weight (pages 40-41)
- **Annual provides strategic horizon** — better for stable industries and longer planning cycles (page 42)
- **Layered cadence is common** — annual company OKRs + quarterly team OKRs + weekly check-ins (page 62)
- **Check-in rhythm matters as much as cycle length** — weekly or bi-weekly reviews keep KRs visible (pages 61-62)
- **Should match business cycles** — fast-paced tech vs. capital-intensive manufacturing demand different rhythms (page 36)
- **Customizable** — different business units can run different cadences within one org (page 40)
- **Reviewable** — cadence itself should be revisited periodically as the org matures (page 39)

## When to use

- **Quarterly**: high-growth startups, tech, product orgs, anywhere market context shifts every 90 days
- **Annual**: regulated industries, capital projects, R&D-heavy orgs, organizations still building OKR maturity
- **Rolling/flexible**: highly agile, autonomous teams with strong communication discipline
- **Customized/hybrid**: large multi-business-unit orgs where one cadence won't fit all

## When NOT to use

- Don't impose quarterly cycles on teams whose deliverables span 9+ months
- Don't run annual-only OKRs in a fast-moving market where priorities shift mid-year
- Don't pick a cadence that exceeds team bandwidth for check-ins and planning overhead
- Don't change cadence mid-cycle — set, complete, then evaluate

## Common pitfalls

- **Short-termism from quarterly cycles** — sacrificing long-term bets for hittable Qs (pages 41-42)
- **Reduced agility from annual cycles** — KRs going stale halfway through (page 43)
- **Cadence-as-bureaucracy** — quarterly planning that absorbs more time than execution
- **Inconsistent check-ins** — skipping reviews means mid-cycle drift is invisible (page 44)
- **Misaligned cadences across teams** — team A on quarterly, team B on annual, with shared dependencies
- **No retrospective at cycle end** — scoring without learning wastes the cycle

## Key metrics

- **Cycle velocity** — time from kickoff to first measurable KR movement
- **Check-in frequency** — actual vs. scheduled check-in adherence
- **Adjustment rate** — % of KRs modified mid-cycle (high = poor initial scoping; very low = no learning)
- **Planning overhead** — hours spent setting OKRs as % of cycle duration

## Decision questions

- How fast does our market or product context actually shift?
- Do our teams have bandwidth for quarterly planning *and* weekly check-ins?
- Are we using cycle length to mask aversion to honest reviews?
- Should different business units run different cadences?
- When was the last time we revisited whether our cadence still fits?

## Canonical examples

- **Google** — quarterly OKRs cascaded company-wide, with annual strategic Objectives at the executive level (page 108)
- **Tech startups** — quarterly cadence for product/engineering, monthly for early-stage growth experiments
- **Toyota** — annual strategic OKRs paired with kaizen-driven continuous review cycles (pages 102-103)
- **Patagonia** — multi-year sustainability Objectives broken into annual and quarterly KRs (page 99)
