# Viral Marketing

## Definition

Viral marketing is a strategy that engineers a marketing message to spread organically and rapidly through user networks — like a virus — by creating content or experiences so resonant that recipients are compelled to share them with others, who in turn share with more people. Pereira identifies four components: compelling content, social sharing mechanisms, incentivization, and network effects. The objective is exponential reach: every share generates more shares, producing brand exposure and user acquisition at a fraction of the cost of paid advertising. Viral marketing is the marketing-side expression of the growth loop — and when it works, it produces the asymmetric outcomes (Hotmail, Dropbox, ALS Ice Bucket Challenge) that paid campaigns cannot.

## Key attributes

- **Exponential, not linear** — each shared piece can generate multiple new shares
- **Four components** — compelling content, sharing mechanisms, incentivization, network effects
- **Emotionally driven** — surprise, awe, humor, status, controversy spread faster than information
- **Friction-sensitive** — every click between "want to share" and "shared" kills virality
- **Network-amplified** — leverages existing user graphs (social, email, contacts)
- **Hard to engineer reliably** — most attempts fail; success skews heavily right-tail
- **Measurable via viral coefficient (K)** — K > 1 means self-sustaining growth

## When to use

- Consumer products where social sharing is natural behavior
- When the product produces a shareable artifact (a result, a meme, a status badge)
- When organic reach is more cost-effective than paid acquisition in the category
- When the brand can credibly create content that earns attention
- Categories where social proof drives purchase decisions

## When NOT to use

- Sensitive or private categories (health, finance) where sharing is taboo
- B2B enterprise products with no social-sharing surface
- When the product is undifferentiated (nothing worth sharing about)
- When brand risk is high and viral content could backfire publicly
- When the team confuses "going viral" with a strategy — virality is an outcome, not a plan

## Common pitfalls

- Trying to manufacture virality with paid amplification (defeats the purpose)
- High-friction sharing mechanisms (multi-step, multi-platform forms)
- Ignoring network effects (the message has to travel along existing user connections)
- Focusing on the content and skipping the incentive structure
- Measuring vanity reach (views) instead of viral coefficient and conversion
- Treating virality as a one-time campaign instead of a built-in product mechanic

## Key metrics

- **Viral coefficient (K)** — average new users invited and converted per existing user
- **Viral cycle time** — how long one share-to-conversion loop takes
- **Shares per user**
- **Share-to-install / share-to-signup ratio**
- **Organic reach** vs paid reach mix

## Decision questions

- What is the shareable artifact our product creates?
- What emotion are we engineering for — surprise, status, humor, outrage?
- How many steps between "wants to share" and "shared"? Can we cut any?
- What is our viral coefficient today, and what would push it above 1?
- Are we amplifying virality with built-in product mechanisms or one-off campaigns?

## Canonical examples

- **Hotmail** — "PS: I love you. Get your free email at Hotmail" footer in every email
- **Dropbox** — referral program with mutual storage rewards (page 99-100)
- **Spotify** — Facebook integration sharing songs/playlists with social network (page 104-105)
- **Pinterest** — visual pins designed to be reshared across social platforms (page 109-110)
- **ALS Ice Bucket Challenge** — gamified, nominated-by-friend viral chain
- **Slack** — viral team invites baked into onboarding (page 107-108)
