# Full-Service Hotel Business Model

## Definition

A full-service hotel offers a comprehensive bundle of lodging plus on-site amenities — restaurants, bars, room service, fitness centers, swimming pools, spa, conference facilities, business centers, and concierge service — under a single roof. Pereira places it as the historical foundation of the modern hospitality industry, traceable to the late-19th-century luxury hotels (Ritz Paris, Waldorf Astoria) and the mid-20th-century chain era (Hilton, Marriott, Holiday Inn) that standardized the experience across geographies. The model trades higher fixed-cost intensity for premium ADR, multiple revenue streams beyond rooms, and the ability to serve business travelers, leisure tourists, families, luxury guests, and corporate event clients from one property.

## Key attributes

- **Comprehensive amenity stack** — F&B, fitness, spa, pool, conference rooms, business center, concierge
- **Multiple revenue streams** — rooms, F&B, banquets, spa/wellness, gym memberships, parking, ancillary services
- **Physical infrastructure-heavy** — dedicated building with lobby, varied room types, public spaces, back-of-house
- **High fixed cost base** — property, maintenance, staffing, infrastructure carry through occupancy cycles
- **Brand-driven** — chain affiliation (Hilton, Marriott) supplies recognition, loyalty program, distribution
- **Distribution stack** — CRS, GDS, OTA, direct, travel agents working in parallel
- **Diverse customer segments** — business, leisure, family, luxury, extended-stay travelers
- **Service-intensive staffing** — front office, housekeeping, F&B, ops management as distinct departments

## When to use

- Markets with sustained mixed demand (business + leisure + groups + events)
- Locations where guests pay a premium for one-stop convenience and brand assurance
- Properties large enough (typically 200+ rooms) to absorb fixed costs
- Brands with the capital and operating discipline to deliver consistent service across departments
- Destinations where conference and banquet revenue is a meaningful adjacent market

## When NOT to use

- Pure budget/transient markets where guests will not pay for amenities they do not use
- Sub-scale properties where fixed costs cannot be supported by realistic occupancy
- Operators without F&B and events capability to run those revenue lines profitably
- Markets where alternative accommodation (vacation rentals, hostels) already captures the price-sensitive segment

## Common pitfalls

- Underestimating fixed-cost drag during low-season or downturn
- Letting F&B and banquets run at a loss while subsidizing them from room revenue without measuring it
- Over-reliance on OTA bookings (10-30% commission) erodes margin and weakens direct-customer relationship
- Brand-standard rigidity preventing fast adaptation to local market shifts
- Failing to invest in loyalty program and direct-booking channel to offset OTA dependence
- Letting amenity quality drift, eroding the premium ADR that justifies the model

## Key metrics

- **ADR** (Average Daily Rate) — total room revenue ÷ occupied rooms
- **Occupancy Rate** — occupied rooms ÷ available rooms
- **RevPAR** (Revenue per Available Room) — ADR × Occupancy; the headline benchmark
- **GOPPAR** (Gross Operating Profit per Available Room) — incorporates total operating costs
- **FBPAR** (Food & Beverage Revenue per Available Room)
- **TRI** (Total Revenue Index) — hotel vs. competitive set or market average
- **Direct-booking share** — % of bookings via own channels vs. OTA/GDS

## Decision questions

- Can we sustain RevPAR and GOPPAR above the breakeven set by our fixed-cost base?
- Is our F&B and events revenue carrying its own weight, or is it a loss-leader for room sales?
- What share of our bookings sits with OTAs, and what's our plan to grow direct?
- Are amenities aligned with what our actual guest mix (business / leisure / luxury) values?
- Do we have the operating depth to deliver service consistency across every department?

## Canonical examples

- **Marriott, Hilton, Hyatt, IHG** — global full-service chain operators
- **The Ritz (Paris), Waldorf Astoria (New York)** — late-19th/early-20th-century luxury founders of the model (page 17)
- **Rotana** — leading MENA full-service hotel operator headquartered in UAE, properties across Saudi Arabia
- **Four Seasons, Mandarin Oriental** — luxury full-service operators
- **Red Sea Global / NEOM hospitality properties** — Saudi giga-project full-service luxury hotel pipeline
- **Sheraton, Le Méridien** (Marriott-owned) — large-format full-service properties common across Saudi cities
