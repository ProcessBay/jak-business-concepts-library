# Strategy Map

## Definition

A Strategy Map is the one-page visual diagram of a Balanced Scorecard that lays out an organization's strategic objectives across the four perspectives (Financial, Customer, Internal Processes, Learning & Growth, stacked bottom-to-top) and connects them with explicit cause-and-effect arrows. Kaplan and Norton formalized the tool in their 2004 book _Strategy Maps_, treating it as the missing link between vision and measurement. Pereira describes it as "a graphic storyboard detailing [the company's] plans" — its job is to make the strategy legible enough that a frontline employee can see how their work connects to the financial outcome at the top (page 38, 41).

## Key attributes

- **Four-row layout** — Learning & Growth at the bottom, Financial at the top, with Internal Processes and Customer between (page 29)
- **Cause-and-effect arrows** — every objective is connected to upstream enablers and downstream outcomes; no orphan objectives
- **One-page constraint** — Pereira: "a one-page summary"; if it does not fit, the strategy is not clear enough (page 42)
- **Objectives, not metrics** — the map shows strategic _objectives_ (verbs like "improve," "reduce"); KPIs sit underneath in the scorecard itself
- **Customer value proposition is central** — the Customer row typically anchors what the firm is selling and to whom (page 38)
- **Theme-based grouping** — strategic themes (e.g. "operational excellence," "customer intimacy") run as vertical swimlanes across all four perspectives
- **Communication tool first, measurement tool second** — its primary value is internal/external alignment, not analytics (page 42)
- **Per-area maps roll up to a corporate map** — each business unit can have its own map that consolidates into the overarching one (page 38)

## When to use

- Communicating strategy to the whole organization on a single page
- Onboarding new executives or board members to the strategy
- Pitching strategy to investors, regulators, or partners (one-page narrative)
- Diagnosing strategic incoherence — gaps and disconnected objectives become visually obvious
- As the first artifact in a BSC build, before defining KPIs

## When NOT to use

- As a substitute for actually doing strategy work (the map visualizes a strategy; it doesn't generate one)
- For tactical/operational planning — too high-level
- When the strategy is genuinely emergent and changing monthly (startups in discovery mode)
- As a glossy poster never referenced again — the map must be living, revisited quarterly

## Common pitfalls

- **Too many objectives** — more than ~15 boxes on the map makes it unreadable; FranklinCovey's "10+ goals = none achieved" rule applies (page 33)
- **Arrows everywhere** — if every box connects to every other, the causal logic is fake; force yourself to draw only the strongest links
- **No customer value proposition** — Customer row becomes a list of metrics rather than a strategic statement
- **Skipping the Learning & Growth row** — most common omission; reflects the bias toward "doing" over "capability building"
- **One map for an entire conglomerate** — at scale, you need one corporate map plus business-unit maps
- **Made by consultants, never owned by executives** — the map dies the day the consultants leave

## Key metrics

- **Strategy execution rate** — % of map objectives on track
- **Objective coverage** — % of map objectives that have at least one KPI and one initiative behind them
- **Causal-chain integrity** — can every top-row Financial objective be traced down through the map to a Learning & Growth driver?
- **Recall rate** — % of employees who can describe the map's top 3 objectives unaided

## Decision questions

- Can we describe our strategy in one page? If not, why not?
- For each objective on the map, what are the upstream enablers and downstream outcomes?
- Does the Customer row actually express a value proposition, or just metrics?
- Which objectives could we cut without weakening the strategy?
- Who owns each objective, and do they know it?

## Canonical examples

- **Mobil NAM&R Strategy Map** — Kaplan & Norton's foundational example, organized around "delight the consumer"
- **Telco company map** (Pereira, page 44–46) — Brand awareness, Customer service, Content partnerships as strategic themes; objectives spread across all four perspectives
- **Project Management strategy map** (Pereira, page 47) — explains business context (constraints + priorities) to a project team in a single visual
- **Public-sector strategy maps** — Mission row at the top displaces Financial; widely used by government agencies post-2000 (page 14, 18)
