# Product Launch Marketing Plan

## Definition

A product launch marketing plan is a strategic marketing plan designed with precision and impact for the introduction of a new product to market. Pereira structures it as a three-phase model: **pre-launch (research roadmapping)** — all research and planning, market analysis, channel selection, strategy formulation; **launch (rollout and reporting)** — implementation of the planned strategy through events, press releases, blog content, email campaigns, ads, videos, and any other content that reaches customers; **post-launch (respondence and retention)** — analyzing data and implementing changes based on customer feedback, helping the project management team establish what product features need to be modified, removed, or added. The plan exists to increase the chances of the product reaching its target market, reduce product-failure risk, generate leads, invite investors and partners, and boost brand reputation.

## Key attributes

- **Three phases** — pre-launch (research roadmapping) → launch (rollout and reporting) → post-launch (respondence and retention)
- **Pre-launch = research + strategy** — all market/product research; channel selection; strategy formulation; budget
- **Launch = execution** — events, press releases, blog content, email, ads, videos, digital and physical content
- **Post-launch = feedback loop** — data analysis + customer feedback drive product modification, removal, or addition of features
- **Real-time performance monitoring** — actively monitors the product's market reception, providing project managers with real-time intel
- **Investor and partner gateway** — well-executed launch attracts investment and partnership interest
- **Reduces failure risk** — disciplined launch lowers the probability of market rejection

## When to use

- Introducing a meaningfully new product to market (not just a feature update)
- Re-launching a repositioned or rebuilt product
- Entering a new geographic market with an existing product
- Launching a product where investor/partner interest is part of the goal
- When the cost of a failed launch is high enough to justify dedicated planning effort

## When NOT to use

- For incremental feature releases — too heavyweight; use a release-note + email approach
- When product-market fit is unproven — premature launch amplifies waste
- For products with no clear target audience yet defined (do that work first)
- When the team lacks bandwidth to execute all three phases — partial execution is worse than smaller scope

## Common pitfalls

- **Skipping the pre-launch research phase** — launching on intuition rather than market intelligence
- **All energy on launch day, none on post-launch** — Pereira: post-launch is where customer feedback drives product evolution
- **Treating launch as a one-time event** — successful launches are sustained campaigns, not a single day
- **No real-time monitoring** — without performance telemetry, course correction is impossible
- **Underestimating the budget** — Pereira emphasizes a detailed budget is part of the plan
- **No investor/partner narrative** — missing the opportunity to convert launch buzz into capital and partnerships
- **Ignoring post-launch retention** — focusing only on acquisition and watching customers churn

## Key metrics

- **Pre-launch** — research completeness, channel-strategy readiness, budget approval, pre-order/waitlist volume
- **Launch** — reach (impressions, unique visitors), share of voice, day-one activations, press mentions
- **Post-launch** — week-one and month-one retention, feature adoption, customer feedback NPS, churn cohort, support ticket volume
- **Cross-phase** — total CAC at launch, LTV/CAC at 90 days, brand-lift measurement

## Decision questions

- Have we completed the pre-launch research before committing to launch tactics?
- Which channels are we using for launch, and why those specifically?
- What is the real-time monitoring infrastructure for launch week?
- How will customer feedback from post-launch flow back to product?
- Is the budget detailed enough that every launch tactic has a line item?
- Are we using the launch moment to engage investors and partners?

## Canonical examples

- **Pre-launch phase** — Pereira: "the step where all the research and planning are done... establish your marketing strategies and the channels best suited to implement them" (page 29)
- **Launch phase** — Pereira: "creating events, press releases, blog content, email marketing campaigns, ads, videos, or any other piece of physical or digital content that will help you reach as many customers as possible" (page 29)
- **Post-launch phase** — Pereira: "analyzes the data and implements changes based on customers' feedback, which helps the project management team establish what product features need to be modified, removed, or added" (page 29)
