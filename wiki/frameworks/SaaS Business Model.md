# SaaS Business Model

## Definition

Software as a Service (SaaS) is a business model where software is hosted centrally on the cloud, licensed on a subscription basis, and accessed by customers via internet browsers or apps. Unlike on-premise software (purchased once, installed locally), SaaS is: (1) centrally hosted by the provider, (2) continuously updated without customer action, (3) priced via recurring subscriptions, and (4) accessible from any device with internet. The model shifts value from "ownership" to "access" and from "one-time purchase" to "ongoing relationship."

Pereira notes: "SaaS is not about the cost only, it is also about the ease of access." The centralization enables real-time data, continuous improvement, and lower barriers to adoption — but creates dependency on the provider and requires sustained customer satisfaction.

## Key attributes

- **Centralized hosting** — software runs on provider's servers, not customer's hardware
- **Subscription pricing** — recurring revenue model (monthly or annual); NOT one-time purchase
- **Continuous updates** — provider controls version; customers get new features without manual installation
- **Cloud accessibility** — use from any device, anywhere with internet
- **Lower upfront costs** — no hardware investment; monthly fees vs. large capital expenditure
- **Shared infrastructure** — one codebase serves many customers (multi-tenancy)
- **Real-time data** — provider can monitor usage, behavior, and performance continuously
- **Vendor dependency** — customer relies on provider for uptime, security, data access

## SaaS vs. related concepts

| Concept | Relationship to SaaS | Key difference |
|---|---|---|
| **Cloud Computing** | SaaS is a **subset** of cloud computing | Cloud = infrastructure layer (storage, compute); SaaS = application layer |
| **PaaS** | PaaS enables SaaS development | PaaS = platform for building apps; SaaS = finished app for end users |
| **On-premise Software** | Historical alternative | On-premise = installed locally, purchased perpetually, customer manages updates |
| **API Business Model** | Often overlaps with SaaS | API = programmatic access; SaaS = user-facing interface (can be both) |

## When to use

- Building software that solves an ongoing problem (not a one-time need)
- Target market prefers low upfront costs and predictable monthly expenses
- Product benefits from continuous improvement and real-time collaboration
- Target customers are distributed geographically
- Product has network effects or benefits from aggregated data
- You can deliver 80% of value immediately upon signup (low time-to-value)

## When NOT to use

- Software requires heavy local processing or real-time latency (e.g., video editing, CAD)
- Target customers have strict data sovereignty requirements prohibiting cloud storage
- One-time need products (e.g., tax filing for individuals, event-specific tools)
- Markets with unreliable internet infrastructure
- When regulatory compliance requires on-premise deployment
- If the product's value is not demonstrable within a free trial period

## Common pitfalls

- **Treating SaaS as "passive income"** — SaaS is a service, not a product. Continuous customer success is mandatory.
- **Underestimating CAC in crowded markets** — SaaS markets are saturated; customer acquisition is expensive and getting worse.
- **Free plan cannibalization** — freemium converts poorly if free tier is too generous. Pereira: "not everyone turns into a premium customer."
- **Churn blindness** — focusing on new customers while ignoring leaking bucket. "Keeping existing customers is much more important than getting a new one."
- **Premature scaling** — hiring sales/marketing before product-market fit. "Premature scaling can lead the company, especially startups, to fail."
- **Misleading metrics** — inflating MRR with annual contracts counted as monthly, or including trial users. "Investors tend to assess revenue figures... there are people working for the investor who knows where to find the weak points."

## Key metrics

| Metric | What it measures | Healthy benchmark |
|---|---|---|
| **MRR** | Monthly Recurring Revenue | Growing 10-20% MoM (early stage) |
| **ARR** | Annual Recurring Revenue | MRR × 12 |
| **CAC** | Customer Acquisition Cost | Recoverable within 12 months |
| **LTV** | Customer Lifetime Value | LTV:CAC ratio > 3:1 |
| **Customer Churn** | % customers lost monthly | <2% for B2B; <5% for B2C |
| **Revenue Churn** | % revenue lost monthly | <2% for B2B; <5% for B2C |
| **Net Revenue Retention** | Revenue from existing cohort (inc. expansions) | >100% is healthy; >120% is excellent |
| **Gross Margin** | Revenue minus COGS | >70-80% for SaaS |
| **Payback Period** | Time to recover CAC | <12 months |

## Decision questions

- Is our product solving a problem that customers face continuously (not one-time)?
- Can we deliver core value within the first 5 minutes of signup?
- What's our realistic CAC, and can we recover it within 12 months?
- What's our projected monthly churn rate, and what would that mean for LTV?
- Do we have the resources for continuous product improvement and customer support?
- Are we building SaaS because it fits the problem, or because it's fashionable?
- What's our competitive moat — switching costs, data network effects, or just features?
- Have we validated willingness-to-pay with actual transactions, not just surveys?
