# Cost Structure

## Definition

Cost Structure is the BMC block that describes the major financial costs incurred to operate the business model — derived from the costs of key activities, key resources, and key partners. Pereira distinguishes two strategic orientations: Value-Driven (premium positioning, willing to spend to deliver high-touch value) and Cost-Driven (lean operations, structural cost advantage). Cost structures combine four characteristics: Fixed Costs (constant regardless of volume), Variable Costs (scale with volume), Economies of Scale (unit cost falls with volume), and Economies of Scope (unit cost falls with diversity of products produced from shared resources).

## Key attributes

- **Two strategic orientations** — Value-Driven (Apple, premium hotels) vs. Cost-Driven (Walmart, low-cost airlines)
- **Four characteristics** — Fixed Costs, Variable Costs, Economies of Scale, Economies of Scope
- **Derived, not standalone** — costs are emergent from chosen activities, resources, partners
- **Margin determinant** — combined with revenue streams, sets the firm's profitability
- **Strategic, not just accounting** — choosing what to NOT spend on is as important as what to spend on
- **Scales differently per type** — fixed costs amortize; variable costs grow linearly; scale economies compound

## When to use

- Designing the cost architecture for a new business model
- Diagnosing margin compression (which cost line is growing faster than revenue?)
- Strategic positioning decisions (premium vs. cost-leader)
- Capital allocation review — should fixed cost increase or shrink?
- Pricing decisions — understanding cost-to-serve per customer enables intelligent pricing

## When NOT to use

- As a substitute for detailed financial modeling (BMC Cost Structure is strategic; the income statement is operational)
- For unit-economics deep-dives (use contribution margin analysis per offering instead)
- In early-stage startups where costs are still emerging — sketch it, don't fix it

## Common pitfalls

- Confusing low cost with cost leadership (cost leadership is structural, not just cheap)
- Underestimating fixed costs — they hit hard if revenue dips
- Ignoring economies of scope where shared resources could lower per-product costs
- Treating costs as line items disconnected from activities/resources/partners
- Optimizing local costs while breaking the system (false economy)
- Failing to revisit cost structure as business model evolves

## Key metrics

- Fixed vs. variable cost ratio
- Variable cost per unit (trend)
- Gross margin, operating margin
- Contribution margin per offering
- Break-even units
- Economies of scale realized (does unit cost actually fall with volume?)
- Operating leverage (revenue growth → margin growth)

## Decision questions

- Are we value-driven or cost-driven, and is our cost structure consistent with that positioning?
- What's our fixed cost burden, and could it sustain a 30% revenue decline?
- Which costs come from activities we should outsource?
- Where are we missing economies of scope (could shared resources lower costs)?
- What single cost line, if reduced 10%, would most improve overall margin?

## Canonical examples

- **Apple** — value-driven; high fixed costs in R&D, design, retail, marketing; premium pricing justifies the cost base (page 182)
- **Amazon** — cost-driven AND scale-driven; massive fixed costs in fulfillment and AWS infrastructure, but per-unit costs fall sharply at scale (page 186)
- **IKEA** — cost-driven; flat-pack design, self-assembly, large-footprint stores in low-rent areas, economies of scale in production (page 205)
- **Walmart** — cost-driven; supply chain scale, low-margin/high-volume, supplier pressure (page 208)
- **Coca-Cola** — value-driven brand AND scale economies in production/distribution via bottling network (page 227)
- **Netflix** — large fixed cost in content production/licensing; marginal cost per viewer near zero (highly scalable) (page 193)
