# Performance Advertising Model

## Definition

Performance advertising charges advertisers only when their audience takes a defined action — a click, an install, a purchase — rather than when the ad is merely shown. Pereira contrasts it with **brand (CPM) advertising**, which charges per thousand impressions: brand pricing rewards reach, performance pricing rewards outcomes. The model's modern incarnation is Google's cost-per-click (CPC) Ads, but the principle predates Google (GoTo introduced PPC) and now spans Facebook's CPA campaigns, Amazon's Sponsored Products, affiliate networks, and influencer pay-per-conversion deals. Pereira emphasises that performance advertising shifted advertising from an exposure-purchasing market to an outcome-purchasing market — and that this shift, more than the search engine itself, is what made Google's economics work.

## Key attributes

- **Pay-on-action** — no action, no charge; risk shifts from advertiser to platform
- **Measurable** — every billable event is logged; ROI is calculable per dollar
- **Auction-priced** — CPC/CPA emerges from competitive bidding, not rate cards
- **Audience-targeted** — relevance multiplies effective ROI per dollar
- **Self-serve** — long-tail SMB advertisers can set up campaigns without sales contact
- **Aligns platform and advertiser incentives** — platform earns only when advertiser gets value
- **Variable yield** — revenue per impression varies wildly by query/audience intent

## When to use

- Direct-response categories (e-commerce, lead-gen, app installs, SaaS trials)
- Long-tail advertiser markets where manual sales is uneconomic
- High-intent surfaces (search queries, marketplace browse, in-app shopping)
- When you can track the post-click action reliably (attribution feasible)
- Markets where advertisers demand outcome accountability

## When NOT to use

- Brand-building goals (awareness, recall, sentiment) — CPM/reach-based is the right tool
- Long-cycle B2B where click-to-conversion takes months and attribution is broken
- Categories where action-tracking is impossible or legally constrained
- Premium-content contexts where pay-on-action signals "discount inventory"
- When the platform can't generate enough action volume to satisfy bidders

## Common pitfalls

- Click fraud — bots and click farms inflate billable actions without value
- Last-click attribution — over-credits performance ads, under-credits brand spend that primed the click
- Race to the bottom on quality — advertisers chase cheap clicks, landing-page quality decays
- Mistaking CPC for total cost — true cost includes landing page, creative, sales follow-up
- Mixing brand and performance budgets in one auction — performance always wins short-term, brand starves

## Key metrics

- **CPC** (Cost Per Click) — what advertiser pays per click
- **CPA** (Cost Per Acquisition) — cost per conversion (sale, signup, install)
- **CTR** (Click-Through Rate) — clicks ÷ impressions
- **Conversion Rate** — conversions ÷ clicks
- **ROAS** (Return on Ad Spend) — revenue ÷ ad spend
- **Quality Score** — platform-assigned relevance multiplier
- **eCPM** (effective CPM) — platform's revenue per thousand impressions (used to compare with CPM inventory)

## Decision questions

- Can we measure the action reliably and attribute it to the ad?
- Do we have enough auction depth to clear at attractive CPC/CPA?
- Is our category intent-driven (search-like) or interruption-based (display-like)?
- Are we building a brand or harvesting demand? (Performance ads harvest, they don't build)
- Can our funnel monetize a click well enough to pay the CPC?
