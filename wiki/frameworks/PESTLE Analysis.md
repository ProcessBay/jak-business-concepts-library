# PESTLE Analysis

## Definition

PESTLE (Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Legal, Environmental) is a macro-environmental scanning framework that maps the external forces shaping an organization's room to operate and its long-run profitability. Originating as PEST in the 1960s and expanded over time, PESTLE complements SWOT by isolating the external lens — where SWOT mixes internal and external factors, PESTLE works exclusively on what lies outside the firm. Pereira positions it as the tool to reach for "after a SWOT analysis," when leaders need a broader, more rigorous scan of the macro environment before entering a new market, launching a product, or revising long-term strategy.

## Key attributes

- **Six external categories** — Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Legal, Environmental (PEST being the original four; LE added later)
- **External-only lens** — unlike SWOT, no internal factors; pairs well with internal frameworks (SWOT, VRIO)
- **Big-picture orientation** — best for understanding the operating environment, not for tactical decisions
- **Inputs feed scenario planning** — PESTLE outputs are the raw material for "what could change" scenarios
- **Resource-intensive** — Pereira flags it as time-consuming; better suited to established firms with research capacity
- **Periodic, not one-off** — environmental factors shift; re-run when the macro picture moves
- **Two-step output** — first identify opportunities, then identify threats, then act on findings

## When to use

- Before entering a new market or geography (the foundational scan)
- During long-term (3-5 year) strategy formation
- When a SWOT keeps surfacing "external factors we don't fully understand"
- Before major regulatory, technological, or geopolitical shifts
- When building scenarios for the Scenario Planning Model
- During M&A diligence on the target's environment

## When NOT to use

- Short-term, tactical questions (use SWOT or operational frameworks)
- Small startups with no resources for environmental research (Pereira's specific caution)
- When the team will produce a document but not act on the findings
- As a substitute for direct customer or competitor research
- When the environment is genuinely stable and well-understood

## Common pitfalls

- Producing a long list with no prioritization (everything is "a factor")
- Confusing PESTLE with SWOT and double-counting external items
- Doing the scan but never feeding outputs into the plan
- Letting it become a one-off exercise instead of a periodic review
- Over-weighting Political/Legal in stable jurisdictions, under-weighting Technological in fast-moving sectors
- Ignoring Environmental in industries that depend on it (tourism, agriculture, energy)
- Conducting it in a vacuum without cross-functional input

## Outputs / signals of good use

- A ranked list of external opportunities and threats by likelihood × impact
- Concrete plan changes triggered by findings (not just a slide)
- Identified scenarios that feed scenario planning
- Cross-functional alignment on what the macro picture looks like
- A periodic cadence (annual or trigger-based) for re-running the scan

## Decision questions

- For each PESTLE category — what's changing in our environment in the next 1-3 years?
- Which factors could fundamentally change our cost structure or revenue model?
- Where are we exposed to political or regulatory shifts we haven't priced in?
- Which technological shifts threaten our value proposition? Which create new openings?
- What environmental/social shifts might change customer expectations?
- Are we acting on findings, or just cataloguing them?

## Canonical examples

- **Inflation impact on consumer goods** — economic factor; pricing pressure forces companies to raise prices, which affects purchasing behaviour and dampens sales (page 91)
- **Tourism, farming, agriculture** — environmental factors are first-order, not optional (page 92)
- **Regulated industries (banking, telecoms)** — political and legal factors dominate strategy
- **EdTech post-2020** — social/technological forces (remote learning normalization) created openings
