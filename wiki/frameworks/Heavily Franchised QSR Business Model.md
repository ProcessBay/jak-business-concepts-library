# Heavily Franchised QSR Business Model

## Definition

The heavily franchised quick-service restaurant (QSR) model is the operating system behind McDonald's, Burger King, KFC, Taco Bell, Wendy's, Subway, Starbucks, and Domino's. Pereira's McDonald's case (pages 58-77) is the canonical worked example: > 93% of ~40,000 restaurants are franchisee-owned, the parent company collects royalties (~4%) plus rent on the land/building under each restaurant, and only ~5-7% of stores are company-operated — kept primarily as test sites for innovation. The model's distinguishing feature versus ordinary franchising is that the parent often **owns the underlying real estate**, making the firm a massive commercial real estate company that happens to sell burgers. McDonald's reports > $41 billion in property and equipment (page 71). The system runs three structural variants: (1) conventional franchising with parent-owned land, (2) developmental license where the franchisee invests fully and pays royalty, (3) affiliate equity-investment markets (used in China, Japan). This combination produces predictable royalty + rent income that is more stable than restaurant operating profit.

## Key attributes

- **> 90% franchisee-owned** — parent runs only a small test estate
- **Royalty + rent revenue stack** — typical structure is 4-5.5% sales royalty PLUS 20-year ground rent
- **Parent owns the land** — turning the brand into a real estate empire (McDonald's $41B in PP&E)
- **Strict franchisee selection** — high net-worth requirements; many franchisees started as in-store staff
- **Long contracts with renewal premium** — 10-20 year terms with ~99% renewal rate (Domino's)
- **Company stores as innovation labs** — test new menu items, ops models, tech before system-wide rollout
- **Three franchise structures** — conventional, developmental license, affiliate equity
- **Brand is the central asset** — the franchise sells access to a brand, ops playbook, and supply chain

## When to use

- Concepts that can be standardized to operate identically in any geography
- Categories where brand recognition is decisive in the consumer's choice
- When the firm wants rapid geographic expansion without bearing all capex
- When the firm can credibly own/lease real estate at scale
- Multi-decade-old categories where the operating system is mature and codified

## When NOT to use

- Concepts that rely on owner-operator artistry (fine dining, chef-driven)
- Markets where franchise law is hostile or franchisee disputes are common
- When the brand is not yet strong enough to command franchise fees
- When the menu/operations are still evolving and need centralized control
- Early-stage businesses that have not codified the playbook

## Common pitfalls

- Franchising before the unit economics are proven and replicable
- Picking franchisees with capital but no operating experience (McDonald's avoids this — 95% of US franchisees started as in-store staff, page 51)
- Allowing brand drift across franchisees, eroding the central asset
- Underestimating the real-estate operating capability required to run as a landlord
- Royalty rates set too low to fund the central marketing/innovation engine
- Losing the test-store discipline that lets you de-risk innovation before system rollout

## Key metrics

- **Franchise count** and net openings per year
- **Percent franchised** (target typically > 90% in mature systems)
- **Royalty rate** (% of franchisee sales)
- **Average rent yield** on owned real estate
- **Same-store sales growth** (organic vs. unit expansion)
- **Revenue per store** by segment / geography
- **Brand value** (McDonald's $154.9B in 2021 — page 70)
- **Franchise renewal rate** (Domino's ~99%, page 51)

## Decision questions

- Is our operating system codified enough that a franchisee can run it identically?
- Do we have the brand power to charge a royalty franchisees will gladly pay?
- Can we operationally run a real estate portfolio at scale?
- What is our franchisee-selection bar, and are we willing to enforce it?
- Are we keeping enough company stores to test innovation before system rollout?
- What is our master-franchise vs. direct-franchise mix per geography?

## Canonical examples

- **McDonald's** — ~40,031 restaurants in 119 countries; 93% franchised; $154.9B brand value; $41B in property (pages 58-77)
- **Domino's Pizza** — 18,800 stores in 90 countries; 98% franchised; uses master franchise in 7 of 10 largest international markets (e.g., Jubilant Foods in India, Dash Brands in China, pages 50-52)
- **Burger King, KFC, Taco Bell, Wendy's, Subway, Starbucks** — same structural pattern (page 60)
- **Saudi context** — Herfy is the KSA-grown QSR analog; most international QSR chains in KSA (McDonald's via Reza Food Services West, Burger King via Olayan, KFC via Americana) operate under master franchise agreements
