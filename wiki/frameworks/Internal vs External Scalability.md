# Internal vs External Scalability

## Definition

The BMI (Business Models Inc.) Scalability Matrix splits scalability into two complementary dimensions: **Internal Scalability** — the business model's design (resources, partners, capabilities) and how capable the enterprise is of expanding customer base and sales rapidly at low cost; **External Scalability** — the business environment (customers, markets, regulations) and how welcoming it is for expansion. Pereira's key insight: both sides are necessary. A great internal model in a constrained market caps out; a great market with poor internal model leaves opportunity unclaimed. Strategy must address both.

## Key attributes

- **Two complementary dimensions** — Internal (you) × External (market)
- **Both required** — strong on one + weak on other = capped growth
- **Internal improvable** — leverage external resources, digitize/automate, build flywheel cycles, hire scalers
- **External improvable** — pick big growing markets, target right segments right way, find market constraints to overcome, do something relevant
- **Matrix-style diagnosis** — score yourself on both axes; identify dominant constraint
- **Different strategies per quadrant** — high internal + low external = market expansion focus; low internal + high external = capability build focus

## Internal scalability — improvement strategies

- **Leverage external resources** — Uber (drivers' cars), Android (developers' phones)
- **Digitize and automate** — biggest scalability driver; AI/algorithms automate more tasks
- **Develop and monitor growth cycle (flywheel)** — feedback loops where serving customers better generates more revenue, which improves the offering
- **Find the right people to scale** — experienced operators with scaling mindset

## External scalability — improvement strategies

- **Pick a big and growing market** — growing > big (if forced to choose); niches within bigger markets work
- **Target the right customers the right way** — fit their habits; early adopters; turn customers into salesforce; cross-side (in two-sided platforms) consumer→producer conversion
- **Look for market constraints** — language, culture, geography, regulation; figure out how to overcome (Google Loon for internet-poor regions)
- **Do something relevant** — higher purpose beyond profit attracts talent and motivates through challenges

## When to use

- Scaling diagnosis when growth has stalled
- Pre-investment / pre-launch market and capability assessment
- Strategic resource allocation (where to invest first)
- Post-Series B planning when scaling pressures intensify

## When NOT to use

- For pre-product-market-fit startups (premature)
- For purely operational decisions (this is a strategic framework)

## Common pitfalls

- Optimizing one dimension while ignoring the other
- Treating market expansion as internal scaling problem (or vice versa)
- Confusing capability building (internal) with capacity building (operational)
- Picking big stable markets over big growing markets (capital flows to growth)

## Key metrics

- Internal scalability score (1-10 self-assessment)
- External scalability score (1-10 self-assessment)
- Market growth rate
- Capacity-to-current-demand ratio (internal slack)
- Customer-acquisition runway in current market

## Decision questions

- Which dimension is more constrained — our model or our market?
- For each Internal strategy: which would unlock the most growth?
- For each External strategy: which barrier is most surmountable?
- What does the BMI matrix score for our business — and where are competitors?

## Canonical examples

- **Uber** — strong internal (driver leverage) + strong external (huge ride-hailing market) (page 16)
- **Android** — strong internal (developer + manufacturer leverage) + strong external (mobile mass market)
- **Google Loon** — internal strength (Google) overcoming external constraint (no internet in rural areas) (page 18)
- **Airbnb** — strong internal (host leverage, technology) + strong external (global travel) (page 16)
