# Key Partners

## Definition

Key Partners is the BMC block that identifies the external organizations a firm depends on to make its business model work — suppliers, non-competitors with complementary capabilities, joint-venture partners, and even competitors (co-opetition). Pereira distinguishes four partner types and three motivations for partnership: optimization & economy of scale, risk/uncertainty reduction, and acquisition of particular resources or activities. Choosing key partners is strategic: the wrong partner concentration creates dependency risk, while the right partnership accelerates growth and reduces capital intensity.

## Key attributes

- **Four partner types** — Suppliers, Non-Competitors (complementary), Joint Ventures, Co-Opetition (competitors collaborating)
- **Three motivations** — Optimization/Economy of Scale, Risk/Uncertainty Reduction, Resource/Activity Acquisition
- **Dependency vs. leverage** — every partner is both an enabler and a dependency
- **Contract-based** — formalized through agreements with explicit terms
- **Critical, not exhaustive** — Key Partners means truly business-essential, not every vendor
- **Selection matters** — alignment of goals, communication, and trust determine success or failure

## When to use

- Defining a business model that requires external capability beyond what the firm has
- M&A and partnership strategy review
- Identifying single-point-of-failure dependencies
- Scaling — partnerships unlock geographies, customer segments, or capabilities the firm can't build
- Risk management — diversifying critical supply chains and capability sources

## When NOT to use

- As a full vendor list (Key Partners is selective — only those without whom the model breaks)
- When the firm can realistically build the capability internally faster or cheaper
- When the partner's interests diverge sharply (high conflict probability)
- Where intellectual property boundaries can't be cleanly defined

## Common pitfalls

- Confusing partners with suppliers (partners are strategic; suppliers are transactional)
- Single-partner dependency that creates business-killing concentration risk
- No exit terms — partnerships that can't end gracefully when conditions change
- Failing to manage the partnership actively (set-and-forget partnerships drift)
- Treating co-opetition naively (competitors-as-partners require explicit guardrails)

## Key metrics

- Partner dependency score (revenue or capability exposure per partner)
- Joint revenue or value share
- Partner NPS / relationship-health score
- Time-to-value via partnership
- Contract renewal rate
- # of partners with single-source dependency

## Decision questions

- For each key partner: what would happen if they exited tomorrow?
- Are our most important partners aligned with our long-term goals?
- Where could partnership replace internal build that we don't have capability for?
- Where are we over-partnered — multiple partners doing similar things, none well?
- What does each partner most need from us, and are we delivering?

## Canonical examples

- **Apple** — suppliers (Foxconn assembly, TSMC chips), distribution partners (carriers), content partners (App Store developers) (page 182)
- **Amazon** — third-party sellers (marketplace), logistics partners (USPS/UPS for last-mile), publishers (books/Kindle) (page 186)
- **Netflix** — content studios (licensing partners — increasingly co-opetition), ISPs (caching infrastructure), device manufacturers (smart TVs, consoles) (page 192)
- **Uber** — drivers (independent contractors, key partners), restaurants (Uber Eats), payment processors (page 189)
- **Google** — Android device manufacturers (Samsung, OEMs), advertisers, search syndication partners (page 174)
- **Coca-Cola** — franchise bottlers worldwide (critical distribution partners) (page 226)
