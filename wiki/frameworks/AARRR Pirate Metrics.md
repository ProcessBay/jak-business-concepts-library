# AARRR Pirate Metrics

## Definition

AARRR — pronounced "arrr," like a pirate, which is why Dave McClure named it that — is a **five-step funnel model for growth that tracks Acquisition, Activation, Retention, Referral, and Revenue**. Pereira describes the framework as "still highly significant for the growth of a firm" and explicitly ties it to product-market fit: "The rate of adoption, to put it another way, is the obvious indicator that you have established a solid product-market fit" (page 36). The framework's contribution is that it forces founders to look past topline new-user counts and check whether those users actually activate, stick, refer others, and generate revenue. Pereira pairs AARRR with a sharp warning about the vanity metric trap (page 36).

## Key attributes

- **Five sequential stages** — Acquisition → Activation → Retention → Referral → Revenue
- **Acquisition** — number of new users entering the funnel
- **Activation** — users who complete the "aha moment" and become engaged
- **Retention** — users who keep coming back over time
- **Referral** — users who recommend the product to others
- **Revenue** — users who pay (or generate value)
- **Activation is the canonical PMF indicator** — Pereira: adoption rate is the obvious PMF signal (page 36)
- **Each stage has a conversion rate to the next** — a stage-by-stage funnel
- **Designed to expose vanity metrics** — high acquisition with low activation is the classic warning sign

## When to use

- Product analytics setup for any consumer or SaaS product
- Diagnosing where in the funnel growth is leaking
- Cross-functional alignment on what "growth" means
- Investor reporting: a structured way to talk about funnel health
- A/B testing prioritization: which stage has the most leverage?

## When NOT to use

- For enterprise B2B with sales-led motions (different funnel mechanics)
- When the product's value loop doesn't fit the funnel shape (e.g., one-time purchase, transactional)
- As the only framework — pair with retention curves and cohort analysis
- When team is too small to actually instrument all five stages

## Common pitfalls

- **Optimizing only Acquisition** — the classic vanity metric trap (page 36)
- **Skipping Activation measurement** — without it, Acquisition tells you nothing
- **No clear activation definition** — "what's our aha moment?" must be answered first
- **Treating each stage independently** — they form a system; improvements compound multiplicatively
- **Confusing topline growth with PMF** — Pereira warns: "skyrocketing client acquisition rates… does not always indicate that your product is a suitable fit" (page 36)
- **RARRA blindness** — some practitioners argue retention should come first (RARRA), not last

## Key metrics

- **Acquisition Rate** — new users per period per channel
- **Activation Rate** — % of acquired users who hit the activation event
- **Retention Rate** — % of activated users still using at N days/weeks/months
- **Referral Rate** — % of users who refer at least one other user (or K-factor)
- **Revenue per User** — ARPU; revenue-side conversion
- **Funnel composite** — multiplied stage conversions = end-to-end yield

## Decision questions

- What is our specific definition of "activation" for this product?
- Where is our funnel leaking the most — which stage has the worst conversion?
- Are we chasing vanity metrics at the top of the funnel? (page 36)
- Is our activation rate the obvious sign that we have PMF, or that we don't? (page 36)
- Which stage would yield the most revenue if we improved it 10%?

## Canonical examples

- **Dave McClure's original 500 Startups talk** — the canonical articulation of AARRR
- **Pereira's pairing with PMF** — adoption rate (Activation) as the obvious PMF indicator (page 36)
- **The vanity metric warning** — high acquisition with low activation as the textbook anti-pattern (page 36)
- **Consumer apps** — AARRR is most commonly applied in mobile/web consumer products
