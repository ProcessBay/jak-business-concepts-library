# Three Pricing Strategy Approaches

## Definition

Pereira organizes pricing strategy into three foundational approaches, each rooted in a different anchor: **cost-based pricing** sets price as production cost plus a target markup; **value-based pricing** sets price based on the customer's perceived value of the outcome; and **competition-based pricing** sets price relative to direct competitors' prices. Most firms blend the three — cost as a floor, competitor pricing as a reality check, and value as the ceiling — but one approach typically dominates the decision logic. The choice has compounding effects on margin, positioning, and how the business communicates worth.

## Key attributes

- **Cost-based** — price = total cost + markup; simple, safe, guarantees cost recovery
- **Value-based** — price = customer's perceived value capture; requires segmentation and willingness-to-pay research
- **Competition-based** — price set relative to competitors; choose to lead, match, or undercut
- **Dynamic pricing** — a fourth, hybrid approach: prices adjust in real time based on demand, customer, or context
- **Most successful firms use all three** — cost as floor, competitor as benchmark, value as anchor
- **Different approaches signal different positioning** — value-based signals premium, cost-based signals utility, competition-based signals parity
- **Choice constrains future moves** — raising prices is harder after anchoring on cost-plus

## When to use

**Cost-Based when:**
- Costs are stable and well-understood (manufacturing, distribution)
- You're new and lack market data to estimate willingness-to-pay
- Margin protection is the top priority
- The category is commoditized and pricing is mostly cost-driven

**Value-Based when:**
- The product delivers measurable economic outcomes for the customer
- You can segment buyers by willingness-to-pay
- You have a defensible differentiator that justifies premium
- You're targeting a high-LTV segment that buys on outcomes, not features

**Competition-Based when:**
- The market has a clear price benchmark customers reference
- You're entering an established category and need to signal positioning quickly
- Customers actively comparison-shop on price
- You need rapid pricing responses to competitive moves

## When NOT to use

- **Cost-Based** in markets where value far exceeds cost (you leave money on the table)
- **Value-Based** when you lack the data or sales motion to communicate value (price becomes a barrier)
- **Competition-Based** when you've built genuine differentiation (you anchor yourself to inferior comps)
- **Any single approach** when category conditions are shifting rapidly — blend instead

## Common pitfalls

- **Cost-Based**: anchoring price too low, ignoring willingness-to-pay, leaving margin on the table
- **Value-Based**: overestimating perceived value, pricing above customers' ability to internalize the value, requiring an expensive sales motion to justify
- **Competition-Based**: racing to the bottom, losing strategic focus to competitor moves, becoming a price-taker
- **All three**: failing to revisit pricing as markets, costs, and competition shift
- Setting a price once and never testing — pricing is a continuous experiment, not a one-time decision
- Confusing pricing with discounting — heavy discount programs reveal the list price was wrong

## Key metrics

- **Gross margin** — the floor; cost-based ensures this stays positive
- **Price realization** — actual selling price / list price; reveals discount leakage
- **Price elasticity** — % change in demand per % change in price (inelastic categories absorb increases)
- **Willingness-to-pay** (WTP) — surveyed maximum price by segment
- **Competitive price index** — your price / competitor benchmark price
- **Contribution margin per unit** — what each sale contributes after variable cost

## Decision questions

- What is the floor (cost recovery) and the ceiling (max willingness-to-pay)?
- Where do we want to be in that range, and what does that signal about positioning?
- How sensitive is demand to a 10% price increase or decrease in our category?
- Who are the comparable products in the customer's mind, and how do they price?
- Can we segment customers and price differently for each segment (value-based)?
- What's our policy when a competitor cuts price — do we follow, hold, or differentiate further?
- When did we last revisit pricing, and what experiment would tell us if we're wrong?

## Canonical examples

- **Cost-Based**: traditional manufacturing, contract services, regulated utilities
- **Value-Based**: Salesforce ROI-driven pricing, enterprise SaaS, luxury goods
- **Competition-Based**: airline ticketing, fuel retail, e-commerce price-matching
- **Dynamic Pricing**: Uber surge pricing, Amazon real-time price adjustment, airline yield management (page 64)
- **Penetration vs Skimming for new products** — initial low price to capture share vs initial high price to harvest early adopters (page 66)
