# PMF Five Core Areas

## Definition

Pereira decomposes the concept of product-market fit into five distinct core areas that "come together to make up the concept" (page 11). The framework is a checklist: a product missing any of these five is not at PMF, no matter how good the others look. The five areas are: (1) identifying a value proposition, (2) building something you need, (3) having a problem worth solving, (4) winning word-of-mouth growth, and (5) creating a pull from the market. Use it as both a pre-launch design checklist and a post-launch diagnostic.

## Key attributes

- **Value proposition first** — determine your value hypothesis BEFORE your growth hypothesis (page 11)
- **Build for yourself** — products that solve the founder's own problem have the strongest origin story; alternatives are "sitcom startups" with fictional problems (page 12)
- **Validate the problem exists for others** — confirming that other people share the problem you have (page 12)
- **WOM is the gating signal** — "Do you have customers who are so enthusiastic about your product that they advise their friends and family to give it a try?" (page 13)
- **Market pull, not company push** — PMF means demand you can barely meet, not just inventory turnover (page 13)
- **Each area gates the next** — no value prop means no growth experiments can save you (page 11)
- **90-day cadence is feasible** — finding others with the same problem can be structured into ~90-day cycles via the Leanstack Continuous Innovation Framework (page 12)

## When to use

- Pre-launch: as the design checklist
- Post-launch but pre-traction: as a diagnostic for which core area is broken
- Pivoting: to identify which of the five is the failure point
- Hiring product leaders: as a shared mental model
- Investor diligence: to assess where in the five a startup actually stands

## When NOT to use

- As a substitute for measurement — the framework is qualitative; pair with metrics
- In late-stage scale companies — too foundational; use cohort analytics instead
- When you've over-rotated on growth hypothesis at the expense of value hypothesis

## Common pitfalls

- **Skipping straight to growth hypothesis** — investing in ads/SEO before validating value (page 11)
- **Sitcom startups** — building solutions to fictional problems no one will pay to solve (page 12)
- **Solving only your own problem** — without checking if anyone else has it
- **Conflating high traffic with WOM growth** — high signup volume without referral spread isn't PMF
- **Forcing the market** — pushing product without listening for pull (page 13)

## Key metrics

- **Value hypothesis validation rate** — % of customer interviews that confirm your problem hypothesis
- **Problem severity score** — qualitative ranking via customer interviews
- **WOM referral share** — % of new customers acquired via existing-customer recommendation
- **Demand vs supply gap** — backlog or wait-time as a pull indicator
- **Churn rate** — high churn is the canonical signal of value-prop failure (page 11)

## Decision questions

- Have we written down and tested our value hypothesis?
- Did we (or our team) experience this problem personally?
- How many other people have we confirmed share this problem?
- Do our customers recommend us unprompted to friends and colleagues?
- Are we struggling to keep up with demand, or trying to manufacture it?

## Canonical examples

- **Leanstack Continuous Innovation Framework** — Pereira's recommended cadence for 90-day validation cycles (pages 12-13)
- **Spotify** — Daniel Ek validated all five areas: market existed (Napster), problem worth solving (legal music access), built for self/peers, WOM via young users, market pull (182M subscribers) (page 17)
- **Slack pivot** — the team realized their internal-comms tool had stronger pull than the game they were building (page 20)
- **Hair-on-fire customer (Andreessen)** — pull is a function of problem severity (page 14)
