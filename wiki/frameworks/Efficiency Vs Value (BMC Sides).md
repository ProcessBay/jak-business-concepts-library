# Efficiency Vs Value (BMC Sides)

## Definition

A second macro lens on the Business Model Canvas, splitting it into the **consumption side** (right) and the **production side** (left). The right side — Customer Segments, Channels, Customer Relationships, Value Proposition — is the **value/customer/consumption side**: external, market-facing, dynamic, requires fast adaptation to changing audience needs. The left side — Key Activities, Key Resources, Partner Network — is the **efficiency/business/production side**: internal, operator-controlled, more static and repeatable, requires long-range strategic thinking. The **bottom band** (Cost Structure, Revenue Streams) holds the financial blocks: cost belongs predominantly to the production side, revenue to the consumption side, but both must be designed together. The two sides demand fundamentally different leadership cadences — agile and customer-listening on the right; strategic and operational-discipline on the left.

## Key attributes

- **Right side = consumption / value / customer** — external, dynamic, agile
- **Left side = production / efficiency / business** — internal, static, strategic
- **Bottom = finance** — cost (lean toward production) and revenue (lean toward consumption)
- **Two operating cadences** — fast/responsive vs deliberate/repeatable
- **Different leadership instincts** — empathic listening vs operational engineering
- **Mismatch is a failure mode** — running the right side like the left (or vice versa) kills the model

## When to use

- Diagnosing organizational misalignment (e.g., product teams optimizing for cost, ops teams optimizing for customer delight)
- Designing operating rhythms: which side needs weekly cycles, which needs quarterly cycles
- Allocating leadership attention between market sensing and operational excellence
- Coaching teams who confuse "what customers see" with "what we do internally"

## When NOT to use

- When the business is pre-revenue (the bottom band is hypothesis, not data)
- For pure cost-cutting exercises (this lens prevents narrow optimization but doesn't drive it)
- When the production side IS the value (e.g., a transparency-branded fair-trade brand where back-stage is the point)

## Common pitfalls

- Optimizing one side without testing the impact on the other
- Letting cost-structure logic dominate value-proposition design ("we can only deliver what's cheap")
- Letting customer-side hype outrun production-side capacity (overselling)
- Running both sides on the same operating cadence
- Treating Channels as a production-side problem (it is consumption-side: how value reaches the customer)

## Key metrics

- **Consumption-side health** — customer satisfaction, NPS, channel conversion, value-proposition fit
- **Production-side health** — unit economics, throughput, partner reliability, resource utilization
- **Bottom-band health** — gross margin, cost-to-serve, revenue per customer
- (Directional split — this lens helps you decide which metrics to watch, not which to invent)

## Decision questions

- Which side is currently the constraint — value creation or production efficiency?
- Are we running both sides on the same cadence by accident?
- Does our cost structure enable or constrain our value proposition?
- Where is the consumption-side changing fastest, and is our production-side capable of adapting?
- Are channels (consumption-side) being designed by production-side logic?

## Canonical examples

- **Dollar Shave Club** — collapsed the channel layer (consumption-side restructure) to undercut incumbent cost structures (page 11)
- **Waze** — moved value-creation onto the consumption side (users-as-producers), inverting the standard production-side mass (page 12)
- **Any SaaS company** — consumption side ships weekly; production side (infrastructure, security) plans quarterly
