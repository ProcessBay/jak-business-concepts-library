# Everyday Low Prices (EDLP)

## Definition

Everyday Low Prices (EDLP) is a pricing posture, pioneered and popularized by Walmart, that commits the retailer to consistently low prices across the catalog **instead of** relying on frequent sales, promotions, and discounting. The strategic bet is that customers eventually learn the brand can be trusted to be cheap without having to time their purchases — eliminating the cognitive overhead of "should I wait for a sale?" The model demands deep operational discipline: bulk supplier negotiation, supply-chain efficiency, cost leadership, and data-driven inventory management. Pereira positions EDLP as the central element of Walmart's competitive pricing playbook and one of the canonical postures in retail (the other being **High-Low** pricing, where regular prices are higher but frequent promotions drive trips). EDLP simplifies pricing operations but requires structural cost advantages that few competitors can match.

## Key attributes

- **No promotional cycle** — prices stay flat; no Black Friday-style swings on the EDLP catalog
- **Trust as the central asset** — customers stop comparison-shopping over time
- **Supply-chain scale dependence** — supplier negotiation power is the underlying enabler
- **Cost leadership posture** — operational efficiency is the moat
- **Long-term frequency play** — drives trip frequency rather than basket size lift
- **Predictable customer behavior** — flat demand vs. spiky promotional demand simplifies operations
- **Hard to copy without scale** — smaller retailers struggle to match supplier terms
- **Often paired with price-match** — guarantees reinforce the EDLP promise (Walmart, Costco)
- **Lower marketing intensity** — less need for sale-event creative and campaigns

## When to use

- When you have genuine structural cost advantages (scale, supply chain, vertical integration)
- For categories where customers value time savings over deal-hunting
- In markets fatigued by constant promotional noise
- When trust and predictability are core brand positioning
- For high-frequency / habitual purchase categories (grocery, household essentials)
- When you want to differentiate from a high-low competitor

## When NOT to use

- Without the supplier scale to back the cost position (you'll bleed margin)
- For premium / luxury categories where discounting drives demand
- For fashion / seasonal goods that need clearance cycles
- In markets where promotional intensity is the customer expectation (apparel, electronics events)
- When your competitors can easily match your EDLP — race-to-the-bottom
- For ecommerce startups without the operational backbone to sustain it

## Common pitfalls

- Claiming EDLP without the cost structure to deliver — customers detect the gap quickly
- Mixing EDLP and high-low across the same catalog confuses customers
- Failing to communicate the EDLP promise; customers default to assuming high-low and wait for sales
- Underinvesting in supplier negotiation — the cost gap closes and margins compress
- Letting select promotional moments creep in and erode the trust position
- Not measuring trip frequency, which is the real EDLP outcome metric
- Conflating EDLP with loss-leader — EDLP applies catalog-wide; loss-leader applies to select SKUs

## Key metrics

- **Trip frequency** (purchases per customer per period) — the headline EDLP outcome
- **Customer retention rate** and repeat-purchase rate
- **Gross margin %** — must hold up despite flat-low pricing
- **Supplier cost per unit** — the operational lever that funds EDLP
- **Sell-through rate** — predictable demand should mean fewer clearance markdowns
- **Promotional lift** (measured for non-EDLP categories) — confirms when to use vs. avoid sales
- **Competitive price index** vs. high-low competitors over a full promotional cycle

## Decision questions

- Do we have a structural cost advantage that a competitor can't replicate?
- Are our customers in a category where they value predictability or deal-hunting?
- Can we sustain supplier negotiation pressure over years?
- Are we willing to lose short-term traffic from competitors' big promotional events?
- How do we communicate the EDLP promise so customers stop waiting for sales?
- Do we measure trip frequency, or are we still optimizing for basket size?

## Canonical examples

- **Walmart** — the EDLP archetype; built around supplier scale, supply-chain efficiency, and price-match (pages 126-128)
- **Costco** — EDLP via warehouse format + membership; trust and value are the brand
- **Aldi / Lidl** — EDLP in European grocery via private-label and operational simplicity
- **IKEA** — EDLP on furniture via vertical integration and flat-pack supply chain
- **Trader Joe's** — EDLP via private-label everything; no flyers, no coupons
- **Amazon Basics** — EDLP at the SKU level layered on top of Amazon's broader algorithmic catalog
- **Decathlon** — EDLP for sporting goods via private-label and global sourcing scale
