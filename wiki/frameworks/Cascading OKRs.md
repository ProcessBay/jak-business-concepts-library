# Cascading OKRs

## Definition

**Cascading OKRs** is the practice of linking and aligning Objectives and Key Results across organizational levels — from executive strategy down to departmental, team, and individual goals — so that every person can trace how their work contributes to the company's overarching direction (Pereira, pages 49-52). A pure top-down cascade is the strawman; effective cascading is **bidirectional**: leadership sets strategic Objectives, teams propose how they will contribute, and the two negotiate. Done well, cascading creates **line-of-sight from CEO to IC** without micromanagement. Done badly, it becomes top-down dictation that strips ownership and produces malicious compliance.

## Key attributes

- **Line-of-sight from top to bottom** — every IC should be able to trace their KR to a company Objective (page 51)
- **Bidirectional, not just top-down** — teams co-create cascaded OKRs (pages 53-55)
- **Each level's KRs become the next level's Objectives** — a CEO KR (e.g., "+20% revenue") becomes the sales VP's Objective (page 50)
- **Breaks down silos** — when team OKRs reference each other, dependencies become visible (pages 52, 85-87)
- **Cascading depth varies** — typically 3-4 levels (company → BU → team → individual)
- **Requires communication infrastructure** — town halls, shared OKR repos, regular alignment sessions (page 51)
- **Adjusts together** — when a parent OKR changes mid-cycle, children must realign (page 47)

## When to use

- Organizations with 50+ employees where strategy translation is non-obvious
- Multi-business-unit companies where dependencies cross team boundaries
- After a strategic pivot or restructure, when alignment is fragile
- When teams complain they don't see how their work matters
- When leadership wants visibility without inserting themselves into team-level execution

## When NOT to use

- Tiny startups (<20 people) where everyone can see the whole picture without scaffolding
- Highly autonomous flat organizations where vertical alignment isn't the bottleneck
- During acute crisis when speed beats process
- When the org is OKR-immature — cascading on top of a broken OKR practice multiplies the dysfunction

## Common pitfalls

- **Pure top-down cascade** — leadership writes all OKRs; teams just receive them (kills ownership; pages 53-55)
- **Mechanical translation** — each level just repeats the parent OKR verbatim instead of translating to its domain
- **Cascade too deep** — individual contributors with their own published OKR set creates planning overhead with no return
- **Silos despite cascade** — team A's KRs depend on team B but neither knows it (page 84)
- **Misaligned cadences** — parent on annual, child on quarterly, with no synchronization (page 43)
- **Cascade as cover for micromanagement** — leaders demanding visibility into IC-level KRs
- **No regeneration** — copy-pasting last quarter's cascade without re-questioning relevance

## Key metrics

- **Alignment score** — % of team OKRs that explicitly link to a parent OKR
- **Cascade depth** — number of levels from top OKR to leaf (3-4 typical)
- **Cross-team dependency count** — number of KRs that reference another team's KR (signal of integration vs silo)
- **Time from leadership OKR finalization to team OKR finalization** — should be days, not weeks

## Decision questions

- Can every IC trace their KR up to a company Objective in two or three jumps?
- Are team OKRs co-created with the team, or handed to them?
- Where are the cross-team dependencies, and have they been negotiated?
- If a top-level OKR changes mid-cycle, do we have a process to cascade the change?
- Are we cascading too deep — burdening ICs with their own published OKRs?

## Canonical examples

- **Google** — full top-to-bottom cascade with company-wide transparency; every employee's OKRs visible (pages 109-110)
- **Toyota** — cascaded "improve production efficiency while maintaining quality" into per-team cycle-time and defect-rate KRs across the kaizen system (pages 102-103)
- **Airbnb** — cascaded "create a world where anyone can belong anywhere" into department-level listing growth, booking velocity, regional CSAT KRs (page 100)
- **Patagonia** — sustainability cascade: corporate carbon goals → manufacturing waste reduction KRs → supply-chain chemical elimination KRs (page 97)
