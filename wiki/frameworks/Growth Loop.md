# Growth Loop

## Definition

A growth loop is a strategic framework that structures growth as a self-reinforcing cycle rather than a linear funnel. Pereira defines it as a system in which every new user contributes — through usage, content, or referral — to the acquisition of more users, creating a compounding effect that scales without proportional marketing spend. Where a traditional funnel ends when the user converts, a growth loop closes back on itself: acquisition feeds activation, activation enables retention, retention generates referrals, and referrals trigger new acquisition. The loop's strength is its compounding nature — small improvements at each stage multiply. Growth loops are the structural answer to the question "why does this company keep growing when its marketing budget is flat?"

## Key attributes

- **Self-reinforcing** — output of one cycle becomes input of the next
- **Compounding, not additive** — growth multiplies rather than stacks
- **Five canonical stages** — Acquisition → Activation → Retention → Referral/Expansion → Data Analysis & Optimization
- **Built into the product** — loops live in product mechanics, not in marketing campaigns
- **Cycle-time-sensitive** — shorter loop cycles compound faster
- **Bottleneck-driven** — the weakest stage caps the whole loop
- **Measurable end-to-end** — each stage instrumented with stage-specific metrics

## When to use

- When the product naturally generates value as more users join (network effects, content, marketplaces)
- When you need growth without proportional acquisition spend
- For SaaS, social, marketplace, and content products
- When the team can instrument and optimize each loop stage
- When organic/viral channels are achievable in the category

## When NOT to use

- Categories with no inherent referral or network mechanic (heavy industrial, regulated B2B)
- When retention is structurally low (the loop leaks and never compounds)
- One-time purchase or transactional categories without repeat behavior
- Early stage before product-market fit — loops amplify good and bad products alike
- When measurement infrastructure cannot track stage-to-stage conversion

## Common pitfalls

- Designing the loop on paper but not instrumenting each stage
- Ignoring the activation step (users acquired but never get to value)
- Treating referral as the only loop component (it's one of five stages)
- Failing to identify and fix the weakest-link bottleneck
- Confusing growth loop with viral loop (viral is one type of loop, not the only one)
- Skipping the data-analysis stage and never optimizing what you built

## Key metrics

- **Loop cycle time** — how long one full cycle takes
- **Stage conversion rates** — A→Activation, Activation→Retention, Retention→Referral
- **Viral coefficient (K)** — referrals per user per cycle
- **Cohort retention curves** — does retention hold up over cycles?
- **Net new users from referral** — referral-driven acquisition share

## Decision questions

- What is our loop, drawn end-to-end? Can the team describe it in one sentence?
- Which stage is the bottleneck — and what is its current conversion rate?
- How long is one full cycle, and can we shorten it?
- Is referral coming from the product itself, or are we bolting it on?
- Are we measuring each stage with stage-specific KPIs, not aggregate vanity metrics?

## Canonical examples

- **Dropbox** — referral loop: invite friend → both get storage → friend invites more (page 99-100)
- **Airbnb** — supply loop: host lists → guest books → guest becomes host → more inventory (page 97-98)
- **Slack** — team-invite loop: one user signs up → invites teammates → workspace gets stickier → more invites (page 107-108)
- **Pinterest** — content loop: users save pins → pins surface in search/feed → drives new users → who save more pins (page 109-110)
- **Spotify** — social-share loop: user listens → shares to Facebook → friends discover → sign up → share again (page 104-105)
