# Business Format Franchise

## Definition

The business format franchise is the dominant modern franchising type: the franchisor licenses not just a brand and product, but a complete turnkey business system — trademark, products/services, operations manual, marketing playbook, training programs, supplier relationships, technology platforms, and ongoing field support. Pereira identifies it as the form that emerged in the 1930s with A&W Root Beer and Howard Johnson's and exploded in the 1950s-60s with McDonald's, Burger King, and KFC. The franchisee receives a "business in a box" and pays an initial franchise fee plus ongoing royalties (typically 4-8% of gross sales) and advertising contributions (1-4%). The model trades franchisee autonomy for proven systems.

## Key attributes

- **Complete operating system** — products, SOPs, marketing, tech, supply chain all provided
- **Comprehensive training & support** — initial classroom/on-site training plus ongoing field visits
- **Operations manual** — detailed playbook covering every facet of running the unit
- **Brand standards enforced** — mystery shopping, audits, inspections to maintain consistency
- **Two-fee structure** — initial franchise fee + ongoing royalty as % of gross sales
- **Advertising fund contribution** — pooled marketing capital for national campaigns
- **Long-term agreements** — typically 10-20 years with renewal options
- **Local execution + central system** — franchisee brings community knowledge; franchisor brings playbook

## When to use

- Concept proven across 3-5+ company-owned locations with strong unit economics
- Operations are documented enough to be replicated by trained non-founders
- Brand consistency is a critical customer value driver
- Local operators in target markets can execute better than corporate
- Capital-light expansion is preferred over corporate-funded rollout
- Industry expects standardized customer experience (QSR, hotels, fitness)

## When NOT to use

- Quality requires founder-level oversight that can't be systematized
- Unit economics not yet proven in multiple locations
- Premium brand where any franchisee quality slip damages the entire system
- Categories with thin margins that can't absorb royalty + ad fees
- Heavy regulatory disclosure burden makes franchisor compliance costs prohibitive
- Markets where franchisees would resist the loss of operational autonomy

## Common pitfalls

- Premature franchising — opening units before unit economics are proven
- Brand erosion — franchisees cut corners on quality to protect margins
- Operations manual becomes outdated; field support can't keep up
- Royalty rate set too low to fund adequate franchisor support
- Encroachment disputes when franchisor opens new units near existing franchisees
- Training program inadequate for the actual operational complexity
- Franchisor revenue concentrates in fees but neglects supporting franchisee profitability

## Key metrics

- **Average Unit Volume (AUV)** — average revenue per unit
- **Same-store sales growth** — like-for-like unit revenue change
- **Franchise unit count and net growth**
- **Royalty revenue** (= AUV × unit count × royalty %)
- **Franchisee satisfaction scores** (Franchise Business Review benchmarks)
- **Brand consistency audit scores**
- **Time-to-profitability for new franchisees**
- **Franchisee renewal rate** at end of term

## Decision questions

- Are our unit economics proven across enough locations to justify scaling?
- Is our operations manual specific enough that a trained outsider can run a unit?
- What royalty rate funds the support franchisees actually need?
- How do we audit and enforce brand standards without destroying the franchisee relationship?
- Where does franchising vs. company-owned make more sense (urban core vs. suburb, domestic vs. international)?

## Canonical examples

- **McDonald's** — the archetypal business format franchise; 93%+ franchised; standardized recipes and procedures globally (page 231)
- **Subway** — 41,000+ locations across 100+ countries; the $5 Footlong campaign (page 232)
- **Dunkin'** — 12,000+ locations; standardized Arabica brewing process; "America Runs on Dunkin'" (page 236)
- **7-Eleven** — 78,000+ stores across 19 countries; rigorous franchisee selection (page 234)
- **Anytime Fitness** — 5,000+ gyms in 50 countries; 24/7 access model (page 237)
- **Sport Clips** — 1,800+ men's haircut units with sports-themed decor (page 245)
- **Marriott, Hilton** — hotel business format franchising
- **Saudi/MENA: Herfy, Kudu, Al Baik** — Saudi QSR with franchised expansion; Americana (Pizza Hut, KFC across GCC); Alshaya (Starbucks, Pottery Barn, etc.)
