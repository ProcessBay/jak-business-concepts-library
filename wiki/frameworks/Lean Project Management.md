# Lean Project Management

## Definition

Lean Project Management applies Toyota Production System principles — eliminating waste, maximizing customer value, and continuously improving — to the project lifecycle. Pereira identifies six core principles: **Customer Value and Focus**, **Elimination of Waste (Muda)**, **Continuous Improvement (Kaizen)**, **Respect for People**, **Just-in-Time (JIT) Delivery**, and **Visual Management**. Its central analytical tool is Value Stream Mapping (VSM), used to distinguish value-added from non-value-added activities and target waste for elimination. Lean classifies waste into types — Transportation, Inventory, Motion, Waiting, Overproduction, Overprocessing, Defects, and underutilized Skills — providing a structured vocabulary for process improvement.

## Key attributes

- **Six lean principles** — customer value, waste elimination, kaizen, respect for people, JIT, visual management
- **Eight waste types** — Transportation, Inventory, Motion, Waiting, Overproduction, Overprocessing, Defects, Skills (TIMWOODS)
- **Value Stream Mapping** — visual analysis of end-to-end process to surface waste
- **Kaizen** — culture of small, incremental, continuous improvement
- **Pull-based JIT delivery** — produce/deliver only what is needed, when needed
- **5S workspace organization** — Sort, Set in Order, Shine, Standardize, Sustain
- **Respect for people** — value-added improvement comes from the people closest to the work

## When to use

- Process-heavy operations where waste accumulates invisibly (healthcare, manufacturing, support)
- Mature processes ripe for systematic optimization
- Organizations that can sustain a continuous-improvement culture (not just a one-off event)
- Combined with Six Sigma (Lean Six Sigma) when quality variation is also the problem
- As a complement to Kanban (shared Toyota lineage) for flow optimization
- Projects where customer value is well-defined and waste reduction translates directly to savings

## When NOT to use

- Greenfield products where the problem is "discover what to build", not "eliminate waste in building it"
- Organizations unwilling to invest in cultural change (Pereira flags this as a primary failure mode)
- Large-scale complex projects with intricate interdependencies that resist streamlining
- Contexts where qualitative continuous-improvement metrics will be dismissed by quantitative leadership
- Short one-off projects where the upfront VSM investment exceeds the return

## Common pitfalls

- Treating Kaizen as one-off events instead of ongoing culture
- Eliminating activities labelled "waste" that were actually serving an unrecognized purpose
- Measuring Lean only by traditional metrics that miss customer-value and waste-reduction gains
- Underinvesting in people training and engagement — Pereira flags this as core to success
- Forcing Lean on contexts where the bottleneck is uncertainty, not waste
- Resistance from teams used to push-based, batch-oriented work

## Key metrics

- **Cycle time** — time from start to completion of a value-stream
- **Lead time** — total time from customer request to delivery
- **Value-added ratio** — value-added time / total cycle time
- **Waste count** — items identified per waste category, tracked over time
- **Defect rate** — quality issues per unit of work
- **First-pass yield** — % of work completed without rework
- **Customer satisfaction** — Lean's ultimate proxy for value delivery
- **Throughput** — units delivered per time period

## Decision questions

- What does our customer actually value, and what proportion of our activity contributes to that?
- Where in our value stream are the bottlenecks, queues, and waste accumulations?
- Do we have the cultural commitment to sustain continuous improvement, or will this be a one-off event?
- Are we trying to fix a waste problem or an uncertainty problem? (Wrong diagnosis kills Lean adoption.)
- Have we engaged the people closest to the work in identifying improvements?
