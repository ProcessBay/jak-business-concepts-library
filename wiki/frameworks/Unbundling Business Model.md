# Unbundling Business Model

## Definition

The unbundling business model decomposes a single corporation into three structurally distinct businesses that should be run as independent units because each is governed by a different economic logic. Pereira, drawing on the Hagel–Singer framing, identifies the three: **Customer Relationship Businesses** (find prospects, build trust, deepen engagement), **Product Innovation Businesses** (invent novel, differentiated products and services), and **Infrastructure Businesses** (operate high-volume, repetitive platforms at scale). Each demands a different culture, cost structure, talent profile, and time horizon — relationship businesses optimize for trust and scope, innovation businesses for speed and talent, infrastructure businesses for scale and efficiency. Most bundled firms perform poorly across all three because the competing priorities are irreconcilable inside one P&L. Unbundling can take the form of internal organizational separation, divestiture, spin-out, or wholesale restructuring; the strategic point is that autonomy — not the legal vehicle — is what unlocks performance.

## Key attributes

- **Three-business decomposition** — Customer Relationship, Product Innovation, Infrastructure (the Hagel-Singer split)
- **Different economic logic per unit** — each has its own scale curve, cost structure, and competitive dynamics
- **Different culture per unit** — relationship businesses are scope-driven, innovation is talent-driven, infrastructure is scale-driven
- **Autonomy is the unlock** — internal separation, P&L isolation, or formal divestiture all qualify
- **Often raises stock price** — analyst coverage and benchmarking improve when units are separable
- **Triggered by acquisition cleanup** — firms buy whole companies for one division, then unbundle the rest
- **Not the same as the unbundling disruption pattern** — this is intra-firm restructuring, distinct from market-level unbundling of incumbents

## When to use

- Conglomerate or multi-business firms where competing priorities are degrading performance
- Post-acquisition cleanup where the acquired bundle has divisions you don't need
- When public-market valuation suggests a sum-of-the-parts discount
- When one unit is starved of capital because it's funding another with different economics
- When talent retention is failing because the org demands incompatible operating cadences
- When customers are demanding sharper specialization than a bundled firm can credibly deliver

## When NOT to use

- When the three units share deep operating synergies that disappear under separation (shared data, shared customer base, shared platform economics)
- When the firm's competitive advantage IS the integration (Apple, until recently; Tesla's vertical integration thesis)
- When unbundling is a financial-engineering move with no operating logic — markets see through it
- When the firm lacks the management depth to run three credible standalone units
- When regulatory or contractual structure makes separation prohibitively costly
- When the bundled offering is the customer value proposition (cable, Microsoft 365)

## Common pitfalls

- Mistaking the three-business framework for "just split into divisions" — autonomy means real P&L isolation, not org-chart cosmetics
- Unbundling for analyst optics without operating conviction — short-term lift, long-term destruction
- Underestimating shared-services unwind cost (IT, finance, HR untangling is brutal)
- Losing the cross-sell and shared-data flywheel that justified the bundle originally
- Creating stranded costs in the unit nobody wanted
- Confusing this pattern with market-level unbundling (Stripe-vs-banks) — different framework, different decisions
- Spinning off the wrong unit (keeping the commodity infrastructure, selling the high-margin relationship business)

## Key metrics

- **Operating margin per unit** — the test of whether decomposition created value
- **Segment profitability** — formerly hidden inside the bundle
- **Time-to-decision per unit** — autonomy should accelerate this dramatically
- **NPS per unit** — separation often surfaces uneven customer experience
- **Talent retention per unit** — different operating cadences attract different people
- **Sum-of-the-parts valuation gap** — pre- vs. post-unbundling analyst coverage
- **Capital allocation efficiency** — capital flows to the unit with the best returns rather than subsidizing across

## Decision questions

- Are our Customer Relationship, Product Innovation, and Infrastructure businesses actually competing for the same resources and priorities?
- What does each unit lose if we separate them — and is the loss worth the autonomy gain?
- Do we have the management talent to run three credible standalone units, or are we creating three weak ones?
- Is this a financial-engineering move or an operating conviction?
- Which unit do we keep, and which do we divest or spin out?
- What stranded costs do we create, and who absorbs them?
- Is the bundle itself our customer value proposition? (If yes, unbundling is suicide.)

## Canonical examples

- **The three-business framework** — Customer Relationship, Product Innovation, Infrastructure (Hagel-Singer, via Pereira pp. 63-64)
- **Ford Motors first-half 20th century** — extreme vertical bundling (iron mines, steel mills, sawmills, assembly); decomposed as markets diversified (page 65-66)
- **Conglomerate spin-outs broadly** — corporate raids of the 80s-90s as institutional unbundling
- **Facebook app family** — internal unbundling of Messenger, Pages Manager, Ads Manager, Groups from the core app (page 68)
- **Yeezy Dating, Grailed, Discord, Imgur** — examples of unbundling-from-community, related but distinct
- **Apple iTunes** — unbundled the album into 99-cent singles (page 58)
- **Amazon e-commerce** — unbundled physical retail into a digital catalog (page 58)
- **Google** — unbundled the newspaper into headline-level discovery (page 58-59)
