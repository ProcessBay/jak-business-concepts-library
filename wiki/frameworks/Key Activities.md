# Key Activities

## Definition

Key Activities is the BMC block that identifies the most important operational actions a firm must take to deliver its value proposition, reach customers, maintain relationships, and earn revenue. Pereira categorizes activities into three types — Production (making physical or digital goods), Problem-Solving (consulting, services, custom solutions), and Platform/Network (running platforms, marketplaces, networks). He further breaks production-driven businesses into R&D, production, marketing, and sales/customer service activities, and offers three identification methods: brainstorming/interviews, capability mapping, and operating model analysis.

## Key attributes

- **Three activity types** — Production, Problem-Solving, Platform/Network
- **Linked to VP** — every key activity must serve the value proposition; if not, it's not "key"
- **Bound by resources** — activities are what the firm DOES with its key resources
- **Cost driver** — most variable costs trace to executing key activities
- **Common subtypes** — R&D, production, marketing, sales, customer service, platform operations
- **Differentiation lever** — being best-in-class at a key activity is a moat

## When to use

- Defining the operating model for a new business
- Diagnosing why outputs are inconsistent (one activity may be broken)
- Make-vs-buy decisions (which activities to own, which to outsource)
- Identifying which activities to invest in for competitive advantage
- Operating model design after M&A integration

## When NOT to use

- As a full process map — Key Activities is selective, not exhaustive
- For purely strategic positioning (use Porter's 5 Forces or Blue Ocean for that)
- In very early startups where activities are still being discovered (use lean experiments)

## Common pitfalls

- Listing every activity the firm does (the block should be 3-7 truly key ones)
- Confusing key activities with key resources (activities are verbs; resources are nouns)
- Treating activities as static when value propositions evolve
- Outsourcing what should be a key in-house capability (loses differentiation)
- Insourcing what should be outsourced (over-builds cost structure)

## Key metrics

- Throughput per key activity
- Cycle time per activity
- Quality yield (defect rate, rework rate)
- Cost per unit of activity output
- Capacity utilization

## Decision questions

- What 3-7 activities, if we stopped doing them, would break our value proposition?
- Are we Production, Problem-Solving, or Platform/Network type — and is our activity mix consistent with that?
- Which key activity is our biggest competitive advantage, and which is our biggest gap?
- Which activities should we own vs. outsource?
- Where is our activity capacity headroom limiting growth?

## Canonical examples

- **Apple** — production (hardware), R&D (chip design, OS), retail operations (page 182)
- **Amazon** — platform operations (marketplace), logistics (fulfillment), software engineering (AWS, search), media production (originals) (page 185)
- **Netflix** — platform/network (streaming), content production, content acquisition, recommendation engineering (page 192)
- **Google** — platform/network (search, ads, Workspace), R&D (algorithms, AI), data center operations (page 173)
- **Uber** — platform/network (matching algorithm), payment processing, driver onboarding, regulatory work (page 189)
- **IKEA** — production design (flat-pack), supply-chain operations, retail operations (page 205)
