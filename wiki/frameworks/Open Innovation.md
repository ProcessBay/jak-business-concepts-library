# Open Innovation

## Definition

Open innovation (originally Chesbrough, 2003) is the practice of collaborating with external partners — customers, suppliers, universities, startups, even competitors — to source new ideas and develop new products, instead of relying solely on internal R&D. The model treats the firm's R&D function as porous: ideas flow inward AND outward, and the firm becomes a hub for innovation that originates anywhere. Pereira illustrates this with Procter & Gamble's use of open innovation to collaborate with external partners on products like the Swiffer cleaning system.

## Key attributes

- **Porous R&D boundaries** — value flows both inward (sourcing ideas, technologies) and outward (licensing IP)
- **External partner network** — customers, suppliers, universities, startups, even competitors are valid sources
- **Hub-and-spoke model** — the firm becomes a coordinator of innovation that often originates outside its walls
- **IP management as critical capability** — knowing what to keep, what to license, what to share
- **Speed advantage** — typically faster than building everything internally
- **Cost-sharing** — external partners often share development cost

## When to use

- Markets where technology cycles are too fast for internal R&D alone
- When the firm lacks specific capabilities but doesn't want to build them
- After identifying a problem space where external talent has visible insight
- When the firm has strong distribution/commercialization but weak ideation
- When opening up R&D would generate licensing revenue from unused IP
- In ecosystems with active developer/researcher communities to tap

## When NOT to use

- When the firm's core differentiation is the secrecy of its R&D (e.g., proprietary formulas, deep platform tech)
- In industries with weak IP enforcement where openness leads to copying
- When the firm lacks the absorptive capacity to integrate external ideas
- When partner-management overhead would exceed the value of external ideas
- For projects in the "core" where confidentiality is strategically necessary

## Common pitfalls

- Treating open innovation as outsourced R&D and gutting internal capability — leaves no absorptive capacity
- Failing to manage IP properly, leading to disputes or accidental disclosure
- Choosing partners by proximity instead of by genuine capability fit
- Underestimating the integration cost of taking external ideas internal
- Not closing the loop with external contributors, damaging future participation

## Key metrics

- % of new products with external IP or input
- External-idea conversion rate (ideas considered → ideas commercialized)
- Active partner count
- Time-to-market for partner-sourced vs. internally-sourced innovations
- Inbound vs. outbound IP flow (licenses in vs. out)
- Royalty revenue from outbound licensing

## Decision questions

- Where in our roadmap could external capability move us faster than internal build?
- Do we have a process for absorbing external ideas, or are we just collecting them?
- Which partners are net contributors and which are net extractors?
- What IP would we never share, and is that boundary clear inside the firm?
- Are we measuring the success of open innovation, or is it a vibe?

## Canonical examples

- **Procter & Gamble** — used open innovation to collaborate with external partners and create products like the Swiffer cleaning system (page 18)
- **IBM + Mayo Clinic** — collaboration combining IBM's AI expertise with Mayo's clinical knowledge to develop Watson for Oncology (page 64)
- **Apple + Nike** — partnership creating Apple Watch Nike+, combining Apple technology with Nike's fitness/athletic expertise (page 63)
