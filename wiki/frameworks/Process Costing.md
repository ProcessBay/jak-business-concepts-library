# Process Costing

## Definition

Process Costing is a method of cost allocation used by businesses in which the **final product goes through different distinct stages of processing**, with the end-product of each stage forming the starting raw material for the next stage. Each stage is both capital and labor-intensive, so determining the total cost (or per-unit cost) is more accurate when the costs of each phase are summed up individually. Pereira also calls this **continuous costing**. The method is particularly advantageous in industries where the end-product of a stage can be sold separately — it lets the business build accurate total costs for each phase — and it's popular among mass producers of identical or near-identical items. Both direct and indirect costs are closely monitored at every stage. Common industries: paper mills, textiles, chemicals, food production, oil processing.

## Key attributes

- **Stage-by-stage costing** — costs accumulated per process phase, not per discrete job
- **Output of stage N = input of stage N+1** — costs flow forward through the chain
- **Continuous production** — built for ongoing, repetitive flow, not one-off jobs
- **Identical/near-identical output** — products are not individually distinguishable
- **Both direct and indirect costs monitored per stage**
- **Stage-sellable intermediate products** — supports selling intermediates if the market exists

## When to use

- Continuous-flow manufacturing (chemicals, oil, food, paper, textiles)
- Mass production of identical units where per-unit tracking is impractical
- When intermediate products have their own market value
- Industries where each stage is a distinct cost center worth managing
- When standardized cost-per-stage data is needed for pricing and benchmarking

## When NOT to use

- Custom or made-to-order production (use job costing)
- Project-based work with unique specifications (use contract costing)
- Service businesses with low repeatability
- Very short production runs where stage-level overhead allocation isn't worth the setup
- Small-batch production with frequently changing specifications (use batch costing)

## Common pitfalls

- Sloppy equivalent-unit calculations for work-in-process inventory
- Failing to assign indirect costs cleanly across stages
- Treating losses at one stage as if they were independent of upstream cost
- Ignoring stage-level standards — variance analysis dies without them
- Picking process costing where job or batch costing fits the production reality better

## Key metrics

- **Cost per stage** — total cost of each processing phase
- **Equivalent units** — partially-completed units expressed as whole-unit equivalents
- **Cost per equivalent unit** — stage cost / equivalent units
- **Work-in-process (WIP) value** by stage
- **Total process cost** — sum across all stages
- **Per-unit cost of final product** — total cost / units completed

## Decision questions

- Where in our process chain is unit cost growing fastest, and why?
- Which stages have sellable intermediate products we're not monetizing?
- Are we measuring equivalent units consistently across reporting periods?
- Which stage absorbs the most overhead — and is the apportionment causal or arbitrary?
- Is the variance at the final unit cost coming from one stage or accumulating across all of them?

## Canonical examples

- **Paper mills** — pulp → sheet → finishing (page 34)
- **Textile manufacturing** — fiber → yarn → fabric → finishing
- **Chemical industries** — multi-stage reaction and purification
- **Food production** — ingredients → preparation → cooking → packaging
- **Oil processing** — crude → refining → distillation → blended product
