# Product-Market Fit Canvas

## Definition

The Product-Market Fit Canvas is a two-column planning tool that forces explicit alignment between **the customer/market side** (left column) and **the product/service side** (right column). Pereira describes it as a way to capture "all of the many characteristics of the customer as well as the market that you are aiming for when using the product market fit canvas" (page 39). Unlike retrospective metrics, the canvas is a forward-looking design artifact — fillable before launch, during beta, or at any growth inflection.

## Key attributes

- **Two-column structure** — customer on the left, product on the right (page 40)
- **Customer column captures** — typical buyer, goals, why they need it, how they access it, what they need to do with it (page 40)
- **Product column captures** — current alternatives, required components, channel benefit, success measurement (page 41)
- **Time-flexible** — can be used pre-launch, during beta, or at scale (page 39)
- **Forces explicit alignment** — gaps between columns reveal misfit
- **Lighter than Business Model Canvas** — focused only on the fit question, not the full model
- **Iterative** — re-fill at each major product iteration or market shift

## When to use

- Pre-launch: to define target customer and MVP scope (page 39)
- Beta: to refine offering and read the existing market environment
- Pre-pivot: to expose where customer and product columns have diverged
- Repositioning: when launching a new product line into an adjacent market
- Workshop format: as a structured team conversation tool

## When NOT to use

- As a substitute for actual customer interviews — it organizes thinking, not data
- For mature products with established PMF — too foundational
- When the underlying market is unknown — do discovery first, then canvas
- As a one-time artifact — it must be revisited

## Common pitfalls

- **Filling it from assumptions** — without real customer data, both columns become wishful thinking
- **Skipping the channel question** — distribution mismatch sinks otherwise good fits
- **Vague KPIs in the right column** — "what will you measure" must be specific and trackable
- **Treating it as one-and-done** — markets shift; the canvas must be re-filled
- **Filling only one column well** — the value is in the alignment, not in either side alone

## Key metrics

- **Customer clarity score** — qualitative: can your team answer all four left-column questions?
- **Value prop validation rate** — % of target customers who confirm the prop in interviews
- **KPI alignment** — do right-column metrics actually measure left-column outcomes?
- **Channel-to-customer fit** — does the channel reach the typical buyer described?

## Decision questions

- Who is the typical buyer of your product, and what goals do they have? (page 40)
- Why do customers need your product to reach those goals? (page 40)
- How do customers access your product or service? (page 40)
- What must the customer do with it to extract value? (page 40)
- How are customers currently accomplishing their tasks? (page 41)
- Which components does your product require to meet customer needs? (page 41)
- How does the distribution channel benefit from selling your product? (page 41)
- What will you measure to know customers receive real value? (page 41)

## Canonical examples

- **Miro PMF Canvas template** — popular digital version of the framework (referenced in Pereira's sources, page 61)
- **Pre-launch SaaS** — classic use case: fill the canvas before writing the first line of code
- **Beta-phase consumer product** — fill to refine messaging and feature set before GA
