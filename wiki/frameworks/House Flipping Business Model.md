# House Flipping Business Model

## Definition

House flipping is the practice of buying a property — typically distressed, mispriced, or in transition — holding it for a short period (weeks to a year), and reselling it for a profit. Pereira distinguishes two flavors: (1) pure speculation — buy and resell as market conditions move; (2) the dominant model — buy, rehab, resell, where forced appreciation through renovation creates the spread. ATTOM data cited by Pereira put the average US gross flip profit at $67,000 in Q1 2022, but he repeatedly emphasizes that the business is among the riskiest real-estate strategies because every additional day of hold compounds carrying costs (mortgage, taxes, insurance, utilities). It's a high-intensity active business that rewards speed, network, contractor relationships, and disciplined acquisition pricing — not patient capital.

## Key attributes

- **Short hold period** — weeks to ~12 months; longer holds erode profit
- **Active value-add** — rehab is the primary margin engine, not market appreciation
- **Speed over maximization** — time is the binding constraint, not asking price
- **Capital-intensive upfront** — purchase + rehab + holding costs all front-loaded
- **Project-management heavy** — contractor selection, scheduling, code compliance, inspections
- **Acquisition discipline is everything** — anchored by the 70% rule (ARV × 0.7 – rehab cost = max offer)
- **Short-term financing typical** — hard-money loans or HELOCs, since banks rarely fund flips
- **Legal exposure** — undisclosed defects, contractor disputes, title issues create lawsuit risk

## When to use

- Investor has construction / project-management expertise (or a trusted GC)
- Market is appreciating or stable with strong buyer demand for renovated stock
- Investor wants active income on a project basis, not passive rental cash flow
- Sufficient capital reserves to survive an unexpected 60+ day market lag
- Neighborhood comps support a clean ARV with low appraisal risk

## When NOT to use

- Investor lacks rehab skills, contractor network, or capital cushion
- Market is softening — flip exits stall and holding costs crush margins
- Properties with structural / title / environmental issues without commensurate discount
- Investor needs passive income or can't tolerate active project-management workload
- Regulatory or zoning constraints prevent meaningful improvements

## Common pitfalls

- **Inadequate funds** — running out mid-project forces shortcuts or distressed sales
- **Poor time management** — every extra month of holding compounds losses
- **Inexperience** — first-time flippers miscalculate rehab scope and market demand
- **Lack of education** — not knowing zoning, tax, or comp methodology
- **Impatience** — buying the first deal rather than the right deal
- **Skipping the 70% rule** — paying too much erases the margin before work starts
- **Underestimating holding costs** — mortgage + tax + insurance + utility runs 2–3% of purchase price per month
- **Overrenovating** — adding luxury features the neighborhood comp won't support

## Key metrics

- **ARV** (After Repair Value) — projected post-rehab sale price
- **70% rule** — Max purchase price = (ARV × 0.7) – rehab cost
- **Gross profit per flip** — sale price minus all-in cost; ATTOM avg $67k in Q1 2022
- **Holding cost per month** — mortgage + tax + insurance + utilities + HOA
- **Days on market (DOM)** — both for purchase and resale
- **Cycle time** — from acquisition close to resale close (target: 90–180 days)
- **ROI** — gross profit / total cash invested

## Decision questions

- Does this deal pass the 70% rule with margin to spare for surprises?
- Do I have the contractor relationships to hit budget and timeline?
- What's the local DOM, and what's my plan if it doubles?
- Am I capitalized to carry the property an extra 3 months if needed?
- Is the neighborhood appreciating, flat, or softening?
- What's my exit plan B — convert to rental, lease-to-own, wholesale assignment?

## Canonical examples

- **HGTV / Fix-and-Flip ecosystem** — popularized the model in US consumer culture (page 61)
- **Zillow Offers (now defunct)** — institutional flipping; Zillow shut down after $1.7B losses (page 130, 134)
- **Opendoor, Offerpad, Redfin Now** — iBuyer institutionalization of the flip model at scale (see [[iBuyer Business Model]])
- **Knock** — flip + trade-in hybrid (page 147)
- **Saudi/MENA**: traditional flipping is less developed in KSA — the dominant pattern is land assembly + holding for development, executed by **Roshn**, **NHC**, and giga-project SPVs. Individual flipping does occur in Riyadh and Jeddah secondary markets but is informal. As secondary-market transparency grows (via the **Real Estate General Authority** and digitization), flip-style models may emerge
