# Venture Debt Financing

## Definition

Venture debt is loan financing specifically designed for VC-backed startups — provided by specialty venture lenders (Silicon Valley Bank historically, Hercules Capital, TriplePoint), some commercial banks with venture practices, and sometimes by VC firms themselves. Pereira describes it as a complement to equity rounds: it extends runway between equity rounds, minimizes equity dilution, and provides capital structured on more favorable terms than traditional bank debt because the lender underwrites against the startup's most recent equity round and investor backing rather than against cash flow or hard collateral. The typical structure pairs a term loan with warrants (the right to buy equity at the prior round's price), giving lenders equity upside while keeping the headline cost of capital relatively low.

## Key attributes

- **Designed for VC-backed startups** — lender underwrites against investor backing
- **Warrants attached** — small equity upside for lender (typically 1-5% warrant coverage)
- **Lower dilution than equity** — debt portion is non-dilutive
- **Runway extension** — bridges between equity rounds
- **Faster to close** — than full equity round
- **Covenant-light** — relative to conventional bank debt
- **Often follows an equity round** — closes within months of Series A/B/C close
- **Repayment from next round** — typical exit is refinancing at next equity raise

## When to use

- Just-closed equity round and want to extend runway 6-12 additional months
- Bridge to a milestone (revenue, product launch) that will improve next-round valuation
- Capital-efficient growth scenarios where debt makes equity stretch further
- Avoiding a down round by extending runway through market downturn
- Equipment or growth capex where the asset itself has predictable ROI
- When dilution at current valuation is unattractive but the business needs growth capital

## When NOT to use

- Pre-Series A — no equity backing for the lender to underwrite against
- Pre-revenue with no clear path to next round (debt accelerates failure)
- Highly volatile cash flow where covenant trips are likely
- When the warrant coverage cost outweighs the dilution savings
- If the startup might pivot or fail before repayment — debt holders are senior to equity

## Common pitfalls

- Stacking venture debt on top of an unhealthy business — accelerates death spiral
- Underestimating warrant dilution effective cost (sometimes more than headline math)
- Covenant breaches triggering acceleration just when the company needs flexibility
- Misaligned maturity vs next-round timing — debt comes due before next raise closes
- MAC (material adverse change) clauses that lenders invoke in downturns
- Senior security interest that complicates subsequent equity rounds or M&A

## Key metrics

- **Loan amount** — typically 20-35% of last equity round size
- **Interest rate** — typically prime + 2-6%; lower than mezzanine or growth debt
- **Warrant coverage** — typically 1-5% of loan principal in equity warrants
- **Runway extension** — months added to current runway
- **Dilution offset vs equity round** — implied dilution from warrants vs equivalent equity raise
- **DSCR or revenue covenants** — what triggers acceleration

## Decision questions

- Will this debt actually buy us a better next-round valuation, or just delay the problem?
- Can we comfortably service the debt if the next round slips by 6 months?
- What's the warrant dilution math vs raising a smaller equity bridge?
- What covenants are we agreeing to, and how do we monitor them?
- How does the senior lien affect our flexibility on acquisition or future rounds?

## Canonical examples

- **Silicon Valley Bank (SVB)** — historically the dominant venture debt provider until 2023 collapse
- **Hercules Capital, TriplePoint Venture Growth, Trinity Capital** — major specialty venture lenders
- **Bridge Bank, Western Alliance, First Republic (historical)** — bank-affiliated venture debt practices
- **Pereira's note** — VC firms increasingly offering debt alongside equity to portfolio companies (page 24)
- **Saudi context — Sanabil and STV** — regional VCs occasionally structure debt-equity hybrids for portfolio companies; pure venture debt market still developing in MENA
- **Saudi context — Kafalah-backed loans** — government-guaranteed SME lending serves analogous role for non-VC-backed Saudi startups
