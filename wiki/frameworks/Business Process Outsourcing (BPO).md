# Business Process Outsourcing (BPO)

## Definition

Business Process Outsourcing (BPO) is the practice of contracting out essential — but typically non-core — parts of business operations to third-party vendors. Pereira traces it from General Motors under Alfred Sloan in the 1920s (splitting business operations into core vs additional functions to outcompete Ford) through Kodak's 1989 IT outsourcing, widely cited as the first recognized strategic outsourcing event. BPO targets "big picture" operational objectives — cost savings, efficiency, staff upscaling — across routine functions such as administrative work, customer service, facilities, janitorial services, accounting, payroll, and supply chain management. KPO, LPO, RPO, and HRO are all specializations of BPO; the parent practice is defined by its scope (routine, rule-bound tasks) and its purpose (operational efficiency, not specialist expertise).

## Key attributes

- **Routine, rule-bound work** — clear operating guidelines; little need for autonomous judgment (page 37)
- **Non-core to value proposition** — essential to operations but not what customers pay for (page 16, 22)
- **Cost- and efficiency-driven** — historically the dominant purpose; quality and capability are secondary motives (pages 16–17)
- **Low autonomy required** — vendor follows the playbook; oversight is light-touch (page 37–38)
- **Infrequent communication cadence** — clear SOPs mean check-ins are not constant (page 38)
- **Specializations** — KPO (knowledge-heavy), LPO (legal), RPO (recruitment), HRO (HR), ITO (information technology), EPO (engineering)
- **Three delivery proximities** — onshore, nearshore, offshore (see [[Outsourcing Location Strategies]])

## When to use

- Routine, well-defined processes that can be documented in an SOP
- Functions essential to operations but not differentiating (payroll, AR/AP, helpdesk, facilities)
- When in-house unit cost > vendor unit cost + management overhead
- When demand is variable and an internal team would be over- or under-utilized
- When the firm wants to free internal capacity for genuinely differentiating work

## When NOT to use

- Tasks central to the value proposition or brand experience
- Tasks that cannot be clearly defined or specified (page 22)
- When data sensitivity or regulatory exposure outweighs cost savings
- When organizational maturity cannot support vendor governance
- When the function is the firm's primary source of comparative advantage

## Common pitfalls

- Outsourcing tasks that turn out to be core once decomposed
- Underestimating hidden vendor costs and contract lock-in (page 14)
- Eroding in-house capability to the point that bringing the function back becomes infeasible
- Internal friction — employees perceive outsourcing as replacement (page 14)
- Confidentiality and IP leakage (page 14)
- Quality control drift as physical and managerial distance grow

## Key metrics

- **Unit cost per transaction** vs in-house baseline
- **SLA attainment** (uptime, resolution time, throughput)
- **Cycle time** end-to-end across the outsourced step
- **Error/defect rate** and rework cost
- **Vendor concentration / dependency ratio** — % of function in a single vendor
- **Total cost of outsourcing** — vendor fee + governance + transition + exit costs

## Decision questions

- Is this task essential to our value proposition, or only essential to operations?
- Can the task be clearly defined and specified to a third party? (page 22)
- Where is the comparative advantage — ours, or the vendor's?
- What's the true total cost once governance, comms, and exit costs are included?
- Are we creating dependency that we cannot reverse?

## Canonical examples

- **General Motors under Alfred Sloan (1920s)** — split core vs additional functions; outsourced secondary functions to grow share from 12% to 41% (page 15)
- **Eastman Kodak (1989)** — outsourced IT systems; widely cited as the first recognized strategic outsourcing event (page 16)
- **Modern U.S. economy** — over 300,000 jobs outsourced annually; top three categories are accounting, IT, and digital marketing (page 17)
- **64% of companies** outsource software development functions (page 17)
- **2018 small business survey** — 37% outsourced some part of their process; 52% planned to do so next year (page 17)
