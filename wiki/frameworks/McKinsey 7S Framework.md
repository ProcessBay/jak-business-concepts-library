# McKinsey 7S Framework

## Definition

The McKinsey 7S Framework, developed by Tom Peters, Robert Waterman, and colleagues at McKinsey in the late 1970s, holds that organizational effectiveness depends on the alignment of seven internal elements: three "hard" (Strategy, Structure, Systems) and four "soft" (Skills, Style, Staff, Shared Values). All seven are interdependent — change one and the others must adjust, or the organization underperforms. Pereira's framing emphasizes 7S as a diagnostic for whether an organization's vital internal elements are curated to deliver on its strategy: structure gives managers an elevated competitive view, strategy is the brain, systems run the daily workflow, and the four soft elements (skill, style, staff, shared value) determine whether execution actually happens.

## Key attributes

- **Seven interdependent elements** — Structure, Strategy, Systems (hard) + Skills, Style, Staff, Shared Values (soft)
- **Shared Values at the center** — the cultural core that holds the other six in alignment
- **Hard vs soft distinction** — hard elements are easier to identify and change; soft elements decide whether change sticks
- **Alignment is the deliverable** — the framework's value is surfacing misalignment, not prescribing a structure
- **Strategy-execution lens** — explains why strategies fail when structure/staff/style aren't reconfigured
- **Diagnostic, not prescriptive** — like SWOT, identifies the landscape rather than the action
- **Used in M&A and major reorgs** — common tool for diagnosing integration readiness

## When to use

- Before or during a major strategy change (will the org execute it?)
- During post-merger integration to map the two organizations' 7S configurations
- When strategy is clear but execution keeps failing — diagnose which S is misaligned
- During leadership transitions (style + shared values change with new leadership)
- Before a major reorganization to model second-order effects on the four soft elements
- When growing across stages (e.g., startup → scale-up) and the soft Ss need to evolve

## When NOT to use

- For tactical, short-term decisions (use issue-based or operational frameworks)
- As a substitute for strategy itself — 7S aligns to strategy, doesn't create one
- When leadership isn't willing to confront soft-element issues (the analysis becomes performative)
- For pure financial restructuring questions
- When speed matters more than depth — 7S is a slow, conversational tool

## Common pitfalls

- Treating it as a checklist instead of a system (the elements are interdependent)
- Focusing only on hard elements (strategy, structure, systems) and ignoring the soft four
- Confusing "shared values" with a values poster on the wall
- Failing to act on identified misalignment — diagnosis without intervention
- Using 7S without clarifying what the strategy actually is (strategy is one of the Ss; you can't align to a vacuum)
- Mistaking staff (recruiting, training, motivation) for HR processes — 7S "Staff" is about the people themselves

## Outputs / signals of good use

- A map showing which of the 7 Ss are aligned with the chosen strategy and which aren't
- A concrete change agenda for misaligned elements
- Cross-functional consensus on what the organization actually values vs. what it claims to value
- Identified second-order effects of a planned strategy or structure change
- Clarity on whether the organization is currently set up to execute its strategy

## Decision questions

- For each S — is it aligned with our strategy? With each other?
- Where is the largest gap between our espoused shared values and our actual decision-making?
- If we change structure to support strategy X, what happens to staff, skills, and style?
- Which of the soft elements is currently the binding constraint on execution?
- What management style does this strategy require, and do we have it?
- Are our systems built for the strategy we have, or the one we used to have?

## Canonical examples

- **Pereira's structure cascade** — structure can start with a "motto" and run through values, vision statements, focus areas, and goals; concrete structure aids strategic effectiveness (page 69-70)
- **Style as a leadership variable** — "team leaders' management styles also influence the outcome of results" (page 70)
- **Staff as performance multiplier** — Pereira: employees are more effective when in good spirits; motivation and welfare contribute directly (page 70-71)
- **Apple under Jobs vs. post-Jobs** — same hard Ss, materially different soft Ss
- **Post-merger integration failures** — almost always traced to misaligned style + shared values
