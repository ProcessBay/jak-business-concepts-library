# Desirability-Feasibility-Viability

## Definition

The Desirability-Feasibility-Viability framework (originated at IDEO; widely used in innovation and venture design) is a three-lens test for whether a business model or product idea is worth pursuing. Each lens is necessary, none is sufficient. **Desirability** — do customers want this? **Feasibility** — can we build it? **Viability** — can we make money doing it? Pereira's treatment emphasizes that all three must hold for a venture to succeed; missing any one is a kill criterion.

## Key attributes

- **Three lenses** — Desirability (customer), Feasibility (tech/operations), Viability (financial)
- **All three required** — failure on any one = unsuccessful venture
- **Sequential testing** — most teams test desirability first (cheapest to validate), then feasibility, then viability
- **Translates to assumption testing** — each lens generates testable hypotheses
- **Common in lean startup / design thinking** — IDEO popularized; widely adopted across innovation methodologies

## When to use

- Early-stage venture validation
- Innovation portfolio prioritization (rank projects by which lens is most validated)
- Pre-investment due diligence
- Pivoting decisions (which lens is failing?)
- Project kill decisions (one lens fundamentally broken = stop)

## When NOT to use

- Late-stage execution where the model is validated and the question is operational
- For pure brand/marketing decisions where all three lenses are assumed met

## Common pitfalls

- Building on desirability alone without testing viability (popular but unprofitable products)
- Building on feasibility (engineer's pride) without desirability (no one wants it)
- Optimizing viability before validating desirability (efficient delivery of nothing)
- Treating the framework as a one-time exercise rather than ongoing validation

## Key metrics

- # of validated assumptions per lens
- Score per lens (often 1-10 self-assessment)
- Cost of validation per assumption
- Time-to-pivot when a lens fails

## Decision questions

- Have we validated each lens with real customer/market data, or are we assuming?
- Which lens is our weakest, and what's our plan to strengthen it?
- If we had to kill one lens-failure project this quarter, which would it be and why?

## Canonical examples

- **IDEO innovation projects** — the framework's origin context
- **Lean Startup / Steve Blank's customer development** — uses the three lenses implicitly
- **Pereira's framing** — "test assumptions around customers' pain and solution developed" (desirability), "test technology and resources" (feasibility), "predict prices, costs, profits" (viability) (page 8)
