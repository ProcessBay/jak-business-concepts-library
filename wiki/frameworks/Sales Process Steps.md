# Sales Process Steps

## Definition

The Sales Process is the codified, sequential set of stages a sales team follows from first contact with a prospect through close and post-sale handoff. Pereira presents the canonical seven-step model — Prospect → Connect & Qualify → Research → Pitch → Handle Objections → Close → Nurture — alongside common variants (Beyond Business Group's 7-step, RingCentral's 6-step, Zendesk's 5-step). The process is the seller-side counterpart to the buyer's journey: where the journey describes what the buyer is doing, the process describes what the rep does at each stage. A documented sales process matters for three reasons: it lets management measure performance at a stage level (not just total deals), it accelerates onboarding for new reps, and it ensures no transaction is dropped through an undefined handoff. The process must remain flexible — modern buyers do not move through stages atomically — but the discipline of explicit stages, exit criteria, and ownership is what separates predictable revenue from heroic-deal randomness.

## Key attributes

- **Seven canonical stages** — Prospect → Connect/Qualify → Research → Pitch → Handle Objections → Close → Nurture
- **Variants by motion** — 5-step (Zendesk, transactional), 6-step (RingCentral, mid-market), 7-step (enterprise)
- **Each stage has exit criteria** — explicit prospect action that triggers stage advancement
- **Buyer-journey aligned** — process should mirror Awareness/Consideration/Decision stages
- **Stage-level measurement** — entry/exit rate per stage exposes bottlenecks
- **Documented or it doesn't exist** — undocumented process means no diagnosis when targets miss
- **Flowchart visualization** — yes/no branches at decision points
- **Flexible, not rigid** — buyers skip stages and loop back; reps adapt

## When to use

- Any sales team larger than 3 reps (process is the scaling mechanism)
- When onboarding new reps (process accelerates time-to-productivity)
- When forecast accuracy is poor (process gives stage-level signal)
- When deals stall and no one knows why (process exposes the stuck stage)
- During CRM implementation (process drives the pipeline schema)
- After a sales reorg (process re-aligns the team to a shared motion)

## When NOT to use

- Pure founder-led sales below ~$1M ARR (process overhead exceeds value)
- Highly creative/bespoke sales (process becomes constraint, not guide)
- When the process is built to serve the rep, not the buyer (kills deals)
- For motions that genuinely don't repeat (one-off enterprise deals, M&A)

## Common pitfalls

- Designing the process around what reps want to do, not what buyers need
- No defined exit criteria per stage (deals "linger" in stages indefinitely)
- Process built once and never updated as the motion evolves
- Reps gaming stage advancement to inflate pipeline (e.g., pushing into "Proposal" prematurely)
- Forcing every deal through every stage at the same pace (page 153 anti-pattern)
- No flowchart visualization — process exists as text only, no one reads it
- Disconnect between documented process and CRM stages (two sources of truth)
- Treating Nurture as optional (post-close handoff drops the relationship)

## Key metrics

- **Sales cycle length** — total days from Prospect to Close
- **Stage conversion rate** — % advancing from each stage to next
- **Time in stage** — average days at each stage; long stages flag friction
- **Win rate** — Closed-Won / Total Opportunities
- **Pipeline velocity** — (opps × deal size × win rate) / cycle length
- **Stuck-deal rate** — % of opps in a stage > 1.5x average time
- **CAC by stage exit** — where money is spent on deals that don't close

## Decision questions

- Do all reps follow the same documented process, or does each rep do their own thing?
- Are exit criteria defined for each stage, or does advancement happen by rep gut?
- Where do deals stall — and is that stage understaffed, mistrained, or just structurally slow?
- Does our CRM pipeline reflect our written process, or is there drift?
- When was the process last updated? (Motions evolve; processes often don't)
- Is post-close Nurture an explicit stage, or do we drop the relationship at signature?

## Canonical examples

- **Beyond Business Group 7-step** (page 161) — fits multi-stakeholder B2B
- **RingCentral 6-step** (page 162) — splits prospecting into inbound/outbound, no post-close
- **Zendesk 5-step** (page 163) — fastest model, single-contact B2B and repeat-purchase B2C
- **Salesforce Sales Cloud pipeline stages** — most common implementation in the wild
- **HubSpot Sales Hub** — process-driven CRM for inbound-heavy motions
