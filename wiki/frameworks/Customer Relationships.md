# Customer Relationships

## Definition

Customer Relationships is the BMC block that defines the type of interaction a firm establishes with each customer segment — across acquisition, retention, and sales expansion. Unlike Channels (how customers reach you), Relationships are about the *nature* of the engagement: is it self-service, automated, community-driven, personal, dedicated, or co-created? Pereira identifies six relationship categories along a spectrum from low-touch (Self-Service) to high-touch (Co-Creation), and motivates the choice by three goals: customer acquisition, customer retention, and sales expansion.

## Key attributes

- **Three motivations** — Customer Acquisition, Customer Retention, Sales Expansion
- **Six categories** — Self-Service, Automated Services, Communities, Personal Assistance, Dedicated Personal Assistance, Co-Creation
- **Touch spectrum** — from zero human contact (self-service) to deep collaboration (co-creation)
- **Cost vs. value trade-off** — high-touch relationships cost more but enable higher LTV
- **Different per segment** — same firm often runs different relationships for different segments (Apple Genius Bar for premium, online support for budget)
- **Acquisition tactics differ** — content marketing, email, social media; each suits different relationships

## When to use

- Designing the engagement model for a new offering
- Diagnosing why retention is dropping (relationship model may not match customer expectation)
- Tiering customers by lifetime value and offering differentiated relationships
- Building moats — deep customer relationships create switching costs
- Onboarding strategy design — first 90 days set the relationship tone

## When NOT to use

- Pure transactional commodity sales where "relationship" is moot
- When the firm can't afford the operational overhead of high-touch relationships at scale
- As a substitute for product quality — relationships can't compensate for a weak offering

## Common pitfalls

- Promising high-touch relationships then defaulting to chatbots (erodes trust)
- Uniform relationship type across all customers when economics demand tiering
- Underinvesting in retention because acquisition feels more "exciting"
- Confusing communities with paid customer support — communities require active community management
- Failing to update relationships as customers mature (a 5-year customer doesn't need the same onboarding as new)

## Key metrics

- Retention / churn rate
- NPS / CSAT
- Repeat purchase rate
- Customer lifetime value
- Time-to-resolution for issues
- Referral rate (relationship strength signal)
- Cost-to-serve per relationship category

## Decision questions

- What relationship are our customers actually getting vs. what we promise?
- Do high-LTV customers get a different relationship than low-LTV ones?
- Where could we automate without degrading the relationship?
- Where does the customer want high-touch and we're under-serving?
- Are we acquiring, retaining, or expanding — and is our relationship designed for that?

## Canonical examples

- **Zappos** — dedicated personal assistance via 24/7 customer service; loyalty program; relationship as the brand (page 23, in Value Creation source)
- **Apple** — dedicated personal assistance via Genius Bar in-store; community via developer forums; personal via AppleCare (page 181)
- **Netflix** — automated services (algorithmic recommendations); self-service support; no personal touch (page 191)
- **Spotify** — automated personalized recommendations; self-service for users; dedicated relationships with major labels (page 219)
- **Airbnb** — multi-sided: review systems, host community, automated support, personal in disputes (page 166)
- **Google** — predominantly self-service for consumers; dedicated for major advertisers (page 172)
