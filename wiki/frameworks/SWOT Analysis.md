# SWOT Analysis

## Definition

SWOT analysis is a structured strategic diagnosis tool that maps a firm across four quadrants: Strengths (internal advantages), Weaknesses (internal limitations), Opportunities (external favorable conditions), and Threats (external risks). The framework's value is in surfacing the internal-vs-external and positive-vs-negative dimensions in one view, enabling leaders to design strategies that leverage strengths against opportunities and shore up weaknesses against threats. Pereira illustrates this with Nike, which used SWOT to identify strengths in brand and innovation, weaknesses in supply-chain and sustainability, opportunities in global sportswear, and threats from changing consumer preferences and competitors.

## Key attributes

- **Four-quadrant structure** — Strengths × Weaknesses (internal); Opportunities × Threats (external)
- **Diagnostic, not prescriptive** — SWOT identifies the landscape; strategy comes from acting on the map
- **Cross-functional input** — best done with diverse perspectives (sales, ops, finance, customer-facing)
- **Time-bounded** — a SWOT is a snapshot; it ages quickly in fast-moving markets
- **Honest internal assessment required** — weaknesses must be named explicitly or the analysis is theater
- **External lens** — opportunities and threats live in the market, not the org chart

## When to use

- Annual strategic planning
- Before entering a new market or launching a new product
- After a leadership change, M&A event, or major industry shift
- As an early step in a value-driver identification process (per Pereira's CVCM Step 4)
- When the leadership team has diverging mental models of the firm's position

## When NOT to use

- As a substitute for deeper analysis (financial modeling, customer research, competitive intel)
- When the team will produce a SWOT just to check a box and not act on it
- As a quick tool when the question demands quantitative rigor (use Porter's 5 Forces, financial analysis, etc.)
- When the participants haven't done their homework — SWOT amplifies whatever's already in the room

## Common pitfalls

- Listing too many items per quadrant — dilutes focus (5-7 strong items per quadrant is the ceiling)
- Conflating internal weaknesses with external threats (or vice versa)
- Failing to convert the analysis into actions — no follow-through equals wasted exercise
- Letting the dominant voice in the room define each quadrant
- Treating it as a one-time event rather than a periodically refreshed map

## Key metrics

- Number of strategic actions generated per quadrant
- Cross-functional alignment score post-exercise
- % of identified weaknesses with assigned remediation owner
- % of opportunities translated into roadmap items
- Time from SWOT completion to first action

## Decision questions

- For each strength: is this still true, and is it defensible against competitors?
- For each weakness: which one would hurt us most if a competitor exploited it?
- For each opportunity: is it real, timely, and within our capability to capture?
- For each threat: which is closest to materializing, and what's our hedge?
- Where do strengths intersect with opportunities? — that's the offensive strategy.
- Where do weaknesses intersect with threats? — that's the defensive priority.

## Canonical examples

- **Nike** — identified strengths in brand recognition and innovation, weaknesses in supply chain management and sustainability, opportunities in the global sportswear market, threats from competitors and shifting consumer preferences; used the analysis to focus on innovation, sustainability, and brand identity (page 26)
