# BCG Matrix

## Definition

The BCG Matrix (also called the Growth-Share Matrix) is a portfolio-management framework developed by the Boston Consulting Group in 1970. It classifies a firm's products or business units along two axes — relative market share (x-axis) and market growth rate (y-axis) — into four quadrants: **Stars** (high share, high growth), **Cash Cows** (high share, low growth), **Question Marks / Problem Children** (low share, high growth), and **Dogs / Pets** (low share, low growth). Each quadrant implies a distinct strategic action: invest in Stars, milk Cash Cows, evaluate Question Marks selectively, and divest or reposition Dogs. The matrix helps allocate capital across a portfolio rather than choosing strategy for a single product.

## Key attributes

- **Two-dimensional classification** — relative market share × market growth rate
- **Four canonical quadrants** — Stars, Cash Cows, Question Marks, Dogs
- **Circle sizing** — each product drawn as a circle whose size represents its revenue contribution
- **Relative market share calculation** — your product's sales divided by leading rival's sales (so 0.4 means you have 40% of the leader's share)
- **Market growth rate** — current year's sales minus prior year's, divided by prior year (industry-wide, not firm-specific)
- **Cash-flow logic** — Cash Cows fund Stars and selected Question Marks; Dogs free up capital when divested
- **Portfolio balance** — a healthy company has Stars maturing into Cash Cows, with selective Question Mark bets and minimal Dog drag
- **Better for larger firms** — assumes enough relative market share variance for meaningful comparison

## When to use

- **Multi-product portfolio reviews** — annual or semi-annual strategic planning
- **Capital allocation decisions** — where to invest, where to milk, where to divest
- **Early stages of marketing campaign or project planning** (per Pereira)
- **M&A target evaluation** — does the target add a Star, Cash Cow, or another Dog?
- **Identifying which products are draining vs. generating cash**
- **Strategic communication with investors** — visual summary of portfolio health

## When NOT to use

- **Single-product or pre-revenue companies** — no portfolio to map
- **Smaller businesses** — Pereira notes the matrix is "typically better suited for larger businesses where there is enough relative market share for analysis"
- When market share isn't a meaningful proxy for profitability (e.g., niche premium positioning)
- For services where market share is hard to measure or boundaries are blurry
- As a sole decision tool — Pereira lists multiple limitations (see Pitfalls)

## Common pitfalls

- **Two-dimension myopia** — relative market share and growth rate are not the only determinants of profitability or strategic value
- **Ignoring synergies** — one brand can boost another's recognition; matrix treats products as independent
- **Penalizing profitable low-share products** — a niche product can be profitable without high market share
- **Missing small rapid-growth competitors** — the matrix doesn't surface emerging threats that haven't yet reached significant share
- **Assuming Cash Cows are forever** — competitors innovate and erode mature market positions
- **Over-investing in chasing share** — high share is expensive to acquire and doesn't always yield proportional profits
- **Misclassification from sloppy market definition** — defining the market too broadly or narrowly distorts every placement
- **Treating Dogs as automatic divestitures** — some Dogs absorb competitor capacity and indirectly protect Stars

## Key metrics

- **Relative market share** = your sales / largest competitor's sales (x-axis)
- **Market growth rate** = (this year's industry sales − last year's) / last year's (y-axis)
- **Revenue per quadrant** — total revenue concentrated in each quadrant
- **Cash flow per product** — Cash Cows positive, Stars roughly net-zero, Question Marks negative, Dogs variable
- **Portfolio Star-to-Cash-Cow ratio** — measures pipeline health
- **Question Mark conversion rate** — what % of Question Marks successfully become Stars
- **Dog drag** — total resources consumed by Dogs without proportional return

## Decision questions

- Which Cash Cow is funding the rest of the portfolio, and how durable is its position?
- Which Question Marks have realistic paths to becoming Stars vs. Dogs?
- Are we over-invested in Stars to the point of underfunding the next generation of Question Marks?
- Which Dogs should be divested, repositioned, or quietly maintained because they block competitor entry?
- Does our portfolio have enough new Question Marks to keep the Star pipeline filled?
- Is our market definition correct, or are we mis-mapping our true competitive position?

## Canonical examples

- **The Coca-Cola Company portfolio** — Minute Maid and Diet Coke as **Question Marks** (moderate appeal, room to expand); Kinley and Dasani as **Stars** (strong market positions in Europe and U.S. with consistent growth); Classic Coca-Cola as both **Cash Cow** (low growth, impressive share) *and* **Dog** simultaneously (legislation against soft drinks and negative public sentiment reducing sales) — illustrates that a single product can occupy multiple quadrants (page 84)
- **Daimler / Mercedes-Benz** — used as example of properly defining the market when applying BCG (must distinguish passenger vs. luxury sub-markets) (page 91)
- **Automobile industry calculation** — competitor at 25% share, your brand at 10% → relative market share of 0.4 (page 92)
