# Hoshin Kanri

## Definition

Hoshin Kanri (Japanese: 方針管理, "direction management" or "policy deployment") is a strategic-planning method developed in post-war Japan and refined by Toyota and other lean organizations. It aligns the entire enterprise — vertically and horizontally — around 3-5 year breakthrough objectives, which are then cascaded into annual objectives, deployed across teams, and reviewed monthly, quarterly, and annually. Its signature mechanism is the **catchball process**: senior management proposes objectives, middle and team leaders respond with feedback and counter-proposals, and ideas are "thrown back and forth" until the entire organization commits to a coherent plan. Pereira frames Hoshin as the model where "everyone is responsible for fulfilling the business strategy."

## Key attributes

- **Seven-step cycle** — Vision → 3-5 year breakthrough objectives → Annual objectives → Deploy → Implement → Progress reviews (monthly/quarterly) → Annual reviews
- **Catchball process** — iterative top-down/bottom-up dialogue that builds commitment, not just compliance
- **Vertical and horizontal alignment** — every layer and every function moves to the same beat
- **Breakthrough objectives, not incremental targets** — 3-5 year ambitions that require step-change, not optimization
- **Layered review cadence** — weekly by work team, monthly by management, quarterly by owners/investors
- **Annual performance review** — explicit reflection and learning loop after each cycle
- **Originates from lean tradition** — closely linked to PDCA and continuous improvement

## When to use

- In organizations large enough to need vertical-horizontal alignment (typically 100+ employees)
- When breakthrough (not incremental) change is required — entering new markets, product rebuilds, transformation
- When the firm has strong middle management capable of running the catchball process
- For lean/operations-heavy organizations where this tradition fits the culture
- When senior leadership commits to multi-year breakthrough goals, not annual budget cycles
- After cultural change initiatives — Hoshin reinforces shared ownership

## When NOT to use

- In small startups (overhead exceeds benefit; OKRs are lighter)
- When leadership won't honour the catchball process (turns into top-down command)
- In organizations with high middle-management turnover (catchball needs continuity)
- For pure execution shops without strategic ambition
- When the planning cycle is faster than the catchball cycle can support
- In cultures with deep hierarchical deference (catchball requires honest pushback)

## Common pitfalls

- Skipping the catchball — turning Hoshin into directive cascade, losing its commitment-building power
- Setting "breakthrough" objectives that are actually incremental — kills the point
- Too many breakthroughs at once (Toyota convention: 3-5 max)
- No review cadence — Hoshin without monthly/quarterly reviews drifts
- Treating annual review as box-ticking rather than genuine reflection
- Confusing Hoshin with OKRs (overlapping but distinct; Hoshin emphasises breakthrough + catchball; OKRs emphasise quarterly cadence and team-level ambition)
- Pereira's specific risk — the product/middle teams pushing back on infeasibility, and senior management refusing to revise (page 62-63)

## Outputs / signals of good use

- 3-5 breakthrough objectives the entire organization can name
- Annual objectives derived from breakthroughs, with clear owners and milestones
- Documented catchball iterations — evidence of two-way dialogue
- Monthly progress reviews that surface and address blockers
- Annual reviews that produce explicit lessons feeding the next cycle
- Cross-functional initiatives flowing from breakthroughs (not siloed departmental plans)

## Decision questions

- What are our 3-5 year breakthrough objectives? Can every leader name them?
- Have we genuinely run the catchball, or are we cascading orders?
- Where did middle management push back, and did we revise our plan accordingly?
- What's our review cadence, and is it actually changing decisions?
- What did we learn from last year's Hoshin that we're applying this year?
- Are our annual objectives building toward the breakthroughs, or drifting from them?

## Canonical examples

- **Pereira's commercial screen printing example** — industry pivots from small-scale retail to large-scale customers, requiring product rehabilitation; catchball with the product group surfaces timeline infeasibility, leading senior management to modify projections (pages 62-63)
- **Toyota Production System** — the canonical Hoshin Kanri implementation
- **Hewlett-Packard, Texas Instruments** — early Western adopters from the 1980s onward
- **Lean enterprise transformations** — Hoshin is the strategic layer above kaizen and value-stream mapping
