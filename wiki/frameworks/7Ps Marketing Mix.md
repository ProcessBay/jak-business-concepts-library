# 7Ps Marketing Mix

## Definition

The 7Ps Marketing Mix is a framework for designing and analyzing a marketing strategy across seven dimensions: **Product**, **Price**, **Place**, **Promotion**, **People**, **Process**, and **Physical Evidence**. The original 4Ps (Product, Price, Place, Promotion) were developed by E. Jerome McCarthy in 1960 for B2C contexts. Bernard H. Booms and Mary J. Bitner extended the model in 1981 by adding People, Process, and Physical Evidence — recognizing that services and customer experience required dimensions beyond physical-goods marketing. In 2021, Dave Chaffey further adapted the framework for digital strategies. The first four Ps remain the "core" mix; the latter three are added for a thorough competitive audit, particularly for service businesses.

## Key attributes

- **Seven dimensions** — Product, Price, Place, Promotion, People, Process, Physical Evidence
- **Original 4Ps vs. extended 7Ps** — Booms and Bitner added the service-specific dimensions in 1981
- **Service-orientation** — extension reflects shift from physical goods to service-dominant marketing
- **Each P decomposes** — every P breaks into sub-elements (e.g., Product = features, packaging, warranties, after-sale support)
- **Audit tool** — used to systematically benchmark competitors P-by-P
- **Versatile across industries** — applicable to B2C, B2B, services, and digital
- **Foundation for differentiation** — gaps in any P relative to competitors signal where to differentiate

## When to use

- **New venture without clear value proposition** — use 7Ps to draft positioning by mapping competitor mix
- **Strategy revamp or new product launch** — identify differentiation opportunities across all seven Ps
- **Shifts in consumer trends or new technology** — re-audit each P to adapt
- **Comprehensive competitor audit** — go P-by-P against rivals
- **Customer perception deep-dive** — use the model to identify which P drives perception in your category
- **Service businesses** especially benefit from People, Process, Physical Evidence dimensions

## When NOT to use

- **Pure-play digital products** with no service component may underuse People and Physical Evidence
- When the depth of analysis required exceeds what a checklist-style framework provides
- For strategic questions about *industry structure* (use Porter's Five Forces) or *portfolio allocation* (use BCG)
- When the team treats it as a fill-in-the-blanks exercise rather than a comparative analytical lens
- Without competitor data — the 7Ps shines when comparing your mix against rivals, not just describing your own

## Common pitfalls

- **Listing without analyzing** — filling in each P with features doesn't generate insight; comparison does
- **Ignoring the latter three Ps** — People, Process, Physical Evidence are often where service differentiation lives
- **Treating Place as only physical location** — for digital businesses, Place includes UX, channel architecture, app store presence
- **Underestimating Process** — order fulfillment, customer support flow, and onboarding are frequently the actual differentiator
- **Mistaking Promotion for advertising** — Promotion spans content, SEO, social media, PR, events, sponsorships, partnerships
- **Treating Physical Evidence as decoration** — warranties, certifications, reviews, store layout all signal quality
- **Static analysis** — competitors' mixes evolve; refresh periodically

## Key metrics

- **Product** — feature parity score, customer satisfaction, NPS
- **Price** — price-position index relative to competitors, price elasticity, discount frequency
- **Place** — distribution coverage, channel partner count, geographic reach, e-commerce conversion
- **Promotion** — share of voice, content engagement, paid vs. organic mix, brand mention sentiment
- **People** — customer service rating, employee satisfaction (proxy for service quality), Glassdoor ratings
- **Process** — order-to-delivery time, support response time, abandonment/return rates
- **Physical Evidence** — warranty coverage, certifications held, review ratings, store/website quality scores

## Decision questions

- Across each P, are we ahead, at parity, or behind our key competitors?
- Which P represents our strongest differentiation today?
- Which P is our weakest, and what's the cost to close that gap?
- Are the three service Ps (People, Process, Physical Evidence) where category leaders actually win?
- Are we under-investing in any P that customers report as a decision driver?
- How would a digital-native entrant attack our mix?

## Canonical examples

- **Restaurants and Physical Evidence** — store layout, staff uniforms, printed menus, and online reviews all act as physical evidence signaling quality experience (page 101)
- **Manufacturing and warranties** — product warranties and quality certifications serve as physical evidence demonstrating trustworthiness (pages 101-102)
- **Process competitive moves** — automation to speed production, express shipping to reduce delivery time (page 102)
- **Digital adaptation (Chaffey 2021)** — application of 7Ps specifically to online platforms (page 96)
