# OKR Framework

## Definition

OKR (Objectives and Key Results) is a goal-setting framework that pairs a qualitative, aspirational **Objective** with three to five quantitative, time-bound **Key Results** that measure progress toward it. Pereira traces the lineage to Andy Grove at Intel in the 1970s, then to John Doerr — an early Google investor — who introduced OKRs to Larry Page and Sergey Brin in 1999; Google has used them at scale ever since (pages 11, 108-109). The framework's value is not the goal taxonomy itself but the operational discipline it forces: alignment of effort to strategy, focus on a small set of priorities per cycle, transparency across the org, and accountability through frequent check-ins (pages 12-13).

## Key attributes

- **Two components** — qualitative Objective (the "what" and "why") + 3-5 measurable Key Results (the "how we'll know")
- **Quarterly default cadence** — short enough to course-correct, long enough to ship meaningful work (page 12)
- **Public and transparent** — at Google, every employee can see every OKR; cascading creates line-of-sight from CEO to IC (pages 109-110)
- **Stretch by design** — ambitious targets that push beyond comfort; full attainment is not the goal (pages 17-18, 109)
- **Decoupled from compensation** — at Netflix and Google, OKRs are a learning/focus tool, not a performance-review weapon (page 106)
- **Iterative** — set, check in regularly, score at cycle-end, refine for the next (pages 44-47, 93-95)
- **Lineage** — Andy Grove (Intel, "iMBOs") → John Doerr → Google (1999) → mainstream adoption post-2010

## When to use

- Organizations that need to align distributed teams behind a small set of strategic priorities
- High-growth startups and scaleups where direction shifts quarterly
- Cultures that can tolerate (and reward) public failure on ambitious goals
- Teams that have outgrown ad-hoc planning but resist heavy performance-management bureaucracy
- After a strategy refresh, when leadership needs to translate vision into operational focus

## When NOT to use

- Mature, slow-moving organizations where annual planning already works well
- Cultures where missing a target carries punitive consequences (sandbagging will follow)
- Pure execution environments with no need for prioritization (e.g., assembly-line operations)
- When leadership won't model the practice — top-down enforcement without top-down participation fails
- During acute crisis when survival horizon is shorter than the OKR cycle

## Common pitfalls

- Confusing OKRs with task lists or KPIs (Pereira distinguishes: KRs are outcomes, not activities; page 27)
- Tying OKR attainment to bonuses, which incentivizes sandbagging (pages 78-79)
- Setting too many Objectives (3-5 max per level)
- Cascading rigidly from top-down without bottom-up input (kills ownership; pages 53-55)
- Skipping the scoring/retrospective step at cycle end (loses learning)
- Treating OKRs as static; not adjusting when context shifts (pages 44-47)
- Vanity Key Results that measure activity (e.g., "publish 10 blog posts") instead of outcome (e.g., "grow organic traffic 30%")

## Key metrics

- **Objective completion rate** — % of Objectives where avg KR score ≥ 0.7 (Google convention)
- **Key Result scoring average** — 0.0-1.0 scale per KR at cycle end
- **Alignment score** — % of team OKRs that explicitly link to a parent OKR
- **Cycle velocity** — time from cycle kickoff to first measurable KR movement
- **Adoption rate** — % of teams/individuals with current-cycle OKRs published

## Decision questions

- What strategic priorities, if achieved this quarter, would matter most? (Limit to 3-5.)
- For each Objective, what measurable outcomes prove we got there?
- Are our KRs outcomes or just activities in disguise?
- Where are OKR conflicts between teams, and which one wins?
- Is leadership willing to publish their own OKRs first?
- Will we score honestly at cycle end, even when scores are low?

## Canonical examples

- **Intel under Andy Grove** — origin of the framework, called "iMBOs" before Doerr renamed them (page 11)
- **Google** — adopted 1999 via John Doerr; every employee's OKRs are visible company-wide (pages 108-110)
- **Netflix** — used OKRs to scale from DVD-by-mail to global streaming; explicitly decoupled from perf review (pages 104-106)
- **Airbnb** — used OKRs through hypergrowth; objective "create a world where anyone can belong anywhere" cascaded into measurable listings/bookings KRs (pages 100-101)
- **Amazon** — customer-centric OKRs drove Prime, same-day delivery (pages 106-108)
- **Patagonia** — sustainability OKRs (carbon reduction, supply-chain chemicals) made public for accountability (pages 97-99)
- **Toyota** — paired OKRs with kaizen; cascaded "improve production efficiency while maintaining quality" into team-level cycle time and defect KRs (pages 102-104)
