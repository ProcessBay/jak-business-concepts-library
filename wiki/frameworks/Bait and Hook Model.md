# Bait and Hook Model

## Definition

The Bait and Hook (also Razor and Blades) model sells a base product at low margin — sometimes at a loss — to lock customers into purchasing high-margin consumable refills, accessories, or follow-on services for the lifetime of the product. Coined after Gillette's pioneering use with razors and disposable blades, the model is a variant of penetration pricing combined with strategic customer lock-in. Pereira frames it as one of the most enduring biz-model archetypes, dominant in printers, coffee systems, mobile carriers, and gaming.

## Key attributes

- **Two-product economics** — the "bait" (base product) acquires customers; the "hook" (refills) extracts profit over time
- **Lock-in mechanism** — proprietary interfaces or formats force customers to buy refills from the same firm
- **Penetration pricing on bait** — base product priced below cost to maximize installed base
- **High-margin recurring revenue** — refills carry 50-80% gross margin typically
- **LTV economics** — profitability depends on bait→blade attach rate × blade purchase frequency × blade margin
- **Vulnerability to third-party** — generic refills (off-brand cartridges) can break the model

## When to use

- Consumable product categories where refills are necessary for use
- Hardware-software ecosystems (printers, gaming, coffee)
- Categories where switching costs can be engineered (proprietary interfaces, DRM)
- Subscription transitions — moving from one-time purchase to recurring revenue model
- B2B equipment-as-a-service where service contracts replace traditional sales

## When NOT to use

- One-shot product purchases with no consumables
- Highly commoditized refill markets where third-parties undercut your refills
- When customers can switch easily between platforms
- When competitive law restricts tying products (regulatory risk)

## Common pitfalls

- Setting bait price too low — losing money before customers buy enough refills to break even
- Overcharging on blades — drives customers to third-party refills (HP printer ink famously vulnerable)
- Failing to defend against compatible-refill competitors
- Misjudging refill purchase frequency in the LTV model
- Letting the lock-in feel exploitative — brand damage

## Key metrics

- Bait attach rate (% who buy blades)
- Average refill purchases per customer per year
- Blade gross margin
- Customer lifetime value (LTV)
- Time-to-payback on bait sale
- Third-party refill market share (the threat metric)

## Decision questions

- What's the average blade purchase frequency that makes our bait economics work?
- How defensible is our lock-in against generic refills?
- At what bait price does customer acquisition slow unacceptably?
- Are we extracting too much margin on blades and driving customers to alternatives?

## Canonical examples

- **Gillette** — the original; razors at low margin, blades at high margin (page 34)
- **HP printers** — printers below cost; ink cartridges at high margin; vulnerable to third-party ink (page 34)
- **Nestlé Nespresso** — coffee machines accessibly priced; pods at premium (page 35)
- **Mobile carriers** — subsidized phones; high-margin service plans (page 35)
- **Gaming consoles** (Sony, Microsoft, Nintendo) — consoles often sold near cost; games at full margin
