# Productized Services

## Definition

A productized service is a service offering deliberately packaged as if it were a product: fixed scope, fixed price, fixed deliverable, fixed timeline, often sold off a public price page. Pereira's "differentiated pricing" with "multiple pricing tiers or packages" (page 31), his bundling and packaging framework (pages 67-79), and HubSpot's tiered "Starter / Professional / Enterprise" structure (pages 87-90) all converge on the same insight applied to services: the further you push services toward a product shape, the more leverage you unlock — shorter sales cycles, comparable pricing, repeatable delivery, productivity gains from templates, and the option to sell without senior-partner involvement. Productized services are how consultancies, agencies, and freelancers escape the utilization ceiling that traps hourly-billing firms.

## Key attributes

- **Named offer with a fixed scope** — "Brand Sprint," "GTM Audit," "Pricing Refresh" — not "consulting"
- **Listed price** — public or semi-public, removing per-deal price negotiation
- **Repeatable delivery method** — templates, playbooks, frameworks reused across clients
- **Fixed timeline** — typically days or weeks, not months
- **Decoupled from senior-partner time** — junior staff can deliver with senior QA
- **Productivity margin** — each repetition gets cheaper to deliver but stays at list price
- **Marketable like a product** — landing pages, testimonials, before/after artifacts
- **Lower-friction sale** — shorter cycle than custom proposals; comparison-shoppable
- **Building block for ladders** — productized offers often funnel into retainers or larger engagements

## When to use

- The firm has done the same engagement 5-10+ times with predictable structure
- Sales drag from bespoke proposals is killing throughput
- Junior talent is underutilized because everything requires senior scoping
- The firm wants to enter a new segment without rebuilding go-to-market each time
- Top-of-funnel offer that earns trust before larger engagements
- Internal IP, templates, or AI tooling create delivery leverage that bespoke pricing can't monetize

## When NOT to use

- Each client's situation is genuinely unique (custom strategy at high stakes)
- The firm's brand depends on bespoke / "tailored to you" positioning
- Productization would commoditize a service whose premium depends on perceived custom work
- Internal delivery hasn't matured enough to be templated reliably
- Buyer category demands a custom proposal (large enterprise procurement)

## Common pitfalls

- **Premature productization** — packaging before delivery is repeatable creates margin disasters
- **Underpricing the productized offer** — list price below value because "it's just a package"
- **Scope creep within the package** — without strict in-scope/out-of-scope language, productized becomes bespoke
- **Senior-talent dependency persists** — package exists on paper but every delivery still pulls in a partner
- **Cannibalizing bespoke margin** — productized offer eats high-margin custom work
- **Treating productized as the whole business** — best used as a wedge or funnel, not as the firm's only model
- **No upgrade path** — productized buyers should ladder into retainers / strategic engagements
- **Stale offers** — packages need refresh cycles; what was novel in year 1 is table-stakes in year 3

## Key metrics

- **Gross margin per delivery** — should improve with each repetition as templates mature
- **Delivery cost per unit** — hours × loaded rate per package delivered
- **Conversion rate** — % of inquiries that become a sale (productized = higher than bespoke)
- **Sales cycle length** — days from first contact to signed engagement
- **Repeatability rate** — % of deliveries that completed within scoped time
- **Upgrade conversion** — % of productized buyers that ladder into retainer or custom engagement

## Decision questions

- Have we delivered this engagement enough times to template the delivery confidently?
- Is the list price anchored on value, or did we default to cost-plus when packaging?
- Are our productized offers a wedge into bigger engagements, or are they the destination?
- What's the upgrade ladder — productized → retainer → custom?
- Does our productized offer commoditize work we used to sell custom at higher margin?

## Canonical examples

- **HubSpot Starter / Professional / Enterprise** — Pereira's worked case (pages 87-90); productized tiers attract small businesses then ladder them into higher tiers
- **Salesforce Essentials / Professional / Enterprise / Unlimited** — productized service tiers (page 37)
- **Brand sprint / design sprint agencies (IDEO, Google Ventures)** — fixed-scope, fixed-timeline named offers
- **Boutique consulting "playbooks"** — named methodology sold as a packaged engagement
- **WP Curve / Design Pickle / Bench.co** — fully productized monthly service subscriptions
- **TechSolutions tiered SaaS plans** (Pereira composite) — Starter / Pro / Enterprise with productized scope (page 34)
