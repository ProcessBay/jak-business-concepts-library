# Drop-Shipping Model

## Definition

Drop-shipping is an e-commerce business model where the retailer doesn't own or hold inventory — when a customer orders a product, the order is forwarded to a supplier who ships directly to the customer. The retailer's margin is the difference between what the customer pays and what the supplier charges. Pereira's archetypal example is Wayfair, which offers 18 million items from 7,000+ suppliers and doesn't handle 95% of the goods sold through its websites. The model dramatically reduces capital requirements but introduces dependence on supplier quality, shipping times, and inventory accuracy.

## Key attributes

- **No inventory ownership** — retailer doesn't buy or store goods
- **Supplier-direct fulfillment** — supplier ships to end customer
- **Low capital requirement** — no warehouse, no inventory financing
- **Margin via spread** — retailer captures difference between price and supplier cost
- **Wide assortment possible** — millions of SKUs viable since no holding cost
- **Quality and delivery dependent** — retailer is exposed to supplier reliability
- **Lower margins than traditional retail** — typically 10-30% vs. 30-50% for inventory retail

## When to use

- Bootstrapping e-commerce without capital for inventory
- Testing product-market fit before committing to inventory
- Scaling assortment beyond what inventory could support (long-tail products)
- Markets where suppliers have shipping infrastructure already
- Categories with low return rates and stable supplier quality

## When NOT to use

- Products where customer experience depends on packaging/branding the supplier can't replicate
- High-margin premium categories where inventory control is critical
- Categories with high return rates (drop-ship returns are operationally complex)
- When customers expect fast shipping (drop-ship adds days)
- B2B where bulk inventory and immediate fulfillment matter

## Common pitfalls

- Supplier quality problems landing on retailer's reputation
- Inventory accuracy issues (overselling phantom stock)
- Long shipping times eroding customer satisfaction
- Margin compression as more competitors adopt the model
- Hidden costs (returns logistics, customer service for supplier issues)
- Lack of differentiation (anyone can drop-ship the same supplier)

## Key metrics

- Supplier count and reliability scores
- Average Order Value (AOV)
- Gross margin (drop-ship typically 10-30%)
- Order-to-delivery time
- Return rate
- Inventory accuracy (% of orders that ship successfully without stock issues)

## Decision questions

- Can we sustain our brand experience when 95% of products ship directly from suppliers?
- What's our differentiation if competitors use the same supplier base?
- How will we handle returns and customer service for products we never touched?
- What's our supplier reliability score, and what's the financial cost of a bad supplier?

## Canonical examples

- **Wayfair** — 18M+ items from 7,000+ suppliers; doesn't handle 95% of goods sold (page 41)
- **Shopify-powered drop-shippers** — many small e-commerce businesses use Shopify + AliExpress
- **Amazon FBA hybrid** — some Amazon sellers use drop-ship-like models
