# Lean Six Sigma

## Definition

Lean Six Sigma combines Lean's waste elimination with Six Sigma's variation reduction into a unified process-improvement methodology. Pereira treats it primarily in the healthcare context, where it is used to streamline patient flow, reduce medical errors, improve operational efficiency, optimize care delivery, and tighten supply chain management. The methodology operates through the **DMAIC** cycle — **Define, Measure, Analyze, Improve, Control** — supported by tools such as Value Stream Mapping, Kaizen Events, Root Cause Analysis, Statistical Process Control (SPC), and the 5S Methodology. Lean reduces non-value-added work; Six Sigma reduces defects and variation; together they target both efficiency and quality.

## Key attributes

- **DMAIC cycle** — Define, Measure, Analyze, Improve, Control (the structured problem-solving spine)
- **Dual focus** — waste elimination (Lean) plus variation reduction (Six Sigma)
- **Data-driven decision-making** — statistical analysis underpins improvement choices
- **Five principles** — customer focus, process efficiency, continuous improvement, data-driven decisions, teamwork
- **Tool stack** — VSM, Kaizen Events, RCA, SPC, 5S, process mapping
- **Cross-functional team execution** — teams pull together domain experts plus methodology specialists
- **Defects-per-million-opportunities (DPMO)** target — Six Sigma targets ~3.4 DPMO

## When to use

- Healthcare operations — Pereira's canonical context (patient flow, medical errors, supply chain)
- Manufacturing process improvement with both waste and quality dimensions
- Service operations with measurable cycle time and quality metrics
- Organizations with data infrastructure to support statistical analysis
- Mature processes where incremental optimization will pay off
- Combined with project management methodologies as a quality discipline overlay

## When NOT to use

- Early-stage products where the problem is "find product-market fit", not "reduce defects"
- Small teams without statistical analysis capacity or training
- Processes still being designed (Lean Six Sigma optimizes existing flows, not greenfield design)
- Cultures that resist data-driven challenge to expert judgment
- Short-cycle projects without time to run DMAIC properly

## Common pitfalls

- Adopting tools (VSM, RCA, SPC) without DMAIC discipline — losing the structured spine
- Optimizing local metrics that harm system-level performance
- Treating Six Sigma as a certification exercise (belts) rather than a problem-solving discipline
- Ignoring the human factor — Pereira emphasizes teamwork and collaboration as core
- Inadequate Control phase — improvements drift back without sustained measurement
- Misdiagnosing the problem as variation when it's actually capability

## Key metrics

- **Defects Per Million Opportunities (DPMO)** — Six Sigma's core quality measure
- **Sigma level** — process capability score (6σ ≈ 3.4 DPMO)
- **Cycle time** — process duration per unit
- **Process variation** (standard deviation of outputs)
- **First-pass yield** — % completed without rework
- **Cost of poor quality** — financial impact of defects and rework
- **Customer satisfaction / patient safety scores** in healthcare contexts

## Decision questions

- Is our problem primarily waste, variation, or both? (Determines Lean vs. Six Sigma vs. LSS emphasis.)
- Do we have the data infrastructure to Measure and Analyze rigorously?
- Will leadership sustain the Control phase, or will improvements drift back?
- Have we engaged the people actually doing the work in DMAIC?
- Is this problem important enough to justify a full DMAIC cycle?
