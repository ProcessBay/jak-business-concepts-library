# Co-Marketing Agreement

## Definition

A co-marketing agreement is a strategic partnership type where two or more companies collaborate to promote each other's products or services through shared marketing efforts — advertising campaigns, events, content, or cross-promotional activities. Pereira positions co-marketing as one of the most lightweight partnership structures, requiring no new legal entity, and increasingly prevalent in the era of digital marketing and e-commerce (e.g., an e-commerce platform partnering with a payment gateway to offer exclusive discounts). The objective is to mutually benefit from each other's customer bases, increase brand visibility, and drive traffic or sales without the structural overhead of a joint venture or equity tie-up.

## Key attributes

- **No new entity** — agreement-based, not equity-based; lighter than a JV
- **Shared marketing assets** — campaigns, content, events, channels co-produced
- **Audience exchange** — each partner exposes the other to their customer base
- **Brand co-association** — credibility transfers in both directions (and both risks)
- **Defined campaign scope** — typically project- or program-bounded, not perpetual
- **Cross-promotional mechanics** — referral programs, co-branded landing pages, joint webinars

## When to use

- Two non-competing brands serve overlapping audiences with complementary offers
- One partner has audience reach the other lacks (and vice versa)
- A specific campaign, launch, or seasonal moment justifies coordinated noise
- Brand credibility from a reputable partner accelerates trust with new segments
- Lightweight collaboration is preferred — no time for JV or equity arrangements

## When NOT to use

- When one partner's brand carries reputational risk that could contaminate the other
- When audiences barely overlap — joint promotion wastes both sides' marketing budget
- When attribution and credit-sharing aren't agreed upfront — measurement disputes follow
- When one partner has materially weaker brand equity — value flows one-way
- When the offer-to-audience fit isn't tested — co-marketing amplifies a weak message

## Common pitfalls

- Vague success metrics — neither party knows whether the campaign worked
- Asymmetric effort — one team drives the campaign while the other coasts
- Brand-guideline friction — co-branded assets get stuck in design back-and-forth
- Audience cannibalization — partners compete for the same wallet after the campaign
- Skipping data-sharing terms — attribution disputes erode trust mid-campaign
- One-off mindset — failing to plan repeat or follow-on campaigns wastes the relationship-building cost

## Key metrics

- Reach lift from co-branded vs. solo channels
- Co-branded CAC vs. solo CAC
- Referral attribution and conversion rate
- Joint pipeline generated (B2B)
- Brand lift / awareness gains (B2C)
- Campaign ROI for each partner separately

## Decision questions

- Do our audiences genuinely overlap, or are we hoping they do?
- Who measures what, and how do we agree on credit for shared conversions?
- Whose brand carries more risk in this co-association — and is the other party comfortable?
- What's the next campaign after this one? (If "none," the ROI bar is much higher.)
- Are creative and approval workflows fast enough not to kill the moment?

## Canonical examples

- **McDonald's + Uber Eats** — Co-branded marketing campaigns, joint promotions, and integrated menu within the Uber Eats app to drive food-delivery adoption (pages 108-110)
- **Spotify + Uber** — Cross-promotional campaigns where Uber offered exclusive Spotify playlists and Spotify ran campaigns encouraging Uber trials (pages 111-112)
- **E-commerce platform + payment gateway** — Pereira's archetype: exclusive discounts that drive traffic and sales for both (page 20)
