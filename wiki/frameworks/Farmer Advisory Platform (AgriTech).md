# Farmer Advisory Platform (AgriTech)

## Definition

A Farmer Advisory Platform is a digital service through which an Agritech firm delivers agronomic expertise — pest identification, seed selection, climate prediction, planting timing, crop nutrition — to farmers, typically over a mobile app. Pereira notes the platform itself is cheap to scale (each additional farmer adds near-zero marginal cost after build-out), but monetization is the central design problem: most smallholders, who are the largest target segment, cannot or will not pay a subscription. The dominant resolution is a two-sided model — free advisory to the farmer, paid B2B data products (aggregated farm data, agronomic intelligence) sold to input suppliers, financial institutions, and processors. A paid premium tier sits on top for the minority of farmers who will pay.

## Key attributes

- **Digital delivery** — mobile-first, often SMS or simple app to work on low-end devices
- **Asymmetric monetization** — free-to-farmer, paid by data buyers (B2B2C structure)
- **Near-zero marginal cost** — once built, each added farmer barely raises opex
- **Trust compounds** — once farmers trust the advice, viral referral within rural communities accelerates growth
- **High-touch content** — pest ID, seed selection, agronomic timing, climate, market prices
- **Data is the product** — aggregated farmer data is the asset commercialized to agri-input firms, banks, insurers
- **Optional premium tier** — paid layer (extra modules, deeper analytics) for the willing-to-pay minority

## When to use

- Smallholder-dominated markets where farmers cannot afford SaaS subscriptions
- Geographies with strong mobile penetration but weak agronomy extension services
- When you have (or can build) a data-buyer side — input suppliers, lenders, insurers, processors hungry for farm-level data
- When your team has genuine agronomic depth (poor advice destroys trust irreversibly)
- Markets where banks, insurers, or input suppliers struggle to find/qualify farmers (your data solves their CAC)

## When NOT to use

- Markets dominated by large commercial farms with in-house agronomists
- Geographies without a data-buyer side (no one to sell aggregated data to)
- When you cannot guarantee advice quality (one bad season destroys the brand)
- Regulated markets where farm data privacy laws prevent commercialization
- When trust signals (local language, on-the-ground field officers) are unaffordable

## Common pitfalls

- Defaulting to a subscription model on smallholders who will not pay — model dies on monetization
- Selling data without farmer consent (regulatory and trust risk)
- Treating advisory as an app-only play (best models layer field officers + WhatsApp/SMS + app)
- Underestimating the cost of building data-buyer side (B2B sales is a separate motion)
- Failing to localize (advice that works in Punjab fails in Kano)
- Optimizing for active users vs. agronomic outcomes (LTV gets you funded; outcomes keep you alive)

## Key metrics

- **Active farmers** (DAU, MAU); engagement per advisory interaction
- **ARPU** (low on farmer side, the real ARPU is per data buyer)
- **Data revenue per farmer** (the unit economic that actually funds the model)
- **Free-to-paid conversion** (premium tier uptake)
- **CAC** (per farmer acquired; per data buyer landed)
- **LTV** (long, because farmers stay for many seasons once trust forms)
- **Advisory engagement rate** (advice consumed / advice pushed)
- **Churn / dormancy** (farmers who stop logging in)

## Decision questions

- Who actually pays — the farmer, or someone else who benefits from farmer data?
- Do we have agronomic depth, or are we slapping AI on weak content?
- What does the data-buyer side look like, and can we serve both motions?
- How do we acquire farmers in markets where digital CAC is unreliable?
- What is our data-consent posture, and is it survivable under tightening privacy regulation?
- Can we deliver in the local language, in the local context?
