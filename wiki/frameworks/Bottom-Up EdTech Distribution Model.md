# Bottom-Up EdTech Distribution Model

## Definition

The bottom-up EdTech model gives the product to teachers and students inside schools for free, then monetizes the same students at home through parent-paid subscriptions. The school acts as a no-cost distribution channel and a lead-generation engine: teachers introduce and validate the product in class, students develop usage habits, and parents are converted via home extension features (more practice problems, advanced content, progress reporting, gamified rewards). Pereira frames it: "Schools qualify as lead generators that lead to consumer adoption … usage of the app in school is free, while parents have to pay for home usage." This pattern sidesteps the long institutional procurement cycle entirely — but it pushes acquisition costs into product virality, classroom UX, and parent-conversion mechanics.

## Key attributes

- **Free for schools, paid for home** — the institutional buyer is bypassed; teachers approve and use the free tier
- **Teacher as evangelist** — adoption depends on teachers recommending and assigning the product
- **Parent as paying customer** — the conversion event happens outside school, usually with the student as advocate
- **PLG mechanics required** — the product must drive its own conversion without a sales team
- **Lower CAC than top-down** — schools effectively donate distribution
- **Equity tension** — students from lower-income families can't convert, creating a two-tier experience
- **Slow revenue ramp** — most users stay free; monetization compounds over years as the habit deepens
- **Network effects within classrooms** — once one classroom adopts, peer pressure / homework requirements pull in others

## When to use

- Consumer-grade product with clear in-classroom AND at-home use cases
- Subjects where home practice is natural (language learning, math drilling, reading)
- Markets where institutional procurement is broken or too slow
- When you have strong product virality and conversion mechanics
- B2C economics work — parents in the target market will pay $5-20/month

## When NOT to use

- Pure institutional workflows (LMS, SIS, gradebook) where there's no home use case
- Markets where parents won't pay for educational supplements
- Products requiring intensive teacher training (the free tier needs to be self-serve)
- Subjects where home-school crossover is weak (lab science, group projects)
- Where ad-supported or sponsored models are politically blocked in classrooms

## Common pitfalls

- Building a school product so good no parent ever needs to pay (free tier eats the paid tier)
- Building a home product so different that classroom use doesn't drive conversion
- Ignoring equity optics — perceived as exploiting children to extract parent dollars
- Underestimating data-privacy obligations (FERPA / COPPA / PDPL still apply even when the school doesn't pay)
- Letting cash burn outrun the slow ramp; free-user economics can break before paid converts
- Failing to give teachers tooling — if the teacher's free version is bad, the parent conversion engine dies

## Key metrics

- **Classroom adoption rate** — % of teachers in target schools who actively use the product
- **School-to-home conversion** — % of student users who get a parent account at home
- **Parent paid conversion** — % of home accounts that upgrade to paid
- **Viral coefficient (K-factor)** — students/teachers each user brings in
- **CAC** — including unpaid teacher-evangelism cost
- **ARPU (home)** — paid parent ARPU
- **Free user count** — top-of-funnel; matters for fundraising narratives even before monetization

## Decision questions

- Does our product have a genuine home use case, or are we forcing it?
- Will teachers actually evangelize, or are we delusional about classroom enthusiasm?
- Can we get to paid conversion before runway runs out?
- How do we handle the equity optics — what's our free-forever path for students who can't pay?
- Are we compliant with student-data regulation even though the school isn't a contracting party?

## Canonical examples

- **Duolingo (Schools)** — free in classrooms, parents pay Super Duolingo at home
- **Khan Academy** — free everywhere, but funded by sponsors rather than parent conversion (the philanthropic variant)
- **Prodigy Math** — free for teachers, parent paid membership unlocks home features
- **ClassDojo** — free communication tool for teachers, monetizes parents via at-home content (ClassDojo Plus)
- **Quizlet** — free flashcards; paid Quizlet Plus removes ads and unlocks advanced features
- **Noon Academy (Saudi Arabia)** — free study groups, paid premium tutoring sessions
