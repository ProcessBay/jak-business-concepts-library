# Triple Bottom Line

## Definition

The Triple Bottom Line expands the definition of value creation beyond financial performance to include social impact (people) and environmental impact (planet) alongside profit. Pereira frames it as the fourth essential factor when creating value-creation models: businesses must consider their social and environmental impact and adopt sustainable practices, contribute to communities, and promote responsibility — not as charity but as part of how they create durable value. Patagonia is the canonical example: commitment to sustainability has built a strong brand identity and loyal customer base; Unilever has set goals like 100% renewable energy by 2030 and reusable/recyclable plastic packaging by 2025, achieving both environmental impact and cost savings.

## Key attributes

- **Three integrated dimensions** — People (social), Planet (environment), Profit (financial) — all measured
- **Long-term vs. short-term tension** — sustainability investments often pay back over years, requiring patient capital
- **Stakeholder-broadened** — accountability extends to communities, employees, environment, not just shareholders
- **Operational integration** — must be embedded in operations (supply chain, energy, packaging), not bolted on
- **Brand multiplier** — TBL credibility increasingly drives consumer choice, especially among younger cohorts
- **Cost-saving potential** — many sustainability initiatives reduce waste and operating cost (Unilever example)

## When to use

- Long-term strategic planning where 5+ year horizons are real
- B2C markets where customers actively weigh ethics in purchase decisions
- Highly regulated industries where environmental compliance is escalating
- When attracting purpose-driven talent matters (recruiting/retention lever)
- When customer or investor pressure on ESG is mounting

## When NOT to use

- As pure marketing ("greenwashing") without operational follow-through — backfires badly when exposed
- In markets where customers genuinely don't care (rare and shrinking, but exists)
- When financial survival is acute — TBL requires capital to invest
- As a substitute for product-market fit — TBL doesn't rescue a weak offering

## Common pitfalls

- Greenwashing: claiming sustainability without changes in operations
- Treating TBL as a CSR add-on instead of integrating it into the core business model
- Measuring only the easy dimensions (carbon footprint) and ignoring social impact
- Failing to communicate the TBL story to customers and investors who would reward it
- Optimizing for ESG ratings (which can be gamed) instead of real impact

## Key metrics

- People: employee engagement, voluntary turnover, diversity metrics, supply-chain labor practices audit
- Planet: carbon footprint, % renewable energy, waste-to-landfill, water usage, packaging recyclability %
- Profit: revenue, gross margin, ROI on sustainability investments
- Integrated: ESG rating (MSCI, Sustainalytics), B Corp certification, sustainability-certified revenue share

## Decision questions

- Are we genuinely changing operations or just marketing it?
- Which TBL investment would have the highest cost-saving AND brand return?
- Are we measuring all three dimensions, or just the financial one?
- What's the proof point we'd offer a skeptical customer who suspects greenwashing?
- Where do our customers' values actually intersect with sustainable practice?

## Canonical examples

- **Patagonia** — promotes sustainable business practices, supports environmental protection initiatives, creates high-quality environmentally-friendly outdoor apparel; built strong brand identity and loyal customer base (pages 31, 38, 41, 48)
- **Unilever** — set ambitious goals: 100% renewable energy use by 2030 and reusable/recyclable/compostable plastic packaging by 2025; reduces environmental impact while creating cost savings (page 49)
- **Salesforce** — 1-1-1 pledge (1% of equity, 1% of profits, 1% of employee time to charitable causes), building strong brand identity around social responsibility (page 49)
- **IKEA** — sustainable materials and packaging-optimized flat-pack design that simultaneously reduces costs and appeals to environmentally conscious consumers (page 53)
