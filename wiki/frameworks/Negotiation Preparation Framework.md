# Negotiation Preparation Framework

## Definition

Pereira's preparation framework is a five-block discipline that determines the outcome of a negotiation long before the parties meet. The blocks: (1) **Define objectives** — primary goal, secondary goals, prioritization, SMART specificity, tangible and intangible outcomes, anticipate the counterparty's objectives; (2) **Conduct research** — subject matter, counterparty identity, market conditions, prior negotiations, leverage points, potential challenges; (3) **Assess the other party** — decision-makers, organizational context, interests and motivations, negotiation style, cultural factors, strengths and weaknesses, likely tactics; (4) **Build the negotiation team** — required skills, defined roles, diversity, internal and external expertise, communication norms, training, contingency planning; (5) **Set parameters and constraints** — objectives, non-negotiables, internal/external constraints, acceptable alternatives, relationship dynamics, decision protocols, timeframes, communicate-and-revisit. The framework's core claim: negotiation power is mostly built before the room, not in it. Skipping prep is the single most common cause of unfavorable outcomes.

## Key attributes

- **Five blocks** — objectives, research, counterparty assessment, team, parameters
- **Pre-room work dominates** — most leverage is built before negotiation starts
- **Bilateral perspective** — anticipate counterparty's objectives, tactics, constraints
- **Non-negotiables defined upfront** — clarity prevents in-room concession drift
- **Team and roles assigned** — lead negotiator, SMEs, legal, finance, relationship manager
- **Iterative** — parameters revisited as new information emerges
- **Contingency planning** — alternative paths if primary objectives blocked

## When to use

- Before any consequential or multi-issue negotiation
- When counterparty is unfamiliar or stakes are material
- In cross-cultural or international deals where assumptions are riskier
- When multiple stakeholders or decision-makers exist on either side
- When deal will be referenced or precedent-set for future negotiations

## When NOT to use

- Trivial transactions where prep cost exceeds value at stake
- Pure repeat transactions on standard terms with known counterparty
- True emergencies where action speed dominates (but even then, minimal prep beats none)
- When prep becomes procrastination — overprep can delay action

## Common pitfalls

- Skipping prep because the deal "feels simple"
- Researching counterparty's positions but not interests
- Failing to model the counterparty's BATNA and likely tactics
- Defining objectives vaguely ("get a good deal") instead of measurably
- Confusing non-negotiables with strong preferences — too many "non-negotiables" produce stalemate
- Assigning team roles loosely so decision authority is unclear in the room
- Treating prep as one-time work instead of revisiting as new info emerges

## Key metrics

- **Prep hours invested** — vs. expected deal value
- **Information gaps closed** — vs. opening list of unknowns
- **Scenarios modeled** — number of paths walked through
- **Team alignment score** — pre-negotiation agreement on objectives and roles
- **BATNA strength** — confidence-weighted value of best alternative
- **Counterparty profile completeness** — coverage of decision-makers, interests, style, history

## Decision questions

- What is my primary objective, and what are my secondary fallbacks?
- What do I know — and not know — about the counterparty?
- What is my BATNA, and what is theirs?
- Who is on my team, what role do they play, and who decides what in the room?
- Where are my non-negotiables, and have I drawn them tightly enough to hold without producing stalemate?
- What are the three most likely paths this negotiation could take, and am I prepared for each?
