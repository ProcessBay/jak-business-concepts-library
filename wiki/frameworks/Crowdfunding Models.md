# Crowdfunding Models

## Definition

Crowdfunding raises capital for a venture from a large number of contributors via online platforms. Pereira distinguishes four primary types, each with different value exchanges: (1) Reward-based — backers receive a product, perk, or experience; (2) Donation/Charity — backers receive no return, motivated by cause; (3) Debt/Peer-to-Peer Lending — backers loan money expecting repayment with interest; (4) Equity — backers receive equity stake in the venture. Crowdfunding platforms typically take 3-10% of funds raised as commission.

## Key attributes

- **Four primary models** — Reward, Donation, Debt (P2P Lending), Equity
- **Platform-mediated** — Kickstarter/Indiegogo for reward; GoFundMe for donation; LendingClub for debt; SeedInvest for equity
- **All-or-nothing vs flexible** — many platforms require funding goal hit before charging backers
- **Community-building bonus** — successful campaigns yield pre-sold customers AND brand advocates
- **Marketing-as-fundraising** — campaigns double as product launches
- **Platform take rate** — typically 3-10% of funds raised + payment processing

## When to use

- Validating market demand for a product before production (reward-based)
- Funding social/charitable causes with broad community support (donation)
- Raising debt capital outside traditional banks (P2P lending)
- Pre-IPO startups seeking small-investor equity (equity crowdfunding, post-JOBS Act)
- Building community around a creative project, brand, or cause

## When NOT to use

- Funding routine operations (crowdfunding is one-shot; not sustaining)
- When you can't dedicate full marketing effort to the campaign
- When the product won't be ready to ship at the promised timeline (reward-based pitfall)
- For products that need expensive testing/regulation (consumer fatigue with delays)

## Common pitfalls

- Underestimating fulfillment cost in reward-based campaigns (shipping perks can erode margins)
- Over-promising on delivery timelines (Kickstarter is full of "delayed indefinitely" projects)
- Insufficient marketing — assuming the platform alone drives backers (it doesn't)
- Choosing wrong model (equity for early consumer product is overkill)
- Failing to deliver post-funding (reputation damage)

## Key metrics

- Funding goal attainment rate
- Average pledge size
- Backer count
- Time to fund (or % of goal at day 7)
- Post-campaign customer retention (do backers become repeat customers?)

## Decision questions

- Which of the four crowdfunding types fits our value exchange?
- Can we deliver on commitments at the funding level we're targeting?
- Is the campaign primarily about funding, or about community-building?
- Have we budgeted for fulfillment AND for post-campaign customer support?

## Canonical examples

- **Reward** — Kickstarter (page 78), Indiegogo (page 78) — Pebble watch, Oculus VR (pre-FB acquisition)
- **Donation** — GoFundMe (page 51), Charity: Water campaigns
- **Debt (P2P Lending)** — LendingClub (page 128), Prosper, Funding Circle
- **Equity** — SeedInvest, Republic, Wefunder (page 109)
