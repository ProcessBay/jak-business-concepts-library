# PRINCE2

## Definition

PRINCE2 (Projects in Controlled Environments) is a structured project management methodology developed by the UK Office of Government Commerce (OGC), now owned by AXELOS. Pereira describes it as built on **seven principles**, **seven themes**, and **seven processes**: principles guide the methodology (continued business justification, learning from experience, defined roles, managing by stages, managing by exception, focus on products, tailoring); themes are knowledge areas applied throughout (business case, organization, quality, plans, risk, change, progress); processes are the activity steps (starting up, initiating, directing, controlling a stage, managing product delivery, managing stage boundaries, closing). PRINCE2 Agile extends the framework to incorporate Scrum, Kanban, and Lean Startup techniques inside its governance scaffolding.

## Key attributes

- **Seven principles, seven themes, seven processes** — the 7-7-7 mnemonic structure
- **Continued business justification** — kill projects whose business case no longer holds
- **Manage by stages** — break project into approval-gated stages, not one long execution
- **Manage by exception** — escalate only when tolerances are breached
- **Product focus** — define what gets delivered, not just what activity happens
- **Tailorable** — explicitly designed to be scaled and adapted to project size and context
- **PRINCE2 Agile variant** — integrates Scrum/Kanban delivery inside PRINCE2 governance

## When to use

- UK public sector and government projects (where PRINCE2 is often mandatory)
- Large enterprise programs needing strong governance and stage-gating
- Regulated industries requiring documented decision trails
- Multi-vendor projects where role clarity prevents accountability gaps
- When continued business-case validity must be re-checked at every stage
- As PRINCE2 Agile — when Agile delivery needs enterprise governance overlay

## When NOT to use

- Small projects where the methodology overhead exceeds the project itself
- Startup or innovation contexts where business case is genuinely unknown until tested
- Teams that need autonomy and self-organization (governance can feel suffocating)
- Organizations unwilling to invest in PRINCE2 training and certification
- Cultures that interpret documentation requirements as bureaucracy rather than discipline

## Common pitfalls

- Documentation overload — Pereira flags the heavy artifact load as a primary criticism
- Rigid governance applied to projects that needed flexibility — failure to actually tailor
- Implementing PRINCE2 ceremonies without the principles underneath (cargo-cult governance)
- Resource intensity — requires dedicated PM team that may not be justified for smaller projects
- Adoption resistance from teams used to lighter methods
- Integration friction between PRINCE2 governance and Agile delivery teams (addressed by PRINCE2 Agile but not automatically)

## Key metrics

- **Business case validity** — re-confirmed at every stage gate
- **Stage gate pass rate** — % of stages passing review on first attempt
- **Exception count** — number of escalations beyond defined tolerances
- **On-time / on-budget stage delivery** — variance per stage
- **Scope variance** — deviation from baselined product descriptions
- **Lessons learned captured** — knowledge retained for future projects
- **Role clarity score** — stakeholder ability to name decision owners

## Decision questions

- Does the project's complexity and stakeholder count justify PRINCE2's overhead?
- Are we actually going to tailor it, or apply it rigidly out of fear?
- Will continued business justification be honored as a project-kill criterion, or just paperwork?
- Do we need PRINCE2 Agile to bridge governance and Agile delivery?
- Are we investing enough in training so the team understands principles, not just ceremonies?

## Canonical examples

- UK public sector projects — PRINCE2's heartland, owned by AXELOS (page 82)
- Large IT and infrastructure programs across European enterprises
- Government IT modernization projects internationally
- PRINCE2 Agile combining PRINCE2 governance with Scrum/Kanban/Lean Startup delivery (page 84)
- Pereira's PM conclusion names PRINCE2 alongside Waterfall, Agile, and Scrum as a top-four framework (page 155)
