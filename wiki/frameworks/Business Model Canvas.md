# Business Model Canvas

## Definition

The Business Model Canvas (BMC) is a strategic management tool that maps a business onto a one-page visual with nine essential blocks, showing how value is created and delivered. Developed by Alex Osterwalder and Yves Pigneur (Business Model Generation, 2010), the canvas splits into customer/external factors on the right side (Customer Segments, Value Propositions, Channels, Customer Relationships, Revenue Streams) and internal/operational factors on the left (Key Activities, Key Resources, Key Partners, Cost Structure), with Value Propositions as the bridge. Pereira positions it as the foundational strategic-design tool for startups and established firms alike — focused enough to fit on one page, flexible enough to iterate as the business evolves.

## Key attributes

- **One-page visualization** — entire business model fits on a single canvas, enabling fast comprehension and iteration
- **Nine integrated blocks** — Customer Segments, Value Propositions, Channels, Customer Relationships, Revenue Streams, Key Resources, Key Activities, Key Partners, Cost Structure
- **Right side = customer-facing** — segments, propositions, channels, relationships, revenue (external)
- **Left side = operational** — activities, resources, partners, costs (internal)
- **Value Propositions = the bridge** — at the center, connecting customer needs to internal operations
- **Iterative** — designed to be modified, tested, and refined; not a one-time exercise
- **Concise vs traditional plans** — replaces 30-page business plans with a digestible visual
- **Scientifically structured** — each block is testable against evidence and data

## When to use

- Founding a new business and defining the model before building
- Validating assumptions in an existing model before scaling
- Pitching to investors when you need to convey the model in 5 minutes
- Mapping a competitor's model to find gaps or differentiation opportunities
- Pivoting — using the canvas to compare current model against proposed pivot
- Aligning a leadership team on the operating model

## When NOT to use

- For deep strategic analysis (BMC doesn't address competition, history, or ecosystem position)
- As a substitute for financial modeling (it shows structure, not P&L viability)
- For execution planning (BMC says WHAT the model is, not HOW to operate it day-to-day)
- In stable mature businesses where the model is well-understood and the question is operational
- As a one-time deliverable to file in a slide deck — its value is in iteration

## Common pitfalls

- Filling out blocks without supporting evidence — wishful thinking dressed as strategy
- Treating it as a static document rather than a hypothesis to test
- Confusing Customer Segments (groups of people) with Customer Personas (specific archetypes within a segment)
- Ignoring the right-side/left-side balance — over-engineering operations before validating customer demand
- Skipping the Cost Structure block until late, leading to financially unviable models
- Mistaking "completed canvas" for "validated business model"

## Key metrics

- Time to complete a first canvas (target: 1-2 hours for a workshop)
- Block-level evidence score (each block backed by data: yes/no)
- Hypotheses tested per quarter
- Canvas iterations over time
- Cross-functional alignment after workshop (do all leaders describe the model the same way?)

## Decision questions

- For each block, what's our evidence — or is it a hypothesis we still need to test?
- Where is the strongest validation? Where is the weakest?
- Could a competitor write a similar canvas and what would distinguish ours?
- Which block, if it failed, would collapse the model?
- What does the canvas reveal about our true bottleneck?
