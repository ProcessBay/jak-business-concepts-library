# Linear vs Exponential Business Model

## Definition

Pereira frames the strategic choice every founder confronts as a binary between two opposed model logics. The **linear (pipe) model** invests in inputs to produce a finite product, sells it through controlled channels, and grows one customer at a time — targeting a steady ~10% improvement in profits or costs per cycle. The **exponential model** prioritizes 10x growth via technology leverage, network effects, and platform/ecosystem architecture, often releasing many products into many territories simultaneously. Neither model is universally superior; the right choice depends on the category, the founder's risk tolerance, the available capital, and whether a 10x technology lever realistically exists in the value chain.

## Key attributes

- **Linear: input-output pipe** — buy materials, make product, sell in one place, repeat (page 22)
- **Linear: customer-by-customer growth** — slow, steady, 10% gains, lower risk (page 23)
- **Linear: brand ownership upside** — full control over IP, story, and unit-level margins (page 25)
- **Exponential: technology leverage** — software, automation, data, and platforms multiply effort (page 16)
- **Exponential: 10x or nothing** — incremental improvements do not produce exponential adoption (page 32)
- **Exponential: continuous product/market launches** — first-mover advantage in many shots (page 23)
- **Exponential: higher capital and risk** — failures are catastrophic at the larger scale of operations (page 28)
- **Exponential: pairs with lean methodology** — fast iteration, fail-fast learning loops (pages 23-24)

## When to use

**Choose linear when:**
- Your product is intrinsically physical/artisanal and the brand premium depends on craft control
- You want to build a defensible single brand with strong IP you may sell later
- Capital is limited and you cannot fund the bet-the-company race to scale
- The category does not have meaningful network effects or platform dynamics
- Founder goals are sustained profitability and personal wealth more than world-changing scale

**Choose exponential when:**
- The problem affects a mass-market global audience
- Software, data, or network leverage can plausibly produce 10x improvement
- Competitors are already platform-based — table stakes for survival
- You have access to risk capital and a high-tolerance team
- An incumbent industry is asset-heavy and ripe for orchestration

## When NOT to use

- Forcing an exponential design on a category where it does not naturally fit produces under-monetized platforms with no liquidity
- Choosing linear in a category being disrupted by platforms can leave you stranded as the addressable market shrinks beneath you
- "Exponential" added as a buzzword without genuine technology leverage produces a linear business with platform overhead — worst of both worlds

## Common pitfalls

- **Picking the model without examining the category** — model fit depends on the structure of the market, not on founder preference
- **Confusing growth rate with model type** — a linear business can grow fast; an exponential business can stall. Model ≠ outcome
- **Underestimating linear's resource drain** — research cited that acquiring a new customer costs 5x retaining an existing one (page 26)
- **Single-product fragility in linear** — if your one product fails, the business fails (page 26)
- **Quality erosion in exponential** — speed and breadth often hurt customer satisfaction (page 27)
- **Wishful-growth assumptions in exponential** — markets crash, supply chains break; exponential math assumes continuous compounding (page 27)

## Key metrics

- **Growth rate** — linear targets ~10% improvement; exponential targets 10x
- **Time to market** — linear allows longer cycles; exponential demands first-mover speed
- **Contribution margin** — linear scales costs with revenue; exponential approaches ~100% marginal margin (page 34)
- **Capital intensity** — linear can bootstrap; exponential typically requires investor capital
- **Risk exposure** — linear concentrates in one product; exponential distributes across many products/territories but at greater absolute scale (page 28)

## Decision questions

- Is the value we create primarily atoms or bits?
- What does our cost structure look like at 10x revenue — does it scale linearly or sub-linearly?
- Where is the network effect in our category, if any, and who is best positioned to capture it?
- Can we genuinely deliver a 10x improvement, or are we deluding ourselves with a 20% one?
- What is our appetite for risk and our access to capital — does it match the model we are choosing?
- Is the incumbent's moat physical or informational? Informational moats are vulnerable to ExOs; physical ones are harder.

## Canonical examples

- **Linear: Apple iPhone marketing approach** — single hero product, brand ownership, premium positioning (page 25)
- **Linear: a US craft whiskey brand** — buy grain and water, produce, sell in one country (page 22)
- **Linear: a single-product Shopify store** — direct, controllable, but fragile (page 26)
- **Exponential: Airbnb** — orchestrated rooms, no inventory, mass market (page 30)
- **Exponential: Uber** — orchestrated drivers, multi-segment platform (page 72)
- **Exponential: Amazon, Facebook, Snap, Slack** — technology + alternative value propositions in one or more BMC blocks (page 30)
