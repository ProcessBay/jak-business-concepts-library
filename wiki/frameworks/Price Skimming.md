# Price Skimming

## Definition

Price skimming launches a new product at a high initial price to extract maximum revenue from early adopters willing to pay a premium, then systematically lowers the price over time to capture progressively price-sensitive segments. Pereira calls it "creaming" — taking the cream off the top of the demand curve. It is the canonical strategy for innovative products with high R&D recovery needs, short windows of pricing power, and a clear curve of decreasing willingness-to-pay across customer segments.

## Key attributes

- **High initial price** — captures early-adopter consumer surplus
- **Planned price decay** — staged reductions over the product life
- **Maximizes early margin** — recovers R&D investment fast
- **Segments customers by willingness-to-pay** — over time, not at one moment
- **Requires real differentiation** — must justify premium initially
- **Vulnerable to competitive entry** — generics, clones erode the high-price window
- **Opposite of penetration** — share comes later, margin comes first

## When to use

- Innovative products with little or no direct competition initially
- Categories with clear early-adopter segments (gadgets, AAA games, prestige pharma)
- High R&D or fixed-cost recovery needs
- Patent-protected or first-mover positions with a defensible window
- Products where staged feature releases justify price tiers over time

## When NOT to use

- Commoditized markets with immediate competitive substitutes
- Network-effect businesses where share matters more than margin (penetrate instead)
- Categories with weak differentiation that can't justify the initial premium
- When fast competitive entry will collapse the high-price window before payback
- Price-sensitive markets with high elasticity even at launch

## Common pitfalls

- Setting the initial price too high — fails to ignite the early-adopter wedge
- Lowering price too slowly — competitors enter and capture the next tier
- Lowering too fast — early adopters feel burned, brand damage
- Confusing skimming with permanent premium (skimming is a curve, not a position)
- No plan for the next price tier or segment — momentum stalls
- Forgetting that competitors learn — second-generation clones price aggressively

## Key metrics

- **Margin per unit** (very high at launch, declining over time)
- **Early adopter revenue** (% of lifetime revenue captured in first 6-12 months)
- **Price elasticity by segment** (informs each price step down)
- **Time-to-competitive-entry** (the window of pricing power)
- **Demand curve analysis** — `Elasticity = %ΔQuantity / %ΔPrice` (page 53)

## Decision questions

- Do we have a real innovation or differentiation that justifies the launch premium?
- How long is our pricing-power window before substitutes appear?
- Do we have segments at decreasing willingness-to-pay we can ladder down to?
- Can our early adopters tolerate seeing the price drop in 12 months?
- What's our R&D recovery target, and does skimming get us there in the window?

## Canonical examples

- **Apple iPhone (2007)** — premium launch, systematic release of more affordable models for additional segments (page 436)
- **Sony PlayStation, Xbox** — high launch price, decline as console matures (page 437)
- **Pharmaceuticals** — high launch price during patent window, generics collapse it later (page 438)
- **Macy's high-low** — full season price, deep discount at end-of-season to clear inventory (page 76)
- **Hotels** — peak-season premium, off-peak markdowns (page 76)
