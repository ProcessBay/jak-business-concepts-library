# Lean Startup Methodology

## Definition

Lean Startup is a business-innovation methodology that develops and launches new products with minimal resources to test whether the underlying business model works — *before* committing to elaborate plans that might turn out to be unworkable. Pereira frames it as the parent philosophy that gave rise to the MVP: it is "better to create a product and get it out into the marketplace as soon as possible, even if it isn't perfect," then iterate based on customer feedback. The methodology built on Steve Blank's **Customer Development Model**, an approach where founders talk to customers early and often as the cheapest, most effective way to learn what they actually need. Eric Ries codified the synthesis around the central tools of MVP, validated learning, and the Build-Measure-Learn loop.

## Key attributes

- **Hypothesis-driven** — every business plan is treated as a set of assumptions to test, not facts
- **Customer Development at the core** — the firm leaves the building to learn from real customers
- **MVP as the testing instrument** — minimum viable product is the primary tool for hypothesis testing
- **Build-Measure-Learn as the operating loop** — continuous iteration over big-bang launches
- **Validated learning is the unit of progress** — not features shipped, not lines of code
- **Pivot or persevere** — explicit decision points based on data
- **Innovation accounting** — tracking learning, not just outputs

## When to use

- Early-stage startups with high uncertainty about market need or solution
- Corporate innovation/intrapreneurship where speed-to-learning matters more than predictability
- New product lines in established companies where market response is uncertain
- Transformations of business model (e.g., Intuit's shift from shrink-wrap software to SaaS used MVP discipline, page 17-18)
- Categories where customer behavior cannot be reliably predicted from interviews alone

## When NOT to use

- Execution-mode businesses where the hypothesis is already validated (just scale)
- Regulated industries where shipping the "minimum" is illegal (medical, aviation, finance)
- Hardware/infrastructure with long, irreversible build cycles
- When organizational politics will punish a documented "failed" experiment
- Categories where the brand cannot survive an obviously rough launch

## Common pitfalls

- **Using MVP as cover for low quality** — managers stretch the definition to ship bad products fast (page 113)
- **Skipping Customer Development** — building without talking to users contradicts the foundation
- **Vanity-metric tracking** — followers, downloads, likes that don't tell you if the model works (page 52)
- **Pivot fatigue** — pivoting on every weak signal, never giving the strategy time to play out
- **Persevere bias** — never pivoting because of founder ego or sunk cost
- **Confusing Lean Startup with cheap** — it's about validated learning per dollar, not just spending less
- **MoSCoW abuse** — stakeholders cramming >60% into "must-have", killing the "minimum" in MVP (page 113)

## Key metrics

- **Validated learning per cycle** — the meta-metric
- **Cycle time** — Build → Measure → Learn duration
- **Customer interview velocity** — qualitative input flowing in
- **Actionable metrics** — conversion, activation, retention, NPS
- **Pivot decisions made (and time-to-decision)**

## Decision questions

- What is the riskiest assumption in our business model right now?
- What's the cheapest experiment that would falsify it?
- Are we measuring learning, or just measuring activity?
- Do we have a pre-committed threshold for pivot vs persevere?
- Is the team rewarded for shipping features, or for resolving hypotheses?

## Canonical examples

- **Dropbox** — couldn't show the product to investors, made an explainer video MVP, got 70,000 signups in a day → validated demand before building (page 17)
- **Intuit** — adopted Lean Startup / MVP discipline to transition from shrink-wrap desktop software to SaaS, testing each business-model change incrementally (page 17-18)
- **Airbnb** — Brian Chesky and Joe Gebbia validated peer-to-peer rental by hosting air-mattress guests in their own apartment (pages 72, 79)
- **Zappos** — Nick Swinmurn manually photographed and shipped shoes from local stores before any inventory commitment
- **Buffer** — Joel Gascoigne's smoke-test landing page validated demand before code existed (page 80)
- **Spotify** — explicitly used build-measure-learn iteration cycles from launch through scale (page 106)
