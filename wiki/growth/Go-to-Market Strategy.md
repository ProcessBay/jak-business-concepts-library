# Go-to-Market Strategy

## Definition

A Go-to-Market (GTM) strategy is a comprehensive plan that outlines how a company will bring a product or service to market and reach its target customers. It encompasses the entire customer journey: from market analysis and product positioning, through sales and marketing execution, to customer success and retention. Pereira: "A GTM strategy is not just a marketing plan or a sales plan — it's a holistic approach that aligns product, marketing, sales, and customer success around a common target."

## Key attributes

- **Integrated** — product, marketing, sales, and customer success aligned
- **Segment-specific** — different customer segments require different GTM motions
- **Channel-defined** — clear choice of distribution and sales channels
- **Metrics-driven** — success measured by customer acquisition and revenue
- **Iterative** — tested, measured, and refined based on data

## GTM methodologies

### Sales-Led GTM
**What:** Sales team drives customer engagement, lead generation, and conversion.

**Best for:** Complex, high-touch, enterprise solutions requiring consultative selling.

**Key components:**
- Market research and segmentation
- Sales strategy development
- Sales training and enablement
- Lead generation and prospecting
- CRM implementation
- Continuous feedback loop

**Pros:** Personalized engagement, relationship building, high deal values
**Cons:** Resource intensive, dependence on individual reps, longer sales cycles, limited scalability

### Product-Led GTM
**What:** Product itself is the primary driver of acquisition, conversion, and expansion.

**Best for:** Self-service SaaS, freemium models, viral products.

**Key components:**
- Product design and usability
- Free or freemium model
- In-product onboarding
- Self-service support
- User analytics and feedback
- Viral loops and referral programs

**Pros:** Lower CAC, faster time-to-value, scalable, viral potential
**Cons:** Requires excellent UX, limited for complex products, lower initial deal values

### Hybrid GTM
**What:** Combines sales-led and product-led approaches.

**Best for:** Most SaaS companies — start product-led, add sales for enterprise.

**How it works:**
- Free/trial tier for self-serve discovery
- Product-qualified leads (PQLs) passed to sales
- Sales team focuses on expansion and enterprise

## The GTM framework

| Component | Key Questions |
|---|---|
| **Market Analysis** | Who is the target market? What are the trends? How big is the opportunity? |
| **Customer Segmentation** | Which segments have the highest need? Which are most accessible? |
| **Product Positioning** | What is the unique value proposition? How do we differentiate? |
| **Pricing Strategy** | What pricing model aligns with value and segment? |
| **Distribution Channel** | How do customers want to buy? Direct, indirect, or hybrid? |
| **Sales Model** | Sales-led, product-led, or hybrid? |
| **Marketing Strategy** | What channels? What messaging? What content? |
| **Customer Success** | How do we onboard, support, and retain? |
| **Metrics & KPIs** | What are we measuring? What does success look like? |

## When to use

- Launching a new product or service
- Entering a new market or geography
- Pivoting the business model
- Scaling from early adopters to mainstream market
- Fundraising (investors expect a clear GTM plan)

## When NOT to use

- Before product-market fit is validated (premature scaling)
- As a static document (GTM must evolve with market feedback)
- In isolation from product development (GTM and product are interdependent)

## Common pitfalls

- **Product-market fit assumption** — launching GTM before validating demand
- **One-size-fits-all** — using the same GTM for all segments
- **Channel mismatch** — selling enterprise through self-serve, or SMB through field sales
- **Ignoring customer success** — acquiring customers but not retaining them
- **Lack of metrics** — not measuring what works and what doesn't
- **Founder-led sales forever** — not building a repeatable sales process

## Decision questions

- Who is our ideal customer profile (ICP), and where do they congregate?
- What is the simplest version of our GTM that could work?
- Should we start sales-led or product-led?
- Which channel has the lowest CAC for our target segment?
- How do we measure GTM success in the first 90 days?
- What is our expansion revenue strategy after initial sale?
- How does GTM change as we move from startup to scale?

## Canonical examples

- **Slack** — Product-led GTM with viral team invites
- **Salesforce** — Sales-led GTM for enterprise CRM
- **HubSpot** — Hybrid: free tools for discovery, sales for enterprise expansion
- **Zoom** — Freemium product-led with viral meeting invites
