# Distribution Channels and Partnerships

## Definition

Distribution channels are the pathways through which products and services reach end customers. The choice between **direct distribution** (company-controlled: own sales force, e-commerce, retail stores) and **indirect distribution** (intermediary-dependent: resellers, distributors, marketplaces, affiliate partners) shapes cost structure, margin profile, brand control, and speed of market penetration. Partnerships extend distribution by leveraging another organization's existing customer relationships, geographic presence, or technical capabilities. A well-designed channel strategy aligns product complexity, target customer preferences, and company resources.

Pereira: "The right channel is the one your target customer already trusts and uses—not the one that is easiest for your company to build."

## Key attributes

| Factor | Direct Distribution | Indirect Distribution |
|---|---|---|
| **Control** | High (brand, pricing, messaging) | Lower (shared with partner) |
| **Margin** | Higher (no intermediary cut) | Lower (partner commissions/fees) |
| **Speed to market** | Slower (build from scratch) | Faster (leverage partner reach) |
| **Capital requirement** | Higher (sales team, infrastructure) | Lower (partner bears costs) |
| **Customer proximity** | High (direct feedback) | Lower (filtered through partner) |
| **Scalability** | Linear with investment | Potentially exponential with partner network |

**Channel partner types:**
- **Distributors** — buy inventory, resell to retailers or end users
- **Value-Added Resellers (VARs)** — add services, integration, or customization
- **System Integrators** — embed product into larger client solutions
- **Affiliates** — performance-based referrals
- **OEMs** — bundle product into their own hardware/software

## Channel partner selection criteria

1. **Market expertise and alignment** — deep understanding of target segments
2. **Complementary capabilities** — fills gaps in technical support, local service, or vertical knowledge
3. **Geographical coverage** — presence where the company is not
4. **Financial stability** — ability to invest in sales and marketing
5. **Track record** — proven history with similar products
6. **Objective alignment** — shared goals and incentive structures

## When to use

**Direct distribution:**
- Complex products requiring education and implementation
- High-value transactions where margin justifies sales team
- Brand control is strategically important
- Customer feedback is critical to product iteration

**Indirect distribution:**
- Entering new geographies where local relationships matter
- Scaling faster than internal hiring allows
- Product is standardized and does not require heavy support
- Target market is fragmented and hard to reach directly

**Partnerships:**
- Technical integrations that expand product utility
- Co-marketing to access new customer segments
- Risk-sharing for large or uncertain opportunities

## When NOT to use

- **Indirect for unproven products** — partners will not invest in selling something with no track record.
- **Direct in highly fragmented markets** — cost per acquisition becomes prohibitive.
- **Partnerships with conflicting interests** — when partners also sell competing products or may commoditize your offering.
- **Channel without enablement** — partners fail without training, collateral, and competitive intelligence.

## Common pitfalls

- **Channel conflict** — direct sales team competes with partners for the same deals
- **Partner over-dependence** — one partner generates >30% of revenue, creating vulnerability
- **Misaligned incentives** — partner optimizes for their margin, not your brand or customer success
- **Lack of partner enablement** — insufficient training, leads, or marketing support
- **Poor partner selection** — choosing based on size rather than strategic fit
- **Ignoring partner performance** — no KPIs or regular evaluation of partner contribution

## Decision questions

1. Where does our target customer currently buy similar products—direct or through intermediaries?
2. What is our fully-loaded CAC by channel, and which channel delivers the best LTV/CAC?
3. Do we have the capital and management bandwidth to build direct channels?
4. What partner capabilities would accelerate our entry, and can we protect our brand within their ecosystem?
5. How do we prevent channel conflict between direct sales and partners?
6. What happens to our revenue if our largest partner terminates the relationship?

## Canonical examples

- **Tesla** — direct-to-consumer sales model bypassing dealerships entirely; maintains brand control and customer data (Pereira, pp. 76–77).
- **Amazon + UPS/FedEx** — indirect distribution via logistics partnerships that expanded reach while Amazon built internal delivery capacity (Pereira, pp. 80–81).
- **Microsoft Office via retailers** — mixed: direct online sales plus indirect through PC manufacturers, retailers, and enterprise resellers (Pereira, p. 79).
- **Salesforce AppExchange** — partnership as distribution: ISVs build on Salesforce platform, expanding product utility and customer lock-in (Pereira, p. 82).
