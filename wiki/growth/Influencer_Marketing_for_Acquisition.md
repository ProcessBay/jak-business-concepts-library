# Influencer Marketing for Acquisition

## Definition

Influencer marketing for acquisition is the practice of partnering with individuals who have established credibility and a dedicated following within a specific niche to drive targeted customer acquisition. Unlike broad brand awareness campaigns, acquisition-focused influencer marketing emphasizes measurable signups, conversions, or sales attributed to the influencer's promotion. Pereira: "Influencer marketing has become a popular and effective strategy for customer acquisition on social media. Influencers can help you tap into their loyal audience and generate awareness and interest in your offerings."

## Key attributes

- **Niche alignment:** Influencer audience demographics and interests closely match the target ICP
- **Authenticity fit:** Influencer's content style and values align with brand positioning
- **Clear CTAs:** Campaigns include specific calls-to-action (signup links, discount codes, landing pages)
- **Attribution mechanisms:** Unique tracking links, promo codes, or UTM parameters to measure direct impact
- **Performance contracts:** Compensation tied to measurable outcomes (CPA, CPS, or hybrid) rather than flat fees alone
- **Content co-creation:** Collaborative content that feels native to the influencer's platform and audience

## When to use

- Target audience is concentrated on specific social platforms (Instagram, TikTok, LinkedIn, YouTube)
- Product benefits are best demonstrated visually or through storytelling
- Traditional paid channels are saturated or prohibitively expensive for the niche
- Trust and social proof are critical purchase drivers (health, beauty, education, B2B expertise)
- Pilot budget exists to test micro-influencers before scaling to macro-influencers

## When NOT to use

- Product is highly technical or enterprise-focused where decision-makers are not influenced by social personalities
- Attribution is impossible or unreliable for the purchase journey (long enterprise sales cycles)
- Influencer's audience includes large numbers of fake or irrelevant followers
- Brand requires tight control over messaging that conflicts with influencer authenticity
- Regulatory environment restricts endorsements (finance, medicine, certain jurisdictions)

## Common pitfalls

- **Follower vanity** — prioritizing reach over engagement rate and audience relevance
- **Inauthentic partnerships** — forced promotions that erode both influencer and brand credibility
- **Weak attribution** — failing to track which influencer drove which conversion
- **One-off campaigns** — treating influencer marketing as transactional rather than relationship-based
- **Ignoring micro-influencers** — overlooking smaller creators with higher engagement and lower CAC
- **Mismatched expectations** — unclear deliverables, timelines, or usage rights leading to disputes

## Key metrics

| Metric | Definition | Target |
|---|---|---|
| **Engagement Rate** | (Likes + Comments + Shares) / Followers | >3% for macro; >6% for micro |
| **CAC via Influencer** | Total campaign cost / attributed customers | Comparable or lower than direct paid CAC |
| **Conversion Rate** | % of influencer-driven visitors who convert | Benchmark against organic and paid |
| **Reach** | Unique users who saw the content | Relevant reach, not just total |
| **Brand Mention Sentiment** | Positive vs. negative mentions during campaign | Net positive |

## Decision questions

- Does this influencer's audience demographics match our ICP with >70% overlap?
- What is the influencer's true engagement rate, and has it been audited for fake followers?
- Are we compensating for reach (CPM) or outcomes (CPA/CPS), and which aligns with our risk tolerance?
- Can we create content that feels authentic to the influencer's voice while conveying our value proposition?
- What is our reactivation strategy — is this a one-time post or an ongoing partnership?

## Canonical examples

- **Airbnb** — Partnered with travel influencers to showcase unique stays, driving both host signups and guest bookings (Pereira, p. 97)
- **Uber** — City launch campaigns leveraged local influencers to build rider and driver awareness in new markets (Pereira, p. 101)
- **Spotify** — Wrapped campaign turned every user into a micro-influencer; organic social sharing amplified by creator partnerships (Pereira, p. 104)
- **Slack** — B2B influencer strategy focused on productivity and tech thought leaders for credible enterprise reach (Pereira, p. 107)
