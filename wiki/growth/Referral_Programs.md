# Referral Programs

## Definition

A referral program is a structured system that incentivizes existing customers to recommend a product or service to their network, turning satisfied users into active acquisition channels. Unlike organic word-of-mouth, referral programs formalize the process with tracking mechanisms, rewards, and optimized sharing flows. Pereira: "Referral programs leverage the power of word-of-mouth marketing by encouraging existing customers to refer new customers to your business."

## Key attributes

- **Dual-sided incentives:** Rewards for both the referrer and the referee increase participation rates and conversion
- **Clear value proposition:** Both parties must immediately understand what they gain from participating
- **Low-friction sharing:** One-click social sharing, pre-populated messages, and personalized referral links minimize effort
- **Tiered rewards:** escalating benefits for multiple successful referrals sustain long-term engagement
- **Tracking and attribution:** Unique codes or links ensure accurate attribution and timely reward delivery
- **Social proof integration:** Displaying testimonials, success stories, or referral leaderboards builds credibility and FOMO

## When to use

- Product has demonstrable value that users are already recommending organically
- Customer satisfaction and NPS are consistently high
- Marginal cost of reward (discount, credit, storage) is lower than paid acquisition CAC
- Target audience has peer networks relevant to the ICP (professionals, parents, gamers)
- Product experience has natural sharing moments (team invites, collaboration, transactions)

## When NOT to use

- Customer satisfaction is low or churn is high — referrals from unhappy users backfire
- Reward cost exceeds the lifetime value of referred customers
- Product is highly personal, private, or stigmatized (health, legal, financial distress)
- Market is saturated with competitor referral programs, creating incentive fatigue
- Fraud detection systems are inadequate — referral programs attract gaming behavior

## Common pitfalls

- **Asymmetric rewards** — over-rewarding referrers while neglecting referees reduces conversion
- **Delayed gratification** — rewards that take too long to arrive kill momentum
- **Overcomplicated rules** — multi-step qualification criteria confuse users and reduce participation
- **Reward mismatch** — offering discounts to users who don't intend to repurchase (e.g., one-time buyers)
- **Neglecting non-referrers** — heavy promotion of referral programs can alienate users who don't participate
- **Fraud vulnerability** — fake accounts, self-referral, and bot farms can drain program budgets

## Key metrics

| Metric | Definition | Target |
|---|---|---|
| **Referral Conversion Rate** | % of invitees who become customers | >10% |
| **Referrer Participation Rate** | % of eligible customers who make at least one referral | 5-15% for mature programs |
| **Cost per Referred Customer** | Total reward cost / number of referred customers | <50% of direct CAC |
| **Referred Customer LTV** | Lifetime value of customers acquired via referral | Higher than direct-acquired LTV |
| **Viral Coefficient** | Average new users per existing user | >0.3 for sustainable contribution |

## Decision questions

- What is the minimum viable reward that motivates our ICP without attracting deal-seekers?
- Should we reward the referrer, the referee, or both — and in what proportion?
- How do we prevent fraud, self-dealing, and low-quality referrals?
- What is the payback period on referral reward costs vs. referred customer revenue?
- At what point in the customer journey are users most likely to refer (peak satisfaction moment)?

## Canonical examples

- **Dropbox** — "Give 500MB, Get 500MB" storage credit; both referrer and referee received tangible value tied to product usage (Pereira, p. 99)
- **Airbnb** — Travel credits for hosts and guests; dual-sided incentive aligned with platform transaction value (Pereira, p. 97)
- **Uber** — Ride credits for both rider and driver referrals; city-by-city launch strategy amplified loop (Pereira, p. 101)
- **Pinterest** — Early invite-only model created scarcity and exclusivity, driving demand and organic referrals (Pereira, p. 109)
