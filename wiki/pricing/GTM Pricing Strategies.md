# GTM Pricing Strategies

## Definition

Pricing strategy is the deliberate choice of how to monetize a product or service within a Go-to-Market plan. It is not merely a financial decision—it is a positioning statement, a customer-segmentation tool, and a competitive weapon. Pereira identifies three foundational pricing approaches—**cost-based**, **value-based**, and **competition-based**—each with distinct logic, advantages, and failure modes. Effective GTM pricing also encompasses packaging (bundling, tiering), psychological tactics (anchoring, charm pricing, decoys), and dynamic adjustments based on market response.

## Key attributes

### Cost-based pricing
Sets price by adding a markup to production/acquisition costs.
- **Pros:** Simple, ensures cost coverage, predictable margins
- **Cons:** Ignores customer willingness-to-pay; may leave money on table or price above perceived value
- **Best for:** Commoditized markets with thin differentiation

### Value-based pricing
Sets price according to perceived customer value and outcomes delivered.
- **Pros:** Captures maximum willingness-to-pay; aligns incentives with customer success
- **Cons:** Requires deep customer research; harder to justify internally
- **Best for:** Differentiated products with measurable ROI

### Competition-based pricing
Sets price relative to competitor offerings.
- **Pros:** Easy benchmark; avoids pricing outliers
- **Cons:** Risk of race to bottom; assumes competitors priced intelligently
- **Best for:** Mature markets with established price anchors

### Dynamic pricing
Adjusts price in real time based on demand, inventory, or user behavior.
- **Examples:** Uber surge pricing, airline yield management, e-commerce personalization

### Bundling and packaging
Groups products/services into tiers or bundles to simplify choice and capture more value.
- **Pure bundling:** only available as package
- **Mixed bundling:** package + individual options
- **Tiered packaging:** good-better-best structures

### Pricing psychology
- **Anchoring:** first price seen sets reference point
- **Charm pricing:** $9.99 vs. $10.00 exploits left-digit bias
- **Decoy effect:** introducing a worse option makes target option appear superior
- **Prestige pricing:** high price signals exclusivity and quality

## When to use

- **Cost-based:** early-stage with no market data; commodity products; cost-plus contracts
- **Value-based:** differentiated offerings; B2B SaaS; outcomes are quantifiable
- **Competition-based:** entering established market; price-sensitive segments
- **Dynamic:** perishable inventory; demand fluctuates significantly
- **Bundling:** cross-selling opportunity; simplifying complex portfolios

## When NOT to use

- **Cost-based for premium products** — destroys margin and brand perception
- **Value-based without proof** — customers will not pay for unproven claims
- **Competition-based in blue oceans** — no relevant competitors to benchmark against
- **Complex tiering for simple products** — decision fatigue reduces conversion

## Common pitfalls

- **Underpricing at launch** — hard to raise prices later without backlash
- **Ignoring price elasticity** — not testing how demand responds to price changes
- **One price for all segments** — SMB and enterprise should rarely share the same price
- **Feature-based pricing without value linkage** — customers pay for outcomes, not feature counts
- **Psychological manipulation overreach** — excessive decoys or urgency tactics erode trust
- **Launching without price testing** — guessing at optimal price point instead of A/B testing

## Decision questions

1. What is the dollar value of the outcome our product delivers, and what fraction of that value should we capture?
2. How do our target segments differ in willingness-to-pay, and do our pricing tiers reflect this?
3. What is the next-best alternative's price, and how do we justify a premium or accept a discount?
4. Should we lead with a low price to gain share, or a high price to signal quality?
5. How does our pricing model (per-seat, usage-based, outcome-based) align with how customers derive value?
6. What is our plan for raising prices over time as product value increases?

## Canonical examples

- **Apple prestige pricing** — iPhone priced above component cost to signal luxury and fund ecosystem (Pereira, pp. 68–69).
- **Amazon decoy effect** — strategically placing higher-priced items alongside target products to nudge choice (Pereira, p. 70).
- **McDonald's combo meals** — bundling simplifies choice, increases average ticket, and promotes cross-selling (Pereira, p. 71).
- **Adobe Creative Cloud tiers** — good-better-best packaging captures diverse willingness-to-pay from students to enterprises (Pereira, p. 72).
- **Uber dynamic pricing** — surge pricing balances supply and demand in real time (Pereira, pp. 64–65).
