# Value-Based Pricing

## Definition

Value-based pricing is a pricing strategy where the price is set primarily according to the perceived value of the product or service to the customer, rather than based on production costs or competitor prices. Pereira: "Customers are more likely to be willing to pay a higher price if they perceive significant value in your offering." The core principle is that price should capture a portion of the value created for the customer — if your product saves a customer $100K/year, charging $10K/year is value-based; charging based on your $2K cost is cost-plus.

## Key attributes

- **Customer-perceived value** — price is anchored to the customer's benefit, not your cost
- **Segment-specific** — different customer segments perceive different value; pricing should reflect this
- **Differentiation-driven** — the more differentiated your value, the more pricing power you have
- **Willingness-to-pay** — requires understanding what customers are actually willing to pay
- **Value communication** — price is justified by clearly articulating the value delivered
- **Dynamic** — as product value increases (new features, better outcomes), price should adjust upward

## When to use

- Product delivers measurable outcomes (cost savings, revenue increase, risk reduction)
- Strong differentiation from alternatives
- Target customers are businesses with clear ROI calculations
- You can quantify the value delivered (e.g., "saves 10 hours/week")
- Premium positioning is desired over commodity positioning
- Customer lifetime value is high enough to justify investment in value research

## When NOT to use

- Product is undifferentiated (commodity market)
- Target customers are extremely price-sensitive with low switching costs
- Value is intangible and hard to quantify (art, entertainment, luxury status)
- Early stage with no proven outcomes data
- Market has established price expectations that are hard to shift
- When cost structure is so high that value-based price would be unaffordable

## Common pitfalls

- **Overestimating value** — founders believe their product is worth more than customers do
- **Under-researching WTP** — guessing at willingness-to-pay instead of testing it
- **Failing to communicate value** — charging value-based prices but marketing features
- **Ignoring segments** — one price for all segments misses value capture opportunities
- **Static pricing** — not raising prices as product value increases over time
- **Confusing value with features** — customers value outcomes, not feature counts

## Value-based pricing process

1. **Define customer value** — What specific outcomes does the product deliver?
2. **Quantify value** — Can you put a dollar figure on those outcomes?
3. **Assess differentiated features** — What do you offer that competitors don't?
4. **Conduct WTP research** — Surveys, Van Westendorp, conjoint analysis
5. **Set price as fraction of value** — Typically 10-30% of value captured
6. **Test and iterate** — A/B test prices, measure conversion and retention

## Key metrics

| Metric | How to measure | Target |
|---|---|---|
| **Willingness-to-Pay (WTP)** | Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity Meter | Clear acceptable price range |
| **Price Elasticity** | % change in demand / % change in price | Inelastic (-1 to 0) = pricing power |
| **Value Perception Score** | Customer survey: "Is this price fair for the value?" | >70% say "fair" or "bargain" |
| **LTV:CAC Ratio** | Customer lifetime value / acquisition cost | >3:1 |

## Decision questions

- What measurable value does our product create for customers?
- Can we quantify that value in dollars or hours saved?
- What percentage of that value should we capture as price?
- How does our differentiated value compare to the next best alternative?
- What would happen to demand if we raised prices by 20%?
- Are we communicating value in terms customers understand (ROI, outcomes)?
- Which customer segment has the highest willingness-to-pay?

## Canonical examples

- **Salesforce** — priced based on CRM value (improved sales productivity), not software cost
- **HubSpot** — value-based tiers: Starter ($0-20) for small teams, Professional ($800) for teams needing automation, Enterprise ($3,600) for organizations needing full platform value
- **McKinsey** — consulting fees based on value of strategic recommendations, not hours worked
- **Apple** — iPhone priced on perceived value (design, ecosystem, status), not component cost
