# Summary

Daniel Pereira's *Scalable Business Model* provides a concise but structured treatment of what makes a business model scalable, why scalability matters for venture-building, and how founders can design for it from day one. The core distinction is between *growing* (adding resources proportionally to output) and *scaling* (increasing output without proportional cost increases). The guide identifies three features of scalable businesses — efficient resource use, endurance under pressure, and network effects — and five patterns of scalability including multi-channel distribution, capacity-limitation elimination, outsourcing capital investments to partners, allowing customers/partners to assume multiple roles, and creating platform models.

A key contribution is the separation of scalability into *internal* (business model design, resources, partners) and *external* (market environment, customers, regulations), with the BMI Scalability Matrix mapping these dimensions. The guide concludes with a discussion of *hyper scalability* — businesses like Airbnb and Uber that leverage intangible assets, technology, and the internet as a zero-marginal-cost distribution channel to serve millions with minimal teams.

## How JAK should use this source

**Strong on:**
- Distinction between growth and scaling (founder-friendly language)
- Practical patterns of scalability applicable to early-stage design
- Internal/external scalability matrix for strategic planning
- Hyper scalability framework (intangible assets + technology + internet distribution)
- Tips for investor readiness and team building for scale

**Weak on:**
- Deep operational or technical implementation detail
- Industry-specific scaling constraints (manufacturing, regulated services)
- Quantitative scalability metrics or benchmarks
- Advanced platform economics (e.g., two-sided pricing, take-rate optimization)

**Best for:**
- Utopian founders evaluating whether their idea is structurally scalable
- ProcessBay engagements assessing client business model scalability
- Pitch-deck language on "why this can get big without getting expensive"
- Early-stage architecture decisions (asset-light vs. asset-heavy)

**Not authoritative for:**
- Heavy-industry or capital-intensive scaling (manufacturing, energy, infrastructure)
- Technical infrastructure scaling (cloud architecture, database sharding)
- Regulatory compliance at scale in highly governed sectors
