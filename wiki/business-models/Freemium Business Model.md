# Freemium Business Model

## Definition

A freemium business model offers a basic version of a product or service free of charge while requiring payment to access advanced features, expanded functionality, or premium experiences. The term is a portmanteau of "free" and "premium." It is fundamentally a customer acquisition and conversion architecture, not merely a pricing tactic.

Freemium works because it lowers the barrier to entry, allows users to experience value before paying, and creates a built-in upgrade path from product-qualified leads (PQLs) who have already demonstrated engagement. The model is dominant in SaaS, mobile apps, games, education, entertainment, and online news — with roughly 95% of Google Play Store apps and 92% of Apple App Store apps offering free basic versions.

## Key attributes

- **Feature limitations:** Core functionality is free; advanced features sit behind a paywall. Paid features should enhance, not merely duplicate, free functionality.
- **Usage quotas:** Monthly/daily credits, storage limits (e.g., Dropbox), or data-processing caps create natural friction that prompts upgrading.
- **Support tiers:** Free users receive limited or no support; higher tiers get faster response times (e.g., 12-hour vs. 24-hour access).
- **Ad-supported free tier:** Free users see ads; premium users pay to remove them and unlock extras (e.g., YouTube, Spotify).
- **Forever free expectation:** Unlike free trials, freemium is not time-bound. Users expect the free tier to persist indefinitely.

## When to use

- The product delivers tangible value in its basic form that can hook users without human sales intervention.
- The marginal cost of serving an additional free user is near zero (digital products, SaaS).
- The target market is large and price-sensitive; upfront payment would significantly reduce adoption.
- There is a clear, desirable feature gap between free and premium that motivates conversion.
- The product benefits from network effects — a large free user base makes the product more valuable for paying users (e.g., LinkedIn, Dropbox sharing).

## When NOT to use

- The product requires heavy human onboarding or customization — free users will exhaust support resources.
- The marginal cost per free user is high (physical goods, high compute per user).
- The competitive landscape already gives away so much for free that the "penny gap" becomes a chasm.
- The business cannot sustain the infrastructure load of a large free-user base without conversion.
- Enterprise or regulated contexts where "free" triggers procurement, compliance, or security review friction.

## Common pitfalls

- **Giving away too much:** If the free tier satisfies 90% of user needs, conversion collapses.
- **Neglecting nurturing:** Assuming free users will convert automatically without prompts, glimpses of premium features, or lifecycle emails.
- **Resource drain:** Free users consume bandwidth, storage, and support. If conversion rates are too low, the model destroys cash flow.
- **Static free tier:** Failing to evolve the free product leads to user fatigue; both free and premium tiers need continuous innovation.
- **Poor onboarding:** A confusing first experience kills retention before conversion can even be attempted.

## Key metrics

- **Free-to-paid conversion rate:** Typically 2–5% for SaaS; below 1% is often unsustainable.
- **ARPU / ARPPU:** Average revenue per user and per *paying* user. ARPPU should trend up as premium tiers mature.
- **CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost):** Freemium CAC should be lower than pure paid acquisition because leads are product-qualified.
- **NPS / Engagement:** Net Promoter Score and feature-usage depth signal upgrade readiness.
- **Free-to-paid ratio:** Investors scrutinize this; too high a ratio may mean the free tier is overly generous.

## Decision questions

1. What is the minimum viable free experience that demonstrates value without satisfying all needs?
2. Which features, if moved behind a paywall, would most motivate our specific ICP to upgrade?
3. Can our infrastructure and support model sustain 10x free users for every paying user?
4. What is our nurture sequence for free users — emails, in-product prompts, time-limited premium previews?
5. How do we extract value from free users who never convert (ads, referrals, data, beta feedback)?

## Canonical examples

- **Dropbox** — free storage limit that naturally fills for active users, pushing upgrade to paid plans (Pereira, p. 19).
- **Spotify** — free tier with ads, skip limits, and no offline listening; premium removes all friction (Pereira, p. 20).
- **Slack** — free workspace with message history limit (10,000); large orgs hit the wall and upgrade (Pereira, p. 19).
- **LinkedIn** — fully functional base product; recruiters and sales reps pay for advanced search and outreach (Pereira, p. 19).
- **Mailchimp** — generous free tier for small newsletters; scaling sends or subscribers requires upgrade (Pereira, p. 21).
