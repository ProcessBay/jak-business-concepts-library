# Land and Expand Model

## Definition

The Land and Expand model is a freemium variant in which a company offers its product free (or at low cost) to individual users within an organization, gains traction bottoms-up, and then sells a premium enterprise license to the organization's executives — effectively converting viral adoption into a B2B contract. The "land" is the initial user foothold; the "expand" is the organization-wide upsell.

This model decouples the user from the buyer. Employees adopt the tool because it solves their immediate problem without requiring budget approval; once a critical mass of users is active, the vendor approaches leadership with an enterprise offering that adds collaboration, security, admin, and integration features the IT department actually cares about.

## Key attributes

- **Bottoms-up adoption:** Individual users or small teams start using the product organically, often without formal procurement.
- **Viral loops within orgs:** Collaboration features (shared workspaces, channels, projects) make the product more valuable as more colleagues join.
- **Executive buyer separate from user:** The economic buyer (CIO, VP) is not the daily user; the sales pitch must shift from user value to organizational value (security, compliance, consolidated billing).
- **Freemium or low-cost entry:** The "land" is usually free or cheap enough that a team lead can adopt it on a corporate card.
- **Enterprise feature gating:** SSO, admin dashboards, advanced permissions, audit logs, and dedicated support sit behind the enterprise tier.

## When to use

- The product has natural collaborative or sharing features that improve with more users in the same domain.
- The target market is organizations, but the end-user is an individual contributor or team lead.
- The sales cycle for top-down enterprise deals is long and expensive; bottoms-up reduces CAC.
- The product can deliver meaningful single-player value before organizational network effects kick in.
- The competitive landscape is crowded with incumbent enterprise tools that are hard to displace through direct sales alone.

## When NOT to use

- The product is purely individual with no collaboration or multi-user dimension (e.g., a solo productivity app).
- The organization size is too small for an enterprise tier to matter (SMBs may never "expand").
- Procurement policies forbid unsanctioned SaaS tools (heavily regulated industries, government).
- The product requires deep IT integration before any user can derive value (top-down is then the only path).

## Common pitfalls

- **Landing without a path to expand:** Free users love the product, but there's no compelling enterprise feature set to justify a paid contract.
- **Ignoring IT/security concerns:** Bottoms-up adoption creates "shadow IT" problems; if the vendor cannot address compliance, the expand motion fails.
- **Sales-team mismatch:** A traditional outbound sales team may struggle with the product-led, usage-data-driven expand motion.
- **Underpricing the enterprise tier:** If the jump from free to enterprise is too small, revenue per customer is insufficient to fund growth.

## Key metrics

- **Organic user penetration per account:** % of employees in a target company who are active users.
- **Time to expand:** Days from first user signup to first enterprise contract in the same domain.
- **Expansion revenue:** % of total revenue from existing accounts upgrading.
- **Free-to-paid conversion rate at the account level:** Not individual users, but organizations converting.
- **Net Revenue Retention (NRR):** Should exceed 120% in healthy land-and-expand businesses.

## Decision questions

1. What is the minimum number of users in an organization that signals "land" success and justifies an enterprise sales conversation?
2. Which enterprise-only features will the CIO/VP actually pay for, and do they solve real pain?
3. How do we identify and alert our sales team when an account hits expansion readiness?
4. What is our policy on "shadow IT" risk — do we offer IT admin tools proactively or reactively?
5. Can we deliver enough single-player value to survive the period before organizational critical mass is reached?

## Canonical examples

- **Slack** — free workspaces spread through engineering and product teams; enterprise tier adds compliance, SSO, and unlimited history (Pereira, p. 16).
- **Atlassian (Jira, Confluence)** — developers adopt freely; IT eventually buys enterprise for governance and scale.
- **Twilio** — developers embed APIs in projects; engineering leadership later negotiates volume contracts.
- **AWS** — individual developers spin up free-tier resources; enterprises eventually commit to reserved instances and enterprise support.
- **Asana / Veeva Systems / Alteryx** — all follow the same bottoms-up → enterprise pattern (Pereira, p. 16).
