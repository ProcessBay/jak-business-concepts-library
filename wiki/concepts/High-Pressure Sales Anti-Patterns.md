# High-Pressure Sales Anti-Patterns

## Definition

High-pressure sales tactics are the cluster of manipulative behaviors that prioritize the short-term close over the long-term relationship — strategies that lean closer to manipulation than persuasion. Pereira's position is unambiguous: these tactics can produce a sale in the immediate term, but the long-run damage to brand, customer relationships, refunds, and word-of-mouth almost always exceeds the gain. The pattern matters because high-pressure sales is what poisons buyers against all salespeople — every prospect's natural wariness traces back to an experience with a manipulative seller. Modern buyers have nearly unlimited access to alternatives, reviews, and pricing comparisons; manipulation gets exposed within days. Recognizing the anti-pattern is essential both to avoid practicing it and to recognize when competitors are using it (which often opens an opportunity to win the deal on honesty alone).

## Key attributes

- **Manipulation, not persuasion** — the line is informed consent
- **Six classic patterns** (Pereira's list): false urgency, fear-based persuasion, product exaggeration, premature pressure, dismissing concerns, fabricated success stories
- **Short-term wins, long-term losses** — sales happen but churn, refunds, and reputation suffer
- **Trust-destroying** — once detected, the relationship is unrecoverable
- **Often legally risky** — false claims can trigger consumer-protection complaints
- **Common in low-trust environments** — used desks, timeshares, MLM, certain SaaS one-call-close motions
- **Recognizable by buyers** — modern buyers are skeptical by default, detect manipulation quickly
- **Cultural infection risk** — one rep doing it normalizes it for the team

## When to use

- Never. This is an anti-pattern — the entry exists to catalog what to avoid, not to invoke.

## When NOT to use

- All conditions. The strategies described here should not be deployed.

## Common pitfalls (i.e., the anti-patterns themselves)

- **False urgency** — making prospects believe they have a problem they don't actually have
- **Fear-based persuasion** — emotional manipulation as the primary lever
- **Product exaggeration** — overstating capabilities, results, or differentiation
- **Premature pressure** — forcing a purchase decision before the buyer is ready
- **Dismissing concerns** — downplaying or ignoring legitimate objections to push past them
- **Fabricated success stories** — invented or exaggerated case studies
- **Pushing borderline deals** (page 149) — closing deals that shouldn't close, damaging brand
- **Hammering with calls/emails** (page 154) — looks desperate, signals weakness
- **PAS misuse** — using Problem-Agitate-Solution to invent problems rather than name real ones (page 76)
- **"Always Be Closing" misread as constant pressure** rather than constant micro-commitment

## Key metrics (i.e., signals you may have a high-pressure culture)

- **High refund rate** — customers backing out after the "close" suggests buyer's remorse
- **High first-90-day churn** — bought under pressure, leaves at first opportunity
- **Low NPS** — customers acquired under pressure don't recommend
- **Negative review velocity** — online reviews flagging "pushy" or "misleading" sales
- **High SDR/AE turnover** — reps don't want to be the bad guy long-term
- **High deal-reversal rate** — closed deals that unwind before kickoff

## Decision questions

- Are we creating urgency or manufacturing it? (Real urgency comes from real necessity, page 76)
- Are we agitating real consequences or invented ones?
- Are we backing every claim with proof, or stretching the truth?
- Are our case studies real and verifiable, or "inspired by" real stories?
- Are we listening to objections or dismissing them to keep momentum?
- Are we proud of how the last deal closed, or quietly uncomfortable?
- Would the customer who just signed describe the experience as honest?

## Canonical examples

- **The classic six anti-patterns** (Pereira, pages 143-144): false urgency, fear-based, exaggeration, premature pressure, dismissed concerns, fabricated stories
- **Pereira's "pushing hard for a borderline deal"** (page 149) — closes deals that should be lost
- **Pereira's "hammering with calls/emails"** (page 154) — desperation signal, not dedication
- **Pereira's "PAS without real problems"** (page 76) — manipulation disguised as persuasion
- **Counter-pattern: "Help at the core"** (page 72) — Hiten Shah's principle that helpful sales never produces a terrible sale
