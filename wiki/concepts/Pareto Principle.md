# Pareto Principle

## Definition

The Pareto Principle (commonly called the 80/20 Rule) states that roughly 80% of outcomes come from 20% of causes. Originally observed by Vilfredo Pareto in 1896 in Italian land ownership (20% of people owned 80% of the land), it has been documented across business contexts: 80% of sales from 20% of customers; 80% of revenue from 20% of products; 80% of complaints from 20% of issues; 80% of defects from 20% of root causes. Pereira invokes it as the foundational asymmetry that justifies both Blockbuster Strategy (focus on the productive 20%) and Long-Tail Strategy (the cumulative 80% of niche items aggregates to substantial revenue if you can serve them economically).

## Key attributes

- **Asymmetric outcome distribution** — small fraction of inputs drive most outputs
- **Observable across domains** — sales, revenue, defects, complaints, time spent, energy use
- **Not literally 80/20** — exact ratios vary (could be 90/10, 70/30) but the asymmetry holds
- **Two strategic implications** — focus on the 20% (Blockbuster) OR aggregate the 80% (Long-Tail)
- **Frequently misapplied** — assuming Pareto means "ignore the 80%" misses long-tail opportunity
- **Diagnostic tool** — when you don't know where leverage is, look for Pareto asymmetries first
- **Underlying math** — power-law distributions are common in social/economic systems

## When to use

- Prioritization decisions (where to invest first)
- Customer segmentation (which 20% drives 80% of revenue?)
- Quality programs (which 20% of defect causes drive 80% of problems?)
- Product portfolio decisions (which SKUs to focus on)
- Time management ("eliminate the 80% of activities that drive 20% of value")

## When NOT to use

- When the distribution is genuinely flat (not all systems are Pareto)
- As an excuse to ignore the long tail (it might aggregate)
- For one-off decisions where statistical patterns don't apply
- In categories where the "20%" is highly volatile (today's hits aren't tomorrow's)

## Common pitfalls

- Treating Pareto as exact 80/20 instead of approximate asymmetry
- Ignoring the long tail when digital/scale makes serving it economic
- Confusing "Pareto" with "ignore most of it" — sometimes the 80% is where opportunity lives
- Misidentifying the 20% (focusing on volume customers when value customers are different)
- Static thinking — Pareto distributions shift over time

## Key metrics

- Concentration ratio (% from top 20% of inputs)
- Distribution skew (Gini coefficient or similar)
- Top-20 contribution trend over time

## Decision questions

- For our revenue: what % comes from our top 20% of customers/products?
- Where in our business is the Pareto asymmetry sharpest, and where flattest?
- Could we economically serve the long tail of our 80%?
- What 20% of our problems would, if fixed, eliminate 80% of customer complaints?

## Canonical examples

- **Original observation** — Pareto noted 80% of Italian land owned by 20% of population (1896)
- **Retail** — 80% of sales from 20% of SKUs (classic Blockbuster justification) (page 10)
- **Customer base** — 80% of revenue from 20% of customers (common in B2B)
- **Quality (Juran)** — 80% of defects from 20% of root causes (Pareto chart in QC)
- **Long-tail rebuttal** — Anderson showed that the 80% can aggregate to equal the 20% when shelved digitally (page 11)
