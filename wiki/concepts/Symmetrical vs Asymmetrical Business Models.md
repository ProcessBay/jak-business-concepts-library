# Symmetrical vs Asymmetrical Business Models

## Definition

Business models split into two structural types based on whether users and customers are the same party: **Symmetrical** models have users = customers (the person using the product is the person paying — Netflix, Zoom, brick-and-mortar retail); **Asymmetrical** models have users ≠ customers (the person using the product is different from the person paying — Facebook, Google, ad-supported media where users get the product free and advertisers pay). Pereira frames this distinction as fundamental because it determines how the business scales: symmetrical models tend toward linear scaling with margin compression, while asymmetrical models can scale non-linearly with margin expansion (the data and audience compound).

## Key attributes

- **Two-party players** — every business has users (who consume) and customers (who pay)
- **Symmetric**: users = customers (Netflix subscribers, Zoom paid users, retail buyers)
- **Asymmetric**: users ≠ customers (Facebook users vs. advertisers, Google searchers vs. ad buyers)
- **Scaling dynamics differ** — symmetric linearizes; asymmetric compounds via data/audience
- **Data asset gravity** — asymmetric models accumulate data on users that becomes the moat
- **Hidden revenue** — asymmetric users typically don't know exactly how the platform monetizes their attention

## When to use

- Distinguishing what kind of business you're actually building
- Investor pitch — asymmetric platforms valued differently than symmetric subscriptions
- Diagnosing why margins are compressing (often a symmetric model fighting linear scaling)
- Strategic shifts (Facebook+Instagram = pure asymmetric; Spotify = hybrid)

## When NOT to use

- As a complete business model classification (multilateral platforms are more complex)
- For pure transactional B2B where the distinction is moot
- As a substitute for actual revenue stream analysis

## Common pitfalls

- Trying to monetize an asymmetric model directly (charging users instead of advertisers — often fails)
- Treating asymmetric platforms as "free" when they monetize attention/data
- Assuming symmetric pricing power is sustainable as platforms scale (margin compression is real)
- Building hybrid awkwardly (Spotify struggles with this — free ad-supported AND paid)

## Key metrics

- User count vs. customer count
- Revenue per user vs. revenue per customer
- Margin trend as platform scales (compression vs. expansion)
- Data asset value (often hidden but central to asymmetric models)

## Decision questions

- Are our users the same as our customers, or different parties?
- If asymmetric, what's our data asset worth, and are we monetizing it well?
- If symmetric, how do we maintain margins as we scale?
- Could we shift from symmetric to asymmetric (or vice versa) and what would change?

## Canonical examples

- **Symmetric** — Netflix (subscribers = users), Zoom (paid users = users), brick-and-mortar retail (customers = users) (page 14)
- **Asymmetric** — Facebook (free users + paying advertisers), Google (free searchers + paying advertisers), Snapchat (free users + ads) (page 13)
- **Hybrid** — Spotify (free ad-supported + paid subs), LinkedIn (free users + premium accounts + recruiter tools), HBO
