# Self-Service Customer Relationship

## Definition

A customer-relationship mode in which the company provides the tools and infrastructure but the customer performs the service themselves — Pereira calls it the "transactional dynamic in its purest form." The airport check-in kiosk is the canonical example: impersonal, fast, designed for throughput. Firms adopt self-service to reduce cost, then return some of that saving to customers as lower prices. The relationship is intentionally minimal: there is no rep, no rapport, no follow-up. Done well it delights customers who value speed and autonomy; done badly it strands them with an unusable interface and no path to a human.

## Key attributes

- **Customer performs the work** — firm supplies tools, customer supplies labor
- **Cost-savings flow back as lower prices** — the value exchange that justifies the mode
- **Optimized for throughput and autonomy** — speed and "I don't want to talk to anyone" are the wins
- **No rapport mechanism** — designed not to build a relationship, but to complete a task
- **Failure mode is severe** — when self-service breaks, the customer has nowhere to go
- **Often paired with a human or automated fallback** — escalation path matters

## When to use

- For high-frequency, low-complexity transactions (check-in, account top-up, password reset)
- When customers actively prefer not to interact with a person
- When unit economics will not support staffed service at the target price
- When digital infrastructure can deliver a clean, well-tested experience
- For scale plays where head-count can't grow linearly with users

## When NOT to use

- For complex, ambiguous, or high-stakes transactions (enterprise contracting, medical advice)
- When the customer segment lacks digital fluency
- When self-service quality is poor — a broken kiosk is worse than no kiosk
- For the onboarding moment of a high-value relationship, where first-impression rapport matters
- When the firm cannot fund an escape hatch to a human

## Common pitfalls

- Pushing self-service to cut cost without investing in interface quality
- No escalation path when self-service fails
- Treating self-service as a one-time build rather than an iterated product
- Forcing self-service on segments that explicitly want a human
- Confusing "self-service" with "no service" — the support content, error states, and escalation are the product

## Key metrics

- **Cost-to-serve per transaction** (vs human-assisted baseline)
- **Deflection rate** (% of issues resolved without a human)
- **Self-service completion rate** / abandonment rate
- **CSAT for self-service interactions**
- **Time-to-completion**
- **Escalation rate** (% who bail out to a human)

## Decision questions

- Is this task simple, frequent, and low-stakes enough for self-service?
- Have we invested enough in interface quality to make the experience clean?
- What is our escalation path when self-service fails?
- Are the customers we're routing to self-service the ones who want it?
- Are we passing meaningful price savings back, or pocketing all the deflection?

## Canonical examples

- **Airport self-service check-in kiosks** — Pereira's canonical example (page 20)
- **ATM cash withdrawal**
- **Vending machines and gas pumps**
- **Self-checkout aisles in supermarkets**
- **SaaS sign-up and onboarding for low-tier plans**
- **IKEA flat-pack assembly** — extreme physical self-service
