# Pure Bundling vs Mixed Bundling

## Definition

Pure bundling and mixed bundling are the two structural archetypes of the bundling business model, distinguished by whether customers retain the option to buy components individually. In **pure bundling**, components are only available as part of the bundle — there is no standalone path. Examples: HelloFresh meal kits (you cannot buy single ingredients), Microsoft Office 365 (channel bundles in cable TV historically), ASOS camera kits. In **mixed bundling**, the same components are sold both as part of a discounted package and as standalone SKUs at a higher per-unit price — the bundle is an alternative path, not the only path. Pereira documents that the choice between these archetypes is not stylistic but consequential: Vineet Kumar's Harvard study found Nintendo's mixed-bundling strategy lifted console sales by 100,000 units (and software by >1M units), while a pure-bundling experiment with the same products dropped console sales 20%. The choice flows from a strategic question — do we want customers locked into the bundle, or do we want the bundle to amplify standalone demand?

## Key attributes

- **Pure bundling: no individual path** — components only available as a packaged set
- **Mixed bundling: dual path** — components sold both bundled (discounted) and standalone (full price)
- **Pure bundling maximizes simplicity** — one SKU, one decision, one fulfillment path
- **Mixed bundling preserves price discrimination** — customers self-segment between bundle-takers and individual buyers
- **Pure bundling risks customer rejection** — Nintendo's 20% sales drop when forced; cable cord-cutting is a pure-bundle backlash
- **Mixed bundling is the default for SaaS, retail, consumer goods** — protects optionality
- **Pure bundling fits when components have no standalone economics** — meal kit ingredients aren't sold separately because the unit economics don't work
- **Mixed bundling is reversible** — pure is structural

## When to use

**Pure bundling:**
- When components have no viable standalone market or unit economics
- When operational simplicity from a single SKU outweighs price-discrimination loss
- When the entire value proposition IS the integration (cable channel packages, all-you-can-eat streaming)
- When inventory or fulfillment overhead of mixed SKUs would be prohibitive
- When you want to force adoption of weaker components alongside strong ones

**Mixed bundling:**
- When the components have legitimate standalone demand
- When you want to capture both bundle-buyers and singleton-buyers (price discrimination)
- When customers expect optionality and pure bundling would trigger rejection
- When you want bundles to amplify rather than replace standalone sales (the Nintendo case)
- When you need a clear reference price for the bundle's discount to register

## When NOT to use

**Pure bundling:**
- When customers have strong standalone preferences for some components (Nintendo case)
- When the bundle includes components customers actively don't want (anti-cable sentiment)
- When standalone availability is a competitive necessity (the market expects it)
- When forcing the bundle would harm hero-product perception

**Mixed bundling:**
- When standalone availability cannibalizes the bundle's economics
- When operational complexity of supporting both paths is prohibitive
- When a pure-bundle pure-play is structurally required (subscription boxes)

## Common pitfalls

- Treating the choice as a tactic when it's an architectural commitment
- Defaulting to pure bundling for operational convenience and missing the 20% sales hit
- Pricing a mixed bundle without enough discount — customers do the math and buy standalone
- Forgetting that mixed bundling exposes the discount level, anchoring customers to the savings
- Building a pure-bundle business when the market is unbundling around you (cable, music albums)
- Confusing the archetype choice with the bundle-composition choice (which products) — both must be right

## Key metrics

- **Bundle take rate** — % of buyers who choose the bundle (mixed-bundle only — pure bundle is 100% by definition)
- **Standalone cannibalization** — % decline in single-SKU sales after bundle launch
- **AOV lift** — bundle AOV vs. average historical AOV
- **Attach rate** — how many components per transaction
- **Refund / return rate by archetype** — pure bundles often have higher dissatisfaction
- **Choice satisfaction (NPS by buyer type)** — bundle-buyers vs. singleton-buyers

## Decision questions

- Do our components have viable standalone economics, or is the bundle the only unit that works?
- If we force pure bundling, what's our Nintendo risk — what % of customers walk?
- Are we using mixed bundling to price-discriminate, or just hedging the commitment?
- Will the standalone pricing serve as a reference price that makes the bundle look great, or expose the cross-subsidy?
- Does our operational stack (fulfillment, support, returns) support both paths, or are we kidding ourselves?
- Are we entering a market where the dominant archetype is itself being disrupted? (Cable: yes. Streaming: not yet.)

## Canonical examples

**Pure bundling:**
- **HelloFresh meal kits** — pure bundle; ingredients not sold separately (page 14, 20)
- **Microsoft Office 365** — historically a pure bundle of productivity apps (page 24)
- **Cable channel packages** — pure-bundled channel tiers; impossible to pick individual channels (page 24)
- **ASOS camera kits** — pure-bundled photography accessories (page 16)
- **Most subscription boxes** — pure bundles by definition

**Mixed bundling:**
- **Nintendo Switch + games** — the canonical mixed-bundle case study; +100K consoles, +1M games (pages 11-12)
- **Adobe Creative Cloud** — apps available individually OR as the suite (page 19)
- **Anastasia Beverly Hills Flawlessly Ever After** — gift bundle with each component priced individually (page 16)
- **H&M sweater bundles** — two sweaters bundled at discount; each available standalone (page 17)
- **Harry's Skincare Essentials Set** — three products bundled or sold separately (page 15)
