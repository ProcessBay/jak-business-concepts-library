# Maverick Spending

## Definition

Maverick spending — also called rogue spend or unapproved spending — is any purchase or expenditure that bypasses the organization's established purchasing policies and pre-negotiated supplier contracts. Pereira identifies it as a common condition in businesses that lack a clear, concise purchase-order approval discipline. Its damage extends well beyond the price premium paid on the off-contract purchase: it breaches supplier contracts (eroding the volume commitments the contract was priced on), complicates contract management, weakens supplier relationships, and ultimately undermines the very cost-reduction program leadership thinks it has in place. The fix is procurement-system hygiene: define the procurement policy explicitly, automate purchase approvals, increase spending analysis, and build a refined strategic sourcing program.

## Key attributes

- **Bypass of approved channels** — purchases made outside policy or off-contract
- **Compound damage** — premium paid + contract breach + relationship erosion
- **Symptom of weak governance** — usually accompanies unclear PO approvals
- **Visible only with spend analysis** — if you can't see it, it's growing
- **Fixable** — automation + policy clarity + strategic sourcing eliminates most of it
- **Recurring** — without sustained controls, drifts back within 12–18 months

## When this becomes the dominant issue

- Decentralized organizations with department-level purchasing authority
- Fast-growth businesses that outran their procurement function
- Post-merger environments with multiple legacy policy sets
- Organizations heavily reliant on credit-card / expense-report spending
- Field operations or distributed sites where central procurement has weak visibility

## When it's tolerated (and shouldn't be)

- Small / urgent purchases where "the policy was too slow"
- Executive privilege exceptions that legitimize bypass for everyone watching
- Categories deemed "too small to matter" individually (death by a thousand cuts)
- Departments with budget autonomy treating spend as discretionary

## Common pitfalls in fixing it

- Punitive crackdowns without easier compliant channels — drives spend further underground
- Policy without automation — humans don't read PDFs at the moment of purchase
- Treating it as a finance issue when it's a process + tooling issue
- Tackling tail spend before consolidating the long-tail supplier base first
- Killing maverick spend on essentials and creating operational bottlenecks
- One-time clean-up without a sustaining control — drift returns

## Key metrics

- **Maverick spend %** — off-contract spend / total addressable spend
- **Contract compliance rate** — % of spend on pre-negotiated terms
- **Spend under management** — % of total spend visible to procurement
- **Supplier count** by category (tail-supplier sprawl is a leading indicator)
- **PO cycle time** — long cycles cause people to bypass

## Decision questions

- Do we actually know our maverick spend rate, or are we guessing?
- Are the compliant channels faster than the rogue ones — and if not, why would anyone use them?
- Which categories have the highest leakage, and why?
- Do we have spend analysis tooling that surfaces off-contract activity in near-real-time?
- Is leadership modelling policy compliance, or signalling that exceptions are normal?

## Canonical examples

- **Travel & expense** — booking outside the corporate tool to "save time"; loses negotiated rates
- **SaaS sprawl** — departments procuring their own tools on credit cards; later discovered as duplicate / redundant
- **Office supplies and tail-spend categories** — historically the highest-leakage spend pools
- **Marketing agency spend** — bypassing procurement's preferred vendor list
- **IT hardware** — local-site purchases outside the master agreement
