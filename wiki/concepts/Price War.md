# Price War

## Definition

A price war is a downward spiral in which competitors repeatedly undercut each other's prices, each move forcing a counter-move, until margins collapse across the entire category. Pereira describes it as the structural endgame of unchecked competitive pricing and the visible symptom of predatory pricing strategies. The most damaging form is the **race to the bottom**, where the lowest-cost or deepest-pocketed competitor wins the war but the category emerges commoditized and value-destroyed for everyone — sometimes including the winner.

## Key attributes

- **Iterative undercutting** — each price move triggers a competitor response
- **Margin erosion** — gross margins collapse across the category
- **Race to the bottom** — the floor is set by the lowest-cost player
- **Winner-takes-most outcome** — survivor often gains monopoly power, raises prices later
- **Customer benefit short-term, harm long-term** — cheap prices now, fewer choices later
- **Triggered by penetration, competitor-based, and predatory pricing strategies**
- **Hard to exit** — first to raise prices loses share

## When to expect / watch for

- Multiple well-capitalized competitors in an undifferentiated category
- A new entrant using penetration pricing aggressively
- Commodity or low-switching-cost categories
- Markets where one player decides to consolidate via predatory pricing
- Periods of oversupply or weak demand
- Industries with high fixed cost / low marginal cost (airlines, telecom)

## When NOT to fight a price war (most of the time)

- You don't have the lowest cost structure
- You can't sustain margin compression for the duration
- Your brand is built on differentiation (price war commoditizes you)
- You have a better strategic move (segment, differentiate, exit)
- The category economics will be permanently destroyed by the war

## Common pitfalls

- Engaging in a price war you can't win on cost structure
- Confusing aggressive penetration with predatory pricing — legal exposure
- Allowing competitor moves to dictate your strategy reactively
- Lowering price across the board when only one segment needs the response
- Failing to re-segment, re-differentiate, or exit when the war is unwinnable
- Watching margins erode without escalating to a strategic response

## Key metrics

- **Gross margin erosion** (vs. pre-war baseline)
- **Market share volatility** (the war's apparent objective)
- **Time-to-price-floor** (how fast the bottom is being approached)
- **Competitor count** (war ends when most exit or consolidate)
- **Cash burn rate** (sustainability of fighting)

## Decision questions

- Are we structurally capable of winning this price war (lowest cost, deepest pockets)?
- What's our exit option if we don't fight (segment, differentiate, withdraw)?
- Is this a war or just a one-time competitor promo we should ignore?
- What's the long-term industry damage we're contributing to?
- Can we change the game with non-price moves (bundle, service, loyalty)?

## Canonical examples

- **Amazon vs. Borders** — predatory pricing on books drove Borders out (page 119)
- **Walmart in local markets** — significantly lowered grocery prices to eliminate local competitors (page 119)
- **Airline route wars** — major carriers drop prices on routes where low-cost carriers operate, to drive them out (page 119)
- **Predatory pricing playbook** — page 117-121: undercut, win, then monopoly-price
- **Contribution-margin pricing pitfall** — aggressive pricing for margin "may lead to price wars" (page 41)
