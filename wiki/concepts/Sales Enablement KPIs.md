# Sales Enablement KPIs

## Definition

Sales enablement KPIs are the quantitative indicators that link enablement investment (training, content, tooling, coaching) to sales outcomes (revenue, velocity, retention). Pereira's framework spans eight KPI families, each tied to a different layer of the enablement stack: lead conversion and pipeline velocity (deal mechanics), content engagement and utilization (asset performance), training effectiveness (skill development), time-to-value for new reps (onboarding efficiency), customer satisfaction and retention (long-term enablement impact), technology adoption (tool ROI), and sales forecast accuracy (decision-quality output). The defining discipline is alignment with business objectives — KPIs that don't ladder up to revenue, market share, or customer satisfaction become enablement-team vanity metrics. The strongest KPI systems pair leading indicators (training completion, content usage) with lagging indicators (quota attainment, win rate, NRR) so cause-effect chains stay visible.

## Key attributes

- **Aligned with business objectives** — every KPI ties to revenue, market share, retention, or satisfaction
- **Leading + lagging pair** — leading indicators (activity) paired with lagging outcomes (revenue)
- **Eight-family taxonomy** — conversion, content, training, ramp, satisfaction, tech, forecast, ROI
- **Baseline before initiative** — measure starting point, not just delta
- **Continuous monitoring** — quarterly review minimum; monthly for high-velocity orgs
- **Cause-effect attribution** — robust enough to attribute results to enablement actions
- **Cross-functional accountability** — shared between sales, marketing, enablement, RevOps
- **Tied to ROI** — ultimately measured in dollars in vs. dollars out

## When to use

- Before launching any enablement initiative — baseline first, then measure delta
- Quarterly enablement reviews and annual planning
- Building business case for enablement headcount or tool investment
- After major changes (new product, new ICP, restructure) to track adaptation
- When defending enablement budget against finance scrutiny
- As input to sales leadership decisions (hiring, territory, methodology change)

## When NOT to use

- As substitute for qualitative rep feedback (KPIs miss the why)
- For micro-level rep evaluation (KPIs are program-level, not individual surveillance)
- When data quality is too poor to trust the numbers (fix the foundation first)
- As single-metric optimization (any one KPI gamed produces bad behavior)
- Before establishing baseline (no delta possible without starting point)

## Common pitfalls

- Tracking activity metrics (courses completed) instead of outcome (win rate)
- No baseline measurement before initiative launch — can't show impact
- Vanity KPIs that don't ladder to revenue (engagement minutes ≠ closed deals)
- One-time measurement with no longitudinal trend
- Single-source data — no triangulation across rep feedback, deal data, customer signal
- Confusing correlation with causation (top reps use the LMS more, but did LMS cause it?)
- Reporting only on what looks good, suppressing inconvenient data
- KPIs designed by enablement team without sales leadership buy-in

## Key metrics

- **Lead conversion rate** — % of leads progressing to opportunity, opp to close
- **Pipeline velocity** — speed of leads through funnel stages
- **Content utilization** — % of content library deployed in deals; engagement on deployed assets
- **Sales training effectiveness** — knowledge retention, application in deals
- **Time-to-value for new reps** — days/months to first deal and to quota
- **Customer satisfaction / NPS / retention** — long-term enablement payoff
- **Technology adoption rate** — active usage of each tool in the stack
- **Sales forecast accuracy** — predicted vs. actual sales results
- **Quota attainment** — % of reps hitting plan; healthy = 60-70%
- **Win rate** — overall and by segment / rep cohort

## Decision questions

- Do our KPIs ladder to revenue, or are they enablement-team vanity metrics?
- What's our baseline, and are we measuring delta?
- Do we pair leading and lagging indicators, or only one?
- Are we triangulating quantitative data with qualitative rep and customer feedback?
- Who owns each KPI — and is the owner the one who can move it?
- How are we attributing outcomes to specific enablement actions vs. macro conditions?
- Are we sharing KPIs transparently across sales, marketing, enablement?
