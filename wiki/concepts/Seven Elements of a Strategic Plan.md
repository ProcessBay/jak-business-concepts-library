# Seven Elements of a Strategic Plan

## Definition

Pereira's anatomy of a strategic plan: seven essential components that, together, give an organization a complete strategic frame. They run from the most abstract (Vision Statement, Mission Statement, Core Values) through the diagnostic (SWOT Analysis) to the increasingly concrete (Long-Term Goals, Yearly Objectives, Action Plans). Each element answers a different question — *who are we becoming, what do we do, what do we believe, where do we stand, what are we aiming for over 3-5 years, what are we doing this year, and how exactly?* A plan missing any element typically fails at the corresponding altitude: missing values produces drift; missing yearly objectives produces vague aspiration; missing action plans produces inertia.

## The seven elements

- **Vision Statement** — How you picture your company in the long term; the dream rendered in motivating language. Pereira's example: Nike — "Bring inspiration and innovation to every athlete in the world." Aspirational, succinct, occasionally revised but rarely overturned (page 30-31)
- **Mission Statement** — What you do *right now*, for whom, and how. The operating manual. Pereira's example: JetBlue — "To inspire humanity — both in the air and on the ground." Concrete enough to inform daily decisions (page 31-32)
- **Core Values** — The principles guiding thought and action. Pereira's example: Patagonia's four — Build the best product; Cause no unnecessary harm; Use business to protect nature; Not bound by convention (page 32-33)
- **SWOT Analysis** — Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats. Identifies the organization's situational position relative to its environment. See [[SWOT Analysis]] (page 33)
- **Long-Term Goals** — 3-5 year statements that operationalize the vision and mission into specific destinations (page 34)
- **Yearly Objectives** — SMART (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Realistic, Time-Based) goals that ladder up to long-term goals (page 34)
- **Action Plans** — The how. Specific steps, owners, and timelines that turn yearly objectives into work. Level of detail scales inversely with team latitude (page 34)

## Why this matters

- A strategic plan is only as strong as its weakest element
- Each element answers a different question — missing one creates a corresponding gap in execution
- Most "strategic plans" in practice have 3-4 elements at best; the missing ones explain the failures
- The hierarchy matters — vision must inform mission, mission must inform values, values must inform SWOT interpretation, and so on down to action plans
- Each element has its own review cadence: vision/mission rarely; values episodically; SWOT quarterly; goals annually; action plans monthly/weekly

## Common pitfalls

- Vision and mission that are interchangeable (they answer different questions)
- Core values that are aspirational marketing copy, not actual decision criteria
- SWOT done once and shelved instead of being refreshed
- Long-term goals that aren't actually linked to vision
- Yearly objectives that fail the SMART test (vague, immeasurable, unrealistic)
- Action plans that detail tasks but skip ownership and dates
- Plans heavy on the abstract layers (vision, mission, values) and thin on the concrete (objectives, action plans)
- Inverse failure — heavy on action plans, light on the strategic frame above them

## Decision questions

- Does our plan have all seven elements? Which is weakest?
- Are vision and mission actually distinguishable in content?
- Do our core values pass the test of "would we choose this even if it cost us"?
- When did we last refresh our SWOT?
- Do our yearly objectives ladder cleanly to long-term goals and vision?
- Do our action plans have named owners, dates, and KPIs?
- What's the review cadence for each element, and is it being honoured?

## Canonical examples

- **Nike vision** — "Bring inspiration and innovation to every athlete in the world. (*If you have a body, you are an athlete.)*" Aspirational, identity-defining (page 31)
- **JetBlue mission** — "To inspire humanity — both in the air and on the ground." Cross-cuts air and ground operations (page 31)
- **Patagonia values** — Four operating commitments that connect directly to product, supply chain, and environmental decisions (pages 32-33). Chouinard Equipment's pivot to aluminum chocks (replacing rock-damaging pitons) is cited as values driving product decisions
- **SMART goals** — Pereira's recommendation for yearly objectives format (page 34)
