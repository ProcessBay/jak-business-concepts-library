# Anchoring Bias

## Definition

Anchoring bias is the tendency to rely too heavily on the first piece of information received when making a decision, even if that information is irrelevant or arbitrary. The initial figure acts as an "anchor" that pulls subsequent judgments toward it — Tversky and Kahneman's classic demonstration had participants estimate the percentage of African countries in the UN, and those shown a high random anchor (65%) gave dramatically higher estimates than those shown a low anchor (10%). Pereira distinguishes numerical anchoring (a first price shapes a final price) from selective anchoring (a first framing makes one aspect of a decision dominate).

## Key attributes

- **First number wins** — initial figure exerts disproportionate gravitational pull on the final estimate
- **Works even when the anchor is arbitrary** — the original Tversky/Kahneman result used random numbers
- **Reinforced by the primacy effect** — first-presented information is better remembered and weighted heavier
- **Affects experts as well as novices** — domain expertise reduces but does not eliminate it
- **Two forms** — numerical (price, %, year) and selective (the first dimension framed becomes the primary lens)
- **Insufficient adjustment** — people adjust from the anchor but stop too early
- **Pervasive across domains** — finance, negotiations, marketing, medical diagnosis, legal awards

## When you're most at risk

- You're making an estimate or negotiation under uncertainty with no objective benchmark
- Someone just gave you a number (asking price, salary expectation, fundraising target)
- You're making the decision quickly
- The anchor came from an authority figure or trusted source
- You're being shown pricing tiers, comparison points, or "before/after" framings

## When it's NOT a problem

- The anchor IS the correct value (market price, settled benchmark)
- You have strong independent objective data and use it as your reference
- The decision is binary and the anchor is irrelevant to which side you pick
- Used deliberately as a negotiation tool when you set the anchor and know what you're doing

## Common manifestations in business

- **Negotiations** — the first offer sets the entire trading range (p.31-32)
- **Pricing tiers** — a high-priced Premium option makes Standard look reasonable (Pereira's $50/$100/$200 example, p.33)
- **Discounts** — "was $400, now $200" anchors perceived value above actual worth (p.33)
- **Salary negotiation** — the candidate's stated current salary anchors the offer
- **Fundraising** — the founder's first valuation ask shapes the term sheet
- **M&A** — initial bid frames the entire deal
- **Performance reviews** — last year's rating anchors this year's
- **Forecasting** — last quarter's number anchors next quarter's forecast

## Indicators you're falling into it

- Your estimate moved a lot when you re-read the question with a different starting number
- You can't justify your number except by reference to the anchor
- You opened with "well, they're asking X, so…"
- You're adjusting from a given figure rather than building up from fundamentals
- Negotiations end suspiciously close to wherever the first offer landed

## Countermeasures

- **Generate your own anchor first** — work out your fair value independently before hearing the counter-party's number (Pereira p.34)
- **Gather multiple anchors** — multiple reference points dilute any single one's pull
- **Establish objective criteria** — market comps, BATNA, intrinsic-value model
- **Use range estimation** — think in upper/lower bounds, not a single point
- **Delay judgment** — don't respond to a number on the spot
- **Counter-anchor in negotiation** — when offered a high anchor, name your own concrete low anchor; the midpoint shifts
- **Cognitive reappraisal** — explicitly imagine the question framed with a very different starting number
- **Reflect post-hoc** — review whether the final number suspiciously tracked the opening

## Canonical examples

- **Tversky & Kahneman UN experiment** — random spinner anchored UN-membership estimates (p.28)
- **Pereira's pricing-tier example** — Basic $50 / Standard $100 / Premium $200 pulls buyers to Standard (p.33)
- **Steve Jobs' iPad launch** — anchored expectations at "under $1000" then revealed $499
- **Real-estate listings** — list price anchors offers more than appraisals do
- **Legal damages** — plaintiff's stated ask anchors jury awards even when arbitrary (p.30)
- **VC term sheets** — first proposed valuation shapes the round
- **"$19.99" pricing** — anchors below the $20 mental threshold
