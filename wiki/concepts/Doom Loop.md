# Doom Loop

## Definition

The Doom Loop is the Flywheel Effect's mirror image: a self-reinforcing *negative* cycle in which momentum decays rather than compounds. Coined by Jim Collins and adopted by Pereira, the doom loop describes companies that lose focus, get complacent, ignore metrics, or repeatedly pivot — and discover that the same compounding dynamics that built their growth can dismantle it. Each missed signal accelerates churn, which inflates CAC, which forces cost cuts in customer success, which accelerates churn further. The danger is not the first turn but the asymmetric reversal: once the wheel slows, every component that previously reinforced growth now reinforces decline.

## Key attributes

- **Mirror of the virtuous cycle** — same loop, opposite direction
- **Asymmetric onset** — slow to start, accelerates once initiated
- **Common triggers** — loss of focus, complacency, short-termism, resistance to change, internal conflict
- **Compounding decay** — small misses become large misses through feedback
- **Often invisible until late** — early signals look like normal variance
- **Multiplies across stakeholders** — customers, employees, and investors all reinforce the spiral
- **Recovery is harder than prevention** — you must re-spin a heavier, slower wheel

## When to use (diagnostically)

- Diagnosing a business that was growing but has now stalled or declined
- Auditing leading indicators for early doom-loop signals (NPS slip, employee NPS slip, sales cycle stretch)
- Pre-mortem analysis before launching a strategic shift that risks loop disruption
- Board / leadership conversations about what would break the existing flywheel
- Post-mortem on a fallen competitor — usually a textbook doom loop

## When NOT to use

- As an excuse for normal cyclical variance (don't see doom in every quarterly miss)
- In categories where the underlying market is shrinking (that's a different problem)
- For pre-product-market-fit companies (they haven't built a flywheel yet to break)

## Common triggers (per Pereira)

- **Lack of focus** — chasing too many initiatives at once, losing core purpose
- **Short-term thinking** — optimizing for the quarter at the expense of the wheel
- **Resistance to change** — refusing to adapt to new market or customer signals
- **Internal conflict** — political fights bleed energy from the flywheel inputs
- **Complacency** — assuming continued growth without continued investment
- **Strategy of the month** — repeatedly interrupting the wheel with new directions
- **CX disinvestment** — cutting customer success during cost squeezes (classic accelerant)

## How to avoid or escape (per Pereira, pages 39-42)

- **Keep close eye on metrics** — establish clear KPIs, review regularly, act on negative trends fast
- **Don't get complacent** — maintain growth mindset, challenge assumptions, keep learning
- **Be willing to pivot** — recognize underperformance early, change course with discipline
- **Focus on the customer** — re-center on CX as the load-bearing flywheel input
- **Stay adaptable** — culture of experimentation, calculated risk-taking, cross-functional collaboration
- **Cultivate singular leadership** — disciplined leadership re-spins the wheel; chaotic leadership stops it

## Key warning metrics

- **NPS decline** — the earliest signal; promoters becoming detractors
- **Cohort retention shifts** — newer cohorts retaining worse than older
- **NRR drops below 100%** — churn now exceeds expansion
- **CAC inflation** — paid acquisition rising as organic referral collapses
- **Sales cycle elongation** — buyers losing confidence
- **Employee NPS drop** — the people running the wheel are disengaging
- **Customer support ticket volume + resolution time** rising together

## Decision questions

- Which of our flywheel inputs is showing the earliest decay signal?
- Are we cutting in places that accelerate the loop's reversal (CS, R&D, content)?
- Have we had three or more strategic pivots in 18 months? (loop interruption)
- Is leadership singular and disciplined, or fragmented?
- What would it cost to re-spin the wheel if we let it stop entirely?

## Canonical examples

- **Sears / Kmart** — classic retail doom loop: cost cuts → CX decay → traffic loss → more cost cuts
- **BlackBerry** — feature-loop dominance reversed when iPhone broke the developer/user flywheel
- **Yahoo (mid-2010s)** — repeated pivots interrupted any flywheel from compounding
- **WeWork (2019)** — over-extension and governance breakdown reversed the growth flywheel
- **Generic enterprise software incumbents** — slow customer success disinvestment is the textbook trigger
