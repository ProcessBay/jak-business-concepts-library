# Characteristics of Transitional Business Models

## Definition

Pereira names four traits that distinguish a transitional business model from a standard one. They are diagnostic — if a model under consideration shows most of these, it's likely transitional (and should be treated as a phase, not a destination). Together they explain why new entrants tend to win these phases and why incumbents struggle: the model is engineered around emerging customer demand, it tends to violate the rules the incumbent operates under, and it leans heavily on brand and adaptability rather than installed-base inertia.

## Key attributes

- **Customer-designed** — built around what's best for the audience, not what's easiest for the firm; easier for new entrants to deploy, harder for incumbents who must abandon status quo
- **Regulation-contesting** — the innovation usually violates current constraints; courts and regulators get pulled in (Uber vs. transportation law worldwide)
- **Brand-reinforcing pre-market** — a strong brand can create demand for a product before the market is ready (Amazon drones — we already associate drone delivery with Amazon before it's widely deployed)
- **Easily adaptable** — built to absorb the shifts the market unfolds; it doesn't predict the future, it stays flexible enough to respond to behavior, technology, and regulation changes
- **Universally applicable** — Pereira's broader claim: every business is transitional at some point; experimentation is the only way to optimize production, predict desires, and discover advantages

## When to use

- As a diagnostic checklist when deciding whether a planned model is transitional or terminal
- When evaluating whether your firm (incumbent or entrant) is well-positioned to run a given transitional model
- When briefing a board or team on why a temporary, customer-led, possibly-illegal-at-first model is the right play
- When studying a competitor's move to determine if it's a phase or their end-state strategy

## When NOT to use

- As a definition substitute — these are traits, not the definition (see [[Transitional Business Model]])
- To justify breaking laws that have hard enforcement (not just contested grey areas)
- As permission to neglect operational discipline because "we're just adapting"

## Common pitfalls

- Confusing "easily adaptable" with "no plan" — adaptability is a deliberate design property, not an excuse for chaos
- Assuming regulatory friction is always survivable — Uber survived, many others didn't
- Over-leveraging brand for pre-market demand without the operational capability to deliver
- Treating customer-designed as "give them what they ask for" instead of designing around their underlying need

## Key metrics

- **Adoption speed** in target segment (early signal of customer-fit)
- **Regulatory friction events** and resolution time/cost
- **Unaided brand recall** for the future offering (brand-reinforcement check)
- **Adaptation cycles** — how many product/model iterations the firm can run per year

## Decision questions

- Which of the four traits does our planned model exhibit, and which does it lack?
- If we're an incumbent — what status quo do we need to abandon to make this work?
- What regulations will this model contest, and is the contest winnable?
- Does our brand carry enough weight to seed pre-market demand, or are we starting from zero?
- How fast is our adaptation loop, and what's slowing it down?

## Canonical examples

- **Uber** — regulation-contesting trait in pure form: didn't fit any existing transportation framework globally, challenged laws and courts as the only path to establishment (page 8)
- **Amazon drones** — brand-reinforcing-pre-market trait: the service is associated with Amazon before drone delivery is commercially proliferated (page 9)
- **Tesla, Netflix, Facebook** — customer-designed and easily-adaptable traits in combination (pages 10-19)
