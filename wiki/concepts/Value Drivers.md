# Value Drivers

## Definition

Value drivers are the specific features, attributes, or benefits that create value for customers — the underlying levers that determine whether they choose, retain, and pay a premium for an offering. Pereira frames value-driver identification as Step 4 of the Customer Value Creation Model: after defining the experience, the firm must identify which drivers matter most and prioritize them in product or service development. Identification typically uses five inputs: financial data analysis, customer feedback, competitor data, industry trends, and SWOT analysis.

## Key attributes

- **Customer-perceived, not internally-declared** — value drivers are what the customer values, not what the firm thinks should be valued
- **Hierarchy of importance** — drivers must be ranked; you can't optimize for all of them simultaneously
- **Quantifiable impact** — each driver should be testable for its effect on a customer outcome (NPS, retention, WTP)
- **Multi-source identification** — uses financial data, customer feedback, competitor benchmarks, industry trends, internal SWOT
- **Dynamic** — drivers shift as customer needs evolve and competitors copy
- **Distinct from features** — a driver is a category (e.g., convenience); features are how it's delivered

## When to use

- Allocating product development resources across competing priorities
- After collecting customer feedback that's too diffuse to act on directly
- When pricing power is eroding and the firm needs to understand why
- During competitive repositioning
- As Step 4 of a Customer Value Creation Model exercise

## When NOT to use

- Before defining target customer and value proposition (you'll get noise)
- When the team confuses "we think it matters" with "the customer told us it matters"
- For pure operational decisions disconnected from customer outcomes

## Common pitfalls

- Mistaking what the team WANTS to be the driver for what actually drives customer behavior
- Listing too many drivers — diffuses focus; usually 3-5 dominant drivers explain most behavior
- Confusing absolute value with marginal value — what matters is the driver where YOU can be uniquely strong
- Forgetting drivers vary by segment — niche customers and mass-market customers value different things
- Treating drivers as static when customer needs evolve quarterly

## Key metrics

- Customer-stated importance of each driver (survey)
- Revealed importance (regression of driver presence vs. retention/CSAT)
- Driver-specific NPS lift
- Competitive parity per driver (where we lead vs. lag)

## Decision questions

- What are the 3-5 drivers our target customer consistently mentions as deciding factors?
- For each driver, are we ahead of, at parity with, or behind our main competitor?
- Where should we invest to widen a lead vs. close a gap?
- Which drivers does the customer say matter but won't actually pay for?
- How have the dominant drivers in our segment shifted over the last two years?

## Canonical examples

- **Apple** — analyzes financial data to understand which products and services drive revenue and profitability, focused on cost reduction via operational efficiency (page 24)
- **Netflix** — uses customer surveys and viewing data to understand what content customers want and how they want to consume it, driving personalized recommendations and UX (page 25)
- **Tesla** — analyzes competitor data to identify differentiation opportunities, focused on high-performance electric vehicles with innovative features and strong brand identity (page 25)
- **Amazon** — identified the e-commerce trend and built fast delivery + selection + personalization as dominant drivers (page 25)
- **Nike** — used SWOT to identify drivers around innovation, sustainability, and brand identity (page 26)
