# Marketing Plan vs Marketing Strategy vs Sales Plan vs Business Plan

## Definition

Pereira distinguishes four planning documents that are routinely conflated. A **marketing strategy** is a blueprint that outlines the campaigns, content, channels, and tools to be utilized to attain the mission and goals of a business — the overarching plan of action. A **marketing plan** is the GPS that charts the business's journey, integrating one or more marketing strategies into a larger framework and adding the implementation details. A **sales plan** is a concise, focused approach for accomplishing revenue targets through selling — it is narrower than a marketing plan and focuses on conversion tactics. A **business plan** is a comprehensive document covering every aspect of the enterprise — physical location, staff, finances, marketing approach — typically offered to creditors or investors and revisited every 5 years.

## Key attributes

- **Marketing strategy = the WHY/WHAT** — overarching plan of action; presents the unique value proposition and reasoning
- **Marketing plan = the HOW/WHEN** — GPS that operationalizes the strategy with tactics, budgets, KPIs, and timelines
- **Sales plan = revenue tactics** — focused, concise; tactics, metrics, and goals to close revenue
- **Business plan = enterprise blueprint** — comprehensive; covers ops, finance, HR, legal, marketing
- **Time horizon differs** — business plan 5 years, marketing plan 1-3 years, sales plan often quarterly
- **Revision cadence differs** — business plan revised on major upheaval, marketing plan refined yearly with budget, sales plan monthly/quarterly
- **Audience differs** — business plan for creditors/investors; marketing plan for marketing/exec team; sales plan for sales team
- **All four interlock** — marketing plan is a strategic section of the business plan; sales plan and marketing plan are siblings

## When to use

- Use the **marketing strategy** when defining the company's positioning, value proposition, and channel philosophy
- Use the **marketing plan** when operationalizing that strategy into a 12-month or multi-year roadmap with budgets and KPIs
- Use the **sales plan** when defining quarterly revenue targets and the tactics to hit them
- Use the **business plan** when raising capital, applying for credit, or codifying the entire enterprise for governance
- Use all four when standing up a new venture so each layer of planning has its own document

## When NOT to use

- Don't substitute one for another — a business plan is not a marketing plan, and using it as one starves marketing execution of detail
- Don't write a marketing plan without a marketing strategy underneath it — you'll produce a list of tactics with no coherent positioning
- Don't write a sales plan independent of the marketing plan — funnel handoff and message consistency break
- Don't write a business plan once and never revisit — it should be updated when major shifts occur

## Common pitfalls

- **Treating "marketing strategy" and "marketing plan" as synonyms** — Pereira: the terms are often used interchangeably but represent different artifacts
- **Conflating sales plan with marketing plan** — sales = conversion-focused tactics; marketing = relationship-focused holistic approach
- **Building marketing plans disconnected from the business plan** — produces marketing aims orthogonal to enterprise goals
- **Five-year marketing plans** — wrong horizon; market shifts too fast
- **Quarterly business plans** — wrong horizon; business plan is the long-arc document
- **No version control** — without dated versions, teams argue over which document is current

## Key metrics

- **Document alignment** — do marketing-plan KPIs map cleanly to business-plan goals?
- **Sales-marketing alignment** — shared definitions of qualified lead, opportunity, win
- **Revision cadence adherence** — is the marketing plan actually being refreshed annually?

## Decision questions

- Which of the four documents are we currently using, and which are we missing?
- Does our marketing plan operationalize a defined marketing strategy, or is it untethered tactics?
- Do our sales plan and marketing plan share a definition of lead, MQL, SQL, and opportunity?
- Are marketing-plan objectives derived from business-plan SMART goals?
- When the business plan changes materially, do we revise the marketing plan in the same cycle?
