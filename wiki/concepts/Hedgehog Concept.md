# Hedgehog Concept

## Definition

The Hedgehog Concept is Jim Collins's principle (from *Good to Great*) that durable companies pick one simple, clear thing they can be best at and refuse to be distracted from it — like the hedgehog who knows one big thing rather than the fox who knows many. Pereira treats it as the fourth of six prerequisites for spinning the flywheel: without hedgehog focus, the organization tries to push the wheel in too many directions at once and never builds enough momentum in any one. The concept is the cure for the doom-loop trigger of "lack of focus" and the operating discipline that makes virtuous cycles possible.

## Key attributes

- **Singular focus** — one thing, done relentlessly, beats many things done partially
- **Intersection of three circles** (Collins's original) — what you're deeply passionate about, what you can be best in the world at, what drives your economic engine
- **Simplicity over sophistication** — the hedgehog answer is usually obvious once found, and hard to live by
- **Anti-distraction discipline** — saying no to good opportunities to protect the great one
- **Compounding by concentration** — momentum builds where effort isn't scattered
- **Cultural, not just strategic** — must be embraced by every level of the organization
- **Slow to identify** — most companies need months or years of honest debate to find their hedgehog

## When to use

- Defining strategy for a company struggling with too many priorities
- Re-spinning a flywheel after a doom-loop episode (focus restores momentum)
- Helping a leadership team say no to good-but-not-great opportunities
- Designing the "Find Your Edge" step of building a flywheel
- Diagnosing why a multi-product organization can't get any single product to dominate

## When NOT to use

- Pre-PMF stage where the hedgehog hasn't been discovered yet (premature commitment)
- Genuinely portfolio-structured businesses (Berkshire-style holding companies)
- Categories where being the *only* generalist is the differentiated position
- Crisis pivots where the prior hedgehog is structurally broken

## Common pitfalls

- Picking a hedgehog based on aspiration rather than evidence ("we want to be best at X")
- Confusing focus with stubbornness — refusing to adapt the hedgehog when reality shifts
- Naming a hedgehog at the leadership offsite, then operating against it on Monday
- Letting the largest customer or loudest stakeholder pull you off the hedgehog
- Mistaking diversification for resilience when it's actually loss of focus
- Picking a hedgehog that isn't true at all three circles (passion, best-in-world, economic engine)

## Key metrics

- **Initiative count** — how many active strategic bets? Hedgehogs have few
- **Revenue concentration** — % of revenue from the core hedgehog activity
- **Win rate in chosen arena** — should be high; if not, the hedgehog might be wrong
- **Talent allocation** — do your A-players sit on the hedgehog or are they scattered?
- **No-ratio** — for every yes, how many strategic nos? Hedgehogs have a high no-ratio

## Decision questions

- Can every employee state our hedgehog in one sentence, the same sentence?
- Are we genuinely best-in-the-world at our hedgehog, or just above average?
- Are we deeply passionate about it, or just doing it because it's profitable?
- Does it drive our economic engine, or is it a passion project we subsidize?
- What did we say no to last quarter to protect the hedgehog?
- If we removed every initiative outside the hedgehog, would we grow faster or slower?

## Canonical examples

- **In-N-Out Burger** — does burgers, fries, and shakes; refuses every menu-expansion temptation
- **Vanguard** — low-cost index funds and high-value customer service (page 30)
- **Costco** — bulk warehouse retail with thin margins, fanatical member loyalty
- **Southwest Airlines (historical)** — short-haul point-to-point on one aircraft type
- **Patagonia** — outdoor apparel from a sustainability-first perspective
- **Amazon (pre-AWS era)** — relentless focus on customer experience in e-commerce (page 25)
