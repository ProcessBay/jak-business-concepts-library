# Marketing Plan Anti-Patterns

## Definition

Pereira identifies a coherent set of failure modes that turn marketing plans into shelf-ware and waste marketing budget. They cluster into four families: **(1) planning failures** — drafted and archived, vague goals, no preparation, no internal feedback; **(2) funnel failures** — bottom-of-funnel obsession, distribution without message, ignoring the full journey; **(3) rigidity failures** — high fixed costs, no flexibility for new opportunities or competitive shifts, one-time annual exercise rather than continuous adaptation; **(4) measurement failures** — no KPIs, vanity metrics, no clearly-defined omissions. The unifying diagnosis: a marketing plan that is a static document rather than a continuously operated artifact will under-deliver, regardless of how well-written it is.

## Key attributes

- **Drafted and archived** — most common failure mode; plan written for the approval meeting, never opened again
- **Vague goals** — "increase return rate," "get more social media followers" instead of measurable, time-bound targets
- **Bottom-of-funnel obsession** — focusing only on conversion because metrics are easier; starves the top of the pipeline
- **Distribution-only thinking** — pouring effort into channels while neglecting a differentiated, evaluated message
- **No internal feedback** — written by marketing leadership in isolation; sales, product, customer service insights missed
- **Rigid plan with high fixed costs** — deposits, minimum spends that lock the team in; can't respond to a new competitor
- **One-time annual exercise** — Pereira: marketing planning "should be something the head of marketing leads throughout the year"
- **No omissions section** — plan doesn't declare what it will *not* address; scope creeps and accountability dissolves

## When to use

- Pre-mortem on a marketing plan before submitting for approval — scan for each anti-pattern
- Post-mortem on a campaign or quarter that under-delivered
- New CMO assessment — diagnose existing marketing-planning maturity
- Coaching marketers building their first plan
- Annual planning kickoff — establish guardrails to avoid these patterns

## When NOT to use

- As a checklist for compliance theater — the goal is behavior change, not box-ticking
- To assign blame after a campaign failure — patterns are systemic, not individual

## Common pitfalls

(All patterns are themselves pitfalls — see Key attributes. Additional meta-pitfalls:)

- **Treating the plan as the goal** — the plan is a tool; results are the goal
- **Confusing activity with progress** — a plan full of activities ≠ a plan that drives outcomes
- **Optimism bias in projections** — most plans assume everything works; few have downside scenarios
- **Skipping the executive summary** — losing stakeholder attention in the first page
- **No declared budget ceiling** — open-ended spend invites scope creep

## Key metrics

- **Plan adherence rate** — are we executing what we planned, or improvising?
- **Plan revision frequency** — refreshed quarterly, or once a year?
- **Goal specificity score** — what % of plan goals are SMART vs. vague?
- **Funnel coverage** — what % of budget supports awareness/interest vs. conversion only?
- **Internal-feedback evidence** — is sales/product/CS input documented in the plan?

## Decision questions

- Will this plan be opened more than once after approval — or is it shelfware?
- Are our goals SMART, or do they include any vague aspirations?
- What % of our budget supports the top of the funnel?
- Have we invested as much in message as in distribution?
- Have sales, product, and customer service reviewed and contributed?
- Where is our plan rigid in a way that prevents response to a new competitor?
- What has the plan declared it will *not* address?

## Canonical examples

- **Drafted and archived** — Pereira: "a marketing plan is not a mere document to be drafted and archived" (page 10)
- **Vague goals** — Pereira: "Do not include vague targets like 'increase return rate' or 'get more social media followers' in your plan" (page 54)
- **Bottom-of-funnel obsession** — "The lure of honing in on the lower grades of the sales funnel — where quantitative metrics tend to be more discernible — is a common one" (page 47)
- **Distribution-only** — "The tendency of marketers to concentrate solely on the mediums of distribution, without proper consideration for the development of a compelling and differentiated message, is a frequent occurrence" (page 48)
- **No internal feedback** — "The process of crafting a marketing plan must not only be confined to upper-level management" (page 48)
- **Rigid plan** — "Having a very rigid plan with high fixed costs like deposits or minimum spending can lock you into plans and create difficult situations" (page 48)
- **One-time annual exercise** — "Marketing planning is so vital that it should not be a one-time, annual activity" (page 49)
- **No omissions section** — "Every marketing strategy should outline the priorities of the marketing department. Yet more clarity is required, precisely what the marketing department will not emphasize" (page 66)
