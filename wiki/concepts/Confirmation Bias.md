# Confirmation Bias

## Definition

Confirmation bias is the tendency to seek out, favor, and remember information that confirms preexisting beliefs while disregarding or downplaying evidence that contradicts them. People cherry-pick evidence to support what they already believe, creating a self-reinforcing loop that strengthens existing views without critical reassessment. Pereira frames it as both conscious (actively seeking validating sources) and unconscious (the mind filtering disconfirming information to avoid cognitive dissonance), and notes that the internet and social-media algorithms have industrialized it via echo chambers.

## Key attributes

- **Selective search** — actively looks for confirming evidence and ignores or fails to seek disconfirming evidence
- **Selective interpretation** — ambiguous data gets read in the direction of the prior belief
- **Selective recall** — confirming instances are remembered, disconfirming ones forgotten
- **Operates consciously and unconsciously** — even people aware of it succumb when emotionally invested
- **Self-reinforcing loop** — each "confirmation" raises confidence in the prior, making future disconfirmation harder to accept
- **Amplified by echo chambers** — social-media personalization filters out opposing viewpoints
- **Cross-domain** — politics, investing, hiring, research, relationships all show it

## When you're most at risk

- You hold a belief with emotional or identity weight (politics, your company's strategy, a hire you championed)
- You're consuming a personalized feed (social media, algorithmic news) rather than a curated source
- You're surrounded by people who share your priors (founding team, partisan circle)
- You're under time pressure and reaching for an existing mental model
- You've publicly committed to a position and changing your mind would cost face

## When it's NOT a problem

- Operating on well-established settled science where seeking "alternative views" is conspiracy-mining
- Tactical execution after a decision is locked — second-guessing every step is its own dysfunction
- Domains where your priors come from genuine deep expertise and the disconfirming "evidence" is noise

## Common manifestations in business

- **Investing**: clinging to an initial thesis on a stock; refusing to sell losers despite warning signs (pages 14-15)
- **Hiring**: forming a snap impression in the first minute and spending the rest of the interview confirming it
- **Strategy**: leaders favoring evidence that their pet strategy is working; dismissing employee dissent
- **Product**: founders who only talk to users that love the product and dismiss critical feedback
- **Research and analysis**: scientists publishing positive results and burying inconclusive ones (page 16)
- **Org politics**: managers selectively remembering an employee's misses to justify a predetermined performance review

## Indicators you're falling into it

- You can't easily articulate the strongest argument against your position
- Disconfirming data feels viscerally annoying rather than interesting
- You find yourself dismissing critics by attacking their motives rather than their argument
- Every new fact you encounter conveniently supports what you already thought
- You haven't changed your mind on anything material in months
- Your information diet is monocultural (one source type, one ideological direction)

## Countermeasures

- **Seek disconfirming evidence deliberately** — write down "what would change my mind?" before acting; then look for that (Pereira p.18)
- **Assign a devil's advocate** — in group decisions, formally task someone with arguing the other side
- **Diversify information sources** — read at least one source you reflexively distrust
- **Practice Bayesian updating** — treat beliefs as probabilities and adjust visibly with new data (p.18-19)
- **Use structured decision frameworks** — cost-benefit, SWOT, pre-mortem force consideration of counter-evidence
- **Cultivate intellectual humility** — say "I might be wrong" and mean it; reward team members who change their minds
- **Take breaks before decisions** — fresh perspective reduces grip of the prior
- **Review past decisions** — keep a decision journal; revisit it to spot patterns

## Canonical examples

- **Investing**: holding losing positions because every negative signal is rationalized as temporary (p.15)
- **Political echo chambers**: voters consuming only same-side media, polarization intensifying (p.14, 112)
- **Wrongful convictions**: police focusing on a suspect that fits a stereotype, ignoring contradicting evidence (p.15)
- **Theranos**: investors and the board interpreting every ambiguous signal as confirming the company's narrative
- **Kodak**: leadership reading market signals as supporting their film-centric strategy long after digital was obviously winning
- **Scientific replication crisis**: publication bias toward positive results (p.16)
