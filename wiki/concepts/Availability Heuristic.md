# Availability Heuristic

## Definition

The availability heuristic is the mental shortcut of judging the likelihood or frequency of an event by how easily examples come to mind. The brain assumes that if something is easy to retrieve from memory, it must be more probable or more common — which gets it wrong whenever memory is biased by recency, vividness, or emotional intensity rather than by actual base rates. Pereira identifies media coverage, personal anecdote, and emotionally charged events as the main sources of distortion.

## Key attributes

- **Ease-of-recall = perceived probability** — the brain conflates memorability with frequency
- **Recency-weighted** — recent events dominate retrieval
- **Vividness-weighted** — dramatic, emotional events are more easily recalled than dry statistical ones
- **Media-amplified** — news prioritizes the sensational, distorting the available pool
- **Anecdote beats statistics** — one vivid story crowds out a thousand data points
- **Self-reinforcing** — the more you hear about something, the more "common" it feels
- **Interacts with other biases** — reinforces representativeness, confirmation, and bandwagon effects

## When you're most at risk

- A dramatic event just happened (plane crash, layoff, IPO pop, product recall)
- You're consuming news rather than data
- You're making a probability or risk estimate without explicit base rates in front of you
- You're early in your career and your sample size is small
- You're surrounded by people who all have the same recent reference experience

## When it's NOT a problem

- The available examples are genuinely representative of the underlying distribution
- Domains where high-quality statistical data doesn't exist and recent vivid cases really are the best information you have
- Pattern-matching from deep, broad expertise (where "what comes to mind" reflects thousands of cases, not three)

## Common manifestations in business

- **Overestimating startup success odds** because the press covers winners, not the 90% that died (p.21)
- **Overweighting recent customer feedback** — the last loud customer drives the roadmap
- **Risk asymmetry**: fearing a competitor that just made news while ignoring a quieter structural threat
- **Investment FOMO**: chasing the asset class your friend just made money on (p.23)
- **Hiring**: weighing one impressive project on the resume over a 10-year track record
- **Crisis response**: budgeting against the last crisis instead of the next probable one

## Indicators you're falling into it

- You can name vivid examples but can't cite the base rate
- Your risk assessment shifted significantly after a single news story
- You're making decisions using "I just read/saw…" as the key input
- The team keeps invoking the same one or two recent cases as the model
- You estimate frequencies in "feels like" rather than numbers

## Countermeasures

- **Demand base rates** — before estimating likelihood, ask "out of all comparable cases, what fraction…?"
- **Look up actual statistics** — fact-check the vivid example against the population (Pereira p.25-26)
- **Seek the unwritten cases** — for every success story, ask how many tried and failed (cf. survivorship bias)
- **Slow down** — availability bias spikes under time pressure; sleep on it
- **Track decisions over time** — a decision journal exposes recency patterns
- **Diversify input sources** — don't let one media diet shape your sense of "what's happening"
- **Use frameworks** — SWOT, pre-mortem, expected-value calculation force base-rate thinking
- **Calibrate against forecasters** — compare your gut probability to data or a prediction market

## Canonical examples

- **Fear of flying vs. driving** — plane crashes are vivid and rare; car accidents are common and forgotten (p.22)
- **Food recalls** — a single recall drives consumers to avoid an entire brand long after risk is gone (p.22-23)
- **Investment manias** — vivid winner stories pull capital into asset classes well past peak (p.23)
- **Stereotyping** — media portrayals shape beliefs about whole demographics (p.23-24)
- **Medical self-diagnosis** — frequently mentioned diseases get over-diagnosed by patients (p.24)
- **Post-9/11** — Americans drove instead of flying, causing thousands of additional road deaths
- **Shark attacks** — drive billions in beach-economy decisions despite microscopic actual risk
