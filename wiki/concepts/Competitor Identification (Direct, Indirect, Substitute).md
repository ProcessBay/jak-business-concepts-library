# Competitor Identification (Direct, Indirect, Substitute)

## Definition

Pereira's competitor typology defines three competitor categories that every analysis must consider: **Direct competitors** offer identical or substitutable products to the same target customers in the same geographic region (e.g., McDonald's vs. Wendy's vs. Burger King). **Indirect / Secondary competitors** offer different products but compete for the same customer need or fall within the same general category (e.g., a winery and a brewery — different products, same "alcoholic beverage" need; or two clothing subscriptions targeting the same audience but with different specializations). **Substitute competitors** exist outside the normal product category but satisfy the same underlying need (e.g., frozen meals and grocery takeout as substitutes for fast food — different category, same "convenience meal" job). Missing any of the three creates blind spots; substitute competitors are the most commonly overlooked and often the most disruptive.

## Key attributes

- **Three competitor classes** — direct, indirect/secondary, substitute
- **Direct = same product + same customer** — most obvious; usually well-tracked
- **Indirect = different product + same general category** — moderately well-tracked; can become direct over time
- **Substitute = outside category + same underlying need** — frequently missed; often the disruptor
- **Selection lenses** — Pereira recommends selecting competitors via three filters: **Customer (Who)**, **Problem (What)**, and **Product Category (How)**
- **Migration risk** — indirect competitors can transition into direct competitors over time
- **Customer-need framing** — substitute competitors are best surfaced by asking customers what they use when your product isn't available
- **Geographic dimension** — direct competition is partly geographic; online businesses often compete globally where physical ones don't

## When to use

- At the beginning of any competitive analysis — competitor set definition shapes everything downstream
- When entering a new market — need to map the full competitor landscape
- After a market disruption — substitute competitors often emerge from adjacent categories
- During customer-loss reviews — losses to substitutes signal a category shift
- When defining the BCG-matrix or Strategic-Group-Analysis market boundaries
- Before sales-tactic or pricing decisions — different competitor types require different responses

## When NOT to use

- As a one-time taxonomy — competitor categories shift; indirect can become direct quickly
- For mature, well-mapped categories where the set is stable and well-known
- When the team treats the categories as rigid (real competitors often span multiple types)
- Without customer input — internal classifications often miss substitutes customers actually use

## Common pitfalls

- **Substitute blindness** — defaulting to direct-competitor lists and missing category-killers (Netflix vs. DVDs, Uber vs. taxis)
- **Confusing direct with indirect** — Pereira's clothing subscription example: two subscription clothing brands targeting the same audience may still be *indirect* if one focuses on workout attire and the other on everyday outfits
- **Geographic over-narrowness** — defining direct competition only as same-city when customers shop online globally
- **Static set** — failing to refresh the competitor list as adjacent companies pivot toward your space
- **Ignoring new entrants** — Pereira notes new entrants can compete by offering "innovative products or services that appeal directly or indirectly"
- **Treating substitute threat as low-probability** — substitutes often disrupt fastest when underlying need is well-defined but category boundary is arbitrary

## Key metrics

- **Direct competitor count** — typically 5-10 in most categories
- **Indirect competitor count** — typically 3-5 across adjacent categories
- **Substitute count** — typically 2-5; small but high-disruption potential
- **Set completeness score** — % of customer-mentioned alternatives captured
- **Substitute-as-% of churn** — customers lost to substitutes vs. direct competitors
- **Refresh interval** — quarterly minimum

## Decision questions

- Who do our customers consider when they're not buying from us?
- What underlying job/need does our product perform — and what else could perform that job?
- Which indirect competitor is closest to becoming a direct competitor?
- What category-adjacent companies are pivoting toward our space?
- If a substitute became 50% cheaper or 50% easier tomorrow, would customers switch?
- Are we tracking substitutes with the same rigor as direct competitors?

## Canonical examples

- **McDonald's competitor set** — direct: Wendy's and Burger King (same product, same customer); indirect: any other fast-food chain; substitute: frozen meals and grocery takeout (different category, same convenience-meal need) (pages 113-115)
- **Sony vs. Nintendo (direct), Microsoft Xbox (direct), mobile gaming and streaming media (indirect)** — Pereira walks through the gaming console competitor set explicitly (page 16)
- **Winery and brewery as secondary/indirect competitors** — different products, same general alcohol-production-and-sale category (page 114)
- **Subscription clothing brands** — two subscription clothing brands serving similar audiences would still be indirect if one focuses on everyday outfits and the other on workout attire (page 110)
- **Netflix and alternative movie-watching services** — substitute competitor scenario where customers use alternatives when Netflix isn't accessible (page 115)
