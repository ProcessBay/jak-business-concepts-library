# Framing Effect

## Definition

The framing effect is the tendency for the same objective information to produce different judgments depending on how it's presented — whether emphasized as a gain or a loss, a high success rate or its equivalent failure rate, a discount or a surcharge. Pereira traces the concept to Kahneman and Tversky's prospect theory, which earned Kahneman the 2002 Nobel in Economics. Framing operates through two key principles: people are risk-averse when contemplating gains but risk-seeking when contemplating losses, and they evaluate outcomes relative to a reference point rather than in absolute terms.

## Key attributes

- **Same information, different decisions** — "90% survival" vs "10% mortality" elicit different choices despite being identical
- **Positive frames evoke risk-aversion** — people lock in sure gains
- **Negative frames evoke risk-seeking** — people gamble to avoid a sure loss
- **Reference-point dependent** — outcomes are judged relative to a benchmark, not in absolute terms
- **Emotionally mediated** — positive frames trigger hope/optimism, negative frames trigger fear/anxiety
- **Context-sensitive** — "20% off" vs "buy-one-get-one" differ even at identical economics (p.100)
- **Used deliberately by marketers, politicians, media** — framing is a craft, not just a bias

## When you're most at risk

- You're shown information by someone with an interest in your decision
- The decision involves risk or uncertainty
- You're under time pressure to decide
- You're emotionally activated (fear, hope, urgency)
- You haven't seen the data presented from multiple angles
- A familiar reference point is being invoked (last year's number, the competitor's price)

## When it's NOT a problem

- All parties understand the frame and use it deliberately
- The frame happens to match the decision-relevant lens (e.g., loss-framing is appropriate when downside genuinely matters more)
- You're consciously using a frame to communicate, while internally evaluating un-framed data
- The frame matches your strategic positioning intentionally

## Common manifestations in business

- **Pricing**: "$1/day" vs "$365/year" — same price, very different appeal
- **Performance reporting**: "we grew 12%" vs "we missed plan by 8%" — same outcome
- **Investment options**: "70% chance of full payout" vs "30% chance of zero" — same math
- **Insurance**: framed as protection (gain frame) sells; framed as cost (loss frame) doesn't
- **Marketing copy**: "90% effective" vs "10% failure rate" — Pereira p.96
- **Negotiation**: "I'll give you X" vs "you'll get X" — first frames as gift, second as entitlement
- **Health communication**: surgery success-rate framing changes patient choices (p.99)
- **A/B testing of pricing tiers** — frame-driven, not value-driven, conversion shifts
- **Public policy**: "tax cut" vs "deficit increase" — same policy

## Indicators you're falling into it

- You haven't actively considered the inverse frame
- Your decision changed when someone re-stated the same information differently
- You can't articulate the data in neutral, frame-free terms
- The choice "feels obvious" but the underlying math is ambiguous
- You're reacting more to the language than to the numbers

## Countermeasures

- **Frame flipping** — actively restate the information in the opposite frame; see if your preference holds (Pereira p.102)
- **Strip to neutral data** — re-express the choice as raw numbers/probabilities without valence
- **Consider multiple frames** — gain frame, loss frame, status-quo frame; check stability of preference
- **Perspective-taking** — how would someone with different priors see this?
- **Long-term reframing** — extend the time horizon; short-term frames often distort
- **Slow down** — framing effects spike under time pressure
- **Use decision frameworks** — cost-benefit, expected value, scenario planning force frame-neutral thinking
- **Solicit alternative framings before deciding** — institutionalize devil's advocacy

## Canonical examples

- **Tversky & Kahneman Asian Disease problem** — saving 200 of 600 vs losing 400 of 600 flips choices
- **Pereira's laundry-detergent example** — "90% effective at stains" beats "10% ineffective" (p.97)
- **Apple/Spotify pricing** — daily/monthly framing
- **Retail "was $400, now $200"** — anchored loss-avoidance frame
- **"9 out of 10 dentists recommend"** — framing as social proof in a positive frame
- **Surgery framing in patient consent** — 90% survival vs 10% mortality (p.96)
- **Political "death tax" vs "estate tax"** — same policy, different polling
- **"Free shipping over $50"** — frames spending more as gaining something free
