# Loss Aversion

## Definition

Loss aversion, introduced by Kahneman and Tversky as a foundation of prospect theory, is the tendency to weigh losses roughly twice as heavily as equivalent gains. Losing $100 hurts more than gaining $100 feels good, so people make systematically risk-averse choices to avoid losses — including avoiding good opportunities, holding losers too long, and refusing to let go of possessions they'd never have bought at the same price. Pereira treats it as the upstream mechanism for several other biases (sunk cost, endowment, status quo), and frames it as the bias most likely to make a competent person make a stupid decision.

## Key attributes

- **Asymmetric weighting** — losses feel ~2x as intense as equivalent gains
- **Drives risk-aversion in the gain domain** — sure $50 beats coin-flip for $0/$110
- **Drives risk-seeking in the loss domain** — gamble to avoid certain $50 loss
- **Foundational to prospect theory** — the empirical violation of classical utility theory
- **Upstream of sunk-cost, endowment, status-quo biases** — they all extend loss-aversion logic
- **Activated by reference-point shifts** — what counts as "loss" depends on the baseline
- **Cross-domain** — money, possessions, time, status, relationships, decisions made

## When you're most at risk

- A decision is framed in terms of what you might lose
- You're protecting an existing position (incumbent CEO, current portfolio, current product)
- The reference point is your peak (peak revenue, peak valuation, peak hire)
- You're being asked to give something up for something better
- The outcome is uncertain and the downside is vivid
- You're operating from scarcity rather than abundance

## When it's NOT a problem

- Some downside protection is genuinely prudent (downside really is asymmetric)
- Loss-aversion-shaped product features (free trials, money-back guarantees) reduce buyer friction legitimately
- In domains where losses really are more consequential than gains (capital preservation, safety-critical)
- Negotiating tactics that exploit your counter-party's loss aversion (when done ethically)

## Common manifestations in business

- **Disposition effect**: investors selling winners too early, holding losers too long (p.105)
- **Refusal to cut failing products** — losing the existing customer base feels worse than gaining new ones
- **Status-quo strategic paralysis** — incumbents fail to disrupt themselves
- **Endowment-effect overpricing** — founders/sellers overvalue what they own (p.105)
- **Sunk-cost continuation** — driven by aversion to recognizing the loss (p.106)
- **Pricing — free trials work** — losing the access you've sampled feels worse than paying
- **Negotiation deadlock** — both sides anchored on their reference point, refusing concessions
- **Hiring** — refusing to fire bad hires because firing makes the bad hire "real"
- **M&A — refusal to walk away** from a deal after announcement because deal failure is publicly humiliating

## Indicators you're falling into it

- The downside dominates your mental calculation even when the math doesn't justify it
- You're holding onto something you wouldn't acquire today at current price
- You can articulate what you'd lose vividly; what you'd gain only vaguely
- You're choosing certainty even when expected value clearly favors the gamble
- Negotiation is stuck because you can't accept a "loss" from a reference point you set yourself

## Countermeasures

- **Reframe choices as gains** — "what's the upside?" rather than "what could go wrong?" (Pereira p.106)
- **Strip the reference point** — would you make this choice as a fresh decision today?
- **Use expected value calculations** — force the math into the open; weight outcomes by probability not by emotional valence
- **Pre-commit decision rules** — set stop-losses, kill criteria, review triggers in advance
- **Diversify** — spreading risk reduces the felt magnitude of any one loss (p.106)
- **Education and awareness** — knowing loss-aversion's ~2x ratio helps you discount your own gut by it
- **Loss inoculation** — practice making small accepted losses to build tolerance
- **External perspective** — someone with no skin in the existing position sees the choice cleanly
- **Long time horizons** — losses sting most in the moment; widening the lens reduces grip

## Canonical examples

- **Kahneman & Tversky coin-flip experiments** — people demand ~$200 gain to accept a $100 loss gamble
- **Endowment-effect mug experiments** — owners demand 2x the price buyers will pay (p.105)
- **Disposition effect in trading** — well-documented across retail investors (p.105)
- **Kodak, Blockbuster, Nokia** — all could see the future but loss-aversion to existing cash flows prevented action
- **SaaS free trials** — convert better than free demos because losing access > never having access
- **Retail return policies** — loose returns dramatically reduce purchase friction (loss-aversion compensated)
- **Health insurance opt-in vs opt-out enrollment** — defaults work because loss-aversion to the default option dominates
- **Salary cuts vs forgone raises** — cuts feel catastrophic, missed raises shrug-worthy, even when economically equivalent
