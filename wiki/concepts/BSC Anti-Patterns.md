# BSC Anti-Patterns

## Definition

BSC anti-patterns are the recurring failure modes Pereira and Kaplan & Norton document across decades of BSC implementations: KPI overload, metric paralysis, perspective imbalance, template copying, weak leadership sponsorship, and gaming behavior driven by misaligned incentives. Pereira's source devotes pages 60–62 explicitly to criticisms and surfaces additional warnings throughout the implementation chapters. The pattern: BSC fails not because the framework is wrong, but because organizations dilute its discipline — measuring everything, copying others, or letting executives delegate ownership.

## Key attributes

- **KPI overload** — FranklinCovey citation: 3 goals → all achieved; 4–10 → one or two; 10+ → none (page 33)
- **Metric paralysis** — Pereira: "when too many metrics are monitored, progress is often stalled" (page 32)
- **All-lagging measurement** — over-indexing on what's easy to count (revenue, headcount) and ignoring leading drivers (page 33)
- **Treating perspectives as parallel silos** — Pereira: "fundamentally wrong"; perspectives form a causal stack (page 34)
- **Template copying** — Kaplan & Norton: scorecard "should be unique in the industry"; copying defeats the model (page 14, 16, 60)
- **Weak leadership sponsorship** — BSC needs all levels of management to support it; absent buy-in, it dies (page 60)
- **Data fatigue** — managers and teams resent the documentation overhead and stop feeding the system (page 61–62)
- **Compensation-coupled KPIs that induce gaming** — Pereira: "bad KPIs are ones that make your team think about how to cheat the system instead of how to improve the product" (page 50)
- **Treating BSC as a measurement system** rather than a strategic management system — Kaplan & Norton's repeated warning (page 16)
- **Operational metrics smuggled into the scorecard** — they belong in operational reporting, not strategic KPI lists (page 32: operational measures are "housekeeping," not KPIs)

## When to use (this anti-pattern lens)

- Auditing an existing BSC that has stopped driving behavior
- Diagnosing why a 6-month-old scorecard has become "wallpaper"
- Pre-mortem before launching a new BSC build
- Reviewing cascaded scorecards across business units for inflation

## When NOT to use

- As an excuse not to build a BSC at all (the anti-patterns are avoidable)
- To dismiss BSC discipline in startups that genuinely need OKRs (use the right tool, don't strawman BSC)

## Common pitfalls (recursive — these traps repeat at every cascade level)

- **KPI sprawl on cascaded scorecards** — each unit adds its own KPIs; total count balloons across the org
- **Selective perspective coverage** — units skip Learning & Growth ("HR's problem") or Customer (internal teams)
- **Stale reviews** — "blue book" review cadence (every 3–4 months, page 27) skipped; scorecard becomes ceremonial
- **Consultant-built, never owned** — scorecard dies the day external facilitators leave
- **Executive theater** — leaders attend the launch workshop, then disengage; middle management is left holding it
- **Tracking everything because dashboards make it easy** — modern BI tools enable, not prevent, KPI overload

## Key metrics (warning signals)

- **KPI count > 25 at corporate level** — high risk of overload
- **Perspective imbalance** — any perspective < 10% of objectives, or any > 50%
- **Leading-vs-lagging ratio skewed lagging** (< 30% leading) — Pereira's warning (page 33)
- **Review cadence compliance < 75%** — scorecard is dying
- **Executive workshop attendance dropping quarter-over-quarter** — sponsorship is eroding
- **Number of KPIs tied to compensation** — every such linkage is a gaming-incentive risk (page 50)

## Decision questions (diagnostic)

- How many KPIs are on our corporate scorecard? On our cascaded scorecards? (If totals exceed FranklinCovey's "rule of 3," cull)
- For each KPI, is it leading or lagging? Can we actually influence it?
- When did our executive sponsor last personally engage with the scorecard?
- Are we measuring this because it matters, or because the data is easy?
- Which KPIs are tied to bonus/comp — and are people gaming them?
- Did we copy our perspective structure from another company without customizing?

## Canonical examples

- **"Kill another tree" program** — Pereira's quoted joke about one company's tendency to add policies/precautions in response to every consultant idea (page 12) — illustrates KPI/policy sprawl
- **Lagging-only strategic plans** — Pereira's observation that "most strategic plans focus on measures that are already behind schedule" because they're easy to count (page 33)
- **Weight-loss analogy** (page 33) — getting on the scale (lagging) tells you the result but does nothing to change it; tracking runs and meals (leading) is what actually moves the outcome
- **Project KPI gaming** — Pereira's warning against tying 100%-on-time delivery to compensation: incentivizes corner-cutting on quality (page 50)
- **Generic-template adoption failures** — Kaplan & Norton's repeated case studies of firms that copied another company's BSC and saw no behavior change
