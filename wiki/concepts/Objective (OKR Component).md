# Objective (OKR Component)

## Definition

An **Objective** is the qualitative half of an OKR — a specific, ambitious statement of what an organization, team, or individual aims to achieve in a cycle. Pereira frames it as the "what" and "why": it answers what needs to be done, why it matters, and provides a rallying point that creates shared vision and purpose (page 11). A well-crafted Objective is inspirational, directional, and meaningful — it is *not* a metric, *not* a task list, and *not* a vague platitude. It sits above the measurable Key Results, giving them context and emotional weight.

## Key attributes

- **Qualitative, not numeric** — the Objective inspires; the KRs measure
- **Specific and clear** — answers what, why, and (broadly) how; "increase sales" fails this test, "increase monthly sales revenue by 10% in Q3 by targeting new customer segments" passes (page 17)
- **Ambitious** — should push beyond the comfort zone; stretch is a design feature (pages 17-18)
- **Time-bound** — typically tied to a quarter or year, never open-ended (page 18)
- **Aligned upward** — laddered to org strategy and vision; cascading should make this visible (pages 22-24)
- **Memorable** — concise enough that team members can recall it without reading the doc
- **Limited in number** — 3-5 per person/team per cycle, no more

## When to use

- At the start of every OKR cycle, before drafting any Key Results
- During strategy translation — when leadership needs to convert vision into operational focus
- When a team needs realignment after a pivot or restructure
- For individual contributors setting their own quarterly focus

## When NOT to use

- Don't write an Objective for routine BAU work — keep OKRs for what genuinely needs focus
- Don't write Objectives as KPI dashboards — that's a metric, not an Objective
- Don't write Objectives copying last quarter's verbatim — refresh the framing

## Common pitfalls

- **Vagueness** — "Improve customer experience" gives no direction
- **Disguised tasks** — "Launch v2 of the app" is a deliverable; ask what *outcome* the launch is meant to produce
- **Sandbagging language** — "Maintain current performance" violates the stretch principle
- **Misalignment** — Objectives that don't ladder to any company priority
- **Over-specification** — collapsing the Objective and KR into one sentence ("Grow revenue 20%") strips the qualitative why

## Key metrics

- **Objective clarity score** — peer rating of whether the Objective is understandable without context
- **Alignment score** — % of team Objectives that explicitly map to a parent Objective
- **Number of Objectives per cycle** — soft cap of 3-5

## Decision questions

- Could a new team member read this Objective and explain what we're trying to do?
- Does this Objective inspire, or does it read as bureaucratic filler?
- Is this an Objective, or is it actually a Key Result hiding as one?
- Does this ladder to a higher-level Objective?
- If we hit this Objective, will anything meaningful change?

## Canonical examples

- **Airbnb** — "Create a world where anyone can belong anywhere" — aspirational, directional, multi-quarter (page 100)
- **Sales team** — "Increase market share by 15% in the next quarter by targeting untapped customer segments" (page 21)
- **Tech company vision** — "Revolutionize the way people communicate by building innovative, user-friendly communication tools that connect the world seamlessly" (page 19)
- **Toyota** — "Improve production efficiency while maintaining product quality" — cascaded into per-team manufacturing KRs (page 102)
- **Generic strong examples** (page 28) — "Increase customer satisfaction," "Improve employee engagement," "Launch a new product successfully"
