# Price Elasticity of Demand

## Definition

Price elasticity of demand (PED) measures how much the quantity demanded of a product changes in response to a change in its price. The formula: `PED = %ΔQuantity Demanded / %ΔPrice`. Pereira positions it as the foundational concept underneath every pricing decision: **elastic demand** means small price changes cause large quantity changes (substitutes available, demand is sensitive); **inelastic demand** means quantity barely moves when price changes (necessities, no substitutes, captive demand). Knowing whether your category is elastic or inelastic determines whether raising prices raises revenue or destroys it.

## Key attributes

- **Formula** — `E_d = %ΔQuantity / %ΔPrice`
- **|E_d| > 1 = elastic** — price change causes proportionally larger quantity change
- **|E_d| < 1 = inelastic** — price change causes smaller quantity change
- **Substitute availability is the main driver** — more substitutes → more elastic
- **Necessity vs. luxury** — necessities tend inelastic, luxuries elastic
- **Revenue direction depends on elasticity** — elastic: raise price → lose revenue; inelastic: raise price → gain revenue
- **Curve shape** — shallow curve = elastic; steep curve = inelastic

## When to use

- Before any meaningful price change (the diagnostic question)
- When designing dynamic pricing algorithms (elasticity drives the model)
- Modeling revenue impact of promotions or discounts
- Choosing between penetration and skimming launch strategies
- Segmenting customers by price sensitivity for differential pricing
- Defending price increases to leadership ("our category is inelastic, here's the data")

## When NOT to use

- As the only input — elasticity ignores brand equity, competitor response, channel dynamics
- For one-off purchase decisions with no historical price-quantity data
- In rapidly disrupted categories where past elasticity doesn't predict future
- For new products with no demand history (use proxies or willingness-to-pay studies instead)

## Common pitfalls

- Confusing short-run and long-run elasticity (long-run usually more elastic — customers find substitutes)
- Measuring elasticity from one segment and extrapolating to all
- Ignoring cross-elasticity (your price change affects substitute demand)
- Mistaking a temporary promo response for the underlying elasticity
- Acting on elasticity without considering competitor response

## Key metrics

- **Elasticity coefficient** (E_d), calculated per product, segment, region
- **Revenue change** = ΔPrice × Quantity at new price (vs. old)
- **Cross-elasticity** (response of your demand to competitor price change)
- **Substitution rate** (how easily customers switch)

## Decision questions

- Is our demand elastic or inelastic in our category?
- Do we have substitutes nearby that would make us elastic?
- If we raise price 10%, what's our best estimate of quantity change?
- Does elasticity vary meaningfully by segment? (Often yes — design for it.)
- Are we modeling long-run or short-run elasticity?

## Canonical examples

- **Necessities (milk, bread)** — inelastic; supermarkets price largely as cost-plus (page 237)
- **Luxury goods** — elastic; people cut first in recessions (page 237)
- **Airline tickets** — segmented elasticity; business travelers inelastic, leisure travelers elastic (page 165)
- **Pereira's airline example** — price $200→$250 cuts demand 20% (E_d = -0.8); price $200→$150 raises demand 40% (E_d = 1.6) (page 169)
- **Salt, gasoline** — classically inelastic in the short run
