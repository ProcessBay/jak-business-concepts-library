# Value Proposition

## Definition

A value proposition is the specific, unique benefit a firm offers customers that differentiates it from competitors — articulated from the customer's point of view, focused on benefits not features, and communicated in clear language. Pereira frames it as Step 2 in the Customer Value Creation Model: after identifying target customers, the firm must define what unique value it offers them. The proposition must be customer-focused (not company-focused), benefit-focused (not feature-focused), and clear (not jargon-laden). Tesla's value proposition — innovative, high-quality electric vehicles that are environmentally friendly and technologically advanced — illustrates the form.

## Key attributes

- **Customer-language** — written in words the customer would use, not internal jargon
- **Benefit-focused** — describes positive outcomes for the customer (save time, reduce cost, improve life), not product features
- **Specific and unique** — must articulate what only this firm offers and why it matters
- **Concise** — testable by whether a customer can repeat it after one exposure
- **Testable** — should be refined through customer feedback over time
- **Linked to evidence** — every claim should be backed by proof points the customer can verify

## When to use

- Designing a new offering before building it
- Refreshing positioning when sales win rates drop
- Standing up sales-enablement and marketing materials
- After customer research reveals current messaging doesn't land
- Pre-launch as the foundation for go-to-market

## When NOT to use

- As a one-time exercise — VPs decay as markets and customer needs shift
- Without customer input — internal-only VPs almost always miss
- As a substitute for product strength — clever positioning can't rescue a weak offering for long
- When the team can't agree on the target customer (fix that first)

## Common pitfalls

- Writing in feature-language ("our platform has 14 modules") instead of benefit-language ("teams ship 30% faster")
- Promising too many things — a VP that tries to mean everything means nothing
- Using superlatives without proof ("the best", "world-class", "innovative") — empty claims
- Failing to differentiate from competitors who can claim the same VP
- Never testing the VP with actual customers, only with internal stakeholders

## Key metrics

- Conversion rate on landing pages featuring the VP
- Customer recall of the VP one week after exposure
- Win rate in head-to-head deals
- Clarity score in user testing (can a stranger explain what you do after 30 seconds?)
- Internal alignment: do sales, marketing, product all describe the VP the same way?

## Decision questions

- Could our biggest competitor write the same value proposition for themselves? If yes, it's not differentiated.
- Does the proposition describe a benefit to the customer, or a feature of our product?
- What evidence do we offer that the proposition is true?
- Have we tested this with five real customers in the last 90 days?
- Can a new hire explain our value proposition without notes after their first week?

## Canonical examples

- **Tesla** — innovative, high-quality electric vehicles that are environmentally friendly and technologically advanced (page 32)
- **Amazon** — wide range of products at low prices with fast and convenient delivery, supported by a highly efficient supply chain (pages 28-29)
- **Patagonia** — high-quality, environmentally-friendly outdoor apparel from a company committed to sustainability (page 41)
