# Growth KPIs and Baselines

## Definition

Growth KPIs and baselines are the measurement infrastructure that distinguishes growth hacking from random tactic-trying. Pereira defines the discipline as a four-step loop: (1) define clear objectives aligned with business goals; (2) select SMART KPIs that directly reflect progress toward those objectives; (3) establish baselines from historical data and industry benchmarks; (4) monitor and track continuously, iterating based on results. Without baselines, you cannot tell whether a tactic worked. Without SMART KPIs, you cannot tell which objective the tactic served. Without alignment to business goals, you optimize the wrong thing. The growth hacker's job is to convert vague growth ambitions into a measurable system — and to defend that system against the gravitational pull of vanity metrics.

## Key attributes

- **Objective-rooted** — every KPI traces back to a stated business objective
- **SMART by construction** — Specific, Measurable, Attainable, Relevant, Time-bound
- **Actionable, not vanity** — KPIs that can be moved by specific actions
- **Baseline-anchored** — current performance documented before any intervention
- **Leading + lagging mix** — early-signal KPIs alongside outcome KPIs
- **AARRR-aligned** — typical growth KPI set maps to Acquisition, Activation, Retention, Revenue, Referral
- **Continuously reviewed** — KPIs evolve as business stage and market conditions change

## When to use

- Before launching any growth-hacking program (no baselines = no learning)
- When establishing a growth team's operating cadence
- During quarterly planning to align tactics to objectives
- When the team disagrees on whether a tactic worked
- When fundraising or board-reporting requires defensible growth metrics
- Whenever a "growth" project starts without a defined success metric

## When NOT to use

- This isn't a question of when not to measure; it's a question of avoiding common failure modes:
  - Don't measure metrics you won't act on
  - Don't add KPIs without retiring stale ones (KPI sprawl kills focus)
  - Don't treat KPIs as performance-review weapons (kills experimentation culture)

## Common pitfalls

- **Vanity metrics** — sign-ups, downloads, page views without conversion linkage
- **Lagging-only indicators** — measuring revenue but not the leading inputs that move it
- **Unrealistic targets** — disconnected from baseline; team disengages
- **KPI sprawl** — 30 KPIs nobody can act on; everything is "important"
- **No baseline documentation** — can't tell whether anything actually changed
- **Cross-functional misalignment** — marketing optimizes leads, sales optimizes deals, no shared metric
- **Set-and-forget** — KPIs from year one still in use at year three despite business evolution

## Key metrics

Pereira's canonical growth KPI set:
- **Acquisition** — new sign-ups, downloads, registrations, website traffic
- **Activation** — % of new users reaching the first-value moment
- **Retention** — cohort retention curves, churn rate, DAU/MAU
- **Revenue** — ARPU, AOV, MRR, ARR, LTV
- **Referral** — viral coefficient (K), referrals per user
- **Efficiency** — CAC, CAC payback period, LTV:CAC ratio, ROI on growth spend

## Decision questions

- What business objective does this KPI directly serve?
- Is the KPI SMART, or vaguely directional?
- What is our current baseline, and where was it documented?
- Do we have both leading and lagging indicators for this objective?
- Will we actually take action when this KPI moves — and what action?
- Which of our current KPIs should we retire?

## Canonical examples

- **Facebook's "7 friends in 10 days"** — single activation KPI that defined product strategy
- **Slack's "2,000 messages sent"** — activation threshold tied to team retention (page 107-108)
- **Dropbox's "store 1 file across 3 devices"** — activation behavior defining stickiness (page 99-100)
- **Airbnb's "host responds within 24 hours"** — operational KPI that drives marketplace health (page 97-98)
- **HubSpot North Star** — Weekly Active Teams using core features
