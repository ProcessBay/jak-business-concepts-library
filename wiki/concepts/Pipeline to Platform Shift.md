# Pipeline to Platform Shift

## Definition

The Pipeline-to-Platform shift describes the fundamental change in where value is created: from linear transactions (one firm produces, customers consume) to multi-sided interactions (the firm facilitates exchanges between participants who create value with each other). Pereira's example — Uber vs traditional taxi service — illustrates the shift: the underlying service (a ride) is similar, but the value trigger moves to interactive information (knowing cost and ETA before getting in the car). Platforms capture exponentially more value per unit of input than pipes because each new participant creates network effects.

## Key attributes

- **Value source shifts from production to interaction** — pipes optimize the production line; platforms optimize the interaction
- **Network effects compound** — each new user makes the platform more valuable for existing users
- **Asymmetric capital efficiency** — platforms don't own the assets (Uber doesn't own cars; Airbnb no hotels)
- **Multiple sides** — typical platform has 2+ distinct customer types
- **Information transparency** — platforms work because they reduce information asymmetry
- **Different metrics** — pipes measure throughput; platforms measure interactions

## When to use

- Diagnosing why a pipe-model competitor lost share to a platform challenger
- Strategic planning when incumbent's pipe is being attacked by platform players
- Designing a new venture in a fragmented market
- Identifying where platform potential exists in your industry

## When NOT to use

- Categories where supply concentration prevents platform aggregation
- Industries with strong regulatory barriers to multi-sided models
- Pure manufacturing where pipe economics still dominate
- When the firm lacks capital to subsidize one side until critical mass

## Common pitfalls

- Calling something a "platform" when it's still operationally a pipe
- Trying to convert a pipe business overnight (massive operational change)
- Failing to manage the chicken-and-egg problem of platform launch
- Letting take-rates rise too high too fast (suppliers exit)
- Treating platform as just "marketplace" — platforms have richer interaction mechanics

## Key metrics

- # of distinct customer types (sides) on the platform
- Interactions per period (vs. transactions in pipe models)
- Network density (active participants on each side)
- Take rate / monetization per interaction

## Decision questions

- Where in our industry could a platform challenger emerge?
- What's our defensible asset if we're a pipe and competitors platformize?
- Could we add a platform layer to our existing pipe business?
- Where's the information asymmetry our customers suffer that a platform could solve?

## Canonical examples

- **Uber vs taxi industry** — pipe (taxi co-op) vs platform (Uber connects drivers + riders) (page 14)
- **Airbnb vs hotels** — pipe (hotel chain owns properties) vs platform (Airbnb connects hosts + travelers)
- **Amazon vs traditional retailers** — partial: Amazon both pipe (1P retail) and platform (3P marketplace)
- **YouTube vs traditional TV** — pipe (network owns content) vs platform (YouTube connects creators + viewers)
- **Spotify vs record labels** — partial; Spotify shifted music industry partly to platform
