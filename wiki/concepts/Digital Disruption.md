# Digital Disruption

## Definition

Digital Disruption is the structural transformation that has reshaped industries across the 21st century, where digital-native challengers (or incumbents who digitalize) capture value previously held by physical/analogue incumbents. Pereira's analysis spans 13 industries — Press, Grocery, Mobility, Hotel, Banking, Entertainment, Music, Travel, Investment, Education, Data Centers, Law, Software — each showing the same pattern: digital natives redefine how value is created, delivered, or captured in ways the legacy incumbents couldn't or didn't. The disruption pattern is so consistent across industries that "you either adapt or die" is the source's conclusion.

## Key attributes

- **Cross-industry pattern** — same disruption dynamic appears in 13+ industries simultaneously
- **Reshapes all three Create-Deliver-Capture components** — usually multiple, not just one
- **Often disintermediates the legacy player** — physical store, agent, branch, dealership
- **Customer-power-shift** — customers gain options, information, and control
- **Network economics replace asset economics** — value shifts from owned assets to network effects
- **Speed advantage to digital natives** — they can iterate faster than legacy can transform
- **Late incumbents lose** — Blockbuster (1990s-2000s), Kodak, taxi medallion holders, traditional bookstores, etc.

## When to use

- Strategic planning to identify exposure to digital disruption
- Diagnosing why a legacy business model is losing share
- Investment decisions — which legacy industries are exposed
- Cross-industry pattern recognition (your industry's disruption playbook is likely already written elsewhere)

## When NOT to use

- For purely operational decisions
- In categories where physical presence still dominates (e.g., heavy construction, on-site healthcare delivery)
- When the firm has already digitally transformed and the question is execution

## Common pitfalls

- Adding a website and calling it "digital transformation" — must change Create/Deliver/Capture, not just channel
- Mistaking digital tooling for digital strategy
- Letting incumbent bias delay action (legacy revenue protects current cash flow, blinds to future)
- Failing to recognize cross-industry patterns (every industry team thinks theirs is unique)
- Half-transformation that combines legacy cost structure with digital expectations

## Key metrics

- Digital revenue share (% of total from digital channels)
- Time since digital inflection point in industry
- Digital-native competitor market share trend
- Customer digital engagement (% of customer journey digital)

## Decision questions

- Which of our three components (Create, Deliver, Capture) is digital-native competitors disrupting?
- What's the analogous disruption playbook from another industry that arrived before ours?
- Where is our legacy cost structure incompatible with digital-native unit economics?
- If we built our company from scratch today as a digital native, what would we do differently?

## Canonical examples per industry (each showing 20th vs. 21st century)

- **Press** — Newspapers/paperboys/ads → Social platforms/users-as-creators/digital ads (page 12-13)
- **Grocery** — Physical store/in-person shopping/cash register → Recipe subscriptions (HelloFresh)/delivery/online subscription (page 14)
- **Mobility** — Dealerships/cars/financing → Uber/app-coordinated drivers/saved card (page 15)
- **Hotel** — Building/short-term rental → Airbnb broker/peer hosts/commission (page 16)
- **Bank** — Branches/safes/fees → Nubank app/digital accounts/lower rates (page 18)
- **Entertainment** — Theaters/seats/tickets → Netflix app/streaming/subscription (page 19)
- **Music** — Stores/albums/sales → Spotify/streaming/subscription (page 20)
- **Travel** — Travel agencies/agents/tickets → Expedia/aggregator/self-service (page 22)
- **Investment** — Floor traders/stocks/trading fees → Questrade/robo-advisors/low fees (page 23)
- **Education** — Campuses/lectures/tuition → Udemy/online videos/per-course pricing (page 24)
- **Data Centers** — Mainframes/installation/hardware sales → AWS/cloud/usage fees (page 25)
- **Law Firms** — Lawyers/legal docs/hourly fees → Wonder Legal/templates/self-service (page 26-27)
- **Software** — Physical media/local install/license fees → Salesforce/SaaS/subscription (page 27-28)
