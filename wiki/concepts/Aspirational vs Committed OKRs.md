# Aspirational vs Committed OKRs

## Definition

OKRs split into two types based on the level of confidence expected at the start of the cycle. **Committed OKRs** (sometimes "roofshots") are goals the team is expected to fully deliver — missing them signals an execution or planning failure. **Aspirational OKRs** (or "stretch" / "moonshot") are ambitious goals where a score of 0.6-0.7 is considered success — they exist to push the team beyond what it knows how to do, and missing them is information, not failure (Pereira develops this throughout pages 17-18, 77-82, and via Google/Netflix case studies on pages 105, 109). Most mature OKR practices mix both: committed OKRs anchor reliability; aspirational OKRs drive innovation and prevent sandbagging.

## Key attributes

- **Committed OKRs** — expected to score 1.0; scoring lower is a problem to investigate
- **Aspirational OKRs** — expected to score 0.6-0.7; scoring 1.0 is a sign the goal was too easy
- **Both types coexist** — teams typically run a mix per cycle, not one or the other
- **Differentiates execution from innovation** — committed = "we know how"; aspirational = "we'll find out how"
- **Google convention** — aspirational OKRs are explicit; teams are protected from punishment for missing them (pages 109-110)
- **Netflix convention** — OKRs decoupled from performance review precisely to allow honest stretch (pages 105-106)
- **Honest labeling matters** — labeling a sandbagged goal as "aspirational" defeats the purpose

## When to use

- **Committed**: customer commitments, regulatory deadlines, dependencies other teams rely on
- **Aspirational**: new market entry, 10x performance goals, R&D bets, breakthrough innovation
- Mix both when the team needs to deliver reliably *and* push into unknown territory
- Aspirational OKRs are especially valuable post-product-market-fit, when execution risk is low but ambition risk is high

## When NOT to use

- Don't label everything aspirational — strips accountability from delivery work
- Don't use aspirational framing as cover for vague Objectives
- Don't introduce aspirational OKRs in cultures that punish missing targets (you'll get sandbagging; pages 78-79)
- Don't tie aspirational OKR attainment to bonuses

## Common pitfalls

- **Sandbagging** — labeling a known-achievable target as "stretch" to look good when overshooting (page 79)
- **Conflating aspirational with vague** — stretch ≠ unmeasurable; aspirational KRs still need to be quantitative
- **Punishing missed aspirational OKRs** — kills willingness to set them next cycle
- **Treating 0.7 as the floor for committed OKRs** — committed should hit 1.0
- **No mix at all** — only committed OKRs produce incremental orgs; only aspirational OKRs produce chaos
- **Failing to declare type upfront** — ambiguity at scoring time creates conflict

## Key metrics

- **Stretch completion rate** — avg score for aspirational OKRs (healthy range: 0.6-0.8)
- **Committed attainment rate** — % of committed OKRs scoring 1.0 (target: >90%)
- **Mix ratio** — aspirational vs committed OKRs per cycle (no fixed rule; varies by team maturity)

## Decision questions

- For each Objective: is this something we must deliver, or something we're trying to learn how to deliver?
- Are we labeling honestly, or hiding sandbagging behind "stretch"?
- Will leadership protect this aspirational OKR from punishment if we miss?
- What's our committed:aspirational mix this cycle, and does it match our risk appetite?

## Canonical examples

- **Google** — explicit aspirational OKRs ("stretch goals") drive moonshots; teams expected to score 60-70%, not 100% (page 109)
- **Netflix** — OKRs as "learning tool, not evaluation tool"; missing a KR not perceived as personal failure (pages 105-106)
- **Airbnb** — "create a world where anyone can belong anywhere" is aspirational; quarterly listings growth targets are typically committed (page 100)
- **Patagonia** — multi-year carbon-reduction Objectives are aspirational; quarterly compliance milestones are committed (pages 97-98)
