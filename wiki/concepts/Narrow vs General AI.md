# Narrow vs General AI

## Definition

The foundational distinction in AI capability. **Narrow AI (ANI / Weak AI)** — systems designed and trained for one specific task: image classification, language translation, recommendation, voice recognition, chess-playing. Cannot transfer to other tasks without retraining. All AI in commercial use today is narrow. **General AI (AGI / Strong AI)** — hypothetical systems with broad cognitive abilities matching or exceeding human intelligence across most tasks, capable of transferring learning across domains. AGI remains unrealized as of 2026, though large language models (GPT, Claude, Gemini) display emergent generalization that blurs the boundary. Pereira frames the distinction as essential for strategy: narrow AI investments are predictable; AGI investments are speculative and high-stakes.

## Key attributes

- **Narrow AI** — task-specific, requires training data per task, dominant in commercial use
- **General AI (AGI)** — hypothetical broad cognition; transfers across domains; unrealized
- **Distinction matters for strategy** — narrow AI has clear ROI per task; AGI is bet-the-company
- **LLMs blur the line** — GPT/Claude show emergent capabilities beyond narrow training
- **Risk asymmetry** — narrow AI failures are bounded; AGI carries systemic/existential risk discussions
- **Regulatory implications differ** — narrow AI regulated per use case; AGI raises civilizational questions

## When to use which framing

- **Narrow AI thinking when**: designing AI features in products; evaluating per-use-case ROI; building specific automation
- **AGI thinking when**: long-term strategic planning; safety/ethics frameworks; speculative R&D investment
- **Hybrid thinking when**: deploying LLMs (which act narrow but have emergent generality)

## Common pitfalls

- Claiming AGI when delivering narrow AI (marketing inflation)
- Underestimating LLMs' emergent capabilities (treating them as pure narrow AI)
- Letting AGI speculation distract from practical narrow-AI value capture
- Treating AGI risks as far-future when intermediate capabilities raise near-term concerns
- Failing to distinguish capabilities the company has vs. claims it makes

## Decision questions

- Are we building narrow AI for specific use cases (most cases), or claiming more?
- Where do LLMs blur narrow/general for our use case?
- How does our AI failure-mode change as capability scales?
- What's our position on AGI timing and what does it imply for strategy?

## Canonical examples

- **Narrow AI**: Image classifiers, recommendation systems, speech recognition, AlphaGo, Tesla Autopilot, fraud detection, medical imaging diagnosis (page 25)
- **Boundary cases**: GPT-4/5, Claude, Gemini — narrow-trained but demonstrate emergent generalization
- **AGI**: aspirational systems described by OpenAI, Anthropic, DeepMind missions (page 27-29)
