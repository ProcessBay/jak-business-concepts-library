# Andreessen PMF Doctrine

## Definition

Marc Andreessen's doctrine — articulated in his essay "The Only Thing That Matters" — is that **the quality of the market a company targets is the single most critical determinant of how successful that business will be**. The right PMF means "participating in the appropriate market while using the appropriate offering," and a successful market is one that contains "a sizable number of genuine people who are considering making a purchase" — a market that is "successful at pulling products from the business/startup" (page 10). The term itself was first articulated by Andy Rachleff (of Wealthfront/Betterment background), but Andreessen made it doctrine for the modern startup era.

## Key attributes

- **Market first, product second** — founder brilliance shows up most in choosing what problem to solve, not in the first solution
- **Market pulls product** — Andreessen: "the market pulls the product out of the company" (page 14)
- **Iteration is mandatory** — "Your first and most original idea about how to address the issue is probably incorrect" (page 14)
- **Self-evident when right** — "the right product for the right market is typically quite evident" (page 10)
- **Diagnostic by negation** — when PMF is missing: no value gained, no WOM, no fast-growing usage, mediocre press, long sales cycle, transactions that don't close (page 10)
- **Diagnostic by affirmation** — when PMF is present: customers buy as fast as you can produce, bank account fills up, you scramble to hire support/sales, reporters call (pages 10-11)
- **Failure path even with PMF** — companies still fail trying to take advantage of the opportunity (page 11)

## When to use

- As a north-star principle for any founder choosing what to build
- When evaluating opportunities — interrogate the market first, the solution second
- When diagnosing why traction isn't materializing (it's almost always the market)
- As an investor lens for early-stage screening
- When pivoting — the market is usually right; the solution usually needs rework

## When NOT to use

- As an excuse to ignore execution quality (market alone isn't sufficient)
- When working in a small market with a defensible niche strategy on purpose
- As permission to chase markets you have no right to win in
- In B2B enterprise where relationship-driven sales overlap with "market quality"

## Common pitfalls

- **Solution-first fixation** — Pereira: "founders often have a tendency to focus excessively on potential remedies while ignoring the issues at hand" (page 14)
- **Falling in love with the first idea** — Andreessen says it's "probably incorrect"
- **Underestimating market severity** — "a user's willingness to try out the first-generation remedy is based on the severity of the issue" (page 14)
- **Confusing the burning-hair analogy** — if the problem is severe, even a stone works (people will hit themselves with it to put out the fire); if the problem is mild, even a hose feels like overkill (page 14)

## Key metrics

- **Severity of the underlying problem** — qualitative; assessed via customer interviews
- **Sales cycle length** — long cycles signal market misalignment (page 10)
- **Transaction close rate** — low rates signal mismatch
- **Speed of word-of-mouth spread** — fast spread signals pull
- **Press / inbound interest** — proxy for market resonance

## Decision questions

- Is the market we're targeting genuinely large and growing?
- Are real buyers actively shopping for a solution in this category?
- If we describe the problem out loud, do prospects nod or stare blankly?
- Are our sales cycles getting shorter or longer over time?
- Are we trying to push a product into a market that isn't pulling?

## Canonical examples

- **Wealthfront** — Andy Rachleff's firm; the originator of the term in venture vocabulary (page 10)
- **Netscape, Opsware, Andreessen Horowitz** — Andreessen's track record makes the doctrine credible (page 10)
- **Hair-on-fire customer** — Pereira's canonical analogy for market severity (page 14)
- **Spotify (Napster aftermath)** — Daniel Ek saw a market that was already pulling (page 17)
