# Hanlon's Razor

## Definition

Hanlon's Razor is the heuristic: "Never attribute to malice that which can be adequately explained by carelessness, incompetence, or stupidity." Pereira renders it as advice "never to attribute to malice that which could be considered careless or foolish," and attributes the modern phrasing to Robert J. Hanlon (a version of the same idea appears as early as 1774, and Goethe also articulated it). The principle is a bias-mitigation tool: in most situations involving other people's harmful actions, the assumption of stupidity, distraction, or error is statistically more accurate than the assumption of malicious intent — and the assumption of malice damages relationships, communication, and decision quality far more than charity does.

## Key attributes

- **Default to incompetence, not malice** — the simpler, less-paranoid explanation is usually right
- **Bias antidote** — counteracts the availability heuristic (vivid recent slights), confirmation bias, and hateful bias (Pereira p. 28–29)
- **Relationship-preserving** — assuming malice destroys trust; assuming error allows recovery
- **Resource-allocation discipline** — instead of preparing for the worst-case adversarial scenario, businesses can redirect resources to non-malicious responses (Pereira p. 30)
- **Communication tool** — especially powerful in conflict, where the malicious interpretation comes most easily
- **Adjacent to Occam's Razor** — both prefer simpler explanations; Hanlon specifically applies that preference to attributions of intent
- **Not absolute** — sometimes the malicious explanation is true; Hanlon's is a prior, not a verdict

## When to use

- Conflict with team members, partners, or family — before responding to a perceived slight
- Interpreting customer or partner behavior that looks adversarial
- Reading news, social media, or competitor moves (modern media monetizes outrage and overstates malicious intent — Pereira p. 29)
- Diagnosing service or product failures (carelessness >> malice, almost always)
- Leadership communication, especially when assigning blame after an incident
- Onboarding new team members where context gaps create misread signals

## When NOT to use

- Repeated patterns of harm from the same actor (the prior should update toward malice with evidence)
- Adversarial domains by design (negotiation, competitive intelligence, security threats)
- When the cost of being wrong about malice is catastrophic (safety, legal, financial)
- When ignoring the malicious possibility creates real exposure (some actors really are bad actors)

## Common pitfalls

- **Over-application** — using Hanlon's as a permanent excuse to avoid confronting genuine bad actors
- **Naive charity** — failing to update when repeated incidents make the malicious interpretation more likely
- **Conflating Hanlon's with passivity** — the razor recommends a different interpretation, not inaction
- **Using it on yourself** — Hanlon's is most useful for interpreting others; self-application can become self-excusing
- **Skipping the diagnostic step** — Hanlon's says assume incompetence first; you still need to verify and respond

## Signals you're using it well

- Your default first reaction to a perceived slight is curiosity, not accusation
- You can name the specific carelessness/error/context-gap that explains the behavior
- Your team conflicts resolve faster because participants assume good faith
- You're updating priors when patterns repeat (not stuck in naive charity)

## Decision questions

- What's the most generous plausible explanation for this behavior?
- Have I assumed malice where carelessness or error fits the facts equally well?
- What context might the other person have that I don't?
- Is this a one-time incident or a repeated pattern? (Update accordingly)
- If I assume good faith and I'm wrong, what's the cost? If I assume malice and I'm wrong, what's the cost?

## Canonical examples

- **Apple's Siri / abortion clinic flaw, 2011 (Pereira p. 29–30)** — media framed Siri's failure to surface abortion clinic results as discriminatory intent; Apple spokesperson Natalie Kerris explained the actual cause: Siri was simply not fully developed yet. The malice-first reading drove outrage; the carelessness-first reading matched the truth
- **COVID-19 pandemic government responses (Pereira p. 30)** — early lockdown decisions in the UK and outbreak handling on the Sydney cruise ship were framed in some media as deliberately reducing the elderly population; the non-malicious explanation (a novel virus, no playbook, short decision windows) was the realistic one
- **Team conflict** — colleagues "deliberately undermining" a project often turn out to be operating from missing information, poor handoffs, or competing priorities
- **Goethe** — used a version of Hanlon's reasoning ("being mean or stupid was the same as being incompetent") well before Hanlon coined the modern phrasing (Pereira p. 55–56)
