# Mission Statement Anti-patterns

## Definition

The recurring failure modes Pereira identifies in mission statements: statements that leave no room for inspiration, statements that lack personality and fun, and statements drowning in buzzwords and jargon (pages 79-80). To these the broader text adds the inverse of the nine compelling-statement traits — statements that are too long, too generic, too unrealistic, too passive, too negative, too rigid, or too untargeted (pages 27-30). The common thread is that anti-pattern missions describe the *past* (what the company has done) rather than the *purpose* (why the work exists), or they substitute corporate vocabulary for genuine conviction. They corrode trust faster than no mission statement, because employees and customers can tell when language is performative.

## Key attributes

- **Activity-list mission** — reads as "we sell X, Y, Z" rather than "we exist to..." — strips out inspiration (page 79)
- **Polished but soulless** — over-corporate tone, no personality, sounds like every other company (page 79)
- **Buzzwords and jargon** — "leverage," "synergy," "world-class," "best-in-class," "innovate" used in place of meaning (page 80)
- **Generic substitution** — any competitor in the category could adopt it unchanged (page 28)
- **Lofty but unrealistic** — promises the company cannot operationally deliver (page 28)
- **Too long** — multi-paragraph statements no one can recite (page 22, page 27)
- **Passive voice** — "are made by," "is provided to" — signals the company is acted upon, not acting (page 74)
- **Negative framing** — focuses on what the company opposes rather than what it builds (page 29)
- **Audience-less** — written for "everyone," reaches no one (page 30)
- **Wall-decoration mission** — exists in the document and the lobby but does not shape decisions (implied across pages 23-26)

## When to use

- As a pre-publication diagnostic on a draft mission
- As a review tool when auditing existing mission statements during a rebrand
- As coaching language when a leadership team is defending a weak draft
- As a teaching tool when training teams on brand and strategy

## When NOT to use

- As a public critique of another company's mission — diagnose internally, not externally
- To dismiss a draft after one read — most drafts hit two or three anti-patterns and are still recoverable

## Common pitfalls

- **Diagnosing without prescribing** — naming the anti-pattern without proposing the fix leaves the team stuck
- **Confusing brevity with absence of detail** — a short statement can still be specific; a long statement can still be vague
- **Mistaking professional tone for soullessness** — formality is not the problem; emptiness is
- **Treating jargon as universal** — what reads as jargon in one industry is legitimate vocabulary in another (medical, legal, technical)
- **Demanding personality at the cost of clarity** — "fun" missions can become inscrutable

## Key metrics

- **Buzzword density** — count of words like "leverage," "synergy," "best-in-class," "innovative" per sentence
- **Generic-substitution test** — can you swap the company name for a competitor's and have it still read true? (If yes, fail.)
- **Recall failure rate** — % of employees who cannot paraphrase the statement
- **Decision-citation rate** — how often the mission is cited in real strategy debates (low = wall-decoration)
- **Recognition by audience** — do customers/recruits recognize the mission as authentic to the brand?

## Decision questions

- If we removed our company name, would the mission still feel like ours, or could a competitor adopt it?
- How many buzzwords does it contain? Could each be replaced with concrete language?
- Does it list what we do, or say why we exist?
- Does our leadership team actually believe this — or did the marketing team write it alone?
- When did anyone last cite this mission in a strategy meeting?
- Does the statement have personality, or does it sound like every Fortune 500 lobby plaque?

## Canonical examples

- **Activity-list failure** — "[Company] provides software, consulting, and services to enterprise clients globally." — describes what; says nothing about why (page 79)
- **Buzzword failure** — "We leverage best-in-class, world-class, innovative solutions to deliver synergistic value." — pure jargon (page 80)
- **Generic substitution failure** — "To be the leading provider of [category] in [region]." — adopted unchanged by hundreds of competitors (page 28)
- **Unrealistic failure** — "To eliminate [vast structural problem] within five years" when the company has 12 employees (page 28)
- **Counter-example (avoids all anti-patterns)** — Patagonia: "To save our home planet." — short, specific, conviction-led, no jargon (page 52)
- **Counter-example (personality without losing clarity)** — Yeti: "Build the cooler we'd use every day if it existed." — has voice, has specificity (page 71)
- **Counter-example (inverse of soulless)** — Old Guys Rule: speaks directly to its niche in their language (page 52)
