# Vision Anti-Patterns

## Definition

The recurring failure modes that turn vision statements from organizational compasses into ignored wall art. Pereira documents these implicitly throughout the *Super Guide* — particularly in the chapters on Clarity & Specificity (pp. 20-21), Characteristics of Effective Vision Statements (pp. 34-47), Challenges in Crafting (pp. 73-82), and Sustaining & Evolving the Vision (pp. 105-109). The anti-patterns cluster into seven categories: (1) Vague — language so general no decision can be filtered through it; (2) Undatable — no time horizon, no way to know progress; (3) Uninspiring — pure profit/share language nobody rallies behind; (4) Confused with Mission — describes today, not aspiration; (5) Unowned — leadership wrote it in a vacuum, employees feel no buy-in; (6) Operationally Disconnected — printed on the wall but never referenced in actual decisions; (7) Stale — never revisited; describes a market that no longer exists. Recognizing these patterns *before* publishing the vision is the single highest-leverage edit a leader can make.

## Key attributes

- **Seven recurring failure modes** — Vague, Undatable, Uninspiring, Mission-Confused, Unowned, Operationally Disconnected, Stale
- **Often co-occur** — a vague vision is almost always also undatable and uninspiring
- **Detectable pre-launch** — a checklist review catches most of these
- **Erode trust when ignored** — when leadership ignores its own stated vision, employee cynicism compounds
- **Symptom not cause** — bad vision usually points to a deeper strategy or governance problem

## When to use

- Pre-launch review of any draft vision statement
- Post-mortem when a vision launched and quietly died
- Diagnostic when employee engagement scores drop and "vision" is cited
- Audit during leadership transition
- Pre-cascade check before running Cascade / OKR / Hoshin Kanri rollouts

## When NOT to use

- As a gotcha against past leadership (use forensically, not punitively)
- As an excuse to avoid having a vision at all (perfect-vs-good trap)

## Common pitfalls

### Anti-Pattern 1: Vague
- "Be the best in the world" / "world-class" / "excellence in everything"
- No decision can be filtered through it — every option passes
- **Test:** could any competitor adopt this verbatim? If yes, it's vague.

### Anti-Pattern 2: Undatable
- No time horizon embedded or implied
- No way to know if the firm is on track or off
- Pereira p. 36-38: time-bound objectives are core to effective vision
- **Test:** five years in, would you know whether you're winning or losing?

### Anti-Pattern 3: Uninspiring
- Pure financial/share-price language ("maximize shareholder value")
- Nobody outside the C-suite rallies behind it
- Talent and customers feel no emotional pull
- **Test:** would a new hire feel proud to repeat this at a dinner party?

### Anti-Pattern 4: Confused with Mission
- Describes what the company *does today* rather than what it *aspires to become*
- Pereira chapter on Differentiating from Mission Statements (pp. 45-47): vision is forward-looking; mission is present-focused
- **Test:** does it describe the future state, or the current state?

### Anti-Pattern 5: Unowned
- Leadership drafted it in a closed room and announced it top-down
- Employees feel no participation, therefore no commitment
- Pereira p. 49-50: stakeholder involvement is crucial to ownership
- **Test:** can a random employee tell you *why* it's the vision?

### Anti-Pattern 6: Operationally Disconnected
- Printed on the wall, never referenced in board decks, strategy docs, or hiring criteria
- Decisions visibly contradict the vision and nobody calls it out
- Pereira p. 83: "Only when the whole team fully embraces the vision can it inspire and direct them"
- **Test:** in the last 12 months, has any major decision been changed because of the vision?

### Anti-Pattern 7: Stale
- Never revisited; describes a market that has since fundamentally shifted
- The vision was right in 2015 and meaningless in 2025
- Pereira p. 105-109: vision must be revisited, not as ritual but as honest assessment
- **Test:** when did we last seriously ask "is this still the right destination?"

## Key metrics

- **Vague-language count** — generic phrases per vision draft (target: zero)
- **Recall failure rate** — % of employees who can't state the vision accurately
- **Decision filter failure rate** — % of major decisions made without explicit vision reference
- **Time since last revision** — flag if >5 years without honest review
- **Vision-strategy drift score** — gap between stated vision and actual capital allocation
- **Employee cynicism score (vision-specific)** — survey: "Do leaders act consistently with the stated vision?"

## Decision questions

- Could any competitor adopt our vision verbatim? (If yes → vague)
- Does it have a time horizon, explicit or implied? (If no → undatable)
- Would a new hire feel proud to repeat it socially? (If no → uninspiring)
- Does it describe the future state, or today? (If today → confused with mission)
- Were employees genuinely consulted, or just informed? (If informed → unowned)
- In the last year, has the vision changed any actual decision? (If no → operationally disconnected)
- When did we last seriously test whether this is still the right destination? (If never → stale)

## Canonical examples

- **The Walmart problem** — "To make every day easier for busy families" (page 65). Inspirational and clear, but undatable and arguably operationally disconnected from the cost-cutting culture employees experience.
- **The Caterpillar problem** — "To be the global leader in customer value" (page 72). Vague (what's "customer value"?), undatable, and could be adopted by any competitor verbatim.
- **The Audi problem** — "To be the BEST automotive sales and repair operation in every market we serve" (page 71). Vague ("BEST"), undatable, no differentiating perspective.
- **The Kraft Heinz problem** — a six-sentence paragraph (page 70-71) that violates conciseness, contains mixed mission/vision elements, and is impossible for any employee to recall verbatim.
- **The contrast — Tesla** (page 64) — dated (21st century), specific (electric vehicles), inspiring (world transition), differentiating (compelling car company), recallable. None of the seven anti-patterns hits.
- **The contrast — Saudi Vision 2030** — explicitly dated (2030), specific (three pillars), inspiring (national mobilization), differentiating (post-oil identity), recallable ("a vibrant society, a thriving economy, an ambitious nation"). Demonstrates that even a national-scale vision can avoid every anti-pattern with disciplined craft.
- **Pereira on the cost of vagueness** — "A clear vision statement paints a vivid picture of where the company wants to go... avoiding ambiguous or vague language that can be interpreted differently" (page 20)
