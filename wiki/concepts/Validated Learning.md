# Validated Learning

## Definition

Validated learning is the principle that the unit of progress for a startup is not features shipped, code written, or money raised — it is **demonstrated, evidence-based knowledge about what creates value for customers**. Pereira frames MVP as the cheapest way to "collect the most validated learning about customers with the least effort." A learning is "validated" when a hypothesis has been confirmed or falsified through real customer behavior — not when a survey was answered, not when a focus group nodded, not when the team had a meeting. The principle underwrites the entire Lean Startup methodology: if a cycle of work doesn't produce validated learning, it didn't make progress.

## Key attributes

- **Behavioral, not declarative** — what customers DO, not what they SAY
- **Hypothesis-based** — every claim starts as a falsifiable statement, not a feature spec
- **Cheap and fast** — value is measured per dollar and per cycle, not per insight
- **Falsification is progress** — disproving a hypothesis is as valuable as confirming one
- **Tied to actionable metrics** — vanity metrics produce false learning
- **Sequential** — knowledge compounds; each cycle informs the next-most-risky hypothesis
- **Distinguishes startups from execution-mode companies** — execution measures output; startups measure learning

## When to use

- Any early-stage startup or new product development
- Corporate innovation programs (intrapreneurship, R&D, ventures)
- Pivoting decisions — should we change direction based on what we've actually learned?
- Innovation accounting — when reporting progress to boards or investors with no revenue yet
- Postmortem analysis — what did we validate or falsify, and was it worth what we spent?

## When NOT to use

- Execution-stage businesses where the hypothesis is already validated (just ship)
- Compliance/regulatory work where "validation" is moot (the law is the law)
- Pure operational work (logistics, fulfillment) where the variable is throughput, not knowledge
- Categories where the cost of testing exceeds the cost of just building

## Common pitfalls

- **Confusing activity with learning** — shipping a lot of features without testing assumptions
- **Vanity metric pollution** — celebrating followers, downloads, page views that don't validate anything (page 52)
- **Confirmation bias** — interpreting ambiguous results as validation
- **Skipping the pre-commit threshold** — moving the goalposts after data arrives
- **Not writing down hypotheses** — making the test deniable post-hoc
- **Stated-preference reliance** — relying on what users say instead of what they do
- **Friends-and-family validation** — non-target-market feedback that doesn't validate anything
- **Survivorship bias** — celebrating the one thing that worked, ignoring the four that didn't

## Key metrics

- Validated learning per dollar (the meta-metric)
- Hypothesis validation rate (% of cycles producing a clear yes/no)
- Cycle time (how fast you can validate one hypothesis)
- Number of pivot decisions made based on data
- Cost of false validation (when later evidence shows an earlier "yes" was wrong)

## Decision questions

- What is the hypothesis we're testing this cycle — written down, falsifiable?
- What pre-committed threshold defines "validated" vs "falsified"?
- Are we measuring behavior or stated preference?
- If the data shows the hypothesis is wrong, will we actually pivot?
- Is the cost of this learning justified by its impact on next decisions?

## Canonical examples

- **Buffer** — Joel Gascoigne explicitly described his two-page landing-page test outcomes as "validated" after email and Twitter feedback confirmed his hypothesis; only then did he build (page 80)
- **Dropbox** — 70,000 email signups in one day from the explainer video was validated learning that demand existed before any product (pages 17, 81)
- **Airbnb** — paying guests sleeping on air mattresses in the founders' apartment validated the peer-to-peer rental hypothesis (page 79)
- **Intuit's SaaS transition** — used MVPs to incrementally test market assumptions about the new business model before fully committing (page 17-18)
- **Steve Blank's Customer Development** — the methodological foundation; talking to customers IS the validation activity (page 15)
