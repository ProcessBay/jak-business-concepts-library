# North Star Vision

## Definition

A North Star Vision is the directional anchor an organization uses to evaluate every significant decision — the fixed point in the sky that the firm navigates by, even as tactical circumstances change. Pereira uses the North Star metaphor explicitly: a well-crafted vision "sets a destination, acting as a North Star for the entire company, guiding its strategic decisions and action plans" (page 18). The function of a North Star is not to dictate tactics but to filter them — when faced with a hard choice, leaders ask "does this move us toward the North Star or away from it?" A North Star is distinct from operational goals (which are dated, quantitative, and short-horizon) and from strategy (which is the route). It is the destination; strategy is the map; goals are the milestones. The Saudi Vision 2030 functions as the North Star for an entire country's ministerial decision-making, even as specific KPIs and giga-projects are added, removed, or rescaled.

## Key attributes

- **Directional, not operational** — points the way, doesn't prescribe the steps
- **Stable across cycles** — survives quarterly, annual, even multi-year shifts
- **Decision filter** — every major bet evaluated against it ("does this move us closer?")
- **Communicable in one sentence** — anyone in the firm can state it
- **Asymmetric** — wrong-direction moves are obvious; correct-direction moves are obvious
- **Independent of current capability** — describes where we're going, not what we can do today
- **Distinguishable from short-term metrics** — North Star is the *why*; metrics are *progress markers*

## When to use

- Filtering opportunities when the firm has more options than capacity
- Maintaining strategic coherence during rapid scaling
- Navigating cross-functional disagreements where local optima conflict
- Preventing strategy drift across leadership transitions
- Communicating "who we are not" — opportunities the North Star *excludes*
- Aligning M&A, capital allocation, and hiring around a single direction

## When NOT to use

- As a substitute for quarterly operational targets (those are necessary too)
- When the firm hasn't decided its category or core customer
- During acute survival crises where the question is "exist" not "where to"
- When leadership won't use it as the decision filter it's meant to be
- As a marketing tagline isolated from internal operations

## Common pitfalls

- **North Star confused with KPIs** — the metric is not the star; the star is the destination
- **Too many North Stars** — by definition there can be only one (others are sub-objectives)
- **Drift without revisit** — circumstances change so radically the North Star is no longer the right one, but nobody notices
- **Privately abandoned** — leadership still cites it publicly but routinely makes contrary decisions
- **Too vague to filter** — "make the world better" doesn't actually exclude any decision
- **Too narrow** — overly product-specific, dies when the product category dies

## Key metrics

- **Decision alignment rate** — % of major decisions that visibly reference the North Star
- **Vision referenced in strategy docs** — does it appear in board decks, OKRs, hiring criteria?
- **Employee North Star recall** — can anyone state it?
- **Strategic drift score** — gap between stated North Star and actual capital allocation
- **Time since last meaningful North Star review** — should be every 3-5 years, not annually
- **Cross-functional citation rate** — do non-strategy teams (product, ops, sales) reference it?

## Decision questions

- When we make a big bet, do we explicitly check it against the North Star?
- Could a new hire find our North Star in writing within their first week?
- Have we said no to a tempting opportunity because it pointed away from the star?
- Has any decision in the past year been changed because of the North Star?
- Is our North Star still the right destination given how the market evolved?
- Are we measuring movement toward it, or only measuring activity?

## Canonical examples

- **Amazon's "customer obsession"** — operates as a decade-spanning North Star: every Bezos-era decision (Prime, AWS, Marketplace, Alexa) explicitly framed against it; visible in the vision statement "Earth's most customer-centric company" (page 63)
- **Google's "organize the world's information"** — North Star that has guided product expansion from search to Maps, Books, Scholar, Workspace, etc. (page 63)
- **Tesla's transition to sustainable energy** — North Star that frames not just cars, but also Powerwall, solar roof, and Megapack as coherent (page 64)
- **Saudi Vision 2030** — national North Star: "A vibrant society, a thriving economy, an ambitious nation" — used to evaluate every government program, sovereign investment, and policy decision. The clearest contemporary example of a North Star operating at scale, with explicit decision-filter governance (Vision Realization Programs) wrapped around it.
- **LinkedIn** — "create economic opportunity for every member of the global workforce" (page 68) — filter for every product decision
- **Pereira on the navigation function** — "A clear and specific vision statement is a unifying force... it aligns the efforts of every employee by providing a common understanding... The vision becomes a guiding principle, ensuring unity and focus toward shared goals" (page 21)
