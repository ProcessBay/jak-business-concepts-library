# Shift Left Healthcare Model

## Definition

The **Shift Left Model** is Pereira's umbrella term (borrowed from the a16z digital-health playbook he cites) for the structural shift in HealthTech business models from **B2B-dominated** (selling to hospitals, payors, providers) to **B2C-dominated** (selling directly to patients-as-consumers). Between 2016 and 2021, the share of funded digital health companies pursuing B2C business models almost doubled. The shift is driven by four reinforcing forces: rising consumer cost-sharing (deductibles, copays, coinsurance) that incentivises patients to shop; technology platforms (smartphone, payment rails, telehealth) that made direct-to-patient delivery cost-viable; demographic shifts (millennials/Gen Z expect Uber-grade UX from every service); and value-based care frameworks that reward outcomes over volume, which is easier to deliver when the patient is engaged. The Shift Left Model isn't a single business model — it's a **strategic posture** that reshapes value proposition (patient experience over institutional workflow), go-to-market (consumer brand and direct acquisition over enterprise sales), revenue mix (out-of-pocket + employer + reimbursement instead of pure payor billing), and product (mobile-first, low-friction, transparent pricing).

## Key attributes

- **Patient is the buyer, not just the user** — value proposition speaks to the patient, marketing reaches the patient, product UX serves the patient
- **Consumer brand discipline** — naming, design, copywriting, content strategy at parity with consumer apps
- **Direct acquisition channels** — paid social, content/SEO, influencer, employer-benefits marketplaces — not just enterprise sales
- **Transparent pricing as a differentiator** — published cash-pay rates vs. opaque insurance-billed pricing
- **Friction obsession** — every step in the patient journey is a churn risk; book → consult → outcome must be sub-minutes, not sub-days
- **Vertical specialisation common** — Hims (men's health), Ro (men's then weight loss), Maven (women's/fertility), Kindbody (fertility) — one population, full journey
- **Hybrid revenue often** — D2C cash-pay anchor + employer benefit + insurance reimbursement secondary; pure D2C is rare at scale
- **Outcomes engagement loop** — wearables, connected devices, in-app tracking; the patient generates the data the provider used to own

## When to use

- Affluent or middle-class markets where patients can and will pay out-of-pocket for convenience and quality
- Conditions with stigma, embarrassment, or workflow friction in traditional care (sexual health, mental health, dermatology, weight loss)
- Wellness/prevention segments that insurance doesn't reimburse anyway (fitness, sleep, nutrition, cognitive training)
- Markets with growing employer-benefits sophistication — employers will pay for products that improve outcomes for their employees
- Geographies with the digital infrastructure to support D2C delivery (smartphone, payments, fulfilment, telehealth licensure)

## When NOT to use

- Acute, complex, or hospital-required care — patient choice doesn't determine routing
- Markets with poor consumer payment capacity and no employer/payor cushion
- Conditions where clinical complexity demands provider-led care coordination
- Categories where regulation (controlled substances, certain prescriptions) prevents the D2C delivery model
- Founders who don't have consumer marketing DNA — Shift Left is a marketing discipline as much as a clinical one

## Common pitfalls

- **D2C unit economics blow-up** — CAC of $200-400 in paid social meets ARPU of $50; LTV:CAC inverts and the company dies
- **Clinical-quality compromise to optimise patient experience** — making the consult easier ≠ making it better; regulators and patients will notice
- **Marketing-led growth without clinical evidence** — works until a celebrity case goes wrong, then trust evaporates instantly
- **Disintermediating providers and over-promising autonomy** — the Babylon Health pattern; telling patients they don't need GPs is a great way to provoke the medical establishment and the press
- **Skipping the provider/payor channel forever** — pure D2C caps at a ceiling; durable healthtech businesses eventually need payor/employer relationships
- **Underestimating regulatory exposure** — D2C prescribing, telehealth licensure, pharmacy fulfilment, controlled-substance rules — every step has compliance burden
- **Brand-led but operations-thin** — a beautiful app with broken fulfilment, slow clinician response, and no escalation pathway is worse than a clunky clinic
- **Confusing engagement with outcomes** — high DAU is not health improvement; investors and payors will eventually demand the latter

## Key metrics

- **B2C revenue share** — % of revenue from direct patient pay vs. payor/employer/hospital channels
- **Patient acquisition cost (CAC)** by channel
- **Patient lifetime value (CLV)** — must include reimbursement, ancillary services, and repeat engagement
- **Patient NPS** segmented clinical vs. experience
- **Repeat engagement rate** — % patients with ≥2 interactions in 12 months
- **Share of health wallet** — for verticalised models, % of patient's spend in the category captured
- **Brand metrics** — aided/unaided awareness, NPS, share-of-voice (standard consumer-brand discipline)

## Decision questions

- What's the patient willing to pay out-of-pocket for, and why is the current system failing them?
- What's our consumer-marketing capability, and is it real or aspirational?
- How do we sustain unit economics when CAC compresses our margin?
- What's our regulatory plan for D2C delivery in each market — telehealth licensure, pharmacy fulfilment, prescription rules?
- Are we vertical (own one condition end-to-end) or horizontal (one feature across many)?
- What's our 3-5 year payor/employer story — when does B2B revenue become structural?
- How do we govern clinical quality at consumer-experience speed?

## Canonical examples

- **Hims & Hers, Ro, Numan** — verticalised D2C men's/women's health
- **Maven Clinic** — D2C-then-employer women's/family health
- **Calm, Headspace** — D2C wellness anchored mental health
- **Livongo** (pre-Teladoc acquisition) — employer-led B2C-flavoured chronic disease
- **23andMe** — D2C genomics; cautionary tale on commercialisation challenges
- **One Medical** (now Amazon) — concierge primary care subscription
- **Forward Health, Parsley Health** — direct primary care subscription
- **Cura, Altibbi** (MENA) — Shift Left into Arabic-language D2C telehealth
- **Kalkin, GoMeds, Sehhaty** (KSA) — D2C consumer-health touchpoints emerging on Vision 2030 digital infrastructure
