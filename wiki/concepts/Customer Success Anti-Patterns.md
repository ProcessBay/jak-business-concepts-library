# Customer Success Anti-Patterns

## Definition

Pereira catalogs ten common failure modes in customer success programs — the recurring patterns that cause CSM functions to deliver poor outcomes even when the headcount, tooling, and intent are in place. They are not skill failures so much as **structural and cultural failures**: incentive misalignment, undefined success, conflation of happiness with outcomes, churn whack-a-mole, missing CS culture, mismanaged expectations, and bad-fit acquisition (pages 54-57). Recognizing these patterns is a prerequisite to building a durable CS program because each anti-pattern has a fix that lives upstream of CSM execution — usually in incentive design, hiring discipline, onboarding sales-CSM handoffs, or executive sponsorship of CS as a culture, not a team.

## Key attributes (the ten anti-patterns)

- **Not putting the customer first** — pushing for contract/revenue actions before the customer is ready (page 54)
- **Overdelivering / forcing into templates** — treating customers as categories instead of catering to specific needs (page 54)
- **Success is not defined** — process-oriented thinking with no agreed KPIs or end-state (page 54-55)
- **Focusing only on required outcome** — meeting baseline expectations without addressing account-specific concerns (page 55)
- **Confusing happiness and success** — customers can be happy while silently failing to realize value (page 55)
- **Forgetting we're working with humans** — treating accounts as logos instead of building relationships across multiple stakeholders (page 55-56)
- **Focusing only on churn mitigation** — patching leaks instead of fixing upstream processes (page 56)
- **No Customer Success culture** — only the CSM team owns retention; rest of company doesn't (page 56)
- **Failing to manage expectations** — letting customers dictate one-way terms; not enforcing mutual responsibility (page 56-57)
- **Acquiring bad-fit customers** — sales closes accounts CSM cannot rescue; no joint authority to terminate (page 57)

## When to use (this lens)

- Diagnosing a struggling CS program with the symptoms (high churn, low NPS, CSM attrition)
- Pre-mortem when launching a new CS function or scaling an existing one
- Board / leadership reviews when CS performance disappoints despite investment
- CSM training and onboarding — pattern recognition before mistakes are made
- Hiring CS leadership — interview signal for which patterns they've personally fought

## When NOT to use

- As a generic checklist applied without context (some patterns don't apply to every business)
- To blame CSMs individually — most anti-patterns are organizational, not individual failures
- In isolation from a positive playbook — list the patterns to avoid AND the patterns to install

## Common compounded failures

- **Quota'd CSMs + undefined success** → CSMs push contract actions chasing numbers no one believes in
- **Bad-fit acquisition + churn mitigation focus** → CSM burns out rescuing accounts that never should have closed
- **No CS culture + over-templating** → CSMs become the only humans in a robotic experience
- **Confusing happiness with success + only-required-outcome** → green dashboards that mask silent churn
- **Failing to manage expectations + forgetting they're human** → customer relationships swing between doormat and adversarial

## Key metrics (signals an anti-pattern is active)

- **Rising churn despite "stable" health scores** — confusing happiness with success
- **High CSM attrition** — burnout from churn mitigation focus
- **Renewal surprises** — customers leaving without warning (no real relationships)
- **Wide variance in account outcomes** — success not defined consistently
- **Low NPS in accounts with high CSM activity** — overdelivering / over-templating
- **Sales closing accounts CSM flags as risky** — bad-fit acquisition pattern

## Decision questions

- Which of the ten patterns is most active in our org right now — and what's the upstream fix?
- Are we measuring happiness (CSAT) and confusing it with success (outcomes realized)?
- Do CSMs have any authority over the front-end (acquisition fit, contract terms)?
- Is "customer success" a team or a culture in our company — honestly?
- Are we patching churn or fixing the upstream process that produces it?

## Canonical examples

- **Generic SaaS pattern** — Pereira draws from a wide cross-section of SaaS companies where these patterns recur (pages 54-57)
- **The "Customer Success theater" company** — CSMs do QBRs, dashboards are green, churn quietly climbs (industry pattern; not in source but extrapolated from page 55 happiness-vs-success critique)
- **Quota'd CSM teams** — common at growth-stage SaaS where finance presses CS to "earn their seat" (pages 54-55)
