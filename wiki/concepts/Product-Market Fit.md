# Product-Market Fit

## Definition

Product-market fit (PMF) means your product addresses the real problems of a real target market — at sufficient scale to support growth and profitability. Pereira frames it as a scenario where "your product solves the problems of a large group of customers, and they are buying it, using it, and also recommending it to others" (page 9). It is not the same as a stock-out: PMF is the state where most of your customers come from referrals, you have a steady inflow of customers, and your product efficiently solves an important problem in a profitable market (page 8). Achieving PMF dramatically compresses the path to profitability — products that fit can succeed "much more quickly" than those that don't (page 9).

## Key attributes

- **Two-sided match** — the product fits the market AND the market is large enough to matter
- **Word-of-mouth pull** — customers are enthusiastic enough to recommend unprompted (page 13)
- **Reduced churn** — retention rises as the value proposition lands
- **Customer self-serve recognition** — buyers see the value without heavy sales push
- **Sales pull, not push** — "the right product for the right market is typically quite evident… the item will almost certainly sell itself" (page 10)
- **Not permanent** — PMF can be lost as markets and competitors evolve (page 50)
- **Not binary** — submarkets exist; you may have fit in one and not another (page 50)
- **Whole-company responsibility** — not just product/marketing; sales, support, finance all contribute (page 24)

## When to use

- Pre-launch: as the primary validation question for a new product
- Pre-scaling: as the gate that must close before pouring money into growth
- Re-evaluation: periodically, since market conditions shift (page 45)
- Pivot decisions: when growth stalls and you need to diagnose root cause
- Investor conversations: PMF is the most-asked question early-stage investors probe

## When NOT to use

- As a one-time checkbox — Pereira: PMF is "in no way the completion of the journey" (page 50)
- As a substitute for execution — fit alone doesn't beat strong rivals with better GTM
- In place of segmentation work — "PMF" averaged across multiple submarkets hides the truth
- As justification to ignore competition (page 50, misconception #4)
- As permission to scale prematurely (the model must also be unit-economic)

## Common pitfalls

- **Confusing PMF with selling out** — restocking-and-selling is not the same as a steady inflow with high WOM (page 8)
- **Building for sitcom problems** — fictional issues people don't actually need solved (page 12)
- **Founder-as-only-user fallacy** — building only for yourself without finding the broader market
- **Treating PMF as binary** — ignoring that submarkets may have different fits (page 50)
- **Complacency** — assuming PMF is permanent (Skype lost it to Zoom in 2020) (page 46)
- **Ignoring detractors** — only listening to promoters loses critical signal (page 27)
- **Premature scaling** — pouring money into growth before fit is validated

## Key metrics

- **Word-of-mouth share of acquisition** — the dominant qualitative PMF signal
- **Retention curve shape** — a flattening curve indicates fit; a continuously declining one doesn't
- **NPS** — promoters minus detractors on the 1-10 scale (page 34)
- **Churn rate** — lower is fit-positive
- **LTV/CAC ratio** — target 3:1 in SaaS (page 35)
- **Sean Ellis score** — % who would be "very disappointed" if the product disappeared (not in Pereira directly, but the canonical PMF benchmark)
- **Growth rate sustained over time** — demonstrates PMF held (page 37)

## Decision questions

- Do customers recommend us unprompted to friends/colleagues? (page 13)
- Is our market large enough to support growth? (page 9)
- Are we solving a real, important problem — or a "sitcom problem"? (page 12)
- Would our customers be very disappointed if we shut down tomorrow?
- Is our growth driven by organic pull or paid push?
- Are we listening to detractors AND promoters? (page 27)
- Has the market shifted such that our fit is at risk? (page 50)

## Canonical examples

- **Spotify** — saw market signal from Napster shutdown; 182M paying subscribers by 2022 (page 17)
- **Uber** — free rides at SF tech events to generate pull; network effects + social proof (page 18)
- **Netflix** — solved late-fee pain from brick-and-mortar; kept evolving from DVD to streaming (pages 18-19)
- **Google AdSense** — found unmet need (ads on non-search pages); 12M+ users, $209B annual revenue by 2021 (pages 19-20)
- **Slack** — pivoted FROM a game studio's internal comms tool TO collaboration software (page 20)
- **Zoom vs Skype** — Zoom captured remote-work surge in 2020 while Skype was complacent (page 46)
- **Parade** — environmentally friendly underwear in plus sizes; identified unmet need in a never-going-away market (pages 21-22)
