# Halo Effect

## Definition

The halo effect is the tendency to let a single positive trait or impression of a person, brand, or thing color the overall evaluation — assuming that someone attractive is also intelligent, that a successful entrepreneur must be a good leader, or that a brand strong in one product must be strong in all. Pereira treats it as the cognitive economy at work: rather than evaluating each attribute independently, the brain extrapolates from the salient impression. The "horns effect" (or reverse halo) is the same mechanism running negatively: a single weakness contaminates the entire assessment.

## Key attributes

- **Single trait dominates the gestalt** — one positive (or negative) feature overrides multi-dimensional evaluation
- **Operates fast and unconsciously** — formed in seconds, hard to dislodge
- **Cross-domain extrapolation** — competence in one area is assumed in unrelated areas
- **Symmetric** — the "horns effect" inverts the same mechanism
- **Strongest with low-information contexts** — fills gaps where actual data is sparse
- **Stable over time** — once formed, resistant to disconfirming evidence (links to confirmation bias)
- **Brand-transferable** — applies to people, products, brands, institutions

## When you're most at risk

- You're making a fast judgment with limited data (interviews, first meetings)
- One attribute of the person/thing is highly visible or salient
- You're evaluating in your area of weakness (founder evaluating designer; engineer evaluating salesperson)
- The decision is consequential and you want quick certainty
- The person has high status or attractive presentation

## When it's NOT a problem

- The salient trait is genuinely correlated with the other traits being judged (a known correlation, not an assumption)
- Branding contexts where you're deliberately leveraging a halo for legitimate transfer of trust
- When you're rapidly screening hundreds of candidates and false positives are recoverable
- When the halo aligns with explicit competency you've already verified

## Common manifestations in business

- **Hiring**: impressive resume + slick interview → assumed competence in all dimensions (Pereira p.80)
- **Performance reviews**: one big win this year → overall "exceeds expectations" (p.80)
- **Leadership appointments**: success in one role → assumed fit for next (p.81)
- **Brand extensions**: brand strong in laptops → assumed strong in phones (p.81)
- **Celebrity endorsement**: the celebrity's brand transfers to the product (p.79)
- **Founder worship**: a successful exit → assumed wisdom on all topics
- **Investing**: strong CEO → assumed strong company financials (p.81)
- **Pitch-deck logos**: prestigious customer logos → assumed product quality
- **VC pattern matching**: founder pedigree (Stanford, ex-FAANG) overweights everything else

## Indicators you're falling into it

- You'd struggle to list specific weaknesses of the person/thing
- Your assessment changed dramatically based on one observation
- You're confident across dimensions you haven't actually evaluated
- The team agrees suspiciously quickly after one positive signal
- You catch yourself defending the person/thing without specific evidence
- Reverse: you've written something off entirely based on one negative trait

## Countermeasures

- **Structured evaluation** — pre-defined criteria; rate each dimension independently (Pereira p.83)
- **Multiple evaluators** — get independent assessments before discussing
- **Blind evaluation** — strip away the halo source (resumes without names; work samples without attribution)
- **Decompose into specific behaviors** — "What exactly did they do?" not "How did they seem?" (p.83)
- **Time-separated assessments** — evaluate trait 1 today, trait 2 tomorrow; let the halo decay
- **Devil's advocate** — formally task someone to list the negatives
- **Multiple data sources** — interviews + work samples + references + tests, scored separately (p.84)
- **Calibration sessions** — review past decisions and outcomes; spot the halo pattern in your history

## Canonical examples

- **Steve Jobs halo extending to Apple Maps** — first version was bad; halo absorbed early criticism
- **Elizabeth Holmes' Stanford-dropout, black-turtleneck halo** — silenced scientific skepticism for years
- **Adam Neumann at WeWork** — founder charisma carried valuation past fundamentals
- **Celebrity-led ventures** — Goop, Honest Company; brand halo from celebrity didn't fix unit economics
- **GE under Jack Welch** — leadership halo masked the financial-engineering problems that surfaced later
- **CEOs hired from one industry into another** — assumed transferable competence
- **Pereira's attractive-CEO research** — physically attractive CEOs receive higher pay and better ratings (p.78)
- **"Halo" brand extensions** — Virgin's many failed launches; Apple's Newton; Google+'s persistence
