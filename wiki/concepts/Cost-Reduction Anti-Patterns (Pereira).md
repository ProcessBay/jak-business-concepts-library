# Cost-Reduction Anti-Patterns (Pereira)

## Definition

Pereira's closing chapter catalogs the eleven most common ways cost-reduction programs destroy value while appearing to create it. The frame is diagnostic: if your program is exhibiting any of these, the cost savings you are reporting are either temporary, illusory, or already being repaid as future losses. The eleven anti-patterns span three families: **the cuts themselves go wrong** (blanket cuts, harmful risks, choking off innovation, missing digital, slowing the organization), **the behavior change doesn't stick** (failing to sustain past the three-year mark), **the basics are missing** (rushing into unfair vendor contracts, not registering operations, not tracking cash flow, not knowing costs, mispricing products). Pereira's headline statistic: fewer than half of leaders realize their year-one savings target, and fewer than 12% of organizations sustain new spending practices beyond three years.

## Key attributes

- **Eleven distinct failure modes** — not a vague warning, a specific checklist
- **Three families** — bad cuts, unsustained behavior, missing basics
- **Headline failure rates** — <50% hit year-1 target; <12% sustain past year 3; only 9% protect innovation; only 6% scale without overhead bloat
- **Compound damage** — most anti-patterns trade short-term P&L improvement for larger long-term cost
- **Diagnostic, not prescriptive** — name the pattern first, then choose the corrective from the Six Approaches

## The eleven anti-patterns

- **Blanket cuts with unrealistic targets** — uniform percentage cuts damage efficient parts of the business and erode sources of value (fewer than half of leaders hit year-1 targets)
- **Failing to sustain behavior change** — <12% of organizations sustain new spending discipline past three years; gains erode
- **Slowing down the organization** — only 6% of companies invest in growth without smothering it in overhead complexity; SKU bloat and slow decision-making consume profits
- **Choking off needed innovation** — only 9% create capacity for innovation; aggressive cuts drain high-impact projects and discourage multi-year bets
- **Missing the boat on digital** — 66% of CFOs intended to boost digital investment in 2022; under-investing here forfeits CX, employee experience, competitive edge, and inflation defense
- **Rushing into unfair contracts with providers** — two in five IT leaders regret tech purchases; common complaints are overpriced fees, unfavorable terms, hidden inefficiencies, and shortfall vs. expectations
- **Introducing harmful risks to the organization** — under-investing in IT cuts costs but invites breach / ransomware exposure whose remediation can eclipse the savings
- **Not registering operations** — incomplete bookkeeping makes it impossible to know inflow vs. outflow, leaving gaping holes in the accounts
- **Not keeping track of cash flow** — without diligent cash-flow records, inconsistencies arise with nothing to fall back on
- **Not knowing your costs** — without product, general, stock, and labor cost visibility, no cost reduction can be sized or tracked
- **Not pricing your products properly** — pricing must combine cost knowledge with customer value perception; misprice and the cost work is wasted

## When to invoke this atom

- Cost-reduction program is delivering short-term P&L but eroding NPS, employee engagement, or innovation throughput
- Board / leadership asking "why did our cuts not hold?" 12–18 months in
- Post-mortem of a previous cost program
- Designing a new program — to pressure-test it against the failure modes before launch
- M&A integration where cost synergies are being targeted aggressively

## When this frame is the wrong frame

- Genuine cash crises where survival depends on cuts that would otherwise be flagged here (acknowledge the trade)
- Pure efficiency programs with no quality or growth implications (the anti-patterns are calibrated to broader cost programs)
- Pre-revenue startups where many of the "basics" antis don't yet apply

## Common pitfalls in using the framework

- Using it to block any cost action rather than to shape better ones
- Cherry-picking the convenient antis and ignoring the inconvenient ones (innovation choke and digital miss are usually the inconvenient ones)
- Treating the basics (registration, cash flow, cost visibility, pricing) as accounting issues rather than leadership issues
- Diagnosing the pattern without designing the corrective from the Six Approaches

## Key metrics

- **Year-1 savings realization rate** vs. target
- **Sustained savings rate at year 3** (industry baseline <12%)
- **Innovation investment ratio** pre/post program
- **Digital investment ratio** pre/post program
- **Incident / breach cost** as % of cost-cut value (the IT-under-investment risk)
- **Employee engagement and NPS deltas** as leading indicators of unsustainability

## Decision questions

- Are we measuring sustained savings, or year-one savings only?
- Which anti-pattern is our program most exposed to right now?
- Are we cutting innovation and digital because they're easy to defer, not because they're low-value?
- Have we verified the basics — registration, cash flow, cost visibility, pricing — before launching a major cut?
- Are the vendor contracts we're signing under cost pressure ones we'd sign with more time?

## Canonical examples

- **Blanket cuts** — across-the-board headcount RIFs that take productive teams alongside underperformers
- **Innovation choke** — Kodak, Blockbuster — both protected the P&L by under-investing in the next platform
- **Missing digital** — retailers that under-invested in e-commerce pre-2020 and paid for it through the pandemic
- **Unfair vendor contracts** — multi-year SaaS lock-ins signed under cost pressure that later prove over-priced and under-functional
- **Not pricing properly** — a classic small-business failure mode where products are priced from cost alone with no view of customer-perceived value
