# First Principles Thinking

## Definition

First Principles Thinking is the practice of decomposing a hard problem down to its irreducible facts — what is provably true — and then reasoning back up from there, rather than reasoning by analogy or precedent. Pereira frames it as "one of the most recommended ways to find out how things work and open up creative possibilities," noting it is sometimes called "reasoning from first principles." The discipline strips away inherited assumptions, exposes the basic elements, and lets new solutions emerge from a clean foundation — Elon Musk's signature method for attacking SpaceX, Tesla, and PayPal.

## Key attributes

- **Decomposition** — break the problem into the most basic facts/elements that cannot be reduced further
- **Assumption audit** — explicitly write down every assumption, then challenge each one by considering its opposite
- **Reasoning forward, not by analogy** — build solutions from the base facts, not from what competitors or precedent suggest
- **Question-driven** — relies on potent, naive-sounding questions ("Why?", "Why is that true?", "What must be true for this to hold?")
- **Innovation-enabling** — by ignoring how things are "usually done," opens novel solution paths
- **Costly and slow** — requires significant cognitive effort; not suitable for routine or low-stakes decisions
- **Munger / Musk pedigree** — central to the Munger latticework and explicitly invoked by Musk: "Good ideas are crazy until they're not"

## When to use

- High-stakes, novel, or strategic problems where the conventional answer is suspect
- Cost or design challenges where competitors have all converged on a similar (possibly inflated) solution
- Industry disruption / category-creation contexts (Tesla on battery cost, SpaceX on rocket reusability)
- When you sense an assumption is doing too much work in the current model
- Product development where existing approaches are heavy with legacy constraints

## When NOT to use

- Routine, low-stakes decisions where analogical reasoning is faster and good enough
- Domains where you lack the expertise to identify what the "first principles" actually are
- Time-critical operational moments — first-principles reasoning is deliberately slow
- When the cost of being wrong is low and reversibility is high (just try the conventional thing)

## Common pitfalls

- Mistaking opinion or convention for a first principle (false floor)
- Decomposing too far — paralysis by infinite regress
- Re-inventing the wheel for problems where the conventional solution actually is right
- Hubris: assuming a fresh first-principles analysis beats deep domain expertise (often it doesn't)
- Confusing "ignoring how others do it" with "ignoring what is physically/economically true"

## Signals you're using it well

- You can state the irreducible facts of the problem in plain language without invoking precedent
- Your solution differs structurally (not just incrementally) from the conventional approach
- You've explicitly challenged at least one assumption others treat as given
- The solution you propose would have been "crazy" to a peer six months ago

## Decision questions

- What are the basic elements of this problem? What is provably true?
- Which of my assumptions am I treating as facts? What if the opposite were true?
- If I were starting from a blank sheet today, would I arrive at the current solution?
- Am I reasoning from first principles, or am I just copying what worked elsewhere?
- What would have to be true for a fundamentally different approach to win?

## Canonical examples

- **Elon Musk on rockets (SpaceX)** — instead of accepting market rocket prices, broke costs down to raw materials (aluminum, titanium, copper, carbon fiber) and found the materials cost was ~2% of the rocket price, opening reusability as the path to 10x cost reduction
- **Elon Musk on batteries (Tesla)** — same decomposition logic on lithium-ion battery cell cost
- **Aristotle** — credited as the original articulator of reasoning from the fewest possible postulates (Pereira, page 52)
- **Pereira's framing** — "Good ideas are crazy until they're not" (Musk, quoted page 32)
