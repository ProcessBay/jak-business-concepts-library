# Strategic vs Tactical Planning

## Definition

Strategic planning establishes an organization's long-term direction — the 3-5+ year goals and the foundational structure required to reach them. Tactical planning is the short-term, ground-level work of turning that direction into reality — defining the specific steps, owners, and short-cycle objectives that operationalize the strategy. Pereira's framing is that strategy without tactics is intention without execution, while tactics without strategy is busywork without direction. The two are interdependent: strategy lays the foundation, tactics build on it, and effective organizations run both layers in continuous coordination.

## Key attributes

- **Time horizon distinction** — strategic = 3-5+ years; tactical = months to one year
- **Altitude distinction** — strategic = foundational and directional; tactical = operational and specific
- **Strategy comes first** — tactics are derived from strategic intent, not the other way around
- **Both required** — strategy without tactics is wishful; tactics without strategy is wasteful
- **Different cadences** — strategic plans reviewed quarterly/annually; tactical plans reviewed weekly/monthly
- **Different ownership** — strategy typically C-suite + board; tactics typically functional leaders + teams
- **Pereira's three-domain illustration** — Sales Recruitment, Marketing, and Tech Infrastructure all show the pattern: strategy defines the *what*, tactics define the *how*

## Why this matters

- Most failed plans confuse the two — leaders set tactics ("hire 5 reps") when they should be setting strategy ("build a repeatable hiring system")
- Strategic-tactical alignment is the binding constraint on execution velocity
- Teams need both layers to know if today's work is moving the organization forward
- Underinvestment in tactical clarity is the most common reason strategy "doesn't get executed"
- Overinvestment in tactics without strategy produces local optimization at the cost of global direction

## Common pitfalls

- Calling tactical plans "strategy" because the org won't do real strategic work
- Setting strategy without tactical translation — the plan dies on the shelf
- Setting tactics without strategic frame — teams build the wrong things efficiently
- Letting tactical urgency crowd out strategic time (Eisenhower matrix failure)
- Switching the two as a cadence problem (annual planning that's really tactical; weekly planning that drifts into strategy)
- Confusing strategy with vision (vision is the destination; strategy is the choice of route)
- Confusing tactics with execution (tactics are planned actions; execution is doing them)

## Decision questions

- For any plan — is this strategic (3+ years, directional) or tactical (months, operational)?
- Do our tactics trace cleanly back to a stated strategy?
- Where are we executing tactics with no clear strategic parent?
- Where do we have strategy with no tactical translation?
- Who owns each layer, and are the review cadences different?
- When the strategy changes, do we explicitly rebuild the tactics?

## Canonical examples

- **Pereira's Sales Recruitment example** — Strategy: build a repeatable evaluation system for hiring salespeople. Tactics: narrow down the specific qualities desired, create a script of interview questions, train recruiters on the standard (page 15)
- **Pereira's Marketing example** — Strategy: improve sales-marketing alignment. Tactics: define SQL qualities, encourage cross-functional collaboration on content, run post-campaign reviews (pages 15-16)
- **Pereira's Tech Infrastructure example** — Strategy: shift toward tech-driven sales operations. Tactics: adopt a CRM, introduce conversational technology for calls, equip teams for virtual sales (page 16)
