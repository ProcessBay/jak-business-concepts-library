# Sunk Cost Fallacy

## Definition

The sunk cost fallacy is the tendency to continue investing resources in an endeavor because of what's already been spent, rather than on the basis of expected future returns. "Sunk costs" are time, money, or effort already expended and unrecoverable regardless of the decision now. Rational decision theory says ignore them; humans don't. Pereira locates the mechanism in loss aversion (admitting a loss is more painful than continuing to bleed) and cognitive dissonance (continuing the commitment lets us avoid concluding the original decision was a mistake).

## Key attributes

- **Past spending shouldn't influence forward decisions** — but emotionally, it does
- **Driven by loss aversion** — abandoning makes the loss feel "real"; continuing keeps it ambiguous
- **Driven by cognitive dissonance** — quitting forces admission that the original decision was wrong
- **Ego-protective** — especially strong when the decision-maker championed the original investment
- **Scales with public commitment** — the more visibly committed, the harder to quit
- **Affects time, money, AND effort** — not just financial
- **Cross-domain** — projects, products, jobs, relationships, partnerships, ventures

## When you're most at risk

- You championed the original decision and your reputation is tied to it
- A lot has been spent publicly (large team, board updates, press)
- You've been working on it long enough that walking away feels like wasted years
- The project is "almost there" (always almost there)
- Quitting would mean admitting an earlier judgment was wrong
- The remaining cost is being paid in small increments rather than one big one

## When it's NOT a problem

- The forward-looking expected value is genuinely still positive
- Persistence in the face of obstacles is the differentiating factor (most ventures fail by quitting too early, not too late)
- The "sunk cost" thinking is actually compound learning that matters for the next decision
- You've explicitly evaluated the path forward on its merits and the answer is still "yes"

## Common manifestations in business

- **Failing projects continued** — pouring resources into a project past the point of viability (Pereira's central example, p.87)
- **Pivot resistance** — refusing to change product/market because of how much was built
- **Bad hires kept** — keeping someone underperforming because of recruiting investment
- **Bad acquisitions doubled down on** — investing more to "make the deal work"
- **Partnerships maintained past expiry** — because of the years already invested
- **Product features that can't be killed** — "but we built it"
- **Cofounder relationships kept past expiration** — equity already split, history already long

## Indicators you're falling into it

- Your strongest reason for continuing is how much has been spent
- You catch yourself saying "we can't quit now after all we've put in"
- The forward-looking case is weak but you can't bring yourself to make the cut
- Project updates focus on past investment rather than future trajectory
- You'd never start the project today, given current information
- Killing the project means a face-losing conversation you keep postponing

## Countermeasures

- **Sunk-cost zero mindset** — mentally erase all past investment; evaluate as if starting fresh today (Pereira p.89)
- **Fresh-eyes test** — "Would we start this project today, knowing what we know now?"
- **Cost-benefit on forward economics only** — what does spending the next dollar/hour return?
- **Pre-set kill criteria** — define quit conditions before starting, so the decision doesn't require fresh courage
- **External input** — ask someone with no skin in the original decision
- **Calculate opportunity cost** — what would you do with the resources if you redirected them today?
- **Reframe sunk cost as paid tuition** — the spending bought knowledge; the knowledge stays even if the project stops
- **Decision journal** — track sunk-cost-influenced decisions to build awareness over time
- **Separate the decider from the original decision-maker** — different person evaluates "continue or kill"

## Canonical examples

- **The Concorde** — Britain and France knew supersonic was uneconomic by the 1970s; flew until 2003
- **Vietnam War escalation** — "we've already lost too many to stop now"
- **Kodak's film business** — couldn't kill film fast enough; bankrupt 2012
- **Blockbuster's stores** — kept opening retail past the obvious shift to streaming
- **Quibi** — kept burning post-launch despite no traction; should have cut sooner
- **Theranos** — investors and the board doubled down rather than write off
- **Pereira's concert example** — going to a concert sick because you can't refund the ticket (p.87)
- **Failing M&A integrations** — "we just need another year to realize synergies"
