# Minimum Viable Segment

## Definition

Pereira's term for the smallest customer segment a company targets when entering a new market or executing a business model transition — the beachhead from which expansion happens. The MVS makes the early phase tractable: customers are easier to understand, the product/service is easier to adjust, and the firm can prepare the base it will scale from. Pereira pairs this with three canonical illustrations: Facebook started with Harvard students, Tesla started with ultra-rich Roadster buyers, Amazon started with book buyers. In each case the MVS wasn't the destination — it was the platform that funded and validated the path to the much larger target market.

## Key attributes

- **Small by design** — not "small because we couldn't get bigger"; intentionally narrow to maximize learning
- **Tractable** — small enough that the founding team can understand the customer in depth
- **Iteration-friendly** — small enough that product and model adjustments are fast and cheap
- **Scale-preparing** — the segment is the foundation of capabilities, brand, and economics for later expansion
- **Often unlikely on paper** — Harvard students, ultra-rich EV buyers, book buyers all looked too small at the time
- **Self-funding when possible** — the MVS economics ideally fund the move to the next adjacent segment

## When to use

- Launching a new product or business model where customer demand is uncertain
- Running a business model transition (step 1 of the [[Business Model Transition Playbook]])
- When capital is constrained and you need to prove the model before scaling
- When the target market is large but heterogeneous and a generic offer would resonate with no one

## When NOT to use

- When the market is small enough that you can serve the whole thing from day one
- When competitors have already locked up obvious beachhead segments and you need an asymmetric path
- When the MVS is so unlike the target market that learnings won't transfer (false beachhead)

## Common pitfalls

- Choosing an MVS that's easy to win but doesn't generalize to the target market
- Staying in the MVS too long — the segment is a launchpad, not a home
- Choosing an MVS by size rather than by learning value or expansion potential
- Optimizing the product so heavily for the MVS that it becomes hard to broaden later
- Letting the MVS economics define what's possible at scale

## Key metrics

- **Segment size** and addressability
- **Penetration rate** within the MVS over time
- **Traction signals** — repeat use, retention, word-of-mouth
- **Time to dominate the segment** before moving to the next one
- **Expansion conversion** — how successfully the MVS playbook generalizes to adjacent segments

## Decision questions

- What's the smallest segment that can both teach us and fund the next phase?
- Why this segment specifically — what asymmetric advantage do we have here?
- How will we know we've "won" the MVS and it's time to expand?
- What learnings from the MVS will transfer to the next segment, and what won't?
- Are we choosing this MVS because it's defensible, or just because it's easy?

## Canonical examples

- **Facebook** — Harvard University students; 1,200 sign-ups in 24 hours, half of Harvard in a month, then Boston schools, Ivy League, US universities, anyone with an email (pages 16-17, 29)
- **Tesla** — ultra-high-net-worth Roadster buyers; revenue funded R&D for the mass-market Model 3 (pages 10-12, 29)
- **Amazon** — book buyers; a "safe bet for a still-emerging market," then music, video, electronics, everything (pages 22-23, 29)
