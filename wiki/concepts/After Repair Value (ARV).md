# After Repair Value (ARV)

## Definition

After Repair Value (ARV) is the estimated market value of a property *after* all planned renovations and repairs have been completed. It is the single most consequential number in fix-and-flip, wholetailing, and BRRRR investing — every downstream economic decision (max acquisition price, rehab budget, refinance loan amount, gross profit projection) flows from it. ARV is derived through comparable-sales analysis ("comps"): the investor or appraiser identifies recently sold properties of similar size, condition, layout, and neighborhood, then adjusts for differences. Pereira repeatedly notes ARV's leverage in the 70% rule, in BRRRR refinance underwriting, and in fix-and-flip business plans, and warns that ARV estimation error is the most common cause of investor losses. Professional practice is to pull at least three to five recent comps (within 90 days, within 0.5 mile, within 20% square footage), validate with an agent BPO or appraisal, and stress-test by using the lowest comp as the anchor rather than the average.

## Key attributes

- **Backward-looking by construction** — derived from sold comps, not future projections
- **Comp-quality dependent** — bad comps → bad ARV → bad deal
- **The anchor input for the 70% rule** — every dollar of ARV moves max purchase price by ~$0.70
- **The refinance trigger in BRRRR** — lender appraises against ARV at the seasoning point
- **Time-sensitive** — markets move; comps older than 90 days lose validity quickly
- **Subject to appraiser discretion** — the bank's appraiser may not agree with yours
- **Often stress-tested by using lowest comp** — protective bias against overestimation
- **Distinguishable from current "as-is" value** — ARV adds the value created by rehab

## When to use

- Acquisition underwriting for any value-add real-estate strategy (flip, wholetail, BRRRR)
- Refinance modeling at the end of a BRRRR cycle
- Wholesale assignment pricing — buyers underwrite to ARV
- Lender approval for hard-money / rehab loans
- Business-plan financial projections for fix-and-flip operations

## When NOT to use

- Pure buy-and-hold underwriting where rent — not exit value — drives the deal
- Markets with thin comparable sales (luxury, rural, unique properties)
- Pre-development land deals — DCF or feasibility analysis instead
- Commercial property — cap-rate-based valuation usually replaces ARV

## Common pitfalls

- **Pulling cherry-picked comps** — confirmation bias on the high side
- **Using stale comps** — markets shift faster than spreadsheets
- **Ignoring condition / location adjustments** — superficial sq-ft comparison only
- **Trusting Zestimate / algorithmic estimates** — they're starting points, not anchors
- **Skipping the agent BPO** — local agents see things algos miss
- **Using average comp value** — better to anchor on the lowest defensible comp
- **Forgetting appraiser conservatism** — bank appraisal may come in 5–10% below your number

## Key metrics

- **ARV** — the dollar estimate itself
- **Comps used** — minimum 3, ideally 5; within 90 days, 0.5 mile, ±20% sq ft
- **Comp adjustment delta** — % adjustments for condition, lot, beds/baths, garage
- **ARV variance vs. appraisal** — track historical accuracy
- **Spread to all-in cost** — ARV − (purchase + rehab + holding) = gross profit
- **ARV × 0.70** — input to the 70% rule

## Decision questions

- How many comps did I pull, and are they within 90 days / 0.5 mile?
- Am I using the lowest defensible comp or the average?
- Have I validated with a local agent BPO?
- What does the bank's appraiser typically come in at vs. my number?
- How will ARV move if the market dips 5% before I resell or refi?
- Did I separately model "as-is value" and ARV, and is the rehab actually creating the lift?

## Canonical examples

- **Pereira's wholetail case** — ARV $130k vs. full-rehab ARV $150k; rehab choice shifts the ARV anchor (page 49)
- **Pereira's 70% rule example** — ARV $180k × 0.70 − $35k rehab = $91k max offer (page 66, 85)
- **Pereira's BRRRR case** — purchase $220k + rehab $15k → appraisal ARV $290k → refi at 75% LTV (page 105-106)
- **Pereira's BRRRR example #2** — rehab cost $8,700 → ARV grew from $100k purchase value to $160k appraisal (page 111-112)
- **Saudi/MENA**: ARV estimation in KSA is harder due to (a) less transparent transaction data, (b) less granular comp sets, (c) limited automated valuation tools. Investors typically use a wider variance buffer (e.g., the 60–65% rule instead of 70%) and rely more on local broker BPOs
