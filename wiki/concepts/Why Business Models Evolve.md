# Why Business Models Evolve

## Definition

Business models evolve for three primary reasons: (1) **Technology Disruptions** — new technologies change what's possible and what customers expect; (2) **Commoditization** — when offerings can no longer compete on value, they fall back to competing on price, forcing model change to escape the commodity trap; (3) **Competition** — competitors force evolution either by direct pressure or by demonstrating new approaches (Blue Ocean creation). Pereira frames evolution as both inevitable and gradual — most business models stay profitable for decades but must continuously adapt to socio-economic, cultural, and technological shifts. Companies that fail to evolve become Blockbuster or Kodak.

## Key attributes

- **Three primary drivers** — Technology, Commoditization, Competition
- **Continuous, not discrete** — evolution is ongoing, not a project
- **Different time scales** — technology shifts fast; commoditization slowly; competition variable
- **Forced or voluntary** — better to evolve voluntarily than be forced
- **Survival pattern** — companies that evolved (GE shifting to data, Netflix to streaming) survived; those that didn't (Blockbuster, Kodak) didn't
- **Customer expectations as carrier** — drivers manifest through changing customer behavior

## When to use

- Strategic planning to anticipate model obsolescence
- Diagnosing why a successful model started failing
- Investment decisions — which business models are at evolution risk
- Recognizing the moment to lead vs follow industry change

## When NOT to use

- For purely operational decisions
- When current model is in early growth — premature evolution wastes capital

## Common pitfalls

- Falling in love with current model; resisting evolution until forced
- Confusing tactical adjustments with model evolution
- Evolving the wrong dimension (cosmetic vs. structural)
- Ignoring weak signals of impending shift (Blockbuster: streaming was a clear weak signal)
- Over-evolving in response to passing trends

## Key metrics

- Time since last major business model update
- Customer behavior shifts (signal of demand-side evolution)
- New entrant frequency in your category
- Technology adoption curves relevant to your category

## Decision questions

- What technology disruption could obsolete our model in 3-5 years?
- Where is our offering commoditizing, and what's our escape strategy?
- Which competitor is forcing the most evolutionary pressure on us?
- Is our model evolving fast enough?

## Canonical examples

- **Newspaper industry** — paper → digital evolution; many incumbents failed (page 7)
- **Telephone industry** — landline → mobile → smartphone; killed traditional players (page 8)
- **Microsoft Office vs Google Workspace** — Google's freemium model forced Microsoft to evolve to cloud (page 8)
- **Blockbuster vs Netflix** — Blockbuster failed to evolve from physical to streaming; bankruptcy (page 9)
- **Kodak** — failed to ride digital photography despite inventing the technology (page 9)
- **Ford Model T** — 1900s mass production exemplifies tech-driven evolution (page 9)
