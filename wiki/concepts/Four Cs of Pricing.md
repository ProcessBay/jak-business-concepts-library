# Four Cs of Pricing

## Definition

Pereira's Four Cs framework names the four inputs every pricing decision must triangulate: **Customers** (who they are, what they'll pay), **Current Positioning** (where the brand sits in the market hierarchy), **Competitors** (the going rate and how rivals will react), and **Costs** (the floor below which you destroy yourself). Each "C" alone produces a brittle pricing strategy. Cost-plus alone ignores willingness-to-pay; competitor-only pricing ignores your distinct value; customer-WTP-only pricing ignores cost reality. The Four Cs forces the practitioner to hold all four in tension simultaneously.

## Key attributes

- **Four inputs in tension** — no single C is sufficient
- **Customers** — target market, income range, willingness-to-pay
- **Current Positioning** — where your brand sits (premium / mid / value)
- **Competitors** — going rate, expected reactions to your price moves
- **Costs** — the absolute floor; unit, fixed, and marginal
- **Diagnostic, not algorithmic** — names the inputs, doesn't dictate the answer
- **Compatible with any base pricing strategy** — overlay on cost-plus, value, dynamic

## When to use

- Setting pricing for a new product or entering a new market
- Annual or quarterly pricing review
- Diagnosing why a current pricing strategy is failing (which C is being ignored?)
- Preparing pricing recommendations for leadership or board
- Onboarding new pricing or marketing team members

## When NOT to use

- As a substitute for actual pricing research (the framework needs inputs)
- When you have only data on one C and are pretending the others are addressed
- For real-time dynamic pricing decisions (too slow as an ops cadence)

## Common pitfalls

- Treating one C as the whole answer (cost-plus shops, competitor-followers, etc.)
- Researching all four but failing to integrate — analysis paralysis
- Letting one C silently dominate while paying lip service to others
- Skipping the positioning C and pricing as if your brand were neutral
- Ignoring competitor reaction (a static rather than game-theoretic view)

## Key metrics

- **Willingness-to-pay** by segment (Customers)
- **Brand positioning index** (premium / mid / value perception)
- **Competitor price index** (your price vs. market avg)
- **Gross margin** (Costs check)
- **Value capture ratio** (price ÷ perceived value)

## Decision questions

- **Customers:** What's our target's income range and disposable income for our category? What would they pay?
- **Current positioning:** Where are we — premium, mid, value — and is our price consistent?
- **Competitors:** What range do they operate in? How will they react if we cut or raise?
- **Costs:** What's our true unit cost? At what price do we lose money?
- Where are the four Cs in conflict, and which one should win?

## Canonical examples

- **Pereira's framing (page 240):**
  - *Customers:* "How do you define your target market? What disposable income range?"
  - *Current positioning:* Where the brand sits, and what price signals reinforce it
  - *Competitors:* "In what price range do your competitors operate? If they cut prices, how will that affect your sales?"
  - *Costs:* The floor that constrains everything else
