# Price Anchoring on Shelf

## Definition

Price Anchoring on Shelf is the retail application of the cognitive anchoring bias: shoppers evaluate a current price against the *first* price reference they encounter. In retail, this reference is typically the manufacturer's suggested retail price (MSRP), the original "was" price, a prominently displayed higher-tier product variant, or a competitor's price. Pereira treats anchoring as foundational to retail pricing psychology (pages 39-42, 55-57, 109-110): the "$200 was/$99 now" ticket on a department-store dress, the higher-tier product placed next to the mid-tier to make the mid look reasonable, the MSRP sticker on big-ticket electronics that the retailer immediately discounts. Anchoring is not itself a pricing strategy — it is the *perception layer* that makes nearly every other retail pricing tactic (markdowns, Hi-Lo events, premium/value tier comparisons, BOGO) feel like a deal to the shopper. Without an anchor, a low price looks normal; with one, the same low price reads as a win.

## Key attributes

- **First-reference cognitive bias** — shoppers anchor on the first price they see, then adjust
- **Two main anchor types** — previous price ("was/now") and product-variation anchor (higher tier next to lower)
- **MSRP / list price anchor** — manufacturer's suggested price often displayed even when never actually sold at
- **Shelf-side execution** — anchor appears on the ticket, sign, or POP display
- **Pairs with discount mechanics** — anchoring is the perception layer of markdowns, Hi-Lo, BOGO
- **Qualitative anchors also work** — "premium quality," "best in class," "rated #1" as comparative anchors
- **Dynamic anchoring (online)** — anchor changes based on user, time, or competitor signal
- **Regulatory exposure** — fake "was" prices trigger consumer-protection enforcement (FTC, ACCC, UK CMA)

## When to use

- On any promotional or markdown ticket — anchor makes the saving legible
- In product-tier merchandising — display a higher tier next to your target SKU to make it look reasonable
- For new product launches — anchor against MSRP or competitor price to position your offer
- For upselling — show the basic price first, then position the upgrade with the anchor as reference
- During seasonal markdown phases — anchor against pre-season price
- In any A/B test where you want to measure perception-driven conversion lift

## When NOT to use

- When the "was" price was never genuine — regulatory and reputational risk is real and growing
- For EDLP positioning — flat-price stores don't run anchors because they undermine the trust promise
- For luxury / premium-priced merchandise where the high price *is* the value signal (no need to discount-anchor)
- In commodity grocery basics where shoppers know the real market price already
- When the anchor is wildly implausible (a $4 item marked "Was $400" reads as deceptive)

## Common pitfalls

- Faking the original price — class actions and regulator fines (Kohl's, JCPenney, Amazon have all faced these)
- Anchor too close to actual selling price — no perceived saving, no conversion lift
- Anchor too aggressive — shoppers see through it, trust erodes
- Using the same anchor for years — shoppers stop reading it as new information
- Inconsistent anchor across channels (in-store ticket says $100, online product page says $80 was-price)
- Treating anchoring as a substitute for genuine value — works once, fails on repeat purchase
- Letting the anchor become the *advertised* price rather than the reference (loses the perception trick)

## Key metrics

- **Conversion lift with vs. without anchor** — A/B testable on product pages and shelf tickets
- **Perceived savings index** — survey measure of how much shoppers *believe* they saved
- **Full-price reference recall** — do shoppers actually remember the anchor price after the trip?
- **Regulatory complaint rate** — leading indicator of anchor-credibility erosion
- **Repeat-purchase rate at anchor-discounted price** — does the lift sustain or fade with repeat exposure?

## Decision questions

- Is our "was" price genuine — was the item actually offered at that price for a meaningful period?
- Are we using product-tier anchoring intentionally (placement and signage) or accidentally?
- Does our anchor make the saving feel legible without crossing the credibility line?
- Are we testing anchor variations or running on intuition?
- Are our anchors consistent across channels (shelf, online product page, mobile app)?
- What's the regulatory regime in our markets — does it require us to substantiate "was" pricing?

## Canonical examples

- **Department-store "was/now" ticket** — $200 was, $99 now — the classic retail anchor (page 40)
- **Apple's product-tier shelf layout** — iPhone Pro Max anchors the iPhone Pro purchase decision
- **Best Buy MSRP ticket on TVs** — manufacturer's list price displayed even though never sold at that price
- **JCPenney 2012 elimination of anchors** — Ron Johnson removed "was" pricing; sales collapsed 25% (anchor was load-bearing)
- **Williams-Sonoma's $429 vs. $279 bread maker** — adding higher-priced product made the $279 sell better (classic case study)
- **The Economist subscription page** — print-only $125, web-only $59, print+web $125 — the print-only acts as anchor for the bundle
- **Amazon "List Price" strikethrough** — subject of FTC scrutiny over genuineness
