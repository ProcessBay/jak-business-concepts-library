# OKR Anti-patterns

## Definition

**OKR Anti-patterns** are the recurring failure modes that hollow out the framework — practices that preserve the form of OKRs while destroying the function. Pereira treats these throughout the "Overcoming Challenges in OKR Implementation" section (pages 73-92) and via what successful case studies *avoid* (notably Netflix's compensation-decoupling, pages 105-106). The four highest-leverage failures: **sandbagging** (setting targets you know you'll exceed), **vanity Key Results** (measuring activity instead of outcome), **vague Objectives** (lofty platitudes that resist measurement), and **OKRs-as-performance-review** (tying scores to comp, which guarantees sandbagging). Most OKR rollouts that "fail" failed at one or more of these — not at the framework itself.

## Key attributes

- **Sandbagging** — setting KRs the team knows it can hit at 100% to look good at cycle end (page 79)
- **Vanity Key Results** — measuring activity ("publish 10 posts") instead of outcome ("grow organic traffic 30%") (page 27)
- **Vague Objectives** — "improve customer experience," "be more innovative" — sound good, measure nothing (pages 16-17, 79)
- **OKRs-as-performance-review** — tying scores to bonuses, which makes honest scoring impossible (Netflix explicitly avoids this; pages 105-106)
- **Top-down dictation** — leadership writes all OKRs; teams just receive them (pages 53-55)
- **Too many OKRs** — 8-10 Objectives per team dilutes attention to zero
- **Set-and-forget** — no check-ins, no mid-cycle adjustment, no retrospective (page 44)
- **Activity-as-OKR** — converting a roadmap into OKRs by reformatting (loses outcome focus)
- **Misaligned cadences** — parent annual, child quarterly, with no sync (page 43)
- **No leadership participation** — leaders mandate OKRs but don't publish their own (page 77)

## Common pitfalls

- **Renaming anti-patterns to hide them** — calling a sandbagged goal "aspirational" doesn't make it stretch
- **Treating one anti-pattern as the only failure mode** — these compound; a sandbagged vanity KR tied to comp is the worst case
- **Blaming the framework** — "OKRs don't work here" usually means "we ran into 3+ anti-patterns and didn't fix them"
- **Anti-pattern fatigue** — discovering all four at once and abandoning OKRs instead of fixing one at a time

## Key metrics

- **Sandbagging indicator** — % of aspirational OKRs scoring ≥ 0.95 (healthy: <30%)
- **Activity-KR ratio** — % of KRs measuring activity vs outcome (healthy: <20%)
- **Objective specificity score** — peer-rated 1-5 on whether each Objective is measurable in principle
- **Leadership OKR publication rate** — % of leadership team with current-cycle OKRs published (target: 100%)
- **Mid-cycle check-in adherence** — actual vs scheduled (target: ≥80%)

## Decision questions

- Did we hit 95%+ of our aspirational OKRs last cycle? (Likely sandbagging.)
- Could a stranger tell from our KRs whether we succeeded — or do they just describe activities?
- Do our Objectives inspire, or do they sound like marketing copy?
- Are OKR scores fed into anyone's performance review?
- Does leadership publish their own OKRs first?
- When was our last honest mid-cycle adjustment?

## Canonical examples

- **Sandbagging in sales-comp cultures** — sales teams routinely sandbag quotas; OKRs imported into this culture without decoupling produce the same pathology
- **Vanity metrics in marketing** — "publish 50 blog posts" instead of "drive 30% more qualified leads"
- **Vague Objectives in HR** — "build a great culture" — unmeasurable; should specialize to retention, eNPS, or specific behavior change
- **Netflix's deliberate avoidance** — explicitly decoupled OKRs from performance review *because* the predictable failure mode is sandbagging (pages 105-106)
- **Top-down dictation** — common in legacy enterprises adopting OKRs; teams "receive" OKRs at quarterly all-hands with no input (pages 53-55)
