# High-Touch vs Low-Touch Sales

## Definition

The two primary SaaS sales motions, distinguished by intensity of salesperson involvement per deal. **Low-Touch Sales** is self-service or lightly-assisted — users sign up, try, and convert with little or no human intervention; typical of SMB SaaS, freemium products, and PLG companies. **High-Touch Sales** is consultative, deal-by-deal — sales reps work each opportunity over weeks or months, with demos, custom proposals, procurement negotiations; typical of enterprise SaaS with 6-figure+ ACVs. Pereira frames the choice as driven by ACV: small ACVs can't sustain high-touch sales economics; large ACVs require it. Many companies operate both motions in parallel — low-touch for SMB, high-touch for enterprise.

## Key attributes

- **Low-touch** — self-service, freemium, online checkout, marketing-driven; CAC low; ACV $100s-$10K
- **High-touch** — AE-led, demos, custom contracts, procurement; CAC high; ACV $50K-$5M+
- **Sales cycle differs** — low-touch days/weeks; high-touch months/years
- **ACV economics determine viability** — high-touch needs high ACV to be profitable
- **Hybrid common** — low-touch for SMB; high-touch for enterprise expansion
- **Customer success function differs** — low-touch automated; high-touch dedicated CSMs

## When to use which

- **Low-Touch when**: ACV <$10K; product simple to evaluate; buyer is end user; volume is the game
- **High-Touch when**: ACV >$50K; product requires customization/integration; buyer is committee; relationship matters

## When NOT to use

- Pure high-touch on SMB (CAC eats all margin)
- Pure low-touch on enterprise (deals slip through unconverted)
- Mixing the two on same account (creates confusion about who owns the relationship)

## Common pitfalls

- Picking the wrong motion for ACV (most common: too high-touch for small deals)
- Failing to design product UX for low-touch (then expecting low-touch to work)
- Hiring sales team before product is sellable low-touch (waste of capital)
- Skipping high-touch when enterprise needs it (leave deals on table)
- Operating both motions but with no clear segmentation rules (chaos)

## Key metrics

- CAC per motion (low-touch much lower than high-touch)
- Sales cycle length (days for low-touch; months/years for high-touch)
- ACV per motion (must align with CAC economics)
- Sales team productivity (deals per AE per quarter)
- Time to first value per motion

## Decision questions

- What's our ACV, and does it support high-touch sales economics?
- Should we segment customer base by ACV and run dual motion?
- Is our product designed for the motion we've chosen?
- What's the CAC payback per motion, and does it work?

## Canonical examples

- **Pure Low-Touch**: Notion (mostly self-serve), early Slack, Calendly, most consumer SaaS
- **Pure High-Touch**: Workday, Oracle, SAP, Salesforce enterprise tier
- **Hybrid**: Salesforce (low-touch SMB + Enterprise sales); HubSpot (similar); Atlassian (mostly low-touch but enterprise sales for largest accounts)
