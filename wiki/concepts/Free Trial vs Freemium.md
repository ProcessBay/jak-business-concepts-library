# Free Trial vs Freemium

## Definition

Free Trial and Freemium are different acquisition mechanics often conflated. A **free trial** gives users access to the FULL product (including premium features) for a limited time, typically with payment details required upfront and automatic conversion to paid if not canceled. A **freemium** gives users a feature-limited basic version of the product FOREVER, with no time limit and no payment details required, and conversion to paid happens when users want features beyond the basic tier. Pereira's verdict: free trials are less effective than freemium for getting customers to sign up — because requiring payment details upfront is a turn-off. But trials can be more effective at converting active users to paid because of the deadline pressure.

## Key attributes

- **Trial = time-limited, full product** — typically 7, 14, or 30 days
- **Freemium = forever-free, limited product** — basic tier never expires
- **Payment-method requirement** — trials often require payment upfront; freemium never does
- **Auto-conversion** — trials auto-convert; freemium requires active upgrade decision
- **Conversion mechanics differ** — trials use deadline pressure; freemium uses value-pull
- **Hybrid possible** — some firms offer freemium PLUS a free trial of the premium tier

## When to use

- **Free Trial**: when the premium product's value is best understood through full use; when payment friction can be accepted; when sales cycle is short
- **Freemium**: when product-market fit benefits from large free user base; when the basic version delivers standalone value; when bottom-up adoption is the GTM motion
- **Hybrid**: when you want to combine freemium's reach with trial's deadline pressure

## When NOT to use

- **Free Trial**: in B2C consumer apps where payment-up-front kills conversion
- **Freemium**: when supporting free users costs more than they generate (no ads, no referrals, no future conversion)
- **Hybrid**: when complexity of mechanic confuses users

## Common pitfalls

- Treating them as equivalent ("free for users to try")
- Using free trial in B2C where conversion-via-deadline doesn't work
- Using freemium where free user support cost is unsustainable
- Failing to instrument the right conversion mechanic per choice
- Not capturing payment method in trials (conversion drops sharply)

## Key metrics

- **Trial**: trial-to-paid conversion rate (typical SaaS: 15-25%)
- **Freemium**: free-to-paid conversion rate (typical SaaS: 2-5%)
- **Trial**: payment-method capture rate at signup
- **Freemium**: free user engagement (proxy for eventual conversion)
- **Both**: time-to-first-value

## Decision questions

- Does our product deliver standalone value at basic tier (freemium fit) or only at full functionality (trial fit)?
- Can we absorb the cost of supporting many free users (freemium)?
- Is payment-up-front a deal-breaker for our audience (rules out trial)?
- Could a hybrid (freemium + premium trial) outperform either alone?

## Canonical examples

- **Free Trial dominant** — Netflix initially offered trials; many enterprise SaaS use 30-day trials with payment captured upfront
- **Freemium dominant** — Spotify, Dropbox, Slack, Mailchimp, LinkedIn, Zoom
- **Hybrid** — Some Atlassian products: freemium + premium feature trial
- **HelloFresh / Blue Apron** — trial-like with first-box discount (not pure trial or freemium)
