# Viral Loop (Steve Blank)

## Definition

A growth mechanism — credited by Pereira to Steve Blank — in which satisfied customers, having received the value proposition the firm promised, tell other people about it, who become customers and in turn tell more people. Each completed cycle expands awareness "quickly and affordably" without proportional marketing spend, functioning as a word-of-mouth channel. The viral loop is downstream of value-proposition delivery: it activates only when customers are genuinely satisfied. It is the cheapest acquisition channel the firm can build, but cannot be manufactured by marketing alone — it is earned by the product.

## Key attributes

- **Downstream of satisfaction** — only triggers when the value proposition is genuinely delivered
- **Self-amplifying** — each cycle creates more potential broadcasters
- **Cheap and fast** — distribution cost approaches zero per net-new customer
- **Earned not bought** — marketing can enable the loop but cannot fake it
- **Distinct from network effects** — virality is about who you tell; network effects are about value-per-user rising with user count
- **Sensitive to friction** — sharing must be effortless or the loop stalls
- **Brand-reputation flywheel** — virality compounds into reputation, which lowers future CAC

## When to use

- When the product produces a genuine "you have to try this" moment for users
- When the segment talks to itself naturally (professional networks, hobby communities, parents)
- As a deliberate design constraint: build features that customers want to show off or share
- When marketing budgets are constrained and earned reach is the only viable path
- After confirming product-market fit; viral loops fail on weak products

## When NOT to use

- Before the value proposition is reliably delivered (virality of dissatisfaction is also a thing — and faster)
- As a substitute for paid acquisition in cold-start phase
- When the category is purely individual / private and customers don't talk about it
- When sharing is socially awkward (e.g., personal-care, financial-trouble categories)

## Common pitfalls

- Engineering "viral loops" while ignoring product quality — engineered sharing without genuine love produces spam, not virality
- Confusing referral-incentive programs with viral loops (incentives can help, but the engine is satisfaction)
- Optimizing for shares per user without checking conversion of those shares to satisfied customers
- Counting noisy/empty virality (views, impressions) instead of activated users
- Assuming virality scales linearly — saturation, fatigue, and platform changes can collapse the loop

## Key metrics

- **Viral coefficient (k-factor)** — net new users invited per existing user; k>1 is self-sustaining growth
- **Referral rate** — % of new customers acquired via existing customers
- **Cycle time** — how long from a customer's first use to their first successful invite
- **NPS** — proxy for likelihood-to-recommend, the upstream driver
- **CAC** — should fall as the viral channel grows
- **Share rate / share-to-conversion rate** for sharing surfaces

## Decision questions

- Is our value proposition genuinely deliverable to most customers, most of the time?
- What is our cycle time from new user to first invite?
- Is sharing friction-free, or buried behind menus?
- Are we measuring real activated referrals, or just impressions?
- If virality is our growth thesis, what k-factor do we need to hit our plan?
- What would collapse our loop (platform change, saturation, category fatigue)?

## Canonical examples

- **Hotmail** — the original viral footer ("Get your free email at Hotmail") that grew the service to 12M users in 18 months
- **Dropbox** — referral-incentivized invites layered on a genuinely loved product
- **WhatsApp / Signal** — communication products that require the other party to install
- **Notion, Calendly, Loom** — workplace tools where using them with others spreads the tool
- **Slack** — channel invitations as a viral mechanic
- **TikTok content sharing** — shareable artifacts as the loop
