# Probabilistic Thinking

## Definition

Probabilistic Thinking is the practice of estimating the likelihood of outcomes using math, logic, beliefs, and updated evidence — rather than treating the future as binary (will / won't happen). Pereira frames it as one of the six core mental models, distinguishing three sub-techniques: (1) **Bayesian updating** — adjusting probabilities as new information arrives; (2) **fat-tailed curves** — recognizing that real-world distributions are often non-normal and tail events are more common than they look; and (3) **asymmetries** — checking whether your probability estimates are systematically over- or under-optimistic. The goal is calibrated uncertainty, not false certainty.

## Key attributes

- **Estimate, don't guess** — assign explicit probabilities (50%, 80%, etc.) rather than soft language ("likely," "probably")
- **Bayesian updating** — start with a prior, update as new evidence arrives; the willingness to update matters more than the math
- **Fat tails** — many domains (markets, viral events, customer behavior) are not normally distributed; rare events dominate outcomes
- **Asymmetry check** — recognize over- and under-optimistic biases in your own estimates
- **Calibration over precision** — being right that something is 70% likely 70% of the time matters more than getting the number exact
- **Decision-quality framing** — good decision-making ≠ good outcomes; probabilistic thinking judges process, not result
- **Compatible with intuition** — you don't need to do explicit math; you need the discipline to attach numbers to your beliefs and revise them

## When to use

- Forecasting (revenue, churn, market response, launch outcomes)
- Investment and capital allocation decisions
- Strategic bets where outcomes are inherently uncertain
- Hiring and promotion (probability this candidate succeeds in the role)
- Risk assessment and pricing
- Whenever you catch yourself saying "definitely will" or "definitely won't"

## When NOT to use

- Pure deterministic problems (math, engineering with known inputs)
- Decisions where you have full information and one obviously dominant option
- When emotion or values should override probability (a moral floor, a non-negotiable principle)
- When the cost of attaching a number creates false precision worse than honest qualitative judgment

## Common pitfalls

- **Anchoring on the base rate** then refusing to update with strong new evidence
- **Refusing to update** when new evidence contradicts your prior (confirmation bias dressed up as "having conviction")
- **Over-precision** — assigning 73.4% probability where 70% or even "high" is the most defensible answer
- **Ignoring fat tails** — assuming normal distributions in domains (startups, markets, social dynamics) where they don't hold
- **Outcome bias** — judging the quality of probabilistic decisions only by whether they worked out
- **Forgetting asymmetric payoffs** — high-probability small wins can be worse than low-probability huge wins

## Key metrics

- **Base rate** — historical frequency for the reference class of similar decisions
- **Posterior probability** — updated probability after new evidence
- **Expected value** — probability × payoff, summed across outcomes
- **Calibration score** — over many predictions, do your "70% likely" calls come true ~70% of the time?

## Decision questions

- What's my base rate for this kind of decision? What's the historical hit rate?
- What would have to be true for me to update my probability?
- Am I treating a 70% probability as if it were 100%?
- What's the distribution shape — normal, or fat-tailed?
- Am I optimizing for expected value, or for "feeling certain"?
- If I made this decision 10 times, what's the distribution of outcomes I'd expect?

## Canonical examples

- **Pereira's dice example (p. 64)** — a friend rolls a die and reveals "it's an even number" — your 1-in-6 prior updates to 1-in-3. She adds "and it's not a 4" — your prior updates to 1-in-2. Each piece of information mechanically revises the probability
- **Munger / Buffett on probability** — Berkshire's investment discipline is fundamentally probabilistic; bet large only when the odds are clearly asymmetric in your favor
- **Nassim Taleb on fat tails** — argument that financial markets are dominated by rare events that normal-distribution models systematically underweight
- **Daniel Kahneman / Tversky** — foundational work on how humans systematically violate probabilistic reasoning, requiring deliberate countermeasures
