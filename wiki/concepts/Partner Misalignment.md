# Partner Misalignment

## Definition

Partner misalignment is the most common failure mode in strategic partnerships — the gradual or sudden divergence between partners on objectives, priorities, or expectations that threatens the collaborative effort's success. Pereira identifies three primary forms: (1) **Divergent Objectives** — partners pursue conflicting business strategies, target markets, or long-term goals; (2) **Priority Mismatch** — partners place different weights on dimensions of the collaboration (one optimizes cost, the other quality or market share); (3) **Expectation Gaps** — partners hold differing views on timeframes, resource allocation, or project outcomes. Misalignment usually surfaces in execution, not formation — which is why it's so destructive.

## Key attributes

- **Manifests in execution** — looks fine at signing; surfaces under operational pressure
- **Three forms** — divergent objectives, priority mismatch, expectation gaps
- **Compounds over time** — small misalignment grows into governance crisis if uncorrected
- **Erodes trust irreversibly** — once one party feels betrayed, recovery is slow
- **Drives most partnership failures** — Pereira's experience identifies this as primary cause
- **Cross-cultural amplifier** — international partnerships double the misalignment risk
- **Communication-resolvable early** — almost always preventable with early dialogue
- **Hard to fix late** — once compounded, often requires structural change or termination

## Why this is an anti-pattern

- It violates the foundational assumption that partners share strategic intent
- It turns governance meetings from coordination into negotiation
- It causes resource and effort waste — partners pulling in different directions
- It erodes the trust that strategic partnerships depend on
- It blocks the speed and synergy benefits that justified the partnership
- It often surfaces only when partners are too invested to walk away cleanly

## How to detect / common symptoms

- Increasing meeting frequency without increasing decision velocity
- Roadmap or priority debates that recur quarter after quarter without resolution
- Each partner advocating for different KPIs as the "real" success measure
- One partner consistently slow on commitments while the other is fast
- Customer-facing or market actions taken unilaterally without partner alignment
- Escalations to senior leadership becoming routine
- Partner NPS or relationship-health scores declining
- "We need to talk about the partnership" conversations recurring

## How to prevent / fix

- **Establish SMART, jointly-agreed objectives** upfront — not internally drafted then sold
- **Build robust, regular communication frameworks** — protocols, not just calendar invites
- **Design flexibility into agreements** — explicit mechanisms to adjust objectives as conditions change
- **Foster joint planning and decision-making culture** — both parties have real voice in critical calls
- **Document conflict-resolution protocols** — identify owners, timelines, processes upfront
- **Define alignment KPIs and review regularly** — measure misalignment, don't wait for crisis
- **Conduct periodic "still-aligned?" reviews** — surface drift before it compounds
- **Use mediation early** — neutral third-party intervention is far cheaper than litigation or dissolution

## Key metrics

- Misalignment incidents identified per quarter
- Escalations to senior leadership per quarter
- Decision velocity (time from question to call)
- Dispute resolution time
- Partner NPS / relationship-health score trend
- Partnership failure rate (firms that institutionalize alignment hygiene see lower failure)

## Decision questions

- When did we last formally check that our SMART objectives still match?
- Are escalations resolved within the partnership, or routinely punted upstairs?
- Does each partner have real voice in critical decisions, or is one dominating?
- What's the early-warning signal we've agreed will trigger a re-alignment conversation?
- Are we measuring relationship health, or only financial KPIs?
- If the partnership ended today, would the exit be clean — or messy?

## Canonical examples

- **JV partner-priority mismatch** — One partner optimizing for cost reduction while the other optimizes for product quality or market share; common in pharmaceutical and consumer-goods JVs (page 85)
- **Cross-cultural communication failure** — International partnerships where direct/indirect communication styles, language barriers, and decision-making norms produce repeated misunderstandings (pages 87-89)
- **Exit-handling failure** — Misalignment compounding into termination, with data, asset, customer, and employee transitions mishandled — eroding both parties' reputations (pages 89-92)
