# Flywheel vs Funnel

## Definition

The Flywheel-vs-Funnel distinction is the core mental-model swap behind modern customer-centric growth. The traditional **marketing funnel** is linear: prospects enter at the top (awareness), narrow through consideration and decision, and exit at the bottom as customers — then the model forgets them. The **flywheel** replaces that linear pipe with a circular loop in which delighted customers become the next turn's marketing engine via referrals, reviews, and repeat purchase. Pereira frames the shift as moving from "acquire and lose" to "acquire and compound": the funnel measures throughput, the flywheel measures momentum.

## Key attributes

- **Topology** — funnel is linear (start ≠ end); flywheel is circular (output feeds input)
- **Customer's role** — funnel: passive endpoint; flywheel: active fuel for next cycle
- **Time treatment** — funnel is single-cycle; flywheel compounds across cycles
- **Department alignment** — funnel naturally silos marketing/sales/CS; flywheel forces shared ownership
- **Friction sensitivity** — funnel hides friction at handoffs; flywheel exposes it as drag
- **Acquisition vs retention** — funnel optimizes top-of-funnel; flywheel optimizes the full loop
- **Effort profile** — funnel needs constant top-up; flywheel rewards consistency, then accelerates

## When to use

**Use a funnel mental model when:**
- Building one-shot transactional campaigns with no realistic repeat motion
- Reporting to stakeholders who need conversion-rate-style metrics at each stage
- Diagnosing a specific stage's drop-off in isolation
- Categories where the customer relationship genuinely ends at purchase

**Use a flywheel mental model when:**
- Building a compounding growth engine over 12+ months
- Aligning marketing, sales, and CS around shared outcomes
- Categories where referrals, expansion, and retention drive most of the value
- B2B SaaS, marketplaces, network-effect platforms, subscription businesses

## When NOT to use

- Don't force a flywheel onto a genuinely one-shot category (you'll invent fake delight stages)
- Don't keep a funnel where retention dominates LTV (you'll under-invest in CS)
- Don't run both mental models simultaneously without explicit reconciliation
- Don't pick the flywheel just because it's fashionable — it needs the operating discipline

## Common pitfalls

- Switching the slide deck to "flywheel" while still operating on funnel KPIs
- Keeping siloed funnel teams under a flywheel diagram (the friction stays)
- Believing the flywheel removes the need for top-of-funnel investment (it doesn't — it just adds retention compounding)
- Measuring funnel conversion-by-stage and missing the lifecycle compounding
- Assuming inbound flywheel replaces outbound funnel rather than complementing it

## Key metrics

- **Funnel-native:** awareness reach, MQL→SQL conversion, win rate, CAC by stage
- **Flywheel-native:** referral rate, repeat purchase rate, NPS, NRR, customer-sourced pipeline %
- **Bridge metrics:** CAC payback period, LTV:CAC ratio, time-to-second-purchase
- **Friction metrics:** handoff conversion (marketing → sales → CS), first response time

## Decision questions

- Does our category have a real second cycle, or is it one-and-done?
- Are we measuring the full loop, or just acquisition?
- Where in the lifecycle does our cost-to-serve concentrate?
- Are our teams structurally aligned with the model we say we're running?
- If we removed all paid acquisition tomorrow, would the wheel keep spinning at all?

## Canonical examples

- **HubSpot** — explicitly retired its funnel diagram in 2018 for the Attract/Engage/Delight flywheel (page 16-17, 26)
- **Salesforce** — classic funnel-led B2B SaaS; flywheel mechanics layered via customer success
- **Netflix** — flywheel of content investment → subscribers → data → better content (page 29)
- **Direct-response e-commerce** — often best modeled as funnel because repeat is weak
- **Enterprise sales** — often funnel for acquisition, flywheel for expansion/renewal
