# Co-Creation Customer Relationship

## Definition

A customer-relationship mode in which the conventional seller-buyer dynamic is restructured so that customers participate in aspects of product creation. Pereira frames this as a "radical shift" from the traditional transactional relationship: customers contribute ideas, content, reviews, or actual product work, and in return develop a heightened sense of investment that often produces brand champions. Co-creation can range from light participation (rating systems, idea forums) to deep collaboration (open-source contributors, customer-designed product lines). The mode trades direct top-down control for distributed authorship and the loyalty that comes with it.

## Key attributes

- **Customers contribute to the product** — not just consume it
- **Investment effect** — participation produces psychological ownership and stronger loyalty
- **Brand-champion emergence** — engaged co-creators become unpaid advocates
- **Spectrum of depth** — from voting/reviewing (light) to building/designing (deep)
- **Requires real process integration** — co-created inputs must actually shape the product, or the relationship collapses
- **Two-way value flow** — firm gets ideas/labor/marketing; customer gets agency and status
- **Community infrastructure underpins it** — forums, contributor recognition, tooling

## When to use

- For products where customer expertise or taste genuinely improves the offering
- In categories where community already exists informally (hobbyists, professionals)
- When the firm can absorb and act on contributed inputs at scale
- For brand-building in categories where loyalty matters more than margin
- When user-generated content or features can outpace internal production

## When NOT to use

- When "co-creation" is a marketing label with no real product process behind it
- For regulated or safety-critical categories where contributor input creates liability
- When the firm has no capacity to moderate, attribute, or implement contributions
- When the contributor pool is too small or unrepresentative to be useful
- When IP, attribution, or compensation terms cannot be cleanly resolved

## Common pitfalls

- Soliciting ideas with no intent or ability to ship any of them (kills trust fast)
- Treating co-creators as free labor while the firm captures all upside
- No attribution, recognition, or compensation for substantive contributors
- Confusing co-creation with feedback collection — co-creation means customers actually shape the artifact
- Letting a vocal minority drive product decisions
- Skipping the legal/IP framework and getting blindsided later

## Key metrics

- **Active contributors / contributor count**
- **Idea implementation rate** (% of submitted ideas that ship)
- **Engagement rate** in co-creation surfaces (forum, voting, design tools)
- **NPS and brand-champion count** among contributors vs non-contributors
- **Referral / word-of-mouth attributed to co-creators**
- **Time from idea to product** (contributors notice when this slows)

## Decision questions

- Does our product genuinely benefit from customer authorship?
- Do we have the capacity to absorb and act on contributions?
- How will we attribute, recognize, or compensate contributors?
- What is the IP framework for co-created work?
- Is this a real product process or a marketing veneer?
- Who moderates, and on what principles?

## Canonical examples

- **LEGO Ideas** — customers design models that become commercial sets
- **Threadless** — community-designed and -voted T-shirts
- **Open-source software** (Linux, Wikipedia, MDN) — co-created by contributors at scale
- **Salesforce IdeaExchange / Trailblazer Community** — customer-suggested features shipped into the product
- **GlaxoSmithKline weight-loss forum** — Pereira-cited example of community informing product understanding (page 21, adjacent)
- **YouTube, TikTok, Reddit** — entire products are co-created by user content
