# Business Plan Common Mistakes

## Definition

Pereira catalogs nine recurring mistakes that founders and entrepreneurs make when writing a business plan. The common thread: each mistake treats the plan as a one-time deliverable rather than as a living strategic instrument tuned to a specific audience. The list synthesizes guidance from Candice Lu (OnPrem Solution Partners), Forbes, HBR, NerdWallet, and Pereira's own consulting experience. The mistakes range from process problems (perfectionism, long-windedness) to substantive analytical gaps (no cash flow, no competition, no target market research) to misalignment errors (writing for the wrong audience).

## Key attributes

- **1. Long-winded plans** — 50-page plans get ignored; outdated before completion (p. 53)
- **2. Underestimating user acquisition** — building product ≠ acquiring users (p. 54)
- **3. Striving for the "perfect plan"** — perfectionism becomes a launch roadblock (p. 54)
- **4. Failing to research the target market** — guts over data (p. 55)
- **5. Forgetting cash flow** — profitable businesses still die from cash mismanagement (p. 55)
- **6. Thinking a formal plan is required to start** — execution beats planning when starting (p. 56)
- **7. Misunderstanding the plan's purpose** — wrong content for the actual audience (p. 56)
- **8. Underestimating the competition** — assuming uniqueness without checking alternatives (p. 57)
- **9. Leaving little room to pivot** — rigid plans break against real market feedback (p. 58)

## When to use

- As a pre-mortem checklist before finalizing any business plan
- During plan review with mentors, advisors, or board members
- When teaching first-time founders the discipline of planning
- After receiving investor feedback — diagnose which mistake triggered it
- When deciding between Traditional vs. Lean variants — which mistakes are you at risk of?
- During quarterly plan reviews — has the plan calcified into one of these patterns?

## When NOT to use

- As a substitute for actually writing the plan — these are diagnostic, not generative
- For very late-stage planning where industry norms (e.g., regulated industries) override
- When the plan's purpose is genuinely a one-time deliverable (rare; usually a sign of mistake #7)

## Common pitfalls (using this concept)

- **Treating the list as exhaustive** — context-specific mistakes also exist
- **Box-ticking** — checking off mistakes without addressing root behaviors
- **Defensive interpretation** — "we're not long-winded, we're thorough" misses the point
- **Using the list to attack other people's plans** — the list is for self-review

## Key metrics

- **Plan pages** — over ~30 for an early-stage startup risks mistake #1
- **Time to first customer** — long delays may indicate mistake #3 or #6
- **Cash runway visibility** — if not in plan, mistake #5
- **Audience specificity** — different versions for VC/bank/recruit avoids #7
- **Pivot indicators in plan** — explicit "if X then Y" language avoids #9
- **Competition section depth** — direct + indirect + substitute = mistake #8 avoided

## Decision questions

- Which of the nine mistakes is this plan most at risk of?
- Have we written distinct versions for distinct audiences?
- Is there explicit pivot language, or is the plan written as commitment?
- Does the cash-flow analysis show 12-18 months of runway under realistic assumptions?
- Is the competition section honest about alternatives, including "do nothing"?
- Have we tested the plan's assumptions with real customers before finalizing?
