# The Four Perspectives of BSC

## Definition

The Four Perspectives are the structural pillars of the Balanced Scorecard, each phrased by Kaplan & Norton as a question the organization must answer about itself: Financial ("How do shareholders see us?"), Customer ("How do customers see us?"), Internal Business Processes ("What must we excel at?"), and Learning & Growth ("Can we continue to improve and create value?"). The four perspectives are not equal-weight silos — they form a causal stack where Learning & Growth enables better Internal Processes, which produces better Customer outcomes, which drives Financial results. Pereira: "you should start at the bottom of the diagram and work your way up" (page 34).

## Key attributes

- **Financial Perspective** — shareholder/financial outcomes: revenue growth, margin, cost reduction, asset utilization, ROI (page 22)
- **Customer Perspective** — how customers perceive value: satisfaction, retention, acquisition, market share, brand perception vs rivals (page 22)
- **Internal Business Processes Perspective** — what the firm must excel at operationally: quality, cycle time, productivity, innovation pipeline (page 23)
- **Learning & Growth Perspective** — the capability foundation: employee skills, information systems, culture, leadership (originally called "Innovation and Learning") (page 23)
- **Causal stack, not parallel** — Learning & Growth → Internal Processes → Customer → Financial; mastering one unlocks the next (page 34–35)
- **Three leading + one lagging** — Financial is the lagging perspective; the other three are leading (page 35)
- **Each perspective gets its own objectives, measures, KPIs, and initiatives** (page 44)
- **Phrased as questions, not categories** — Kaplan & Norton's original framing forces user-perspective answers (page 11)

## When to use

- Designing a scorecard from scratch — start by listing 1–3 objectives per perspective
- Auditing an existing performance system for blind spots (most companies over-index on Financial)
- Communicating strategy to non-finance stakeholders (Customer and Learning & Growth perspectives translate better than financial ratios)
- Diagnosing why financial results are deteriorating despite "everything looking fine" operationally — look upstream in the causal stack

## When NOT to use

- As four independent dashboards owned by different VPs (collapses the causal logic)
- For pure operational reporting where you just need an ops dashboard, not a strategy framework
- In organizations where "Customer" is genuinely not a primary stakeholder (some B2G or regulatory bodies — use a customized perspective set instead)
- When forcing a startup into four perspectives prematurely creates bureaucracy over focus

## Common pitfalls

- **Treating Financial as the only perspective that counts** — defeats the entire purpose of "balanced" (page 12)
- **Treating Learning & Growth as HR's job** — it is the foundational driver of the other three, not an HR vanity metric (page 23)
- **Mixing operational metrics into the scorecard** — Pereira: operational measures are "housekeeping," not KPIs (page 32)
- **Skipping the causal logic** — if you cannot explain how a Learning & Growth metric eventually moves a Financial outcome, it does not belong on the scorecard
- **Naming the perspectives but not customizing them** — public-sector and nonprofit BSCs often reorder or relabel perspectives (Mission may displace Financial at the top)
- **Five-or-more-perspective creep** — some organizations add ESG, risk, innovation perspectives until the model loses focus

## Key metrics

- **Per perspective: 1–3 strategic objectives**, each with a leading and a lagging KPI
- **Perspective balance score** — % of total scorecard objectives in each perspective (target: rough balance, no perspective < 15%)
- **Causal-chain integrity** — can every Financial objective be traced back through Customer → Internal → L&G drivers?

## Decision questions

- Which perspective is currently being neglected in our management reporting?
- For each Financial objective, what Customer behavior drives it?
- For each Customer objective, what Internal Process must we excel at?
- For each Internal Process objective, what skill/system/culture in Learning & Growth enables it?
- Have we customized the perspective names and order for our context (private/public/nonprofit)?

## Canonical examples

- **Telco example** (page 44): Financial = +5% profit; Customer = +2.5% CSI; Internal = 90% UX score; L&G = 95% development plans complete — note the explicit causal chain (page 45)
- **Strategy Map perspective stack** (page 29): the four perspectives laid out vertically with cause-and-effect arrows
- **Public-sector adaptation**: Mission/Beneficiary perspective often displaces Financial at the top (Pereira, page 14, 18)
- **Mobil NAM&R** (Kaplan & Norton): four perspectives organized around "delight the consumer" customer theme
