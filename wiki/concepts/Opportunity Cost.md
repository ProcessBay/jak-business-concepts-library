# Opportunity Cost

## Definition

Opportunity Cost is the value of the next-best alternative you give up when you commit to a choice. Pereira frames it crisply: "Every decision comes at the cost of another. If you decide to write a script after school hours, you can't use that time to write a whole book." Crucially, opportunity cost is not the sum of all alternatives — it is the single most valuable choice forgone. It also extends beyond money to time, energy, attention, risk tolerance, and missed pleasure — any value-bearing resource that is depleted by the choice.

## Key attributes

- **The next-best alternative** — not all alternatives, only the most valuable one forgone
- **Non-monetary scope** — time, attention, risk capacity, relationship bandwidth, missed experiences
- **Invisible by default** — opportunity costs are usually unstated and unmeasured; they require deliberate surfacing
- **Compounds with scarcity** — the tighter the resource (founder time, capital, focus), the higher the opportunity cost of any commitment
- **Asymmetric for irreversible decisions** — opportunity cost is highest for choices that lock you out of alternatives (one-way doors)
- **Strategic-clarity tool** — saying yes to one thing is saying no to everything else; opportunity cost makes the trade-off explicit
- **Pereira anchor** — "If all the choices can't be made at the same time, then the opportunity cost is not the sum of all the choices. It is the most valuable choice passed over"

## When to use

- Capital allocation (should this $X go to product, sales, or hiring?)
- Founder/executive time allocation (every hour in a meeting is an hour not in product, customer, or strategy)
- Strategic focus decisions (entering market A vs market B vs deepening in current market)
- Hiring decisions (this candidate vs the next-best one for the same role)
- Roadmap prioritization (which feature gets built first costs every other feature its launch)
- Personal career decisions (one role accepted = many others foreclosed)

## When NOT to use

- Decisions where alternatives are functionally identical (the opportunity cost is zero)
- Genuinely additive choices that don't compete for the same resource
- When the analysis paralysis from comparing every alternative outweighs the gain from explicitness

## Common pitfalls

- **Ignoring time as a cost** — treating founder/team hours as free
- **Comparing to a fantasy alternative** — measuring against an idealized option that wasn't actually available
- **Sunk cost confusion** — letting past investments distort current opportunity-cost reasoning ("we've already spent so much on this")
- **Underweighting irreversibility** — failing to charge an extra "premium" for one-way-door decisions
- **Treating opportunity cost as the sum of all forgone options** — it's only the next-best (Pereira p. 70)
- **Skipping the explicit alternative** — without naming what you're giving up, opportunity cost stays vague

## Signals you're using it well

- You can name the specific next-best alternative for any major commitment
- You routinely ask "what does this cost me in time/focus, not just money?"
- Your "no" list is longer than your "yes" list, and the no's are explicit
- You charge an extra premium for irreversible decisions (margin of safety on commitment)

## Decision questions

- What's the next-best alternative I'm giving up by saying yes to this?
- What else could I do with this time, money, or attention?
- Am I ready to let go of that alternative? Permanently?
- Is this a one-way door (high opportunity cost) or a two-way door (low)?
- If I had to choose between this and that single alternative, which wins?

## Canonical examples

- **Pereira's writing example (p. 70)** — choosing to write a 20K-word super guide means giving up YouTube scripts during the same period — the time is non-fungible
- **Bezos one-way / two-way doors framing** — Amazon's explicit recognition that irreversible decisions deserve much higher opportunity-cost scrutiny than reversible ones
- **Munger / Buffett "hurdle rate"** — every new investment has to beat the best alternative they already own (their own portfolio's expected return)
- **Founder time** — every meeting you take is a customer call, product cycle, or recruiting conversation you didn't have
