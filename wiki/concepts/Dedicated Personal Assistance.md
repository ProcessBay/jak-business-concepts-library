# Dedicated Personal Assistance

## Definition

The highest-intimacy form of customer relationship: a single named expert assigned to a customer who can respond to any question or concern at any time, and who leverages accumulated history with that customer to tailor every interaction. Pereira identifies private banking as the archetypal example — financial institutions assigning a single point of contact to their most significant long-term clients. The mode is expensive (one human serves a small number of accounts) and is therefore reserved for high-value segments where the lifetime value comfortably exceeds the human cost. Done well it produces near-irrational loyalty; done badly it creates dependency on a single employee whose departure puts the account at risk.

## Key attributes

- **One named person per customer** — not a queue, not a team rotation
- **Time + expertise required** — the rep needs both bandwidth and domain depth
- **Continuity is the asset** — accumulated history with the customer is what makes interactions feel tailored
- **Reserved for high-value accounts** — the unit economics only work above a value threshold
- **Single point of contact** — customer has one number, one inbox, one face
- **Key-person risk** — when the rep leaves, the relationship can leave with them
- **Often paired with executive sponsorship** for the very top tier

## When to use

- For high-value B2B accounts where churn would be expensive (six-figure ARR and up)
- For premium consumer segments (private banking, concierge medicine, ultra-luxury retail)
- During complex implementation or change-management phases where continuity matters
- When customers are buying a relationship as much as a product (advisory, wealth management)
- When competitive differentiation in the segment is service quality, not product features

## When NOT to use

- For mid-market or SMB segments where the rep cost exceeds plausible LTV
- When the same value can be delivered by a pooled team without quality loss
- When the firm cannot retain experienced reps long enough for continuity to matter
- As a sales tactic that disappears post-close
- When automation or community could deliver an equivalent experience at fraction of cost

## Common pitfalls

- Promising a dedicated contact and delivering a pooled queue
- No succession plan when the named rep leaves (account follows the rep out the door)
- Reps becoming order-takers rather than advisors as workload grows
- Failing to capture institutional knowledge — all the history lives in the rep's head
- Extending dedicated assistance to too many accounts and starving the high-value ones
- Using junior staff in dedicated roles, undermining the "expert" promise

## Key metrics

- **Retention rate / NRR** for dedicated-assistance accounts (should be markedly higher than pooled-service accounts)
- **CLV** for the segment, validated against the cost of the named rep
- **NPS** for the dedicated-service tier
- **Account growth** (expansion revenue under the rep)
- **Share of wallet** (% of customer's category spend captured)
- **Rep tenure** (proxy for continuity)

## Decision questions

- Does the segment's LTV justify a fully loaded named-rep cost?
- What happens to the account if this rep leaves tomorrow?
- Are we capturing the customer history in the CRM, or only in the rep's memory?
- Is the rep an advisor or just a glorified inbox?
- Could a hybrid (named rep + automated tooling) deliver equivalent intimacy at lower cost?

## Canonical examples

- **Private banking relationship managers** — Pereira's canonical example (page 22)
- **Wealth management / family office advisors**
- **Enterprise Customer Success Managers** at top-tier SaaS (Salesforce, ServiceNow, Snowflake) for largest accounts
- **Concierge medicine / direct primary care**
- **Luxury retail personal shoppers** (Hermès, Bergdorf Goodman)
- **Major-gift officers** at universities and nonprofits
