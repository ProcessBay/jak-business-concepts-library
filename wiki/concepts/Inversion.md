# Inversion

## Definition

Inversion is the practice of attacking a problem from its opposite end — instead of asking "how do I succeed?", asking "how would I fail?" and then systematically removing the failure modes. Pereira invokes Munger's framing: "Invert, always invert," noting that "many problems can't be solved from the front." The model rests on a structural asymmetry: it is often easier and more reliable to identify and avoid stupidity than to engineer cleverness, and the absence of catastrophic mistakes compounds into success.

## Key attributes

- **Reverse the question** — rewrite the goal as its negation, then solve for avoiding the negation
- **Avoid-stupidity bias** — "more important to avoid being stupid than to try to be smart" (Munger via Pereira)
- **Pre-mortem mechanic** — pair inversion with imagined future failure to surface threats the team hasn't named
- **Bias-neutral framing** — by changing the question's direction, escapes confirmation bias built into the original framing
- **Versatile** — applies across promotion decisions, productivity ("what should I stop doing?"), team collaboration ("what blocks cross-functional work?"), and innovation ("what's killing my team's creativity?")
- **Munger pedigree** — central to the Berkshire Hathaway decision-making tradition
- **Cheap to run** — costs minutes; can be applied to almost any decision in flight

## When to use

- Before any major commitment (hire, launch, acquisition, strategic bet)
- When a plan keeps hitting the same wall and front-on solutions aren't working
- During promotion or hiring decisions ("what could make this person fail at the next level?")
- When the team is locked into groupthink and needs a structured dissent mechanism
- When optimizing for survival / not blowing up (investing, safety-critical systems)
- Quarterly or annual planning — invert the goals to surface the threats

## When NOT to use

- Pure exploration / open-ended brainstorming where the absence of constraints is the point
- When the failure mode is obvious and well-managed (inversion adds friction, not signal)
- When team morale is already low — relentless failure-framing can become demoralizing if not paired with action

## Common pitfalls

- Stopping at the inverted question without converting findings into actions
- Treating inversion as pessimism rather than as a structural risk audit
- Inverting trivially ("how could the meeting go badly?") and missing the strategic-level failure modes
- Using inversion to rationalize inaction (every plan can fail somehow)
- Forgetting to invite dissenters — if the room agrees on the failure modes, you're probably missing them

## Signals you're using it well

- The pre-mortem surfaced at least one failure mode no one had named before
- The team produced an action list, not just a worry list
- You can articulate "what would have to be true for this to fail" as clearly as "what would have to be true for this to succeed"
- People who initially agreed with the plan have flagged new doubts

## Decision questions

- What does failure look like 12 months from now? What's the post-mortem story?
- What am I assuming will go right that historically goes wrong?
- If I wanted to sabotage this project, what would I do?
- What should I stop doing today that's actively making this harder?
- Who on the team is too aligned, and would benefit from being asked to argue the opposite?

## Canonical examples

- **Charlie Munger** — codified "invert, always invert" as core to Berkshire decision-making (Pereira, p. 44)
- **Pre-mortem (Kahnemann, via Pereira p. 48–49)** — Daniel Kahneman's prescription: before a major decision, gather a small informed group, ask them to imagine "it is a year from now, the plan was executed, and the result was terrible — write what happened." Bypasses groupthink and gives dissenters psychological cover
- **Promotion decisions (Pereira p. 49–50)** — instead of "is this person ready?", ask "what could make this person fail at the next level?"
- **Productivity (Pereira p. 50)** — instead of "what should I do today?", ask "what should I stop doing today?"
- **Innovation (Pereira p. 50)** — instead of "how do I make my team more creative?", ask "what should I stop doing that kills creativity?"
