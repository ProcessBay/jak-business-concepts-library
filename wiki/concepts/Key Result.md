# Key Result

## Definition

A **Key Result** is the measurable, time-bound half of an OKR — a quantitative outcome that demonstrates progress toward (or attainment of) the parent Objective. Pereira specifies that each Objective should carry **3-5 Key Results** that are quantifiable, specific, and time-bound, allowing teams to determine objectively whether they have succeeded (page 11). Crucially, KRs measure **outcomes, not activities**: "publish 10 blog posts" is an activity; "grow organic traffic by 30%" is a Key Result (page 27). A good KR set covers leading indicators (predictive of success) and lagging indicators (the ultimate outcome), giving teams both early signal and final verdict (page 28).

## Key attributes

- **Quantifiable** — a number, percentage, or yes/no milestone; "improve customer satisfaction" is not a KR, "achieve CSAT ≥ 90% in Q3 survey" is (page 29)
- **Outcome-focused** — measures impact, not effort (pages 27, 30)
- **Time-bound** — has a deadline that fits the cycle (page 26)
- **Ambitious yet achievable** — stretch goal, not certain win nor impossible loss (pages 26-27)
- **3-5 per Objective** — fewer dilutes coverage; more dilutes focus (page 27)
- **Leading + lagging mix** — leading KRs give early signal; lagging KRs confirm final outcome (page 28)
- **Linked to a single Objective** — cause-and-effect should be visible (pages 32-33)

## When to use

- After writing the Objective — never before; KRs are derived from the Objective
- When converting strategy into measurable accountability
- When teams need a way to course-correct mid-cycle (KR trends are the signal)
- When establishing what "success" concretely means for an ambiguous goal

## When NOT to use

- Don't author KRs without an Objective — you get KPIs, not OKRs
- Don't use KRs to track routine operational metrics (those belong on dashboards, not OKRs)
- Don't measure things you can't actually track — pick metrics with reliable data sources (page 31)

## Common pitfalls

- **Activity disguised as outcome** — "ship feature X" instead of "feature X drives 15% lift in DAU"
- **Too many KRs** — diluting attention across 8-10 KRs per Objective destroys focus (page 27)
- **Unmeasurable or subjective targets** — "improve culture" with no instrument
- **Sandbagging targets** — setting KRs the team knows it will hit at 100% (pages 78-79)
- **All lagging, no leading** — finding out at cycle end that you missed, with no early signal
- **Disconnection from Objective** — KRs that, even if hit, don't advance the parent Objective (page 33)
- **Confusing KR with task list** — a 30-item to-do is not a Key Result

## Key metrics

- **KR scoring average** — 0.0-1.0 per KR at cycle end
- **KR attainment rate** — % of KRs hitting ≥ 0.7
- **Leading/lagging balance** — ratio of predictive to outcome KRs per Objective
- **Data availability lag** — time between KR measurement window closing and score being readable

## Decision questions

- If I hit this KR, will the Objective demonstrably move?
- Can I measure this with data we already collect or can quickly start collecting?
- Is this KR an outcome, or am I measuring my own effort?
- Will I get early signal mid-cycle, or only at the end?
- Is the target a stretch — or a foregone conclusion?

## Canonical examples

- **Increase customer satisfaction** → KR1: CSAT score ≥ 90% in quarterly survey; KR2: support response time < 24h; KR3: +20% positive reviews QoQ (page 28)
- **Improve employee engagement** → KR1: eNPS +10 points; KR2: monthly pulse survey 80% participation; KR3: turnover -15% YoY (page 28)
- **Launch a new product successfully** → KR1: 10,000 pre-orders before launch; KR2: 80% retention at 6 months; KR3: positive coverage in 5+ major publications (page 29)
- **Increase market share in smartphone industry** → KR1: +15% sales volume in target markets; KR2: CSAT avg 4.5/5; KR3: launch 2 new innovative models in Q (page 34)
- **Enhance operational efficiency** → KR1: -20% production cycle time; KR2: -15% waste; KR3: +50% training hours (page 35)
