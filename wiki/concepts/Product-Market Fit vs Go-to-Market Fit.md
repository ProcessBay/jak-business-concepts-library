# Product-Market Fit vs Go-to-Market Fit

## Definition

**Product-market fit** is achieved when a company has identified a market that wants its product; **go-to-market fit** is achieved when the company has identified the most effective way to reach that market and convert demand into sales. Pereira draws the distinction sharply: "Once a company has identified a market that is willing to buy its product, they need to determine the best way to reach these customers, which is known as go-to-market fit" (page 15). The two are sequential — you must achieve PMF first, then build the GTM engine — but they are equally necessary for sustainable success. Even with PMF in hand, a poorly aligned GTM strategy will leave a company struggling to scale.

## Key attributes

- **Sequential, not parallel** — PMF first, GTM second (page 16)
- **Both required** — PMF without GTM still struggles; GTM without PMF wastes money
- **GTM channels vary by audience** — mass marketing for consumer, direct sales or partnerships for B2B (page 15)
- **Alignment matters more than either alone** — GTM must align with overall business strategy (page 15)
- **PMF is about demand existence; GTM is about demand capture**
- **GTM design depends on PMF signal** — what you learned about your customer shapes the channel strategy
- **GTM unlocks scale** — without it, even a perfect product won't grow

## When to use

- Sequencing investment decisions: validate PMF before building a sales/marketing org
- Diagnosing stalled growth: is it a product issue (PMF) or a channel issue (GTM)?
- Hiring decisions: a VP of Sales hired pre-PMF is a classic over-hire
- Investor conversations: distinguish "we have PMF" from "we have a repeatable GTM motion"
- Post-Series A planning: GTM build-out becomes the dominant project once PMF is locked

## When NOT to use

- As an excuse to delay GTM thinking — early channel experiments inform the PMF picture
- As a binary gate — PMF is not always a single clear moment; some GTM work runs in parallel
- For products with embedded channels (network-effect plays) where PMF and GTM are linked

## Common pitfalls

- **Building a sales team pre-PMF** — burning cash to push a product the market isn't pulling
- **Assuming PMF means the channel is solved** — early WOM customers don't tell you what scalable acquisition looks like
- **Copying competitor GTM** — what works for an incumbent may not for a startup
- **Mass marketing a B2B product** — channel-audience mismatch
- **Direct sales on a low-ACV product** — economics don't support the motion
- **Underfunding GTM after PMF** — assuming the product will sell itself forever

## Key metrics

- **CAC payback period** — GTM efficiency indicator
- **Sales cycle length** — channel-audience fit
- **Channel yield** — qualified leads per channel investment
- **Win rate** — GTM message and channel resonance
- **LTV/CAC ratio** — combined PMF + GTM health (target 3:1, page 35)

## Decision questions

- Have we validated PMF before designing the GTM engine?
- Does our channel strategy match the buyer behavior of our target market?
- Are we pushing a B2C playbook on a B2B audience (or vice versa)?
- Is our GTM aligned with our overall business strategy?
- Are we measuring channel-level economics, not just topline?

## Canonical examples

- **Mass marketing for consumer products** — advertising-led GTM (page 15)
- **B2B direct sales or partnerships** — relationship-led GTM (page 15)
- **Uber free rides at SF tech events** — early GTM that doubled as PMF validation (page 18)
- **Google AdSense** — the product fit was clear; the GTM was sign-up-and-self-serve (pages 19-20)
