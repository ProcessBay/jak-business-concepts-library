# Servitization

## Definition

Servitization is the strategic shift from selling products outright to selling services or outcomes around those products. Pereira frames it as the underlying transformation driving the rise of aaS: manufacturers and product companies wrap (or replace) their products with ongoing service contracts, recurring fees, and outcome-based pricing. Classic examples: Rolls-Royce sells "power by the hour" instead of jet engines; Michelin charges per kilometer driven instead of selling tires; John Deere sells farming outcomes via IoT-equipped equipment. Servitization aligns the provider's economic interest with the customer's success: the provider profits when the customer uses more or achieves better outcomes.

## Key attributes

- **Outcome-based pricing** — customers pay for results, not for things
- **Recurring revenue replaces transactional** — annuity-style revenue stream
- **Aligned incentives** — provider profits when customer succeeds
- **Requires operational excellence** — providers must deliver service consistently, not just ship products
- **IoT and connectivity enabled** — measuring usage/outcomes requires instrumented products
- **Cultural transformation required** — sales force, support, finance all need to retool
- **Customer relationship depth increases** — from one-time sale to ongoing partnership

## When to use

- Categories with high-value products where service contracts add real differentiation
- B2B equipment with measurable utilization (jet engines, MRI machines, tractors)
- Markets where customers want to convert capex to opex
- When competitors are starting to servitize (forces strategic response)
- When IoT/connectivity can instrument products to enable outcome-based pricing

## When NOT to use

- Pure commodity products with no service overlay potential
- Markets where customers genuinely prefer ownership
- When the firm lacks the operational infrastructure for sustained service delivery
- When servitization would cannibalize a profitable product business too fast (timing matters)

## Common pitfalls

- Shifting pricing model without rebuilding cost structure
- Failing to retrain sales force (product reps can't sell outcomes)
- Underestimating capital implications (product sale = upfront revenue; service = deferred)
- Operating in both worlds inefficiently (some customers product, some servitized — split costs)
- Letting service contracts under-price what they cost to deliver

## Key metrics

- % revenue from services vs. products
- Services attach rate (% of product sales with attached service contract)
- Service LTV (Lifetime Value of service relationships)
- Customer engagement depth (sessions, support touches, success measures)
- Capital-intensity ratio (lower with full servitization)

## Decision questions

- Where in our product line could servitization unlock higher LTV?
- Do we have IoT/connectivity to enable outcome-based pricing?
- Can our sales force transition to selling outcomes vs. things?
- What's the capital implication of shifting revenue forward?
- Could competitors servitize us out of position if we don't move?

## Canonical examples

- **Rolls-Royce "Power by the Hour"** — airlines pay per engine hour, not per engine; engine health monitoring via IoT (page 20)
- **Michelin** — tires-as-a-service for fleet operators; charged per km
- **John Deere** — IoT-equipped farming equipment with outcome-based contracts (page 20)
- **GE** — shifting from selling industrial equipment to selling Predix-platform services
- **Xerox** — pioneered "managed print services" in 1990s
- **HP Instant Ink** — ink subscription replacing cartridge sales
