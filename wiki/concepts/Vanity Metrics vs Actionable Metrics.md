# Vanity Metrics vs Actionable Metrics

## Definition

The distinction between **vanity metrics** and **actionable metrics** is one of the bedrock concepts of Lean Startup analytics. Vanity metrics are numbers that look good in a pitch deck — Twitter followers, Facebook fans, downloads, page views, blog subscribers — but reveal nothing about whether the business creates value or generates revenue. Actionable metrics measure customer behavior and business outcomes that can actually inform decisions: conversion rate, customer lifetime value (CLV/LTV), customer acquisition cost (CAC), retention, return on marketing investment, activation rate. Pereira's distinction: "You can have thousands of followers on Twitter and never make a sale. The number of followers... doesn't tell you anything about the viability of your product, but rather how well you can publicize/market it."

## Key attributes

- **Vanity = aggregate counts of activity** — followers, likes, downloads, views
- **Actionable = behavior tied to value** — conversion, retention, payment, referral
- **Vanity metrics lag truth** — they grow even when the business is failing
- **Actionable metrics enable decisions** — pivot, persevere, prioritize
- **Vanity flatters the team; actionable challenges it**
- **Vanity is easier to grow** — buy followers, run ads, get downloads
- **Actionable requires honest measurement** — including the painful numbers

## When to use (which type)

**Vanity metrics — when:**
- Reporting top-of-funnel scale to non-technical stakeholders (sparingly)
- Compared as ratios to actionable metrics (e.g., followers-to-customer conversion)
- Tracking brand awareness as a leading indicator (with caveats)

**Actionable metrics — when:**
- Making any product or strategic decision
- Validating hypotheses in the Build-Measure-Learn loop
- Reporting to investors who understand startup mechanics
- Diagnosing what's working vs what's failing

## When NOT to use

- **Vanity metrics — never as the primary success measure** for a startup
- **Actionable metrics — when sample size is too small to be meaningful** (early-MVP traffic)
- Both are misleading when used without context (cohort, time period, segment)

## Common pitfalls

- **Pitch-deck pollution** — front-loading vanity metrics because they're impressive, hiding the actionable
- **Confirmation bias** — celebrating the metric that's going up, ignoring the one that matters
- **Cohort blindness** — looking at aggregate retention without splitting by acquisition cohort
- **Channel attribution failure** — not knowing which marketing channel drove which actionable result
- **Goodhart's law in action** — when a metric becomes a target, it ceases to be a good metric
- **CAC without LTV context** — you cannot evaluate CAC alone
- **Measuring downloads instead of active users** — Pereira warns explicitly that "the high number of app downloads doesn't mean all customers are active users" (page 92)

## Key metrics (actionable, by category)

**Acquisition:** CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost) — money to get one paying customer
**Activation:** Activation rate — % of new users reaching the value moment
**Retention:** Cohort retention, churn rate, customer lifetime
**Revenue:** MRR (Monthly Recurring Revenue), ARPU (Average Revenue Per User)
**Referral:** NPS (Net Promoter Score) — Promoters minus Detractors on the 0-10 scale (page 93)
**Value:** LTV / CLV — total revenue from a customer over the relationship

## Decision questions

- For each metric we track: would moving it change a decision we'd make?
- Are we celebrating numbers that grew, or numbers that matter?
- Does our dashboard balance acquisition, activation, retention, revenue, and referral?
- Have we segmented this metric by cohort, channel, and persona?
- What's our actionable equivalent of every vanity metric we report?

## Canonical examples

- **Buffer (page 80)** — Joel Gascoigne measured signups and tweet response, not just landing-page visits; the signups were actionable, the visits would have been vanity
- **Dropbox (page 81)** — 70,000 email signups was actionable (intent to use); raw video views would have been vanity
- **Many failed pre-2015 mobile apps** — celebrated millions of downloads while DAU and retention crashed; classic vanity-actionable confusion (page 92)
- **NPS as the canonical actionable customer metric** — Pereira describes the Promoter/Passive/Detractor scoring at pages 93-94
