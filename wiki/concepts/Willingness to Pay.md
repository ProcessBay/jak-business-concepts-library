# Willingness to Pay

## Definition

Willingness to pay (WTP) is the maximum price a customer will accept for a product or service before walking away. It is the upper bound of pricing for any given customer at any given moment. The difference between WTP and the price actually charged is **consumer surplus** — the value the customer captures rather than the firm. Pereira invokes WTP repeatedly: it's the engine behind value-based pricing, the segmentation axis behind price discrimination, and one of the four Cs (the Customer C) that any sound pricing strategy must triangulate.

## Key attributes

- **Maximum price per buyer** — above which they refuse
- **Varies by segment, context, time, channel** — never a single number
- **Consumer surplus** = WTP − Price paid
- **Hard to measure directly** — customers won't volunteer their WTP honestly
- **Indirectly observed** — choice experiments, conjoint analysis, A/B price tests, sales-cycle losses
- **Determined by perceived value, alternatives, budget, urgency**
- **Foundation for value capture** — pricing closer to WTP captures more value, leaves less surplus

## When to research / use

- Setting price for a new product or feature
- Designing pricing tiers or segments
- Modeling price increases — at what WTP threshold do customers leave?
- Negotiating B2B deals (knowing buyer WTP shifts the bargaining range)
- Diagnosing why win rate is too high (price too low — WTP exceeds your price by too much) or too low (price above WTP for the target segment)
- Justifying a value-based pricing strategy to leadership

## When NOT to over-rely on it

- Commodity categories where market price dominates over individual WTP
- When you can't ethically or legally segment customers by WTP
- Without considering competitor pricing (WTP shifts with substitutes available)
- Early-stage products with no real customer base to research
- When stated WTP differs materially from actual behavior (the survey problem)

## Common pitfalls

- Asking customers directly "what would you pay?" — answers are systematically biased downward
- Pricing at the maximum WTP (zero consumer surplus → buyer regret, churn)
- Treating WTP as a single number rather than a distribution across segments
- Confusing perceived value with WTP — WTP also reflects budget and alternatives
- Forgetting WTP changes with context (urgent need, seasonal mood, recession)
- Capturing too much WTP — customers feel exploited even though they technically paid voluntarily

## Key metrics

- **WTP by segment** (distribution, not a single number)
- **Consumer surplus** = WTP − Price (the value left on the table per buyer)
- **Value capture ratio** = Price ÷ WTP (the % of upper-bound captured)
- **Win rate by price tier** (calibrates WTP indirectly)
- **Price elasticity of demand** (related but distinct)

## Decision questions

- Do we know — even approximately — our top segment's WTP?
- How wide is the WTP distribution across our segments? Wide enough to justify discrimination?
- What's our value capture ratio — are we leaving too much surplus, or capturing too much?
- What's the customer's next-best alternative, and how does that anchor their WTP?
- How do we measure WTP — surveys, A/B tests, conjoint, choice experiments?

## Canonical examples

- **Pereira's WTP examples** — supermarket pricing: Mr. Jones WTP $100/week, Miss Ryan WTP $80; differential pricing captures both (page 132-133)
- **Airline business vs. leisure** — business traveler WTP is high and inelastic; leisure WTP is low and elastic
- **Pereira's project management SaaS** — perceived value of 10 hours saved × $30 = $300 WTP per month (page 178)
- **Coffee shop Company Z** — customers willing to pay 2× competitor for perceived experience (page 177)
- **Bentley vs. Honda** — both transport; Bentley buyer's WTP reflects status, not utility (page 128)
