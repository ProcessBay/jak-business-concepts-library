# BSC vs OKR

## Definition

The Balanced Scorecard (Kaplan & Norton, 1992) and Objectives & Key Results (originated at Intel by Andy Grove, popularized by Google via John Doerr) are the two dominant strategy-execution frameworks of the last 35 years. Both translate strategy into measurable goals, but they differ on time horizon, organizational scope, structural rigidity, and where they fit on a company's maturity curve. The Balanced Scorecard is a multi-year management system organized around four causal perspectives; OKRs are a quarterly goal-setting cadence with no fixed perspective structure. Pereira's BSC source establishes the BSC side of this comparison; OKR descriptions here reflect the canonical Doerr/Google framing.

## Key attributes

- **BSC: multi-year management system** — Pereira: not a tool that can be "finished in a few weeks or months" (page 13)
- **OKR: quarterly goal-setting cadence** — designed for 90-day cycles with mid-quarter check-ins
- **BSC: four prescribed perspectives** — Financial, Customer, Internal Process, Learning & Growth (page 21)
- **OKR: no prescribed perspectives** — objectives are free-form, structured only as Objective + 3–5 Key Results
- **BSC: top-down + cascading** — corporate scorecard cascades to units, then teams, then individuals (page 40)
- **OKR: bidirectional** — ~60% bottom-up, ~40% top-down in Google's original practice
- **BSC: leading + lagging KPIs explicitly balanced** — Pereira: most strategic plans over-index on lagging (page 33)
- **OKR: Key Results are typically outcome-oriented** — closer to lagging, with separate KPIs for leading indicators
- **BSC: tied to formal strategic planning** — annual strategy refresh, quarterly reviews (page 27)
- **OKR: tied to operational cadence** — quarterly setting, weekly/biweekly check-ins
- **BSC: built for large orgs** — requires CEO sponsorship, facilitators, multiple workshops (page 22, 25–27)
- **OKR: built for fast-moving teams** — Andy Grove designed it at Intel for engineering velocity

## When to use

- **Use BSC when** the org has a stable multi-year strategy, multiple business units that need alignment, formal board/investor reporting requirements, public-sector accountability obligations, or a mature management culture that can absorb the build process
- **Use OKRs when** the org is in a high-uncertainty phase (startup, scaleup, pivot), strategy is emergent and refined quarterly, teams have meaningful autonomy and ambition matters more than predictability, or the executive team values speed over comprehensive coverage
- **Use both** when the BSC supplies the multi-year strategic frame and quarterly OKRs operationalize a slice of it (common in mature enterprises with innovation arms)

## When NOT to use

- **Do not use BSC** in early-stage startups still discovering product-market fit (too heavyweight, page 60–62)
- **Do not use OKRs** as your sole strategy framework in a large regulated enterprise where multi-year financial accountability matters
- **Do not stack both** without clear seams; double-reporting kills both systems
- **Do not adopt either** without executive sponsorship — both fail the same way when leadership disengages

## Common pitfalls

- **Treating them as interchangeable** — they answer different questions (BSC: "is our strategy on track across all dimensions?"; OKR: "what is the team ambitiously trying to ship this quarter?")
- **Forcing the BSC's four perspectives onto OKRs** — collapses OKR's flexibility without gaining BSC's causal structure
- **Running OKRs at BSC cadence** (annual) — loses the velocity advantage
- **Running BSC at OKR cadence** (quarterly resets) — destroys the multi-year strategic story
- **Tying compensation to OKR Key Results** — the original Google practice explicitly decouples them; Pereira's BSC warning on KPIs and cheating applies equally (page 50)
- **Adopting either as a vanity exercise** — both die without engaged executive sponsorship

## Key metrics

- **Strategy execution rate** — % of scorecard objectives / OKRs on track
- **KPI alignment score** — % of unit metrics laddering to corporate strategy (BSC) or objectives (OKR)
- **Cadence fit** — does the framework's cycle match the organization's pace of change?
- **Engagement rate** — % of leaders/teams actively using the system vs treating it as overhead

## Decision questions

- What is our planning horizon — 3–5 years (BSC) or 1–4 quarters (OKR)?
- How predictable is our strategy? (Stable → BSC; emergent → OKR)
- Do we have board/regulatory accountability that requires comprehensive perspective coverage?
- Can our executives commit to the BSC's multi-workshop build, or do we need something teams can adopt next quarter?
- Are we trying to align a large multi-unit org (BSC strength) or accelerate ambitious team velocity (OKR strength)?
- Do we already have one framework? If so, what gap is the other supposed to fill?

## Canonical examples

- **BSC: Mobil NAM&R, CIGNA, Wells Fargo** — Kaplan & Norton's foundational enterprise cases
- **BSC: Telco worked example** (Pereira, page 44) — four perspectives, four objectives, four KPIs, four initiatives
- **OKR: Intel under Andy Grove** — the original implementation
- **OKR: Google** — adopted in 1999, scaled with company; still core today
- **Both: mature enterprises** — common pattern is corporate BSC providing 3-year strategy frame, with product/engineering orgs running quarterly OKRs against BSC objectives
