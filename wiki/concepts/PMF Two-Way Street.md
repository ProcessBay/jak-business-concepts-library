# PMF Two-Way Street

## Definition

Product-market fit is a **two-way street**: it requires not just that the right customers want your product, but that **you have the discipline to refuse the wrong ones**. Pereira frames this with founder-blunt language: "Don't just welcome everyone who comes through your door; instead, learn to respectfully refuse business from prospective customers if they don't share your values and vision for the future" (page 47). The principle has two corollaries — (1) don't build features just because a paying customer requested them, and (2) don't take revenue that pulls you off your strategic line. Short-term revenue is not the metric a long-haul business should be optimizing for (page 47).

## Key attributes

- **PMF is bilateral** — the product fits the customer AND the customer fits the company (page 47)
- **Saying no is a feature** — refusing wrong-fit customers protects long-term focus (page 47)
- **Feature-request discipline** — don't build because someone asked; build because it's on your roadmap (page 47)
- **Short-term revenue ≠ business health** — Pereira: "short-term revenue is not the business statistic that you should be striving for" (page 47)
- **Prioritization framework**: rank requests by impact × people-affected ÷ effort (page 48)
- **Vision-aligned customers pay better** — they value what you do, not just what you make (page 47)
- **Wrong-fit customers steer you off-course** — "anything else will steer you away from where you ultimately want to go" (page 47)
- **Respectful refusal is a skill** — not all founders are comfortable saying no

## When to use

- Sales pipeline qualification: refuse leads that don't match ICP
- Feature request triage: filter requests through roadmap alignment
- Customer success reviews: identify wrong-fit customers churning early
- Strategic planning: evaluate revenue mix by fit-quality
- Founder coaching: when the founder feels pressure to take any revenue

## When NOT to use

- During genuine PMF discovery — you don't yet know who the right customer is
- In survival mode where any revenue keeps the lights on (be honest about this trade-off)
- For platforms with long-tail customers where bilateral fit isn't binary
- As a blanket excuse to be precious — listening to customers is still core

## Common pitfalls

- **Saying yes to all paying customers** — adverse selection: the wrong customers churn loudly and consume resources
- **Building feature-by-request** — Pereira: "Do not begin the development of a feature just because someone has requested it" (page 47)
- **Optimizing for short-term revenue** — burns roadmap discipline (page 47)
- **Founder people-pleasing** — emotional inability to refuse customers
- **Avoiding the prioritization math** — taking requests in order of who asked loudest
- **Mistaking conviction for stubbornness** — discipline is informed by data, not just preference
- **Treating PMF as one-way** — assuming the only question is whether the customer wants you

## Key metrics

- **Customer fit score** — qualitative ICP-match rating per customer
- **Churn rate by ICP segment** — wrong-fit customers churn at higher rates
- **Feature request acceptance rate** — % of requests that make the roadmap
- **Revenue concentration by ICP** — % of revenue from ideal vs adjacent vs wrong-fit
- **Net Revenue Retention by segment** — fit customers expand; wrong-fit shrink
- **Sales cycle length by fit segment** — wrong-fit deals often take longer and close worse

## Decision questions

- Are we ever saying no to revenue, and if not, why not?
- What share of our active customer base would we describe as "ideal fit"?
- Are we building features because they advance the roadmap, or because someone asked? (page 47)
- When we rank requests by impact ÷ effort, what falls off the list? (page 48)
- Are we being steered off-strategy by a vocal minority of customers?
- Do our customers pay because they value what we do, or just because we're cheaper? (page 47)

## Canonical examples

- **Wealthfront's competitive focus** — concentrated on passive investors with small accounts rather than trying to please all users (page 51 echoes this principle)
- **Feature-request prioritization matrix** — Pereira: "arrange them in descending order of significance according to the impact… and the amount of work that will be required" (page 48)
- **The "drawing in additional paying customers" temptation** — Pereira's named anti-pattern (page 47)
