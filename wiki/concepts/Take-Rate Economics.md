# Take-Rate Economics

## Definition

Take-rate economics describes how platforms, marketplaces, and brokerages capture value: by taking a percentage of each transaction they facilitate. The take rate (also called commission, transaction fee, or platform fee) typically ranges from 2-30% depending on industry, platform power, and value delivered. Pereira's case studies reveal wide variance: Uber takes 20-50% (driver fees + surge), Etsy ~3.5% + listing fees, Airbnb 3-5% from hosts + 0-20% from guests, Uber Eats 30% from restaurants, DoorDash similar 15-30%, PayPal 2.9% + $0.30 per transaction.

## Key attributes

- **% of GMV captured** — take rate is the share of transacted value the platform keeps
- **Wide variance by category** — 2-3% (payment processing) to 30-50% (food delivery, Uber)
- **Power-driven** — high-take-rate platforms have supplier/buyer lock-in
- **Two-sided take** — some platforms charge both sides (Airbnb), some only one (PayPal)
- **Tiered/dynamic** — many platforms have take rates that vary by volume, geography, or category
- **Sensitive to competition** — high take rates create incentive for alternatives (anti-platform sentiment)

## When to use

- Platforms facilitating transactions between providers and consumers
- Markets with high search/discovery cost the platform eliminates
- Categories where platform infrastructure (payments, trust, search) adds real value
- When platform power is sufficient to defend the take rate against alternatives

## When NOT to use

- Pure linear B2C where there's no transaction to take from
- When the take rate would push providers off-platform to direct relationships
- In commoditized payment processing (race to the bottom)
- When the platform doesn't add real value beyond aggregation

## Common pitfalls

- Take-rate creep that pushes high-volume suppliers off-platform (Amazon merchant flight)
- Setting take rate before achieving platform power (suppliers won't pay)
- One-sided pricing without considering the other side's economics
- Failing to differentiate take rate by category (one-size-fits-all leaves money or pushes suppliers off)
- Race to zero in payment processing where competitors compete on lower take

## Key metrics

- Take rate % (per category, per geography, per volume tier)
- GMV (Gross Merchandise Value)
- Take rate trend over time (rising = power gain or supplier squeeze)
- Supplier LTV / churn correlated with take rate
- Contribution margin per transaction

## Decision questions

- What take rate is sustainable given supplier economics?
- Where are suppliers disintermediating us, and what take rate would they accept?
- Is our take rate justified by the value we deliver (payments, trust, search, scale)?
- How does our take rate compare to alternative platforms, and is the gap defensible?

## Canonical examples

- **Uber** — 20-50% from drivers; surge multiplies on rider side (page 18)
- **Airbnb** — 3-5% from hosts + 0-20% from guests; 20% on experiences (page 21)
- **Etsy** — 3.5% transaction fee + $0.20 listing + ad CPC + currency conversion + subscription (pages 27-28)
- **Uber Eats** — 30% commission from restaurants + delivery fees + small-order fees (pages 37-38)
- **DoorDash** — fees + commissions + subscription (DashPass) + ads (page 84)
- **PayPal** — 2.9% + $0.30 per transaction; lower for high volume (page 53)
- **App Store / Google Play** — 30% (15% after first year for subscriptions)
- **GitHub** — 25% commission on marketplace apps (page 45)
- **OnlyFans** — 20% commission (page 144)
