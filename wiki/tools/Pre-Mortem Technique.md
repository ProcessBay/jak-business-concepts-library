# Pre-Mortem Technique

## Definition

The Pre-Mortem Technique is a structured group exercise — popularized by Gary Klein and Daniel Kahneman — performed before a major decision is finalized: assemble a small informed group, ask them to imagine that the decision has been executed and the outcome was terrible, and have each person write a 5–10 minute narrative of "what happened." Pereira renders Kahneman's prescription (from Thinking, Fast and Slow): "When the organization is close to making a big decision but hasn't made it official yet, [Klein] suggests getting a small group of people who know about the decision together for a short meeting. The starting point of the session is a short speech that says, 'Imagine that it is a year from now. We put the plan into action as it is now. The result was terrible. Please spend 5 to 10 minutes writing a short summary of what happened in that disaster.'" The technique is operationalized Inversion — converting "how will we succeed?" into "how will we fail?" — and it gives dissenters psychological cover to surface concerns they wouldn't raise in support of a not-yet-finalized decision.

## Key attributes

- **Structured imagined failure** — the team commits to imagining the outcome has already failed, then writes the narrative
- **5–10 minute writing block** — written, not just discussed, to surface independent rather than groupthink-aligned concerns
- **Pre-commitment timing** — done before the decision is officially made, while the team is still in the "decide" mode
- **Psychological cover for dissent** — gives even people who agreed with the decision an explicit license to voice concerns
- **Imagination-leveraging** — uses the team's expertise in a generative, low-status-cost way
- **Inversion in operational form** — the structural antidote to overconfidence and groupthink at the decision point
- **Originator** — Gary Klein (cognitive psychologist studying naturalistic decision-making), popularized by Kahneman
- **Cheap and fast** — full exercise can run in 30–45 minutes

## When to use

- Before launching a major project, product, or initiative
- Before signing a partnership, acquisition, or strategic bet
- During quarterly or annual planning, applied to each major OKR or initiative
- When the team is in too much consensus too quickly (consensus is a red flag, not always a green light)
- Hiring decisions for senior roles (imagine 12 months in, the hire failed — what happened?)
- Risk-review stages in regulated environments
- Any one-way-door decision

## When NOT to use

- Trivial or fully reversible decisions (overhead exceeds value)
- When the decision must be made in minutes (pre-mortem needs at least 30 minutes done properly)
- After the decision has been irreversibly committed (use a post-mortem instead)
- When the team culture punishes dissent — the technique only works if surfaced concerns are taken seriously

## Common pitfalls

- **Going through the motions** — running the exercise but ignoring the output
- **Skipping the writing step** — verbal-only surfaces the loudest voices, not the most informed ones
- **Running it too late** — once the decision is committed, the exercise becomes ceremony, not signal
- **Asking the wrong people** — invite only champions of the decision, and you get champion-shaped failure narratives
- **No action conversion** — the pre-mortem must produce an action list (mitigations, kill criteria, monitoring), not just a worry list
- **Confusing it with a post-mortem** — pre-mortem is before-the-fact imagined failure; post-mortem is after-the-fact analyzed failure
- **One-and-done** — for long projects, re-run as new information emerges

## Key metrics

- **Failure modes identified** — count of distinct failure narratives the exercise surfaced
- **Action items generated** — count of mitigations, monitoring, or kill criteria produced
- **Decision changes triggered** — did the exercise actually change anything (scope, timing, ownership, go/no-go)?
- **Predicted vs actual failures** — track over time: of failures that materialize, what fraction were named in the pre-mortem?

## Decision questions

- Imagine the project has failed catastrophically one year from now. What's the story?
- What did we ignore, underestimate, or refuse to see?
- Who could have killed this and didn't?
- What external event made our plan look naive?
- What internal dynamic (politics, communication, capability) failed?
- What's the earliest signal we'd see if this trajectory were materializing?

## Canonical examples

- **Kahneman / Klein original framing (Pereira p. 48–49)** — described in Thinking, Fast and Slow as the prescription Gary Klein offers for high-stakes organizational decisions
- **Pereira's general framing (p. 49)** — "The best thing about the pre-mortem is that it gives people a reason to have doubts. Also, it makes even people who agree with the decision look for possible threats they hadn't thought of before. The main benefits of the pre-mortem are that it stops teams from getting stuck in groupthink once a decision seems to have been made, and it lets knowledgeable people use their imaginations in a much-needed way"
- **Amazon decision practice** — long memos paired with explicit "what could go wrong" sections are an institutionalized form
- **Product launch reviews** — many product teams run a pre-mortem before final go/no-go on launches with significant reach
- **Strategic acquisition review** — investment committees increasingly use pre-mortems before committing capital
