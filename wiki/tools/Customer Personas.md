# Customer Personas

## Definition

Customer personas are fictional but research-grounded representations of a firm's ideal customers, built from demographic, psychographic, and behavioral data. They translate abstract market segments into concrete, named, decision-ready archetypes that product, marketing, and sales teams can design around. Pereira describes a five-input method for constructing them: define your product or service, conduct market research, analyze the competition, develop personas, then use data and analytics to refine them — all in service of identifying target customers before any value proposition is written.

## Key attributes

- **Research-grounded, not invented** — drawn from real customer data, not internal guesses
- **Concrete and named** — "Marketing Manager Maya, age 34, mid-market SaaS, frustrated by tool sprawl" beats "B2B buyer 25-45"
- **Multi-dimensional** — demographic + psychographic + behavioral data, not just age/income
- **Decision-anchored** — should clarify what the persona buys, why, when, and what they reject
- **Living documents** — refreshed as customer base evolves, not set-and-forget
- **Aligned across functions** — same personas used by product, marketing, sales, and support

## When to use

- Before defining a value proposition (Step 1 of CVCM precedes Step 2)
- When sales and marketing are pursuing different customer profiles
- During product development to prioritize features by persona impact
- When onboarding new team members who lack customer context
- After significant market shifts (new segments emerging, old ones fading)

## When NOT to use

- As a substitute for talking to actual customers — personas inform but don't replace customer development
- When the firm has only one or two customers (the customers themselves are the personas)
- For products with truly heterogeneous users where over-segmentation creates paralysis
- As a marketing-only artifact disconnected from product or sales execution

## Common pitfalls

- Inventing personas without research — they end up reflecting the team's biases, not customers
- Creating too many personas — focus is lost; usually 3-5 dominant personas is the ceiling
- Skipping the psychographic and behavioral layers, leaving demographic shells that don't drive decisions
- Failing to use personas in actual decisions — they sit in a slide deck and gather dust
- Treating personas as static when buying behavior shifts quickly

## Key metrics

- Persona completeness score (data coverage across dimensions)
- Conversion rate of messaging tailored per persona
- % of product decisions explicitly referencing a persona
- Customer research depth per persona (interviews, surveys per cohort)

## Decision questions

- Can our team name our top 3 personas and what each one buys?
- For each persona: what's their dominant pain, alternative we beat, and reason to choose us?
- Are personas reflecting actual customer data or internal projection?
- Which persona is growing fastest, and is our offering tuned for them?
- When did we last refresh our personas, and what changed since?
