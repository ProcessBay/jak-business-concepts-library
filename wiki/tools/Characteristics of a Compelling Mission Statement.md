# Characteristics of a Compelling Mission Statement

## Definition

Pereira's nine-trait quality checklist for evaluating whether a mission statement is doing its job. A compelling mission statement is **short, unique, expectation-setting, realistic, memorable, active, positive, adaptable, and targeted** (pages 27-30). The traits work as a final-pass evaluation: after the five-step writing process produces a draft, this checklist tells you whether to ship it or send it back. Crucially, the nine traits operate as a *package* — a statement can be short and memorable yet still fail because it is generic, or unique and active yet still fail because it sets unrealistic expectations.

## Key attributes

- **Short** — fewer words make it memorable; brevity forces clarity (page 27)
- **Unique to your business** — a competitor must not be able to adopt it unchanged (page 28)
- **Creates expectations** — sets a clear promise to employees, customers, investors (page 28)
- **Realistic** — inspiring but grounded in actual capability and resources (page 28)
- **Memorable** — easy to recall, sticky, leaves an impression (page 29)
- **Active** — a call to action, not a description; uses verbs (page 29)
- **Positive** — upbeat, optimistic, focuses on what the company solves rather than what it fights (page 29)
- **Adaptable** — flexible enough to evolve with the company without rewriting from scratch (page 29)
- **Targeted** — written with a specific audience in mind (customers, staff, stakeholders) (page 30)

## When to use

- As a final pass on a draft before publishing internally
- As a diagnostic when an existing mission feels stale or unread
- As a coaching tool when a leadership team is stuck rewriting their mission
- As an evaluation rubric when consulting on someone else's mission

## When NOT to use

- As an early-draft tool — fight for one good draft first, then evaluate
- Mechanically as a tick-box — the traits are interdependent, not independent
- To justify minor wordsmithing — if more than three traits are missing, restart the process

## Common pitfalls

- **Optimizing one trait at the expense of others** — chasing brevity often kills specificity; chasing memorability often kills realism
- **Confusing memorable with cute** — a memorable statement says something true, not just something rhyming
- **Setting expectations the company cannot meet** — promising "delight" while delivering "adequate" erodes trust
- **Writing in passive voice** — fails the Active test; signals the company is acted upon rather than acting
- **Treating Adaptable as Vague** — adaptable means the language ages well; vague means it never said anything
- **Writing for no one in particular** — fails the Targeted test; the statement has to know whose attention it is competing for

## Key metrics

- **Trait count passed** — how many of the nine traits the statement clearly earns (target: 8-9)
- **Uniqueness test** — pasted into a competitor's deck, does it still ring true? (Should fail.)
- **Memorability test** — can a teammate paraphrase it 30 days after first hearing it?
- **Brevity score** — word count under 25 is a strong signal
- **Active-verb test** — does the statement start with or center on an action verb?
- **Expectation clarity** — can a new hire describe what the company promises after one read?

## Decision questions

- Could a competitor adopt this statement unchanged? (Should be no.)
- Could an employee recite this from memory next month? (Should be yes.)
- Does the statement open with a verb or a description? (Verb is better.)
- Does the statement promise something we actually deliver?
- Will this statement still make sense in five years if we expand the product line?
- Is this written for a specific audience, or for "everyone"?

## Canonical examples

- **Short + Active + Memorable (Patagonia)** — "To save our home planet." (page 52)
- **Active + Inspirational (Tesla)** — "To accelerate the world's transition to renewable energy." (page 62)
- **Unique + Targeted (Nike)** — "Bring inspiration and innovation to every athlete in the world. (*If you have a body, you are an athlete.*)" — the asterisk makes the target audience radically inclusive in a way no competitor would mimic (page 50)
- **Positive + Memorable (Walmart)** — "Save money, live better." (page 68)
- **Expectation-setting (Microsoft)** — "Empower every person and organization on the planet to achieve more." (page 46)
- **Targeted (Old Guys Rule)** — speaks directly to its niche audience in the language that audience uses (page 52)
- **Failed Active (passive description)** — generic "We are a leading provider of..." statements — fail Active, Unique, Memorable simultaneously
