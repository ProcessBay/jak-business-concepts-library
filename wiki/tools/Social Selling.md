# Social Selling

## Definition

Social selling is the practice of using social media — primarily LinkedIn, but also Twitter/X, Instagram, and industry-specific platforms — to find prospects, build relationships, and move conversations toward sales. Pereira cites research that social salespeople have a 51% greater chance of meeting or exceeding their quotas, and that ROI per dollar invested in social selling can be tripled. The discipline is fundamentally about giving before getting: developing helpful content, engaging substantively with prospects' posts, sharing industry insight, and building rapport long before any pitch. Pereira's warning is sharp: cold DMing strangers with a pitch is the worst version of social selling and a primary driver of public skepticism toward the practice. The right motion is content + conversation + curiosity, often layered with cold calling, email, and live chat into a multi-channel cadence. Also note: simply having a social account is not social selling (page 151 anti-pattern).

## Key attributes

- **Give before getting** — value-first engagement, not pitch-first
- **Daily time investment** — 30-60 minutes per day commenting, sharing, messaging
- **Content-driven** — original posts + thoughtful comments on others' posts
- **Multi-platform** — LinkedIn for B2B, Twitter for tech, Instagram for B2C/creator
- **Trigger-event monitoring** — watch for funding, leadership change, hashtag mentions
- **Relationship before transaction** — build authority first, ask second
- **Multi-channel cadence** — pairs with cold call, email, live chat
- **Skeptical default** — buyers tune out pitch-first DMs instantly
- **Measurable** — LinkedIn Sales Navigator SSI, mentions tracking, engagement metrics

## When to use

- B2B markets where buyers are active on LinkedIn (most knowledge-economy industries)
- Long-cycle considered purchases where trust must compound
- ABM motions targeting named accounts
- Founder-led or executive sales where personal brand carries weight
- Thought-leadership categories (consulting, agencies, advisory)
- As a warm-up layer ahead of cold outreach
- Re-engaging dormant prospects with relevant content

## When NOT to use

- Transactional commodity purchases (social selling overhead exceeds value)
- Industries where buyers are not active on social (some manufacturing, blue-collar trades)
- When the rep won't commit to consistent daily activity (one-off posting fails)
- When the company brand is silent — personal brand without company support is fragile
- As a pitch-spamming channel (destroys reputation faster than no presence at all)

## Common pitfalls

- Cold DMing a pitch on the first connection (page 55-56 — Pereira's explicit warning)
- Sporadic posting that signals neglect rather than expertise
- Recycling marketing-department content without personal voice
- Treating social selling as a numbers game (connection-count over relationship-quality)
- Ignoring engagement signals (likes/comments on prospects' posts are warm leads)
- "Being on social is enough" anti-pattern (page 151 — presence ≠ activity)
- Pitching in comments on others' posts (instantly burns goodwill)
- No CRM integration — social conversations don't get tracked

## Key metrics

- **Connection growth** — net new relevant connections per week
- **Engagement rate** — likes/comments/shares on own content
- **Reply rate to outreach** — social DMs vs cold email benchmark
- **Connections-to-meetings rate** — % of new connections that become meetings
- **Inbound inquiries from social** — buyer-initiated conversations
- **SSI (Social Selling Index)** — LinkedIn's composite score
- **Time invested** — minutes per day; consistency matters more than volume

## Decision questions

- Are we giving real value in our content, or just recycling brochures?
- Is our motion content-first or pitch-first? (Pitch-first will fail)
- Do reps commit to daily activity, or is social selling a quarterly campaign?
- Are we monitoring trigger events (mentions, hashtags) systematically?
- Do we layer social touches with email and phone, or treat them as separate channels?
- Are executives modeling the behavior, or just asking reps to do it?

## Canonical examples

- **LinkedIn Sales Navigator** — the dominant B2B social-selling platform
- **Gong, Drift, HubSpot reps** — heavy social-selling cultures, executives included
- **Founder-led B2B SaaS** — Linear, Notion, Vercel founders built distribution via social
- **Twitter/X for tech sales** — DevTool and infra companies sell heavily via Twitter
- Pereira's LinkedIn first-contact pattern (page 44-45) — connect, comment, share insight, then ask
