# Framing Techniques in Negotiation

## Definition

Framing in negotiation is the discipline of shaping how information is presented so that the counterparty perceives value, risk, and reasonableness in a way that favors your position. Pereira documents four framing techniques: (1) **Positive framing** — emphasize benefits, gains, and opportunities to evoke favorable emotions; (2) **Loss aversion framing** — exploit the cognitive bias that people weigh losses more heavily than equivalent gains by highlighting risks of not reaching an agreement or costs of refusing an offer; (3) **Comparative framing** — present information in a comparative context that favorably positions your proposal against alternatives or competitors; (4) **Framing through questioning** — use strategic questions to direct attention, highlight specific aspects, and elicit responses that frame the discussion in your favor. Framing pairs naturally with anchoring: the anchor sets the number, the frame sets how the number is interpreted. Pereira insists framing must stay within ethical bounds — transparency, fairness, and trust-building, not manipulation — because misused framing damages credibility and the relationship.

## Key attributes

- **Four sub-techniques** — positive, loss-aversion, comparative, question-driven
- **Pairs with anchoring** — anchor sets the number, frame sets the interpretation
- **Loss aversion is asymmetric** — losses feel ~2x heavier than equivalent gains
- **Comparative framing requires reference points** — needs alternatives to compare against
- **Questions frame implicitly** — what you ask shapes what gets discussed
- **Ethics-bounded** — manipulative framing destroys trust and credibility
- **Adaptive** — frame must adjust as the negotiation evolves and counterparty reveals priorities

## When to use

- When presenting a proposal where multiple value dimensions exist
- When the counterparty is undervaluing benefits or overvaluing concessions
- In persuasion-heavy negotiations (sales pitches, partnership proposals, fundraising)
- When you need to redirect focus away from a weak point in your position
- When you can ethically tap into loss aversion (real risks of inaction)

## When NOT to use

- When the counterparty has deep expertise and will see through transparent framing
- When framing veers into deception or material misrepresentation
- In cultures or relationships where direct, low-spin communication is the norm
- When framing distracts from substantive interest exploration in integrative deals
- When the relationship value exceeds any single-deal value extraction

## Common pitfalls

- Over-relying on loss-aversion framing — counterparties get defensive or feel manipulated
- Comparative framing with bad reference points (counterparty cites better alternatives)
- Positive framing that erases real risks and damages credibility post-deal
- Question-framing that feels leading or interrogative
- Conflating frame with substance — strong framing of weak content still fails
- Using framing tactically while neglecting the strategic frame of the whole negotiation

## Key metrics

- **Acceptance rate by frame variant** — A/B-style comparison across deals
- **Perceived value lift** — counterparty's stated valuation before vs. after framing
- **Concession rate** — concessions earned per framing move
- **Counter-frame count** — how often counterparty re-frames your position
- **Trust score** — post-deal sentiment after framing-heavy interactions

## Decision questions

- What is the counterparty's reference point, and how can I shift it?
- Where does loss aversion apply ethically — what real risk are they actually avoiding?
- What comparison makes my proposal look strongest without inviting refutation?
- What question can I ask that frames the discussion toward my preferred terrain?
- Am I framing substance, or framing in place of substance?
