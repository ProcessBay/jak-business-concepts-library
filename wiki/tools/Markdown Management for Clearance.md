# Markdown Management for Clearance

## Definition

Markdown Management for Clearance is a retail tactic that uses a planned, multi-step price-reduction cadence to move aging inventory out of the assortment while recovering as much margin as possible. Pereira frames it on pages 82-84: clearance pricing is "a strategic process that involves finding the right balance between maximizing revenue and clearing out inventory." The standard mechanic is a *markdown ladder* — start at a moderate discount (e.g., 25%), step deeper at fixed time intervals (40% after two weeks, 60% after four, 70%+ if still on the floor), so that price drops only as fast as needed to clear. Pereira identifies key tactics: cost-recovery floor, gradual reduction, bundle deals on clearance items, dedicated clearance events, and inventory-management software to recommend optimal markdowns. Common in apparel, fashion, seasonal, and any category with limited shelf life where carrying old stock blocks new arrivals.

## Key attributes

- **Stepped markdown ladder** — predefined cadence (e.g., 25% → 40% → 60% → 70%+) at time triggers
- **Cost-recovery floor** — final markdown must at minimum recover landed cost where possible
- **Time-trigger driven** — drops fire at week 2, 4, 6 regardless of price elasticity (or by remaining units)
- **Dedicated clearance zones** — "Last Chance," back-of-store racks, end caps consolidate slow movers
- **Bundle and BOGO at deep markdown** — "buy one get one 50% off" mechanics move pairs of slow units
- **Inventory-management software** — modern markdown engines (Oracle Retail, Blue Yonder) recommend depth
- **Clearance events** — dedicated end-of-season sales create urgency around residual inventory
- **Margin-vs-velocity trade-off** — every week of delay costs carrying cost and opportunity cost

## When to use

- Categories with seasonal cycles (apparel, holiday, seasonal home, gardening)
- When new collections need shelf space and old inventory must clear
- For perishables and date-sensitive merchandise approaching expiry
- When carrying costs (warehouse, shrink, opportunity cost of space) exceed the margin you'd recover
- After a forecast miss — too much depth in a SKU that didn't sell at full price
- For end-of-life merchandise (discontinued, manufacturer-EOL, last-call)

## When NOT to use

- For evergreen core assortment that doesn't go stale (don't markdown the basics)
- When EDLP positioning makes promotional markdowns confusing
- Without a clearance zone or signage to differentiate from regular price (cannibalizes full-price sales)
- For luxury / premium-positioned categories where markdowns damage brand equity
- When markdowns become a shopper-trained pattern — they wait for the markdown instead of paying full
- Without inventory visibility — you don't know what's actually aging and how much

## Common pitfalls

- Marking down too shallow, too late — inventory still doesn't move, deeper cut needed anyway
- Marking down too deep, too early — leaves margin on the table for units that would have sold
- No clearance zone — markdowns at the regular shelf cannibalize full-price demand
- Markdowns trigger competitor cuts — race to the bottom in the dying category
- Failing to factor carrying cost into the markdown decision (storage isn't free)
- Static ladder regardless of category — fashion needs faster cadence than housewares
- Letting the markdown ladder run automatically without buyer override on hero or test SKUs

## Key metrics

- **Sell-through rate** — % of units sold at each markdown level (target full-price >50% before first markdown)
- **Markdown rate** — total markdown dollars / gross sales (apparel industry ~30%+; well-managed <20%)
- **Weeks of supply** — units on hand / weekly sell rate (markdown trigger when >threshold)
- **Gross margin recovery** — actual margin captured vs. plan after markdown cycle
- **End-of-season residual** — units left after final markdown (writedown / liquidation cost)
- **Inventory turn** — annual cost of goods sold / average inventory (faster turn = less markdown exposure)

## Decision questions

- What's our markdown ladder by category — and is it tuned to category lifecycle, not one-size-fits-all?
- What's the sell-through threshold that triggers the first markdown?
- Where is the carrying-cost crossover — when does delay cost more than the markdown saves?
- Do we have a dedicated clearance zone, or is markdown happening at the main shelf?
- Are we using inventory-management software to recommend depth, or running it manually?
- What's the liquidation channel for residual inventory (jobber, off-price, charity)?

## Canonical examples

- **Nordstrom Rack, Saks Off Fifth, Neiman Marcus Last Call** — dedicated clearance-banner stores absorbing parent-banner markdowns
- **Macy's Last Act** — in-store clearance section with engineered markdown cadence (page 83)
- **H&M / Zara end-of-season sales** — fast-fashion forces aggressive markdown cadence to clear weekly drops
- **Holiday décor on December 26** — classic 50% markdown trigger; January 70%+ (page 83 timing example)
- **Winter apparel marked down in late winter to clear for spring** (page 84)
- **Best Buy Open Box / Outlet** — manufacturer-EOL and returned tech run through markdown ladder
- **Costco "asterisk" pricing** — final markdown signal: price ending in .97 means won't be restocked
