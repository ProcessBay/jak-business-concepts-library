# Hourly and Time-and-Materials Pricing

## Definition

Hourly / time-and-materials (T&M) pricing charges the client a per-hour rate for each consultant or specialist on the engagement, plus reimbursement for direct expenses (travel, materials, third-party costs). Pereira frames it as the canonical **cost-based pricing** form for service businesses: the firm tracks direct cost (consultant salaries, expenses) and indirect cost (rent, admin, marketing) per project, allocates overhead, then layers a profit markup to derive the billable rate (pages 15-20, 23-25). The model is the dominant default in law, accounting, management consulting, and many agencies because it directly couples revenue to effort, but Pereira flags that it explicitly **ignores customer-perceived value and competitor pricing** — both of which can leave significant margin on the table (page 21-22).

## Key attributes

- **Effort-coupled revenue** — every billable hour produces revenue; idle hours produce none, making utilization the central business metric
- **Cost-plus mechanics** — direct labor + allocated overhead + target margin = billable rate (page 18-20)
- **Transparent and easy to justify** — invoices show hours × rate, which clients can audit (page 20-21)
- **Risk sits with the client** — overruns pass through directly; the firm is not punished for inefficiency
- **Caps utilization at 100%** — revenue ceiling is total billable headcount × target hours
- **Blended rates** — many firms publish a single "blended" rate hiding the partner / associate / analyst mix
- **Realization gap** — billed hours ≠ collected hours; write-offs and discounts erode the headline rate

## When to use

- Scope is genuinely uncertain or discovery-heavy (early-stage diagnostics, litigation, custom dev)
- Client wants line-item transparency and effort-based audit trail
- Junior-heavy work where leverage on partner time is the main margin lever
- Regulated professions where hourly is the market norm (law, audit)
- Short engagements where building a value-based pricing case isn't worth the sales cycle

## When NOT to use

- The firm has a defensible value-based pricing story (outcomes are measurable in client dollars)
- Senior talent is doing work where one expert hour delivers what would take a junior 10
- Clients are sophisticated buyers who reward outcome-based commitment
- The firm's productivity gains (templates, AI tooling) would *reduce* revenue under T&M
- Long-term retainer-shaped work where predictability matters more than itemization

## Common pitfalls

- **Disincentive to get efficient** — productivity improvements directly erode revenue (page 22)
- **Margin compression in competitive bids** — buyers compare hourly rates without comparing seniority or quality
- **Utilization fixation** — chasing billable hours over client outcomes erodes long-term trust
- **Realization rot** — partner-promised discounts and write-offs quietly cut the effective rate
- **Scope-creep without re-pricing** — small "while you're at it" asks accumulate into unbilled work
- **Talent disincentive** — top consultants resent being measured by hours instead of impact
- **Stuck at cost ceiling** — pricing tied to cost ignores the client's willingness to pay for outcomes (page 22)

## Key metrics

- **Billable hours** — hours actually invoiced to clients
- **Utilization rate** — billable hours ÷ available hours (target 60-80% in consulting, varies by role)
- **Realization rate** — collected $ ÷ standard billing rate × hours worked
- **Effective hourly rate** — collected revenue ÷ total hours worked (the honest number)
- **Leverage ratio** — junior staff ÷ senior staff on an engagement
- **Write-off rate** — unbilled hours / total hours worked

## Decision questions

- Is the scope uncertain enough that the client genuinely values flexibility over a fixed number?
- What's our utilization, and how close are we to the revenue ceiling implied by headcount?
- Where is our realization rate masking pricing weakness?
- Could we credibly quantify the dollar value of our outcomes for a value-based alternative?
- Do our AI / template / playbook investments make hourly a self-inflicted wound?

## Canonical examples

- **Mid-sized management consulting firm** — Pereira's worked example: $50,000 of project cost + 25% markup = $62,500 fixed fee, derived from hourly cost build-up (page 23-25)
- **Big Law (Cravath, Skadden)** — partner / associate / paralegal hourly rate ladder
- **Big Four audit and advisory** — staff / senior / manager / partner rate cards
- **Freelance contractors** — daily or hourly rate is the default starting position
- **AT&T-style telco professional services** — bills internal projects via blended hourly rates
