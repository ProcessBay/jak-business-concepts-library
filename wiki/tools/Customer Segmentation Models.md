# Customer Segmentation Models

## Definition

Customer Segmentation Models are the seven approaches for dividing a market into addressable groups, each emphasizing different attributes of the customer. Pereira enumerates them: Demographic (age, gender, income, education, marital status), Geographic (country/state/city), Psychographic (attitudes, values, interests, personality), Technographic (device + app usage patterns), Behavioral (purchase habits, product usage, loyalty), Needs-Based (specific problems being solved), and Value-Based (economic value each customer brings). Choosing the right segmentation model is a strategic decision: the wrong model produces statistically valid groups that aren't actually predictive of behavior.

## Key attributes

- **Seven distinct approaches** — Demographic, Geographic, Psychographic, Technographic, Behavioral, Needs-Based, Value-Based
- **Different predictive power** — behavior and needs typically more predictive than demographics alone
- **Often combined** — best segmentations layer multiple models (psychographic + behavioral + value)
- **Tied to use case** — pricing decisions need value-based; product decisions need needs-based; ad targeting needs technographic
- **Five evaluation criteria** — Depth of Pain, Budget, Reach, Market Size, Value (does this segment matter?)
- **Refinable** — segmentation isn't done once; refine as data accumulates

## When to use

- Designing a GTM strategy that requires targeting specific groups
- Pricing optimization (value-based segmentation reveals willingness to pay)
- Product roadmap prioritization (needs-based segmentation reveals what to build)
- Ad campaign targeting (psychographic + technographic for digital, demographic for broadcast)
- Sales territory or account-tier design (often value-based)

## When NOT to use

- As a one-time exercise — segments shift; refresh annually minimum
- For very small customer counts (need enough customers to find statistical patterns)
- When the customer is highly heterogeneous and segmentation creates more confusion than clarity
- Without validation — internal segmentations not tested against real customer behavior are guesses

## Common pitfalls

- Demographic-only segmentation that ignores behavior (age tells you less than purchase pattern)
- Too many segments — dilutes focus and resources
- Segmentation without action — pretty deck, no decisions
- Confusing segmentation with persona development (segmentation is statistical; personas are vivid representations of a segment)
- Failing to evaluate segments against the 5 criteria (depth of pain, budget, reach, market size, value)

## Key metrics

- Segment distinctness (do segments actually behave differently?)
- Conversion rate variance by segment
- Willingness to pay variance by segment
- NPS / CSAT variance by segment
- Segment growth rate

## Decision questions

- Which 1-2 segmentation models are most predictive for OUR business decisions?
- Are our segments actually distinct, or are they statistical artifacts?
- For our highest-value segment, which model best describes them?
- Does our segmentation change buying behavior, or just describe customers?

## Canonical examples

- **Luxury car brand** — uses demographic (income bracket) + value-based segmentation (page 31)
- **Online retailer** — value-based tiering by annual spend; high-value get different relationship (page 37)
- **Sports apparel** — needs-based segmentation (runners, weight lifters get different products) (page 36)
- **Companies using technographic** — target customers by mobile vs desktop usage, app usage patterns, OS version (pages 33-35)
