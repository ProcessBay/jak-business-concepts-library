# Loss Leader Pricing

## Definition

Loss Leader Pricing is a retail tactic where a retailer deliberately prices a specific product at, near, or below cost — accepting margin loss on that SKU — in order to drive foot traffic into the store. The economic bet: the shopper who comes in for the discounted milk, rotisserie chicken, console game, or Black Friday TV will add higher-margin items to their basket, more than offsetting the loss on the lead item. Pereira specifically calls out loss leaders as a brick-and-mortar tactic (page 86): "certain products are sold at or below cost to attract customers into the store" with the goal of "stimulating additional sales of higher-margin items." The mechanic only works when (a) the loss-leader item has genuine demand pull, (b) the store layout forces shoppers past higher-margin merchandise, and (c) the retailer can model the blended basket margin to ensure the trip is net-profitable.

## Key attributes

- **Below-cost or at-cost pricing on a single hero SKU** — the lead item is the bait
- **Traffic-driver economic logic** — pays for itself in attached basket, not in unit margin
- **Brick-and-mortar specific** — relies on physical store layout to expose shoppers to other merchandise
- **High-demand items only** — must be a known-price item shoppers actively seek (eggs, milk, console)
- **Adjacent merchandising design** — loss leaders placed deep in store to force walk-through
- **Limit per customer** — caps abuse and stockouts (e.g., "limit 2 per household")
- **Event amplification** — Black Friday TVs, holiday turkeys, back-to-school crayons
- **Regulated in some jurisdictions** — illegal below-cost selling laws (Germany, France) cap how far retailers can go

## When to use

- To anchor a promotional event (Black Friday, holiday weekend, store-opening grand sale)
- When you have known-value items in your assortment where shoppers track and compare price
- In Hi-Lo retail formats — the loss leader is the event's headline
- When you can model basket attach mathematically — shopper who buys milk also buys X, Y, Z
- To win back lost traffic after a competitor opening or seasonal dip
- For seasonal traffic peaks (turkey at Thanksgiving, BBQ at Memorial Day)

## When NOT to use

- In an EDLP format — incompatible with the "stable flat price" promise
- For online-only retail — no store-walk-through mechanic to capture attached basket
- When the loss-leader SKU has no organic demand pull (no one comes for it)
- In jurisdictions where below-cost selling is regulated/illegal (parts of EU)
- When shoppers can cherry-pick (buy only the loss leader, no attached basket)
- Without inventory depth — running out mid-promotion creates bait-and-switch perception

## Common pitfalls

- Loss leader sells out within the first hour, generating complaint volume and class-action risk
- Shoppers cherry-pick the loss leader and leave (especially with online ordering or curbside pickup)
- Failing to forecast the attached-basket math — actual basket lift doesn't cover the per-unit loss
- Selecting a low-pull SKU as the loss leader (no traffic generated, just margin destruction)
- Competitor matches the loss leader, neutralizing the traffic advantage
- Triggering regulatory scrutiny for below-cost selling or unfair-competition complaints
- Eroding brand perception by making the *whole* category look discount-driven

## Key metrics

- **Traffic lift on promo day** — footfall and door-counter delta vs. baseline
- **Basket size of loss-leader buyers** — units and dollars per transaction containing the leader SKU
- **Attached margin per loss-leader unit** — total basket margin minus loss on lead item
- **Cherry-pick rate** — share of buyers who purchase only the loss leader
- **Blended category margin during promo** — net margin including the loss-leader hit
- **Inventory sell-through** — did the limit-per-customer mechanism work?

## Decision questions

- Does the candidate SKU have enough organic demand pull to actually drive traffic?
- Have we modeled the basket attach — can we recover the per-unit loss across the trip?
- Is the store layout designed to force the shopper past higher-margin merchandise?
- Have we set per-customer purchase limits to prevent cherry-pick and stockout?
- Is below-cost selling legal in our jurisdiction at this discount depth?
- What's the competitor's likely response — match, ignore, or undercut?

## Canonical examples

- **Supermarket milk, eggs, rotisserie chicken** — classic grocery loss leaders that anchor the weekly trip (page 86)
- **Costco $1.50 hot dog combo** — loss-leader food court drives membership renewal and trip frequency
- **Best Buy Black Friday doorbuster TVs** — below-cost flagship items advertised to anchor the entire event
- **GameStop console launches** — hardware sold at thin/negative margin; software and accessories carry margin
- **IKEA $1 cinnamon bun and $1.99 breakfast** — café loss leaders that extend the visit
- **Walmart Thanksgiving turkey** — annually priced at or near cost to anchor the holiday basket
- **Black Friday "first 50 customers" deals** — engineered as loss-leader scarcity
