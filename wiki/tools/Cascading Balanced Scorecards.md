# Cascading Balanced Scorecards

## Definition

Cascading is the practice of translating a corporate-level Balanced Scorecard down through business units, support functions, teams, and ultimately individual employees, so that every level has a scorecard whose KPIs ladder up to the strategy above. Kaplan and Norton dedicated a full book to this discipline (_Alignment_, 2006). Pereira calls it the "Alignment" step of BSC implementation: "the high-level corporate strategy trickles down through the company's first business and support units until it reaches each employee" (page 40). Done right, every employee can answer: "How does my work connect to the corporate strategy?"

## Key attributes

- **Top-down translation, not top-down dictation** — each level builds its own scorecard; corporate just supplies the strategic context
- **Each level keeps the four-perspective structure** — a marketing team's scorecard still has Financial, Customer, Internal Process, L&G perspectives, scaled to its scope
- **Line of sight** — every KPI at every level should trace upward to a corporate strategic objective
- **Business units AND support units cascade** — Finance, HR, IT cascade just like revenue-generating units (page 40, 43)
- **Individual scorecards close the loop** — employee/team scorecards are the final layer (page 40)
- **Quarterly review cadence** — Pereira: managers review "every three or four months" with annual strategy refresh (page 27)
- **"Second-level metrics" for distributed teams** — implementation includes creating second-level metrics for teams that work in different places (page 27)
- **Cascading is an alignment mechanism, not a control mechanism** — it makes strategy visible, not micromanaged

## When to use

- After the corporate-level BSC and strategy map are stable (do not cascade an unsettled scorecard)
- In multi-unit organizations where business units have meaningful autonomy
- When line-of-sight between frontline work and corporate strategy is weak
- During post-M&A integration where two organizations need to align on shared strategy
- When implementing performance management at scale and you need a coherent KPI architecture

## When NOT to use

- Before the corporate scorecard has been used for at least one full cycle (cascading premature scorecards spreads incoherence)
- In flat organizations with < 50 people (overhead exceeds value; OKRs may fit better)
- When unit leaders will not own their cascaded scorecards (becomes paperwork)
- If the corporate strategy itself is unclear (cascading does not fix bad strategy)

## Common pitfalls

- **Copy-paste cascading** — units inherit the corporate scorecard verbatim instead of translating it for their scope
- **KPI inflation at lower levels** — each level adds its own KPIs, scorecard count balloons, fatigue sets in (page 61)
- **Compensation tied to cascaded KPIs prematurely** — Pereira's warning on project KPIs: "bad KPIs are ones that make your team think about how to cheat the system" (page 50)
- **Support functions ignored** — IT, HR, Finance scorecards skipped; alignment breaks at the support layer
- **No review cadence** — cascaded scorecards reviewed once, then forgotten (page 27)
- **Cascade depth too deep** — going below team level to individual scorecards creates HR-grade complexity without strategic gain in most contexts
- **Treating cascading as control** — units rebel; cascading should empower local ownership of strategy

## Key metrics

- **Cascading depth** — number of organizational levels with a live scorecard
- **Line-of-sight rate** — % of unit KPIs that explicitly link to a corporate strategic objective
- **Scorecard review cadence compliance** — % of units reviewing their scorecard quarterly
- **KPI density per level** — average KPIs per scorecard (target: < 15 per level; Pereira: focus, focus, focus, page 33)
- **Employee strategy recall** — % of employees who can describe how their team contributes to corporate strategy

## Decision questions

- Has our corporate BSC been stable for at least one annual cycle?
- Which business and support units cascade first? (Pick high-leverage, willing leaders)
- Who owns the scorecard at each level — and do they have authority to act on it?
- How do we keep KPI count from exploding at lower levels?
- What is our review cadence, and who runs the review?
- Will we tie any compensation to cascaded KPIs? (Default: no, until KPI quality is proven)

## Canonical examples

- **Telco company scorecards by strategic priority** (Pereira, page 45) — Brand Awareness, Customer Service, Content Partnerships each get their own scorecard rolling up to the corporate BSC
- **Project Management BSC** (page 46–52) — cascaded scorecard for a project, with Scope/Cost/Time/Risk/Quality/Resources KPIs tied back to organizational strategy
- **IT Balanced Scorecard** (page 53) — support-function cascade covering Finance (IT spend), Customer (internal user experience), Internal (security, prevention), L&G (learning from past failures)
- **HR Balanced Scorecard** (page 55–58) — HR-function cascade with Hire / Retention / Engagement / Turnover / ROI KPIs
- **Kaplan & Norton's _Alignment_ (2006)** — the canonical reference on cascading across business units, support units, and external partners
