# Loyalty Program

## Definition

A loyalty program rewards repeat customers with points, discounts, exclusive access, or status tiers to incentivize ongoing patronage. Pereira treats it as both a retention tactic and a data-collection mechanism — well-designed programs raise repeat purchase rates AND surface customer-level behavioral data that informs marketing, merchandising, and product decisions. Loyalty mechanics range from punch-card simplicity to sophisticated tier systems (airline miles), and increasingly include subscription overlays (Amazon Prime, Costco membership).

## Key attributes

- **Reward mechanism** — points, discounts, free items, tier upgrades, exclusive access
- **Tiered structure** — many programs use Bronze/Silver/Gold/Platinum tiers with escalating benefits
- **Data engine** — captures purchase data for personalization and segmentation
- **Retention focus** — designed to raise LTV by reducing churn and increasing purchase frequency
- **Switching cost creation** — accumulated points/status discourage defection
- **Coalition variants** — some loyalty programs span multiple brands (American Express MR, airline alliances)

## When to use

- High-frequency purchase categories (coffee, grocery, retail)
- Categories with intense competition where switching is easy
- When customer data would enable better segmentation/personalization
- When repeat business is the primary growth lever
- In service categories where loyalty drives word-of-mouth (hotels, airlines)

## When NOT to use

- Pure transactional one-off purchases (loyalty cost > revenue benefit)
- When customers are price-sensitive only (discount > loyalty)
- When the cost of program operations exceeds the retention benefit
- In commoditized categories where loyalty perks are easily matched

## Common pitfalls

- Treating loyalty program as substitute for product quality
- Reward economics that erode margins more than they boost retention
- Complex tier systems customers don't understand
- Stale program — same rewards for years lose engagement
- Failing to leverage the data — capture but don't act on customer behavior
- Letting "everyone is platinum" creep dilute status value

## Key metrics

- Enrollment rate (% of customers in program)
- Active member rate (engagement, not just enrollment)
- Redemption rate (rewards earned vs. used)
- Revenue per member vs. non-member
- Repeat purchase rate by tier
- NPS member vs. non-member

## Decision questions

- What incremental purchase behavior do we need to see for the program to pay back?
- Are we using the data we capture, or just collecting it?
- Are our tiers meaningful enough that customers strive to advance?
- Where would a coalition or partnership amplify our program's value?
- What's our break-even on average reward cost vs. retention lift?

## Canonical examples

- **Starbucks Rewards** — points (Stars) earned per purchase; tiered rewards (page 197 in BMC Explained)
- **Sephora Beauty Insider** — points + tier-based perks; tier (Insider, VIB, Rouge) (page 200 in BMC Explained)
- **Airline miles** (United MileagePlus, Delta SkyMiles) — points + status tiers
- **Hotel rewards** (Marriott Bonvoy, Hilton Honors) — similar to airlines
- **Amazon Prime** — flat-fee loyalty/subscription hybrid
- **Costco Membership** — paid loyalty; entry cost gates lower prices
