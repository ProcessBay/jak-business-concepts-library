# Lightning Decision Jam

## Definition

The Lightning Decision Jam (LDJ) is a 40-to-60-minute structured workshop format for moving a team from open-ended problems to concrete next actions — created by the European design agency **AJ&Smart**. Pereira places it among the brainstorming techniques, but it's really a full ideation-to-decision pipeline compressed into one session. The LDJ runs eight tightly time-boxed steps: write problems silently → present them → vote to prioritize → reframe the top problem as a "How might we?" challenge → silently generate solutions → vote on solutions → prioritize on an effort/impact scale → assign actionable tasks. It's designed to solve "open-ended problems that don't have clear answers or ready-made solutions" — the kind of meeting that usually drags for hours and ends without decisions.

## Key attributes

- **40-60 minutes total** — designed to fit a standard meeting slot
- **Eight steps** — problems → present → vote → reframe → solutions → vote → prioritize → tasks
- **Silent-then-share rhythm** — most steps start with individual writing
- **Quantity over quality** in the solution phase — explicit AJ&Smart rule
- **Effort/impact matrix** for prioritization — speeds up "what do we act on?"
- **"How might we?" reframing** — converts complaints into solvable challenges
- **Producible outcome** — every session ends with actionable tasks and owners

## When to use

- Sprint retros where the team needs decisions, not just discussion
- Post-mortem-style sessions when something has gone wrong
- Weekly team syncs that have devolved into venting
- Cross-functional workshops with limited time and lots of issues
- Remote teams using a shared digital board (Miro, Mural)
- Any meeting that historically produces "we'll figure it out later"

## When NOT to use

- Pure divergent ideation (LDJ is convergent; it terminates in tasks)
- Strategic decisions that require deep analysis (LDJ is fast, not deep)
- Teams that genuinely need to vent without resolving (use a different format)
- Very large groups (>10) — the voting steps get unwieldy
- When there's no problem to solve (LDJ needs friction as fuel)

## Common pitfalls

- Skipping the time-boxes ("just five more minutes...") — kills the rhythm
- Letting the facilitator dominate (the format is silent-first for a reason)
- Mis-framing the challenge ("How might we?" not "Why is this broken?")
- Stopping at voting and never producing actionable tasks (defeats the format)
- Choosing too many problems to solve in one jam — pick ONE per session
- Treating the prioritization as a vote rather than a structured effort/impact placement

## Key metrics

- **Time to first action** (target: < 60 min from session start)
- **Action items per session** (typical: 3-7 concrete tasks with owners)
- **Problems resolved per quarter** (run weekly = ~12 problems addressed)
- **Action completion rate** (did the assigned tasks actually happen?)

## Decision questions

- What single problem are we solving in this jam?
- Who owns each step's timer, and will they enforce it?
- How will we capture solutions and tasks (board, doc, ticket system)?
- Who owns the resulting tasks after the session?

## Canonical examples

- **Sprint retro** — replaces traditional "what went well / what didn't" with an action-producing format
- **Weekly team unblock** — surface, prioritize, and assign owners to blockers
- **Cross-functional product/design issue** — designers and engineers solve UX friction together
- **Customer-support process improvement** — surface pain points, reframe as challenges, ship fixes
- **Remote workshop standard** — runs natively on Miro/Mural with the AJ&Smart template (page 88)
