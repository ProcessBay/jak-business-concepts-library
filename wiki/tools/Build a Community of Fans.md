# Build a Community of Fans

## Definition

This is Pereira's design principle for the Customer Relationships block of any exponential business: rather than acquiring "users" one at a time, design the product so that customers become active participants who co-create value, customize the experience, and bring others in. The mechanism is to give users meaningful control over the product's expression — filters, lenses, mashups, playlists, profiles — so that personal expression becomes the seed of viral distribution. Each customer becomes a brand ambassador as a side effect of using the product, not as a separate ambassador program. The deeper claim is that "community" is not a marketing channel; it is a value-production mode that turns retention, virality, and word-of-mouth into structural properties of the business rather than line items in the marketing budget.

## Key attributes

- **Users as co-producers** — features that let customers create something they want to show off (Instagram filters, Snap lenses, Musical.ly mashups) (page 37)
- **Personal expression as viral distribution** — every share is a free acquisition channel (page 37)
- **Belonging as a retention force** — fans stay because they feel part of something, not just because the product is good (page 37)
- **Product becomes part of identity and routine** — daily use of customizable features deepens identity attachment (page 37)
- **Community is the flywheel seed** — Pereira's implementation prescription: "Creating a community is the first step in implementing flywheel concepts in your organization" (page 87)
- **Ambassador programs as accelerant, not substitute** — formal ambassadors amplify but cannot replace organic community
- **Customization tools as the primary investment** — the leverage is in giving fans creative power, not in talking at them
- **Two-way value** — communities need to receive something (status, tools, voice), not just be harvested

## When to use

- The product has emotional or expressive surface area (creative tools, content platforms, lifestyle brands)
- You compete in a category where consumer acquisition costs are high and word-of-mouth is structurally cheap
- The customer base is large and socially connected enough that referral compounding clears meaningful volume
- The business model can sustain investment in community before monetization (long horizon)
- You are designing a platform or ecosystem and need supply-side enthusiasm as well as demand-side advocacy

## When NOT to use

- The product is purely functional with no expressive surface area (B2B utilities, infrastructure)
- The customer base is too small or too dispersed for community dynamics to compound
- You cannot resource the community operations (moderation, tools, events, content)
- The category is sensitive and customers actively want privacy or low-profile use (security, healthcare)
- The brand cannot tolerate community-driven content (regulated industries, fragile brand contexts)

## Common pitfalls

- **"Community" without community management** — opening a Discord and walking away
- **Harvesting without giving** — using the community for free marketing without providing tools, status, or revenue
- **Confusing audience with community** — followers consume; community members create
- **Toxic community drift** — moderation neglect produces communities that drive customers away rather than in
- **Over-controlling expression** — fans use products that give them creative freedom; locked-down "branded" expression dies
- **Ambassador programs without organic seed** — paid ambassadors layered on a non-community produce hollow advocacy
- **Confusing community with customer success** — both matter; they are not the same function

## Key metrics

- **User-generated content share** — % of in-product content created by customers vs. brand
- **Referral rate** — % of new customers attributable to existing customers
- **Community engagement** — active community members / total customers
- **Time spent in community surfaces** vs. time spent in product
- **Brand advocacy / NPS** — willingness to recommend (proxy for fan status)
- **Customization usage rate** — % of users who exercise expressive features
- **Organic-vs-paid mix** — share of new users from non-paid channels (rises as community matures)

## Decision questions

- What is the smallest meaningful piece of creative control we can give customers?
- Are we acquiring users or recruiting fans — and what's the difference in our funnel?
- Who in the org owns community as a function, and is that role resourced to succeed?
- What do we give the community that they could not get for free elsewhere?
- Are our customization features being used, or are they fancy buttons no one presses?
- If our community went silent for a quarter, would we notice — and what would break first?

## Canonical examples

- **Instagram filters** — every shared photo carries the Instagram aesthetic and acts as an ambassador (page 37)
- **Snap lenses** — augmented-reality face filters create personal expression and viral content (page 37)
- **Musical.ly mashup functionality** — user-generated content fundamental to the platform's growth (page 37)
- **Waze contributors** — community-supplied road and traffic data, $1.3B exit at 100 employees (page 38)
- **Spotify playlists** — user-curated public playlists turn listeners into curators
- **GitHub** — developer community is the platform's gravity, $7.5B acquisition by Microsoft (page 49)
- **Reddit / Discord communities** — moderator-led communities that hold platforms together
