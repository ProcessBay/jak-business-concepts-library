# User-Generated Content Strategy

## Definition

A user-generated content (UGC) strategy is a deliberate system to encourage, capture, amplify, and reuse content created by customers — reviews, testimonials, photos, videos, social posts, hashtag campaigns — to drive marketing reach, conversion, and brand loyalty. Pereira identifies UGC as one of the most powerful growth-hacking levers because it solves three problems simultaneously: it generates a high-volume, low-cost content pipeline; it converts better than brand-produced content because it carries authenticity and peer-credibility; and it builds community by giving users a stake in the brand. The strategy is not "wait and hope users post" — it's an engineered system of incentives, prompts, hashtags, contests, and amplification mechanics that makes contribution easy and rewarding.

## Key attributes

- **Authenticity-driven** — peer-created content converts better than brand content
- **Engineered, not accidental** — built on contests, hashtags, prompts, recognition mechanics
- **Multi-format** — reviews, photos, videos, testimonials, social posts, blog posts
- **Low-cost content engine** — UGC scales without proportional content-production cost
- **Social-proof amplifier** — surfaces real customer voices at conversion points
- **Community-building** — gives users identity and recognition within the brand
- **Requires moderation** — brand safety and quality guardrails are non-negotiable

## When to use

- B2C brands with visual product appeal (fashion, beauty, food, travel, fitness)
- Categories where social proof drives purchase decisions
- When the brand cannot scale content production internally to required volume
- During product launches (build hype through customer participation)
- When community engagement is a strategic moat
- After product-market fit when customers naturally want to talk about the product

## When NOT to use

- B2B enterprise where customers can't or won't post publicly
- Highly regulated categories (finance, pharma) where UGC creates compliance risk
- Sensitive product categories (health, family-planning) where users won't share
- When the brand cannot invest in moderation and rights management
- When brand voice is so controlled that UGC dilutes positioning

## Common pitfalls

- Reposting without permission (legal and trust violation)
- No moderation — off-brand or harmful UGC published without review
- Inconsistent recognition (some contributors featured, others ignored)
- Vague hashtag campaigns with no clear participation mechanic
- Failing to amplify UGC where it converts (product pages, paid ads, email)
- Treating UGC as a one-off campaign instead of an always-on program
- Incentivizing volume over quality (low-effort posts dominate)

## Key metrics

- **UGC volume** — pieces of content per period (by format)
- **Engagement rate** — likes, comments, shares on UGC vs brand content
- **Share rate** — % of UGC reshared by other users
- **UGC conversion lift** — conversion of pages with UGC vs without
- **Hashtag reach** — total reach of a branded hashtag campaign
- **Contributor retention** — % of UGC contributors who contribute again

## Decision questions

- What naturally shareable moment does our product create?
- Where in the customer journey do we ask for UGC — and is it the highest-satisfaction moment?
- Do we have a clear, easy-to-remember hashtag and submission mechanic?
- How do we recognize and reward contributors at scale?
- Where do we amplify the best UGC — product pages, paid ads, email, social?
- What's our moderation policy and who owns it?

## Canonical examples

- **GoPro** — entire marketing strategy built on customer-captured footage
- **Apple #ShotOniPhone** — UGC hashtag campaign across billboards and ads
- **Starbucks #RedCupContest** — annual UGC contest with prize incentive
- **Glossier** — Instagram-first UGC model, customers as brand ambassadors
- **Airbnb** — guest photos and stories as primary marketing content (page 97-98)
- **Pinterest** — entire platform is UGC by design (page 109-110)
