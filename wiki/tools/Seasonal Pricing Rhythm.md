# Seasonal Pricing Rhythm

## Definition

Seasonal Pricing Rhythm is the planned cadence by which a retailer moves the price of seasonal merchandise across the season's lifecycle — typically high (or full) price at pre-season and peak, transitional discount mid-season, and steep markdowns at end-of-season. Pereira frames this on pages 78-80 and 117-119: seasonal demand peaks (summer outdoor gear, winter holiday décor, back-to-school) command full-price selling, while off-season periods require demand-stimulating discounts or pre-season teasers. The rhythm is calendar-driven, not elasticity-driven — every retailer in the category runs roughly the same calendar (back-to-school in August, Black Friday late November, post-Christmas clearance December 26), so the discipline is *executing the rhythm well* (right inventory depth, right price at each phase, clean transitions) rather than inventing a unique calendar. Dynamic bundling, anchor pricing, and demand forecasting are all sub-tactics inside the seasonal rhythm.

## Key attributes

- **Calendar-driven, not elasticity-driven** — the season sets the pricing windows
- **Three phases per season** — pre-season (build), peak (full price), post-peak (markdown)
- **Peak commands full price** — shoppers pay full because demand and urgency are highest then
- **Off-season demand stimulation** — discount-based clearance moves residual inventory
- **Pre-buy forecasting drives risk** — over-buy → end-of-season clearance hit; under-buy → missed peak revenue
- **Cross-category rhythm coordination** — back-to-school, holiday, spring reset run in parallel
- **External shock sensitivity** — weather, supply disruption, macro events shift the curve
- **Dynamic bundling within season** — bundle seasonal items together to lift average transaction

## When to use

- For any seasonal category (apparel, holiday décor, gardening, outdoor furniture, seasonal food)
- When demand has a known annual peak that you can model from prior years
- For event-driven retail (Halloween, Valentine's, Mother's Day, back-to-school, Black Friday)
- When pre-season inventory commitments lock you in — the rhythm tells you when to start moving stock
- For weather-sensitive merchandise (winter coats, sunscreen, snow shovels)

## When NOT to use

- For evergreen core assortment that sells year-round (groceries, basics, household commodities)
- In categories with no seasonal demand signal (commodity B2B supplies, daily-use consumables)
- When supply chain lead times make pre-season inventory commitments impossible
- For markets with anti-seasonal demand patterns (cruise apparel, year-round resort wear)
- When you can't differentiate seasonal from core assortment in merchandising

## Common pitfalls

- Over-buying for the peak based on prior-year performance, ignoring shifting demand signals
- Starting markdowns too early — sacrificing peak full-price revenue to clear early
- Starting markdowns too late — caught with deep inventory after peak passes, deeper markdowns needed
- Treating every season the same — winter cadence differs from summer, holiday differs from back-to-school
- Failing to read weather signals (mild winter destroys coat sales; rainy spring stalls outdoor furniture)
- No transitional pricing — abrupt jump from full price to 70% off (training shoppers to wait)
- Ignoring competitor markdown moves — falling behind on price after they start cutting
- Holiday creep (starting Halloween in August) erodes peak full-price weeks

## Key metrics

- **Peak-season margin** — gross margin captured during full-price selling weeks
- **Full-price sell-through by season week** — % units sold before first markdown
- **Off-peak promotional lift** — units moved during stimulation periods relative to baseline
- **End-of-season residual** — units left after final markdown (writedown exposure)
- **Inventory turn by season** — speed of conversion from pre-season buy to end-of-season clearance
- **Weather correlation index** — sensitivity of sell-through to weather variance

## Decision questions

- What's our pricing cadence for each major seasonal category — pre-season, peak, post-peak?
- How accurate were prior-year demand forecasts, and what's changed in this year's signals?
- When does our first markdown trigger fire — and is it elasticity-tested or just calendar-set?
- How do we read weather and macro signals to adjust the rhythm mid-season?
- What's our liquidation plan for end-of-season residual (jobber, off-price, charity, write-down)?
- Are we coordinating cross-category resets so floor space transitions cleanly?

## Canonical examples

- **Halloween décor** — full price September-late October, 50% off November 1, 75% off November 7
- **Christmas décor** — peak November-December 24, 50% off December 26, 75% off January
- **Back-to-school apparel** — full price July-August, transitional September, clearance October
- **Outdoor furniture** — full price spring, transitional July, clearance August-September (page 78)
- **Winter coats** — full price October-January, markdown February, clearance March (page 84)
- **Macy's** Black Friday/Cyber Monday spike and post-Christmas Bonus Days
- **REI** end-of-season Garage Sales — dedicated seasonal clearance events
- **Costco** seasonal aisles rotate quarterly with predictable markdown asterisks
