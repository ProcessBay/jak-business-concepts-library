# Explainer Video MVP

## Definition

An Explainer Video MVP is a validation tactic where you produce a short narrative video showing how a product *would* work — and measure demand based on how many viewers take action (sign up, request access, share). The product itself does not exist. The video does. Pereira's archetypal example is Dropbox: founders Drew Houston and Arash Ferdowsi couldn't easily explain file-sync to investors or users, so they made a 3-minute screencast showing the imagined experience. Posted to Hacker News, it drove 70,000 email signups in a single day — validation that funded development and proved the concept before any code shipped. The hypothesis under test is whether the *value proposition*, communicated clearly, attracts the right audience.

## Key attributes

- **No working product required** — the video IS the experiment
- **Story-driven** — illustrates how the product solves a real problem in a narrative arc
- **Paired with email capture** — converts video viewers into a waitlist asset
- **Cheap and fast** — a screencast + voiceover can ship in days
- **Highly shareable** — works on Hacker News, Twitter/X, Reddit, Product Hunt
- **Tests value-prop clarity** — if the video can't convert, the proposition isn't sharp enough
- **Limitation: tests interest, not retention** — signups are not active users

## When to use

- Products that are conceptually novel and hard to explain in text (file-sync, AI, new categories)
- Pre-build demand validation when engineering cost is high
- When the value proposition is visual or demonstrable
- For fundraising — investors respond to a clear narrative + traction signal
- To build a launch list before any code ships
- For products that require behavior change (video can show "before/after")

## When NOT to use

- Products whose value is obvious in a screenshot or one-sentence pitch (just use a landing page)
- Categories where viewers are skeptical of video promises (B2B finance, regulated)
- When you can't produce a video that genuinely conveys the experience
- When making the video would cost more than building a single-feature MVP
- For incremental features within an existing product (use Fake Door instead)

## Common pitfalls

- **Vanity-metric trap** — counting views instead of signups
- **Overproduction** — spending weeks on cinematography when a screencast would have sufficed
- **Promising things you can't build** — sets up disappointment and bad PR when you launch
- **No follow-up sequence** — the email list goes cold while you build
- **Wrong distribution channel** — video on the wrong platform produces no signal
- **Conflating viral hit with product-market fit** — a great video drives novelty signups, not necessarily product demand
- **Building exactly what the video showed** — when the right product, post-feedback, would differ

## Key metrics

- Email signups (absolute and rate)
- Signup conversion rate (signups / unique viewers)
- Video completion rate (% who watch to the end)
- Cost per signup (if running paid distribution)
- Share-through rate (how viral)
- Geo / segment breakdown of signups (who actually resonated)

## Decision questions

- Is our product novel enough that a video is the cheapest way to convey it?
- What conversion threshold, pre-committed, would we call "validated"?
- Do we have a follow-up sequence ready for the email list?
- Are we testing the value prop — or just our video-making skills?
- What's our plan if the video drives 70,000 signups (capacity, communication, prioritization)?
- What's our plan if it drives 70?

## Canonical examples

- **Dropbox** — Drew Houston's 3-minute screencast (2007) showed file sync working seamlessly across devices. Posted to Hacker News, drove 70,000 email signups in one day, validated demand, secured funding (pages 17, 81)
- **Buffer** — used a hybrid: explainer landing page + tweets describing the concept; validated before building (page 80)
- Many post-2007 startups — Dropbox-pattern videos became a standard pre-launch tactic
