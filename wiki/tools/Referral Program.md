# Referral Program

## Definition

A referral program is a structured system that incentivizes existing customers to bring new customers to the business in exchange for a reward — typically benefiting both the referrer and the new user. Pereira positions referral programs as the most leveraged form of customer acquisition: they convert satisfied users into a distributed sales force, leverage trusted personal recommendations, and produce CAC that is structurally lower than paid channels. The canonical example is Dropbox's double-sided storage reward, which drove the company from 100,000 to 4 million users in 15 months. A successful referral program is not a coupon — it's an engineered incentive structure built on a clear value proposition, low-friction sharing, and double-sided rewards.

## Key attributes

- **Double-sided incentive** — both referrer and referee get value (one-sided programs underperform)
- **Low-friction sharing** — one-click links, pre-filled messages, multiple channels
- **Tracked end-to-end** — unique codes/links attribute each referral
- **Tiered or stacked rewards** — escalating benefits for power referrers
- **Promoted in-product** — surfaced at moments of high satisfaction (post-purchase, post-task)
- **Personalized** — incentive and messaging tailored to user segment
- **Continuously optimized** — incentive structure A/B tested for conversion lift

## When to use

- After product-market fit, when users have genuine positive sentiment to share
- In categories with high personal recommendation influence (consumer SaaS, finance, e-commerce)
- When paid CAC is rising and a cheaper channel is needed
- When the product has natural use-with-others mechanics (team SaaS, marketplaces)
- When the team can build and instrument tracking infrastructure

## When NOT to use

- Before product-market fit (you'll amplify churn through referrals)
- In categories where referrals are taboo (regulated finance, sensitive health, B2B with strict procurement)
- When the unit economics don't support a double-sided incentive
- When the user base is too small to produce meaningful referral volume
- When tracking infrastructure can't attribute referrals accurately

## Common pitfalls

- One-sided rewards (only the referrer benefits) — kills conversion of the referee
- Rewards that don't relate to the product (gift cards instead of more product)
- Hidden or hard-to-find referral surface in the product
- High-friction sharing flows (multi-step forms, manual code entry)
- Not tracking referral cohort retention — referred users may churn faster than organic
- Treating it as a campaign instead of a permanent program
- Ignoring referral quality (fraudulent self-referrals, low-intent users)

## Key metrics

- **Referral conversion rate** — referrals sent → new sign-ups
- **Referrals per active user** (per cohort)
- **Referral CAC** vs paid CAC
- **Viral coefficient (K)** for the referral channel
- **Referred-user LTV** vs organic-user LTV
- **Time-to-referral** — how long until a new user refers someone else

## Decision questions

- What is the smallest reward that still drives action, and the largest the unit economics support?
- Is our reward double-sided? Why not?
- How many clicks between "I want to refer" and "shared"?
- Where in the product do we surface the referral CTA — and is that the highest-satisfaction moment?
- Do referred users retain and monetize as well as organic users?

## Canonical examples

- **Dropbox** — 500MB of storage for both referrer and referee (page 99-100)
- **Airbnb** — travel credits for both sides (page 97-98)
- **PayPal** — $10 sign-up bonus + $10 referrer bonus (early-stage)
- **Uber** — free first ride for new user + ride credit for referrer (page 101-102)
- **Tesla** — referral-only rewards (free Supercharging, Roadster access) with zero paid ads
- **Robinhood** — free stock for both sides at sign-up
