# Anchoring in Negotiation

## Definition

Anchoring in negotiation is the tactic of strategically setting a reference point — usually through the first offer — that biases subsequent offers, counteroffers, and perceptions of "reasonable" outcomes in the anchor-setter's favor. Pereira documents four anchoring techniques: (1) **Initial offer** — make the first move and shape the bargaining range; (2) **Extreme anchoring** — set an aggressive anchor that shifts the perceived midpoint, even if the anchor itself is unrealistic; (3) **Multiple anchors** — present a range of options (e.g., MESOs — multiple equivalent simultaneous offers) to position favorably within a self-defined space; (4) **External standards** — anchor on industry benchmarks, market prices, or independent valuations to lend legitimacy. Research cited by Pereira shows the first mover often gains an advantage by setting the psychological reference point — but the anchor must be ambitious *and* defensible. An anchor that is too extreme risks alienation, walk-away, or loss of credibility. Crucially: this is distinct from [[Anchor Pricing]] (the retail/pricing strategy of placing a high-priced decoy next to a target product to shift consumer perception).

## Key attributes

- **First mover often wins** — initial offer sets the reference point
- **Four sub-techniques** — initial, extreme, multiple, external-standard
- **Ambitious but defensible** — anchor must be aggressive yet justifiable
- **MESO option** — multiple equivalent simultaneous offers position you within your own range
- **External standards lend legitimacy** — industry benchmarks anchor objectively
- **Counter-anchor required** — never accept an unfavorable anchor without re-anchoring
- **Distinct from Anchor Pricing** — this is bargaining tactic, not retail price-architecture

## When to use

- When you have prepared and have credible market data to defend the anchor
- When the counterparty has weaker information about the value range
- In distributive negotiations on price, salary, or single-issue deals
- When you can move first without surrendering information advantage
- When external benchmarks favor your position

## When NOT to use

- When the counterparty is far better informed and an aggressive anchor will appear naïve
- When relationship preservation outweighs short-term value extraction
- In integrative negotiations where premature anchoring shuts down interest exploration
- When you lack the information to set a defensible anchor — guessing wide is worse than letting them go first
- In cultures where extreme first offers signal bad faith

## Common pitfalls

- Anchoring so aggressively the counterparty walks away or stops engaging
- Failing to counter-anchor when the other side moves first
- Anchoring on a position before learning the counterparty's interests (kills integrative play)
- Treating MESOs as easier — they require *more* prep, not less
- Citing external standards that the counterparty can refute
- Letting the counterparty anchor unchallenged ("that becomes the number")
- Confusing this tactic with Anchor Pricing — they share psychological mechanism but operate in different contexts

## Key metrics

- **Anchor premium** — gap between anchor and your target outcome
- **Distance from anchor to close** — how much you conceded from opening
- **Counter-offer lift** — how much the counterparty moved from their anchor
- **First-mover advantage** — empirical: who moved first, who captured more value
- **Walk-away rate** — % of anchors that ended negotiation entirely

## Decision questions

- Do I have enough information to set a defensible anchor, or should I let them go first?
- How aggressive can my anchor be without losing the counterparty?
- What external benchmarks support my number?
- If I present MESOs, are all three genuinely equivalent from my side?
- How will I counter their anchor if they move first?
