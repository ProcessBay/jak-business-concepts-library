# Sales Coaching Framework

## Definition

Sales coaching is the structured, ongoing practice of frontline managers (or dedicated coaches) developing individual reps' skills, judgment, and confidence through individualized plans, real-time feedback, and observed deal work — distinct from generic training, which delivers content to groups. Pereira positions coaching as the reinforcement layer that turns enablement content into changed behavior; without it, training decays within weeks. The framework rests on individualized plans tied to measurable goals, real-time feedback loops on live calls and deals, deliberate observation/shadowing, skill-development workshops, role-play and simulation, positive reinforcement, and a growth-mindset culture. Coaches need high emotional intelligence to read motivation and tailor approach. Effective coaching is the single highest-leverage activity a sales manager performs — more impactful than pipeline review, forecast scrubbing, or deal escalation.

## Key attributes

- **Individualized plans** — each rep gets a plan based on specific skill gaps, not generic curriculum
- **Goal-oriented with metrics** — coaching tied to measurable outcomes (closing rate, deal size, specific skill)
- **Real-time feedback** — timely course-correction during/after calls, not annual review cycles
- **Observation and shadowing** — coach joins live client interactions to see actual behavior
- **Role-play and simulation** — controlled practice of hard scenarios (objections, negotiation, discovery)
- **Positive reinforcement** — acknowledging wins, not just correcting errors
- **Continuous learning frame** — coaching is ongoing, not event-based
- **Emotional intelligence required** — coaches must understand rep motivation and emotional state
- **Growth-mindset culture** — frames challenges as learning opportunities, not failure

## When to use

- Every sales team — coaching is the multiplier on enablement investment
- After onboarding, to convert classroom learning into field competence
- When ramp time is too long or quota attainment is below 60-70%
- When specific skill gaps surface (objection handling, discovery, closing)
- After major changes — new product, new ICP, new methodology, competitive shift
- Before any rep is put on a PIP — coaching first, then performance management

## When NOT to use

- As a substitute for hiring better — coaching can't fix wrong-profile reps at scale
- As disguised micromanagement (call inspection without development intent)
- When managers don't have time or skill — bad coaching is worse than no coaching
- In place of structured enablement content (coaching reinforces, doesn't replace, content)
- For pure activity metrics (calls per day) rather than skill development

## Common pitfalls

- Manager coaches reps on the deals they're personally involved in, ignoring the rest of the book
- Generic advice ("be more consultative") instead of specific behavioral coaching
- All feedback, no reinforcement — reps disengage
- Coaching only the bottom performers — top performers also need coaching to grow
- Frontline managers never trained in coaching, defaulting to deal review
- No cadence — ad-hoc coaching when something goes wrong
- Confusing coaching with shadowing (passive observation without follow-up plan)
- Skipping the growth mindset — coaching that punishes failure shuts down learning

## Key metrics

- **Coaching sessions per rep per month** — leading indicator (target ≥2)
- **Win rate lift** — does coached cohort outperform uncoached?
- **Quota attainment uplift** — coaching's impact on % at-plan
- **Deal velocity** — shorter cycles signal better in-deal coaching
- **Skill certification or assessment scores** — track skill development directly
- **Ramp time reduction** — coached new hires reach productivity faster
- **Rep retention** — coached reps stay longer (correlated, not causal)

## Decision questions

- Are our frontline managers actually coaching, or are they just running pipeline reviews?
- Do we have a coaching cadence (e.g., 2 sessions/rep/month) that's instrumented?
- Are managers trained in coaching technique, or did we promote our top rep and hope?
- Are we coaching to specific skill gaps, or giving generic feedback?
- Do top performers get coached, or only those at risk?
- How are we measuring whether coaching is working — beyond manager opinion?
- Are we using observation/shadowing, or coaching only from CRM data?

## Canonical examples

- **Salesforce** — embedded coaching cadence supported by CRM-integrated call analytics
- **HubSpot** — Sales Hub Academy combined with manager-led 1:1 coaching framework
- **Force Management** — third-party coaching methodology widely adopted in B2B SaaS
- **Pereira's framework** — nine techniques: individualized plans, goals, real-time feedback, observation, workshops, role-play, reinforcement, continuous learning, emotional intelligence/growth mindset (pages 60-66)
