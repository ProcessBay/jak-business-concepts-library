# Sustainable Business Practices

## Definition

Sustainable business practices are the concrete operational changes a firm makes to reduce environmental impact and create social value — things like adopting renewable energy, reducing waste, using sustainable supply chains, designing reusable/recyclable packaging, supporting communities. Unlike the broader Triple Bottom Line framework (which is the strategic orientation), sustainable practices are the tactical implementations. Pereira illustrates with Unilever's commitments — 100% renewable energy use by 2030 and reusable, recyclable, or compostable plastic packaging by 2025 — both of which reduce environmental impact AND create cost savings.

## Key attributes

- **Operational, not strategic** — practices are concrete changes to how things get made, shipped, sold
- **Often cost-saving** — many practices reduce input costs (energy, waste, packaging) alongside impact
- **Supply-chain wide** — sustainability extends beyond the firm's walls to suppliers and partners
- **Measurable** — practices have specific KPIs (energy %, waste %, certified-material %)
- **Time-bound commitments** — typically expressed with explicit target dates (e.g., 100% by 2030)
- **Audit-able** — practices should withstand third-party verification (ESG audits, B Corp, etc.)

## When to use

- In response to customer/investor pressure for verified sustainability
- When energy/waste/materials costs are significant and rising
- For brands where sustainability is a credible differentiator
- In regulated industries where environmental compliance is intensifying
- When recruiting purpose-driven talent (sustainability matters to younger cohorts)

## When NOT to use

- As marketing claims without operational follow-through (greenwashing exposes badly)
- In industries where customers genuinely don't care AND there's no regulatory pressure
- As a substitute for product strength — sustainability won't rescue a weak offering
- When financial survival is acute and sustainability investments require capital you don't have

## Common pitfalls

- Greenwashing — making claims without changing operations
- Cherry-picking metrics (lower carbon but worse labor practices)
- Treating sustainability as a CSR side project disconnected from operations
- Failing to verify supply-chain compliance — your supplier's practices become yours
- Missing the cost-saving upside by framing sustainability purely as cost

## Key metrics

- Carbon footprint (Scope 1, 2, 3)
- % renewable energy in operations
- Waste-to-landfill percentage
- Recyclable / reusable / compostable packaging share
- Supplier sustainability audit pass rate
- ESG rating (MSCI, Sustainalytics)
- Payback period on sustainability investments

## Decision questions

- Which of our largest cost line items would shrink if we became more sustainable?
- For each sustainability claim we make: what's the verifiable proof point?
- Where in our supply chain is the biggest environmental exposure we don't control?
- Are our commitments time-bound and specific, or generic and aspirational?
- Would our practices withstand a third-party audit?

## Canonical examples

- **Unilever** — 100% renewable energy use by 2030, reusable/recyclable/compostable plastic packaging by 2025; reduces environmental impact while creating cost savings and improved brand reputation (page 49)
- **Patagonia** — sustainable supply chain, environmental protection initiatives, recycled materials; built a strong brand identity that drives customer loyalty (pages 31, 38, 41, 48)
- **IKEA** — uses sustainable materials and designs products for easy assembly and transportation, simultaneously reducing costs AND appealing to environmentally-conscious consumers (page 53)
