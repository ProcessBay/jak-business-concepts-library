# Falsifiable Hypothesis Building

## Definition

A falsifiable hypothesis is a specific, testable claim about a business model assumption that can be definitively confirmed or refuted by evidence. Pereira's method: (1) Identify the assumption underlying a BMC block; (2) Define the customer segment with whom to test; (3) Determine a specific metric that would prove or disprove the hypothesis. Falsifiability — Popper's scientific criterion — is what separates a real test from confirmation-bias-prone "validation." A good hypothesis has a clear failure condition; if you can't articulate how you'd know you're wrong, you're not testing.

## Key attributes

- **Specific, not vague** — "20% of doctors download the app" beats "doctors will like the app"
- **Falsifiable** — must be possible to disprove with data
- **Bounded to a segment** — applies to a defined customer group, not "the market"
- **Time-bounded** — typically expressed with a deadline ("within 30 days of launch")
- **Measurable** — has a concrete metric that defines success or failure
- **Pre-registered** — set the success threshold BEFORE the test, not after

## When to use

- Step 3 of the Business Model Testing Cycle
- Before any A/B test, MVP, or experiment
- For investor pitches — convert claims into falsifiable predictions
- For team alignment — falsifiable hypotheses force shared mental models
- Post-launch — examine which hypotheses held and which didn't

## When NOT to use

- For purely qualitative research (interviews, observation) where the goal is discovery
- When you can't actually measure the outcome
- In domains where ethical/operational constraints prevent the test

## Common pitfalls

- Vague hypotheses that can't be falsified ("customers will like our product")
- Setting success thresholds AFTER seeing results (post-hoc rationalization)
- Confirmation bias: stopping the test as soon as data supports the hypothesis
- Testing too many things simultaneously (can't attribute results)
- Falsification: refusing to accept disconfirming evidence

## Key metrics

- Hypothesis specificity score (would a stranger know exactly what to measure?)
- Success criterion clarity (pre-registered vs. post-hoc)
- Validation rate (% of hypotheses that held vs. were refuted)
- Time-to-test per hypothesis

## Decision questions

- For each hypothesis: what specific data would convince me I'm wrong?
- Did we set the success threshold before starting the test, or after?
- Are we testing one variable at a time, or confounding multiple?
- When the test refutes our hypothesis, will we accept it or rationalize?

## Canonical examples

- **App for doctors** — "20% of doctors in town will download the app within 60 days of launch" (page 12)
- **Subscription product** — "10% of free-tier users will upgrade within 30 days"
- **Pricing test** — "50% conversion rate at $9.99 vs. 35% at $19.99"
- **Feature adoption** — "30% of users will use the new feature within 14 days"
