# Ad Pricing Mechanisms

## Definition

Ad pricing mechanisms are the four primary ways digital ads are sold, each transferring risk between media and advertiser differently: **CPM** (cost per thousand impressions — advertiser pays for views regardless of action); **CPC** (cost per click — advertiser pays only when user clicks); **CPL** (cost per lead — advertiser pays when a lead form is submitted); **CPA/CPS** (cost per action or sale — advertiser pays only when a final conversion event occurs). Each model shifts the risk balance between buyer and seller of the ad inventory. Pereira frames CPM as lowest-conversion-risk for media, highest for advertiser; CPA as highest-conversion-risk for media, lowest for advertiser.

## Key attributes

- **CPM** — pay per 1,000 impressions; advertiser bears conversion risk; standard for brand campaigns
- **CPC** — pay per click; risk shared; standard for performance (Google Ads, social ads)
- **CPL** — pay per qualified lead; advertiser pays for intent signal; common in B2B
- **CPA / CPS** — pay only on conversion or sale; media bears all conversion risk; common in affiliate
- **Risk-reward trade-off** — lower risk for advertiser typically means higher unit price
- **Attribution complexity** — CPC/CPL/CPA require tracking; CPM doesn't

## When to use

- **CPM**: brand-building campaigns, mass reach, top-of-funnel awareness
- **CPC**: performance marketing with clear conversion measurement
- **CPL**: B2B lead generation where each lead has known qualification value
- **CPA / CPS**: e-commerce, affiliate, performance contexts with measurable end conversion
- Layered: combine CPM for awareness + CPC/CPA for performance

## When NOT to use

- CPM in contexts where the advertiser can't justify spend without conversion data
- CPA in brand-building where conversion isn't the goal
- CPC on inventory where bots or accidental clicks distort data
- Any mechanism without tracking infrastructure to support attribution

## Common pitfalls

- Choosing CPM without conversion measurement — can't tell if it's working
- Choosing CPA without sufficient data — small samples lead to volatile rates
- Confusing CPC bid (what advertiser pays per click) with eCPM (effective revenue per 1,000 to publisher)
- Failing to set frequency caps on CPM ads (over-exposure annoys users, kills brand)
- Attribution disputes between channels (CPA fights for credit)

## Key metrics

- eCPM (effective revenue per 1,000 impressions, the publisher-side equivalent)
- CTR (click-through rate) — converts CPM to CPC equivalent
- Conversion rate per click (CPC → CPA equivalent)
- Fill rate (% of inventory that sells)
- Advertiser ROI (return on ad spend, ROAS)
- Attribution window (how long after the ad before conversion counts)

## Decision questions

- For each campaign objective (awareness, consideration, conversion): which mechanism fits?
- Is our attribution infrastructure good enough for CPC/CPL/CPA, or should we default to CPM?
- What's our eCPM across mechanisms, and which is most profitable for our inventory?
- Where are advertisers leaving us due to mechanism limitations?

## Canonical examples

- **Google Ads** — primarily CPC; also CPM (Display Network), CPA (Google Ads Performance Max) (page 17)
- **Facebook / Meta Ads** — CPM, CPC, CPA depending on objective (page 18)
- **Snapchat** — CPM as primary (page 40 in HCMM source)
- **YouTube** — CPM (TrueView) and CPC for sponsored ads (page 30 in HCMM source)
- **LinkedIn Marketing Solutions** — CPC, CPM, CPS (Sponsored InMail) (page 33 in HCMM source)
