# How to Write a Mission Statement

## Definition

A practical five-step process and two associated tactics — the **VIPS method** and a **fill-in-the-blank template** — for drafting a mission statement that earns the nine characteristics of a compelling one. Pereira separates the *what* (what makes a mission good) from the *how* (how to actually produce one), giving founders and leadership teams a sequence that goes from offering description, to core values, to alignment, to condensation, to cleanup. The intent is to produce a statement that is brief, unique, realistic, memorable, active, positive, adaptable, and targeted — without lapsing into buzzwords or losing personality (pages 72-78).

## Key attributes

- **Five-step sequence** — (1) explain the offering, (2) identify core values, (3) connect offering to values, (4) condense into one statement, (5) cut the fluff (pages 76-78)
- **VIPS method as quality test** — Value, Inspiration, Plausibility, Specifics (page 75)
- **Sentence template** — "We [what you do] by [how you do it] for [target customers] to [ultimate value]" (page 72)
- **Three answers to embed** — What the company sells, Who it serves, Why (core values) (page 78)
- **Active voice required** — "[Company] makes [X] to ease mobility," not "These [X] are made by [Company]" (page 74)
- **External review built in** — get feedback from team or advisers before finalizing (page 78)
- **Post-launch rollout** — present to CEO/exec team, distribute company-wide, recognize employees who live it (page 81)

## When to use

- Founding a venture and codifying purpose for the first time
- Re-writing an existing mission after a strategic pivot, merger, or rebrand
- Refreshing a mission that the team can no longer recite or that has lost relevance
- Onboarding new leadership who need to ratify or rewrite the existing statement
- Preparing for funding rounds, IPO, or major external communications

## When NOT to use

- Before product-market fit is even sketched out — premature missions ossify wrong commitments
- As a solo-founder exercise without team input — the process loses its alignment value
- As a marketing campaign — the writing process should produce strategic clarity first, marketing copy second
- When leadership is in active disagreement about direction — resolve the strategy first

## Common pitfalls

- **Skipping step 2 (core values)** — produces a statement that describes the product but has no soul
- **Skipping step 5 (cleanup)** — leaves buzzwords, qualifiers, and corporate filler intact
- **Solo drafting** — no external review means blind spots stay invisible
- **Writing for investors only** — language optimized for pitch decks alienates employees and customers
- **Confusing "ambitious" with "vague"** — using grand language to dodge specifics fails the Specificity test in VIPS
- **Skipping the rollout** — a mission that exists only in the deck does not shape culture

## Key metrics

- **Mission recall rate** — % of employees who can paraphrase the mission a month after launch
- **Endorsement vote** — % of leadership/team who actively endorse the final wording
- **Brevity score** — word count under 25 (single sentence) is a strong signal
- **VIPS pass rate** — does the statement pass all four VIPS criteria? (Value, Inspiration, Plausibility, Specifics)
- **Iteration count** — most usable missions land at draft 4-8, not draft 1

## Decision questions

- What does our company actually sell, in one plain sentence?
- What three to five core values would still hold even if the product changed?
- Where does our offering and our values converge — what is the unique intersection?
- Could a competitor copy this statement word-for-word? If yes, return to step 2.
- Does the draft pass VIPS — Value, Inspiration, Plausibility, Specifics?
- Have we removed every buzzword, every "leverage," every "synergy"?
- Have we shown it to someone outside the room?

## Canonical examples

- **Template applied (Asana)** — "To help humanity thrive [value] by enabling all teams to work together effortlessly [how], for teams [who]." (page 35)
- **Template applied (Microsoft)** — "Empower every person and organization on the planet to achieve more." — value + scope + who (page 46)
- **Active-verb model (Tesla)** — "To accelerate the world's transition to renewable energy." — verb-first (page 62)
- **Brevity model (Patagonia)** — "To save our home planet." — four words, full meaning (page 52)
- **VIPS-Inspiration exemplar (Nike)** — "Bring inspiration and innovation to every athlete in the world." — passes Inspiration test (page 50)
- **VIPS-Plausibility exemplar (Walmart)** — "Save money, live better." — promises only what they deliver (page 68)
