# How to Build a Decentralized Organization

## Definition

A five-step playbook from Pereira for organizations transitioning from centralized to decentralized management. The sequence is not optional — Pereira treats each step as a prerequisite for the next: without explicit values, distributed authority drifts; without broad input, decisions stay narrow; without training, employees can't exercise authority well; without a teamwork culture, autonomy fragments into silos; without growth pathways, talent leaves (the Zappos holacracy lesson on page 59). The model is structural — it builds the conditions under which decentralization actually works rather than just declaring authority decentralized. It also explicitly recognizes the challenges Pereira documents (page 58): slower change implementation, risk of team disorganization without strong reporting, function duplication across units, and the risk that lower-level decisions on corporate social responsibility issues backfire on the whole firm.

## Key attributes

- **Step 1 — Establish company values** — a robust workplace culture acts as the compass for distributed leaders
- **Step 2 — Include all employees** — gather input from a broad set of knowledgeable people in decisions (committees, consultation)
- **Step 3 — Prioritize training** — make ongoing training mandatory for everyone, not just new hires
- **Step 4 — Develop a culture of teamwork** — distributed authority depends on collaboration and trust
- **Step 5 — Encourage growth** — reward skill expansion and promote from middle layers into strategic roles
- **Pre-emptively address the four challenges** — slow change rollout, team disorganization risk, function duplication, lower-level CSR mis-judgments
- **Phase to firm size** — too early (small startup) wastes resources; too late (large entrenched hierarchy) requires major change effort

## When to use

- Established companies whose hierarchical depth has become a decision bottleneck
- Medium-sized businesses where decentralization can unlock innovation and engagement
- Multinationals expanding into regions where local context matters
- Companies preparing for growth — Pereira advises designing the model before you need it
- Organizations that have just shifted to flexible / remote / fluid management and need a coherent operating model
- Firms whose talent base is high-agency and will leave without autonomy

## When NOT to use

- Very early startups with limited resources — Pereira notes radical decentralization is often impractical at this stage
- Companies where the leadership team isn't genuinely willing to cede control
- Regulated functions (finance, compliance, safety) where central guardrails are non-negotiable
- Cultures that haven't built the trust or psychological safety for autonomy to work
- Firms where the competitive advantage is uniformity and central scale — decentralizing would erode the moat

## Common pitfalls

- Declaring values without behaviorally enforcing them — values become wall posters
- Consulting employees performatively while real decisions stay centralized — corrodes trust faster than no consultation
- One-time training programs instead of continuous reinforcement
- Encouraging teamwork without restructuring incentives — individual KPIs still drive siloed behavior
- Promoting from middle layers without clear pathways or criteria (Zappos lost employees because the holacracy buyout was easier than navigating an unclear progression path, page 59)
- Skipping reporting systems — Pereira's named risk: "teams run the risk of becoming disorganized when there is no central authority figure" (page 58)
- Letting function duplication metastasize (separate customer-service teams in every department instead of a coordinated capability)

## Key metrics

- **Values clarity** — % of employees who can articulate the firm's values consistently
- **Training hours per employee per year** — must include established staff, not just new hires
- **Cross-team collaboration rate** — frequency of inter-team projects and consultations
- **Internal promotion rate** — % of leadership roles filled from middle layers
- **Decision velocity** — time from issue surfacing to action
- **Function-duplication count** — number of overlapping capabilities across units
- **Voluntary attrition at middle layers** — proxy for whether autonomy and pathway feel real

## Decision questions

- Are our values explicit enough that a regional manager can act on them without checking HQ?
- Whose input are we actually gathering, and is it shaping decisions or window-dressing them?
- Is training a one-time event or a continuous investment?
- Are our incentives aligned to teamwork, or are individuals still optimizing alone?
- Do middle-layer employees see a real path forward without exiting?
- What reporting cadence prevents drift without recentralizing authority?
- Where are duplicated functions emerging — and should we coordinate them or accept the cost?

## Canonical examples

- **Johnson & Johnson** — operates the playbook at scale across 130,000+ employees in 100+ countries (page 40)
- **Unilever** — decentralized after expansion; regional branches act as independent businesses adapting to local context (page 60)
- **Zappos (cautionary)** — adopted holacracy, but employees lacked progression paths and quit en masse via the buyout (page 59)
- **Eventbrite** — event organizers double as managers; regional managers run global development offices (page 41)
- **Bee Token (home-sharing)** — decentralized newcomer cited by Pereira as disrupting a traditionally centralized industry (page 64)
- **General Motors (1920s historical)** — du Pont and Sloan's pioneering implementation; took ~3 years to take hold (page 22)
