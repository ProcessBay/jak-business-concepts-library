# Pitch Deck for Investors

## Definition

A pitch deck is the structured slide-based investment proposal a founder uses to present their startup to angels, VCs, and corporate investors. Pereira describes it as both a standalone artifact (sent for screening) and a live-presentation aid (used in pitch meetings). Its job is to capture investor attention quickly, communicate the startup's vision and traction, demonstrate market opportunity, and make the case for why the investor should write a check at the proposed terms. A good deck balances data density with narrative clarity — most investors scan in 3-5 minutes on first pass, so each slide must earn its place.

## Key attributes

- **Executive summary upfront** — vision, value proposition, market in the first slides
- **Problem-solution narrative** — clearly articulated pain point and how the startup solves it
- **Market analysis** — TAM/SAM/SOM, growth rate, opportunity sizing
- **Business model and revenue streams** — how the startup makes money
- **Team showcase** — founders' relevant expertise and track record
- **Financial projections** — realistic, well-supported revenue/profit trajectory
- **Investment ask and use of funds** — explicit amount sought and allocation
- **Exit strategy** — acquisition or IPO scenarios for investor return
- **Supporting materials** — demos, testimonials, press coverage as appendix
- **Professional design** — clean visual hierarchy, brand consistency

## When to use

- Any time approaching angels, VCs, corporate investors, or accelerators
- Warm-intro outreach (concise version, often 10-12 slides)
- Live pitch meetings (longer-form, ~15-20 slides)
- Demo days and pitch competitions (timed versions, often 5-10 slides)
- Strategic partner conversations where investment may follow

## When NOT to use

- Customer sales meetings — different artifact (sales deck, not pitch deck)
- Early discovery conversations where the deck pre-empts dialogue
- When the relationship calls for narrative-only conversation (very early angel)
- Internal team alignment — use a strategy doc, not a fundraising deck

## Common pitfalls

- Burying the lede — vision slide too late; investor loses interest
- Over-claimed financial projections that fail diligence
- Team slide with weak relevance — investors back people first
- Missing or vague "use of funds" — investors need to see where the money goes
- Too much text per slide — investors stop reading at slide 3
- Generic "huge TAM" claim without bottom-up SOM defensible math
- No clear ask — what amount, what valuation, what runway it buys

## Key metrics

- **Investor meeting conversion** — % of decks sent that yield meetings
- **Pitch-to-term-sheet ratio** — meetings to term sheets received
- **Average response time** — investor reply latency post-deck
- **Time per slide in presentation** — pacing benchmark
- **Q&A density** — investor questions per pitch (engagement signal)

## Decision questions

- Does the first slide make an investor want to read slide 2?
- Are our financial projections defensible under diligence?
- Does our team slide answer "why us, why now"?
- Is the ask explicit (amount, valuation range, use of funds)?
- Have we tailored this deck for the specific investor's thesis?
