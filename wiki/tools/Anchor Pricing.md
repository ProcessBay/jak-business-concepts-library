# Anchor Pricing

## Definition

Anchor pricing displays a higher reference price alongside the actual selling price to create a favorable comparison and make the selling price feel like a bargain. The mechanism is the cognitive **anchoring bias** — people weight the first or most prominent number they see when judging value. Pereira's classic example: showing a shirt at $19.99 crossed out, then $14.99 as the final price. The mathematical model: `PV = A − ((A − T) × X)` where A is anchor, T is target, X is the influence factor (page 223). Anchor pricing is the engine behind almost every "Was $X, Now $Y" sticker in retail.

## Key attributes

- **Reference price displayed** — original, MSRP, or competitor price next to actual
- **Anchoring bias mechanism** — first/prominent number distorts value perception
- **Lifts perceived value of target** — without changing the actual sale price
- **Mathematical model** — perceived value depends on anchor-target gap and influence factor
- **Works across categories** — retail, travel, SaaS tiers, hotel rates
- **Vulnerable to false-reference scrutiny** — regulators and consumers police fake anchors
- **Different from decoy** — anchor lifts perceived value of one option; decoy reframes choice among options

## When to use

- E-commerce product pages — strikethrough original price next to sale price
- Travel and hospitality — show "regular rate" before showing booked rate
- SaaS tier pages — show high-end plan to make mid-tier feel affordable
- Promotional periods — anchor against full price to communicate savings
- Negotiated sales — open with a high anchor to set the reference

## When NOT to use

- The anchor isn't a defensible real price (legal/regulatory exposure)
- Customers are price-savvy and verify reference prices (Amazon shoppers)
- Brands built on transparency — anchoring feels manipulative
- B2B sophisticated buyers who treat anchors as bargaining setups
- When the anchor undermines your premium positioning by signaling discounts

## Common pitfalls

- Fake or inflated anchors — legal exposure (FTC, ASA, consumer protection)
- Anchor too close to selling price — no perceived savings
- Anchor too far from selling price — appears unbelievable
- Chronic anchoring conditions customers to expect discounts
- Customers anchor on competitor instead of your anchor — reference outside your control
- Confusing anchor pricing with promotional pricing — anchor is a presentation tactic; promo is a real time-limited discount

## Key metrics

- **Conversion rate** with vs. without anchor displayed
- **Perceived savings** (anchor minus target)
- **AOV** lift attributable to anchoring
- **Anchor-to-target ratio** (Pereira's 70% influence factor example, page 223)
- **Cart abandonment rate** (anchoring can also create skepticism)

## Decision questions

- Is our anchor a real, defensible price (former MSRP, competitor list, our regular rate)?
- What anchor-target gap maximizes perceived savings without breaking believability?
- Are we anchoring against ourselves (former price) or the market (competitor)?
- Will sophisticated buyers see through this, and does that matter for our brand?
- What's the regulatory environment for reference pricing in our jurisdiction?

## Canonical examples

- **Retail electronics** — $2,000 OLED next to $1,200 LED makes the latter feel like a deal (page 223)
- **Travel websites** — strikethrough "regular" $300 hotel price next to "$150 today" rate (page 223)
- **SaaS subscription tiers** — $49 plan looks reasonable when a $99 premium plan is shown (page 223)
- **Apparel e-commerce** — $19.99 crossed out, $14.99 as the buy price (page 217)
- **Negotiated B2B sales** — open with high anchor to frame negotiation range
