# Executive Summary (Business Plan)

## Definition

The executive summary is a one-half to two-page condensation of the entire business plan that delivers the essential thesis on its own — the business concept, vision, product/differentiation, target market, marketing strategy, current and projected financials, the ask, and the team. Pereira treats it as the highest-leverage section because it determines whether the rest of the plan gets read: "It should be detailed enough for a quick glimpse into what your business is about and succinct enough that it doesn't overwhelm the reader" (p. 31). It is written **last** but read **first**.

## Key attributes

- **Length: one-half to two pages** — anything longer defeats its purpose
- **Standalone** — must communicate the full thesis even if no other section is read
- **Nine standard components** — concept, goals/vision, product/differentiation, target market, marketing strategy, current financials, projected financials, the ask, the team
- **Written last** — even though it appears first, it summarizes work done elsewhere
- **Audience-specific** — VC version emphasizes upside; bank version emphasizes repayment
- **The ask must be explicit** — amount, use, terms, timeline, expected outcome
- **The team is a section, not a footnote** — investors bet on teams

## When to use

- Front of every business plan, regardless of variant (Traditional, Lean, Nonprofit)
- As a standalone teaser sent to investors before the full plan
- As the basis for the first slide of a pitch deck
- For internal alignment — distilled mission for the team
- When responding to grant applications that require an "abstract" or "summary"

## When NOT to use

- As a substitute for the full plan when the audience expects detail
- When you have not yet completed the underlying analysis (don't summarize what doesn't exist)
- When the "ask" is unclear — wait until you know what you're asking for
- As the place to first introduce new strategic concepts (it summarizes, doesn't invent)

## Common pitfalls

- **Writing it first** — it should distill, not predict, the rest of the plan
- **Burying the ask** — investors need to see amount, use, and terms immediately
- **No financials** — current state + projection is non-negotiable
- **Vague differentiation** — "we're unique" is not differentiation; specifics are
- **Generic team description** — bullet credentials, not adjectives
- **Length creep** — two pages is the absolute ceiling, not a target
- **One version for all audiences** — VC, bank, partner, and recruit each need a different framing

## Key metrics

- **Length** — half a page to two pages maximum
- **Reader completion rate** — proxy: % of readers who request the full plan
- **Follow-up meeting rate** — % of investors who take a meeting after reading
- **Ask clarity score** — can a reader state the ask in one sentence?
- **Time to read** — should be under 5 minutes

## Decision questions

- Can the reader state our business in one sentence after reading?
- Is the ask (amount, use, timeline, expected return) impossible to miss?
- Does the financial snapshot pass a smell test for our stage?
- Is the team section credible — specific names, roles, and relevant experience?
- Have we written distinct versions for VC, bank, and partner audiences?
- If only this page were read, would the reader take the next step?

## Canonical examples

- **YC-style one-page summary** — concept, traction, ask, team
