# Influencer Marketing

## Definition

Influencer marketing engages individuals or content creators who have established credibility, expertise, and a substantial following in a specific niche, partnering with them to amplify brand messages to their audience. Pereira frames it as a trust-borrowing tactic: brands rent the influencer's authenticity and parasocial relationship rather than building their own from scratch. Done well, it bypasses traditional ad-blindness and reaches segments at a fraction of the cost of equivalent paid media. Done badly — wrong influencer, inauthentic content, no measurement — it burns budget and brand equity. The discipline now spans mega-influencers (millions of followers) to micro- and nano-influencers (under 100K, often higher engagement) and is increasingly a permanent line item in modern GTM, not a one-off campaign tactic.

## Key attributes

- **Trust-borrowing mechanism** — leverages parasocial relationships built over years
- **Five execution components** — identification/selection, relationship building, collaborative campaigns, content integration, ROI measurement
- **Audience-fit beats follower count** — micro-influencers (10K-100K) often outperform mega-influencers on conversion
- **Authenticity is the moat** — heavily scripted content destroys the format's value
- **Content integration extends reach** — repurpose influencer content across owned channels
- **Long-term relationships outperform one-offs** — recurring partnerships build credibility
- **Disclosure required** — FTC / ASA rules mandate #ad / #sponsored disclosures
- **B2B variant exists** — LinkedIn thought leaders, technical creators (devrel, YouTube tutorials)

## When to use

- Reaching young, social-native audiences who tune out traditional ads
- Launching consumer products that benefit from demonstration or styling content
- Niche categories where credible experts already aggregate the target audience
- D2C brands without legacy distribution that need rapid awareness
- Categories where authenticity and social proof drive purchase (beauty, fitness, fashion, food)
- Geographic markets where local influencers map to regional preferences

## When NOT to use

- For complex B2B sales that require multi-stakeholder consensus (use thought leadership instead)
- When you can't measure attribution (becomes a black-box budget drain)
- When influencer audience demographics don't match buyer demographics
- When the product would expose influencer to brand risk (high return rates, quality issues)
- When budget can only afford one giant campaign vs sustained smaller ones (sustained usually wins)

## Common pitfalls

- Selecting influencers by follower count alone — ignores engagement quality and audience fit
- Over-scripting content — kills authenticity, audience tunes out
- One-off transactional posts vs sustained relationships
- Failing to disclose paid partnerships (regulatory and trust risk)
- No clear KPIs upfront — can't tell after if it worked
- Choosing influencers whose values misalign with brand (PR risk)
- Ignoring micro-influencer ROI — chasing reach instead of conversion
- Treating influencer content as one-and-done vs amplifying across owned media

## Key metrics

- **Engagement rate** — likes + comments + shares / followers; micro often 3-7%, mega often <1%
- **Click-through rate** — % of viewers clicking trackable links / promo codes
- **Cost per engagement (CPE)** — campaign cost / total engagements
- **Conversion rate** — sales from influencer-attributed traffic
- **Brand mentions / earned media** — incremental conversation about the brand
- **Audience overlap** — % of influencer audience matching target persona
- **Sentiment shift** — pre/post campaign brand sentiment in social listening

## Decision questions

- Does this influencer's audience overlap meaningfully with our target persona?
- Is the influencer's content style authentic to our brand, or does the collab feel forced?
- Are we set up to measure attribution (UTMs, promo codes, dedicated landing pages)?
- Are we negotiating one-off content rights or ongoing usage / amplification rights?
- What's the influencer's track record on disclosure, controversy, and brand safety?
- Should we go broad (one mega) or deep (10 micros) for our budget?
- How will this fit into a sustained creator program vs a one-shot campaign?

## Canonical examples

- **Peloton** — celebrity partnerships and fitness influencer collaborations to expand reach and credibility (page 129-130)
- **Glossier** — built D2C beauty brand largely through micro-influencer seeding
- **Daniel Wellington** — watch brand scaled via mass micro-influencer Instagram campaigns
- **Athletic apparel (Gymshark, Alo)** — fitness creator-led brands
- **Tech YouTubers** — Marques Brownlee, Linus Tech Tips as B2C tech influence
- **B2B variant** — devrel influencers in cloud/AI categories
