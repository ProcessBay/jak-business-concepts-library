# Franchise Disclosure Document

## Definition

The Franchise Disclosure Document (FDD) is the legally mandated information document a franchisor must deliver to prospective franchisees in the United States under the Federal Trade Commission's Franchise Rule. Pereira describes the FDD as a 23-section document covering the franchisor's history, financials, litigation, fees, territory rights, training, trademarks, renewal/termination terms, and audited financial statements. The FDD must be delivered at least 14 days before the prospective franchisee signs any agreement or pays any money — the "14-day cooling-off rule." Beyond legal compliance, the FDD is the central transparency mechanism of franchising: it lets franchisees conduct due diligence, lets franchisors demonstrate credibility, and creates the evidentiary record for later disputes. Outside the US, equivalent documents exist under each jurisdiction's franchise law (Australia's Franchising Code of Conduct, Canada's provincial Arthur Wishart Act in Ontario, etc.).

## Key attributes

- **Legally mandated in the US** — FTC Franchise Rule requires it for any franchise sale
- **23 standardized sections** — franchisor background, fees, investment estimate, territory, trademarks, financing, financial statements, etc.
- **14-day rule** — must be delivered ≥14 days before signing or payment
- **Audited financial statements** — typically last 3 years
- **Item 19 — Financial Performance Representations** — optional but increasingly expected unit economics disclosure
- **Litigation and bankruptcy history** — full disclosure required
- **Receipt page** — prospect signs and dates acknowledgment of FDD receipt
- **Updates required** — annual revision; material changes must be disclosed promptly

## When to use

- Any US franchise sale (legally mandatory)
- Internationally, when local franchise law mandates equivalent disclosure
- As a due diligence tool for prospective franchisees (the central artifact to review)
- As franchisor screening — well-prepared FDD signals operational maturity
- During franchise litigation as evidentiary baseline

## When NOT to use

- N/A for the document itself — it is mandatory where franchise law applies
- Caution: do NOT rely on FDD alone for due diligence; supplement with franchisee interviews, store visits, and independent legal/financial review

## Common pitfalls

- Franchisor under-discloses material information; opens fraud/misrepresentation liability
- Item 19 financial performance reps drafted misleadingly; FTC and franchisee lawsuits follow
- 14-day rule violated (signing too soon); voids the deal and triggers penalties
- Prospect skims the FDD and misses critical territory restrictions or termination triggers
- Stale FDD (not updated for material changes) creates legal exposure
- Litigation history concealed; surfaces in due diligence and kills the deal
- International franchisor uses US-style FDD in jurisdictions with different requirements

## Key metrics

- FDD delivery compliance rate (% sales with documented ≥14-day delivery)
- Item 19 disclosure rate (vs. competitors)
- FDD-related litigation count
- Annual FDD update timeliness
- Disclosure completeness score (sections fully populated)
- Prospect-to-franchisee conversion rate post-FDD review

## Decision questions

- Have we included Item 19 financial performance representations? If not, why not?
- Is our litigation history fully and accurately disclosed?
- Has our FDD been reviewed by a franchise attorney within the past year?
- Are we documenting FDD delivery to prove 14-day compliance?
- As a prospect — what does this FDD tell me about the franchisor's history, financials, and any red flags?

## Canonical examples

- **McDonald's FDD** — model of disclosure depth and Item 19 transparency
- **Subway FDD** — historically scrutinized after franchisee profitability concerns
- **Franchise Disclosure Document Item 19** — the financial performance representation section that distinguishes mature franchisors
- **US FTC Franchise Rule** — the regulation establishing the FDD requirement (16 CFR Part 436)
- **State-level franchise registration states** — California, New York, Illinois, others requiring FDD filing in addition to delivery
