# Cold-Start Playbook

## Definition

The cold-start playbook is the set of tactics for bootstrapping a network-effect product from zero to critical mass — the threshold past which growth becomes self-reinforcing. Pereira synthesizes the strategies into a single chapter (pages 60-65) and adds a critical caveat (page 79): grow *too fast or wrong* and the network breaks. The core tactics: first-mover advantage, lock-in, complementary products, platform layering, subsidizing adoption, building community, exploiting tipping points, increasing economies of scale on both sides, partnering with complements, and operational excellence. Across all of them, the throughline is the same — *deliberately seed a defined cluster (often the hard side first) until self-reinforcement begins, then expand cluster by cluster*. The Facebook Harvard-only launch, eBay's auctions, Airbnb's hosts-first focus, and Uber's per-city driver subsidies are all instances of the same pattern.

## Key attributes

- **Per-cluster seeding** — pick a defined market, segment, or community; densify before expanding
- **Hard-side first** — identify and prioritize the harder-to-acquire side (drivers for Uber, hosts for Airbnb)
- **Throttling for quality** — Facebook's edu-only gate; protects perceived value during latency
- **Subsidy capital** — discounts, free credits, paid acquisition explicitly bridging to critical mass
- **Community as catalyst** — Airbnb hosts community, eBay collector hobbyists, both cited
- **Complement / platform layering** — App stores, Xbox game catalog as second-order acquisition magnets
- **First-mover advantage** — costly but defensible (eBay, Pereira's example, never overtaken by Amazon/Yahoo)
- **Tipping-point exploitation** — word-of-mouth campaigns timed to the inflection
- **Operational excellence** — Netflix-style retention investment so the virtuous cycle isn't broken by failure
- **Survival caveat** — grow wrong (too fast, wrong segment, low-quality power-users) and the network breaks

## When to use

- Launching any network-effect product from zero
- Entering a new geography for a local-NE product (per-city cold start)
- Pivoting an existing network into a new segment
- Diagnosing why a network is stuck (often: insufficient seed density or expanded too early)

## When NOT to use

- Pre-PMF products — fix the product first; cold-start tactics don't fix bad value props
- Pure single-user products (no network to bootstrap)
- Categories where regulatory or structural fragmentation prevents critical-mass formation
- When subsidy capital is unavailable and product alone is insufficient to bridge the gap

## Common pitfalls

- **Spraying instead of densifying** — launching nationally before any city crosses threshold
- **Wrong-side subsidy** — subsidizing the easy side (riders) when the hard side (drivers) is the bottleneck
- **Over-throttling** — gates so tight that momentum dies (Google+ vs Facebook)
- **Power-seller capture** — letting a few "power sellers" dominate, restricting inventory variety (Pereira's failure mode, page 79)
- **Running out of capital pre-threshold** — runway mismatched to time-to-critical-mass
- **Treating subsidy as long-term price** — markets later resist removing the subsidy
- **Confusing virality with NE-driven growth** — viral spikes don't equal sustained network value

## Key metrics

- **Time to critical mass per cluster** — runway anchor
- **Seed cluster density** — actual / target liquidity in the chosen cluster
- **Subsidy burn / market** — capital required per market to bridge
- **Hard-side CAC** — track separately from easy-side
- **Match rate** in the seeded cluster — leading indicator of self-reinforcement
- **Organic share of new users** — should rise as threshold is crossed
- **Cohort retention in seed cluster** — confirms NE is real, not subsidy-induced

## Decision questions

- Which cluster (city, school, segment) are we seeding first, and why?
- Which is the hard side, and what tactic addresses it (subsidy, community, partnership)?
- What is the per-cluster critical-mass threshold, and what runway is required to reach it?
- What's our throttling policy — open access or gated?
- What is the kill criterion for a cluster that fails to reach threshold on schedule?
- How do we avoid the "grow too fast / wrong way" failure mode (page 79)?

## Canonical examples

- **eBay** — first-mover auctions; Amazon and Yahoo gave up after 10 years (page 61)
- **Facebook Harvard → edu → world** — textbook throttled cluster seeding (page 56)
- **Airbnb** — host-first seeding + community building + word-of-mouth tipping (pages 63, 77)
- **Uber / Lyft** — per-city driver-side subsidy spend (page 30-31, 78)
- **Microsoft Xbox** — game catalog as complement layer (page 64)
- **Amazon + UPS / FedEx** — partner-with-complement strategy (page 64)
- **YouTube free in 2005 → Premium in 2014** — market-share-first pricing (page 21)
- **WhatsApp free SMS substitute** — subsidize-adoption with eliminated fee (page 78)
- **Netflix** — operational excellence as defense (page 65)
