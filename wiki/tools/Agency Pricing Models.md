# Agency Pricing Models

## Definition

The five pricing structures a digital marketing agency can deploy — hourly, fixed project fees, monthly retainers, performance-based, and tiered packages — usually in combination rather than alone. Pereira's pricing chapter (pages 85–89) frames pricing design as the intersection of three constraints: (1) the agency's value proposition (what differentiates you), (2) cost and target margin (the floor below which delivery is unprofitable), and (3) industry benchmarks (the price the market will currently bear). The right pricing model is rarely a single answer — most mature agencies mix retainers with project fees and tiered packages, layering performance bonuses where attribution allows.

## Key attributes

- **Five primary structures** — Hourly, Fixed Project, Retainer, Performance-Based, Tiered Packages (Basic/Standard/Premium)
- **Hybrid is the norm** — pure single-model agencies are rare
- **Floor-and-ceiling logic** — costs set the floor; perceived value sets the ceiling
- **Tiered packaging** — Basic / Standard / Premium accommodates diverse client budgets (page 87)
- **Flexible payment terms** — monthly, quarterly, or annual billing; multiple payment methods (page 87)
- **Continual optimization** — pricing is reviewed regularly against feedback and market shifts (page 87)
- **Pricing IS positioning** — the structure signals what kind of agency you are

## When to use

- Setting up a new agency and choosing the default commercial model
- Repricing the existing book after a margin review
- Designing new service tiers or productized offerings
- Negotiating with a client whose default purchasing pattern doesn't fit your model
- Diagnosing why some clients are profitable and others bleed margin

## When NOT to use

- As a substitute for value-discovery — pricing without understanding what the client values commoditizes you
- When you copy competitor pricing without their cost structure
- Rigid pricing models in markets that demand customization (e.g., government tenders in KSA)
- When you have no cost data — you cannot price what you cannot measure

## Common pitfalls

- Pricing only against competitors, ignoring your own cost-of-delivery
- Underpricing initially "to get the logo" — never raised, kills lifetime margin
- Hourly pricing on highly leveraged work (you punish your own efficiency)
- Retainers without scope-change procedures — silent margin leakage
- Performance pricing on channels you don't actually control
- Tier sprawl — too many packages confuses clients and operations
- Treating pricing as one-time decision instead of a quarterly review

## Key metrics

- **Gross Profit Margin** by service and by pricing structure (page 94)
- **Operating Profit Margin**
- **Average Revenue Per Client** by tier
- **Effective Hourly Rate** — revenue ÷ hours, even if not billed hourly
- **Discount %** vs. rack rate
- **Win Rate** by pricing structure
- **Client Profitability Ranking** (top 20% vs. bottom 20%)

## Decision questions

- What's the cost-of-delivery floor for each service we offer?
- Where is our pricing power coming from — niche expertise, brand, or just relationships?
- Are our retainers priced for senior attention or only execution hours?
- Which clients are losing us money, and why are we still serving them?
- When did we last raise rates, and what stopped us from raising them sooner?

## Canonical examples

- **Hourly** — junior boutiques, consultancies billing $150–$500/hr
- **Fixed project** — web builds, brand audits, campaign launches
- **Monthly retainers** — WebFX, Neil Patel Digital, Digital Silk recurring engagements (pages 113–118)
- **Performance-based** — affiliate / CRO / paid-media specialists
- **Tiered packages** — Basic / Standard / Premium productized offerings (page 87)
- **KSA government tenders** — Memac Ogilvy and M&C Saatchi Riyadh price large RFPs as fixed-fee projects with annual extension options
- **Mid-tier Saudi agencies** — Sociable, Tribal MENA mix Basic / Standard / Premium social-media packages priced in SAR for SMEs
