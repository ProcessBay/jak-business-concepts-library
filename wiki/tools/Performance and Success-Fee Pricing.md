# Performance and Success-Fee Pricing

## Definition

Performance pricing ties some or all of the fee to a measurable outcome — closing a deal, hitting a revenue target, recruiting a hire, winning a case, achieving documented cost savings. The provider takes on outcome risk in exchange for upside above what hourly or fixed-bid would yield. Pereira lists "performance-based fees" as one of three engagement models McKinsey explicitly offers (page 121), alongside project-based and retainer-based, and frames it as a flexibility tool: it lets clients with "budget limitations" engage on outcomes rather than effort. The model only works when (a) the outcome is unambiguously measurable, (b) attribution to the provider's work is defensible, and (c) the upside is large enough to compensate for engagement losses on the failures.

## Key attributes

- **Outcome-contingent fee** — payment triggered by a defined event (deal closed, hire placed, savings verified)
- **Pure or hybrid forms** — 100% contingent, or retainer + success-fee hybrid that protects downside
- **Provider takes outcome risk** — failed engagements pay nothing or just cover cost
- **Demands sharp definition of "success"** — disputes arise from ambiguous trigger criteria
- **Attribution must be defensible** — was it our work, or would the outcome have happened anyway?
- **Higher expected value than fixed-bid when leverage is real** — top recruiters earn 20-30% of first-year comp, far above hourly
- **Concentration risk** — one big win can outweigh ten losses, making revenue lumpy
- **Selection effect** — providers can only offer this on engagements they expect to win

## When to use

- The outcome is binary or numerically clear (deal closed, target hit, hire placed)
- The provider has a credible edge that materially improves the success probability
- Client refuses to pay full price upfront but is happy to share upside
- The fee, if won, is large enough that the math works across portfolio of attempts
- Existing market norm uses this model (M&A, contingent recruiting, contingent litigation)

## When NOT to use

- Outcome attribution is muddy (the provider can't credibly claim the win was theirs)
- The "success" definition can be gamed by the client to avoid paying
- The provider lacks the portfolio breadth to absorb individual failures
- Client's outcome depends heavily on client-side execution outside provider control
- Engagement is short — overhead of defining triggers exceeds the deal

## Common pitfalls

- **Loose trigger language** — "successful implementation" without a measurable definition invites payment disputes
- **No attribution clause** — client achieves the outcome via parallel efforts and refuses to pay
- **Underpricing the success premium** — if downside is "zero," the upside should be 3-5x hourly equivalent, not 1.2x
- **No cost recovery floor** — pure contingency on long engagements can bankrupt the provider mid-delivery
- **Cherry-picking distortion** — the firm only takes easy wins, building a portfolio that overstates skill
- **Misaligned incentives** — provider optimizes for the trigger event, not the client's broader interest
- **Stripe / NRR-style metrics gameable** — performance tied to "revenue lift" can be gamed by short-term tactics that erode long-term value
- **Concentration risk underestimated** — one bad year can wipe out the cumulative gains of three good ones

## Key metrics

- **Hit rate / success rate** — % of engagements where the trigger fires
- **Expected value per engagement** — (fee × hit rate) − (delivery cost × all engagements)
- **Realized fee-to-value ratio** — provider fee ÷ client value created (typically 5-30%)
- **Downside coverage** — % of engagement cost covered by retainer or minimum fee
- **Time-to-trigger** — average days between engagement start and trigger event
- **Portfolio variance** — standard deviation of monthly revenue (will be high)

## Decision questions

- Is "success" defined sharply enough that we can't be talked out of payment?
- What's our honest hit rate on this kind of work — does the expected-value math work?
- Do we have a retainer or floor to cover delivery cost if the trigger never fires?
- Is attribution defensible — can we prove the outcome was ours?
- Can our cash flow absorb the lumpiness, or do we need a hybrid (retainer + success-fee)?

## Canonical examples

- **McKinsey performance-based engagements** — explicitly offered as one of three engagement models (page 121)
- **M&A investment banking advisory** — % of deal value (typically 1-2% on large deals, 3-5% on mid-market)
- **Contingent executive search** — 20-33% of first-year compensation, paid only on successful hire
- **Litigation contingency fees** — 25-40% of recovered damages in plaintiff-side US legal work
- **Sales consulting on commission** — % of incremental revenue attributable to the engagement
- **Cost-savings consulting (gain-share)** — % of verified annualized savings, capped or uncapped
- **PR / influencer campaigns on CPA** — payment per acquired customer
