# Building a Balanced Scorecard

## Definition

Building a Balanced Scorecard is the structured 8-step process Kaplan and Norton documented for going from "we want a BSC" to "we have a live, cascaded scorecard." Pereira presents two complementary process views: the 8-step "typical project profile" (Preparation → 3 interview/workshop rounds → Implementation → Periodic Reviews, pages 25–27) and a 9-step usage cycle (Assessment → Strategy → Strategic Objectives → Strategy Mapping → Performance Measures → Strategic Initiatives → Performance Analysis → Alignment → Evaluation, pages 37–40). Both versions share the same core: heavy upfront executive engagement, iterative drafting through interviews and workshops, then continuous review.

## Key attributes

- **Eight project steps** — Preparation, 1st Interviews, 1st Workshop, 2nd Interviews, 2nd Workshop, 3rd Workshop, Implementation, Periodic Reviews (page 25–27)
- **Nine usage steps** — Assessment, Strategy, Strategic Objectives, Strategy Mapping, Performance Measures, Strategic Initiatives, Performance Analysis, Alignment, Evaluation (page 37–40)
- **Heavy executive interview load** — facilitator works 90 minutes with each senior manager in round 1 (page 26)
- **Three workshops, not one** — successive workshops drive convergence: vision → measures → stretch targets → implementation plan
- **Bottom-up build, top-down review** — Pereira: start at Learning & Growth, work up to Financial (page 34)
- **Periodic review cadence** — "blue book" reviewed every 3–4 months; strategic refresh annually (page 27)
- **Facilitator role is structural** — synthesizes interview data, drafts scorecard, owns the workshop arc (page 26)
- **Implementation is its own project** — connects KPIs to databases and IT systems; not just a strategy exercise (page 27)

## When to use

- Standing up a BSC for the first time in an organization or business unit
- Refreshing a stale BSC after a strategy shift, M&A, or leadership change
- When the team needs a structured cadence to force executive convergence on strategy
- When the BSC will be the formal management system, not an experimental dashboard

## When NOT to use

- For a one-team dashboard (too heavyweight; use OKRs or a simple KPI tree)
- When executive calendars cannot absorb the workshop load
- Without a facilitator (internal or external) who owns synthesis between sessions
- If the strategy itself is undefined — Pereira: Assessment step requires existing mission/vision/values to validate (page 37)

## Common pitfalls

- **Skipping the interview rounds** — workshops without prep produce shallow consensus
- **One workshop instead of three** — Pereira's process explicitly uses three to drive convergence and stretch (page 26–27)
- **No facilitator** — without someone synthesizing between sessions, scorecard drifts
- **Implementation step underestimated** — wiring KPIs to data systems and IT is its own project (page 27)
- **No periodic review** — without the 3–4 month "blue book" cadence, the scorecard dies (page 27)
- **Treating Assessment as optional** — skipping the current-state diagnosis means the scorecard does not address real problems (page 37)
- **Building scorecard without strategic themes** — themes (operational excellence, customer intimacy) are how the strategy clusters objectives (page 38)
- **No Strategic Initiatives** — defining KPIs without the projects that move them produces measurement theater (page 39)

## Key metrics

- **Time to first scorecard** — weeks from kickoff to signed-off v1 (typical: 12–16 weeks)
- **Executive engagement hours** — total hours of senior-manager time invested
- **Strategic objective count** — should land at 10–20 for corporate level (FranklinCovey: 10+ goals = none achieved, page 33)
- **KPI count** — should land at 15–25 total (1–2 per objective, mix of leading and lagging)
- **Review cadence compliance** — % of scheduled 3–4 month reviews actually held

## Decision questions

- Who is the executive sponsor, and will they personally attend all three workshops?
- Who is our facilitator (internal vs external), and what is their authority?
- What is our Assessment input — do we have validated mission/vision/values/market analysis?
- What 3–4 strategic themes will organize our objectives?
- Are we resourcing the Implementation step (IT integration) as its own workstream?
- What is our quarterly review cadence and who runs it?
