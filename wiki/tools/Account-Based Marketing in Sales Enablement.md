# Account-Based Marketing in Sales Enablement

## Definition

Account-Based Marketing (ABM) inside a sales enablement context is the joint sales-marketing motion that focuses resources on a defined list of high-value target accounts and treats each as a market of one. Pereira frames ABM as a sales-marketing alignment engine: rather than marketing generating broad leads and sales filtering them, both teams collaborate from list-build through personalized engagement to deal close. The strategic value comes from precision targeting (fewer, better accounts), personalized engagement (messaging tailored to each account's pain points and stakeholder map), deeper relationships (sustained multi-touch over months), tighter sales-marketing collaboration (shared accountability for named accounts), data-driven iteration via integrated CRM/MAP stack, and clearer ROI measurement (small denominator makes attribution tractable). It is uniquely suited to complex B2B sales with multiple stakeholders and long cycles, where mass-marketing logic breaks down.

## Key attributes

- **Precision targeting** — defined named-account list, not lead-volume goals
- **Collaborative strategy** — joint sales-marketing planning and execution
- **Personalized engagement** — messaging tailored to each account's context
- **Deep relationship investment** — sustained, multi-touch, multi-stakeholder
- **Aligned with sales enablement platforms** — CRM and MAP integration mandatory
- **Data-driven iteration** — account engagement data drives strategy refinement
- **Measurable ROI** — small target list enables clean attribution
- **Adapted to complex cycles** — works where many stakeholders, long sales cycles
- **Balance of automation and personalization** — scale without losing the human touch

## When to use

- B2B enterprise or upper-mid-market with multi-stakeholder buying committees
- Complex sales cycles (3+ months) where relationship investment pays off
- High average contract value where named-account focus is economically justified
- When sales has clear ICP and can name the 50-500 target accounts
- When sales-marketing alignment is mature enough for joint accountability
- When mass marketing is producing low-quality leads sales rejects
- For land-and-expand motions where account depth drives long-term value

## When NOT to use

- SMB / transactional sales with low ACV — economics don't support per-account investment
- When you don't actually know your ICP or target account list is fuzzy
- When sales and marketing operate in silos (ABM amplifies dysfunction)
- When CRM data is too poor to support account-level intelligence
- As marketing-only initiative without sales co-ownership
- When you can't dedicate AE capacity to named-account development

## Common pitfalls

- "ABM" as rebranded broad-based marketing with a target list bolted on
- Sales and marketing never genuinely co-own accounts — old silos persist
- Target account list set once and never refreshed
- Personalization that's superficial (insert {firstName}) rather than account-context-aware
- Over-automation losing the human touch ABM is supposed to deliver
- Measuring ABM with broad-based marketing metrics (MQL volume) instead of account engagement
- No tiering — treating all 500 accounts identically instead of 1:1, 1:few, 1:many
- Leadership impatience — ABM payback often 6-12 months, not quarterly

## Key metrics

- **Account engagement score** — composite of touches, content engagement, meeting attendance per account
- **Target account coverage** — % of named accounts with active engagement
- **ABM pipeline contribution** — pipeline and revenue from target accounts vs. non-target
- **Target account win rate** — typically 30-50% higher than non-target baseline
- **Deal size uplift** — ABM deals tend to land larger than broad-based
- **Sales cycle length on ABM accounts** — often shorter due to multi-stakeholder warmth
- **Account stakeholder coverage** — # contacts engaged per account (buying committee depth)

## Decision questions

- Is our ICP defined precisely enough to name the target account list?
- Do sales and marketing genuinely co-own accounts, or is it a handoff?
- Does our tech stack (CRM + MAP + intent + content) support account-level personalization?
- Are we tiering accounts (1:1 vs. 1:few vs. 1:many) by potential value?
- How are we measuring ABM — at account level, not lead level?
- Are we patient enough? ABM payback is 6-12 months, not weeks.
- Do our AEs have the capacity to invest in named accounts, or are they pipeline-chasing?
