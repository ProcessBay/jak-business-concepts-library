# Landing Page MVP

## Definition

A Landing Page MVP is a low-fidelity validation tactic where you publish a single web page that describes the offer, lists benefits, and includes a call-to-action (typically email signup or pre-order) — *before* the product exists. The hypothesis under test is whether your target audience finds the offer compelling enough to take a measurable action. Pereira describes it as a variant of the Fake Door approach without the deceptive "coming soon" framing: "you provide visitors to your site with a variety of options to choose from and the benefits of such options." It is cheap, fast, and produces real conversion data — but converts visitors only into signups, not customers.

## Key attributes

- **Single page, real traffic** — typically paired with ads to drive segmented visitors
- **Clear value proposition** — must communicate the offer in seconds
- **One measurable action** — usually email capture or pre-order
- **No product required** — you can ship a landing page in hours
- **Generates a waitlist asset** — the emails become your launch list if you build
- **Connects to target audience** — gathers feedback about likes/dislikes pre-build
- **Limitation: signup intent ≠ purchase intent** — soft signal compared to a pre-order

## When to use

- Pre-build validation of whether the offer resonates at all
- Testing multiple value propositions or audience segments (A/B variants)
- Before investing in any product engineering
- When you have a clear pitch but no audience yet
- Building an early waitlist to launch into
- Validating which benefit to lead with in eventual marketing

## When NOT to use

- When you need to validate willingness to PAY (use pre-order MVP instead)
- For products where the experience can't be conveyed in copy/imagery alone
- When you can't drive segmented traffic (random visitors give noisy signal)
- Categories where signup intent is unrelated to purchase intent (free email harvesting)
- When you need product feedback (landing pages don't give that)

## Common pitfalls

- **Vanity-metric pollution** — celebrating visits/likes instead of conversion
- **Untargeted traffic** — visitors who don't match the target audience produce noise
- **Overpromising in copy** — sets up disappointment when the real product ships
- **No A/B testing** — running one version means you can't disentangle "the offer" from "this copy"
- **Conflating sign-ups with revenue** — the most common landing-page-MVP failure
- **Not tagging signups by acquisition source** — you can't tell which channel works
- **No follow-up plan** — you collect 500 emails and never email them

## Key metrics

- **Conversion rate** — % of visitors who take the CTA
- **Email signups** — absolute count, segmented by source
- **Click-through rate on the CTA**
- **Cost per signup (CAC for the waitlist)**
- **Source-segmented conversion** — which audience cares most
- **Bounce rate / time on page** — engagement signal

## Decision questions

- What single, falsifiable hypothesis does this page test?
- What conversion rate, in advance, would we call a "yes"?
- Are we driving segmented, target-matched traffic — or anyone with a click?
- Does our CTA capture intent that's meaningfully correlated with future purchase?
- What's our plan for the signups if the page validates?

## Canonical examples

- **Buffer** — Joel Gascoigne's two-page landing-page MVP tested whether people would consider using the app. He tweeted the link, got email/Twitter feedback, considered the idea "validated," then built. Now a major social-media SaaS (page 80)
- **Dropbox** (paired with explainer video) — used a landing page with email capture to gather 70,000 signups in one day from the explainer video (pages 17, 81)
- Many YC-era SaaS startups — landing-page-first launch became standard practice
