# Starbursting

## Definition

Starbursting is a **question-focused** brainstorming approach: instead of generating answers or solutions, the team generates questions across six dimensions — Who, What, When, Where, Why, and How. The idea is drawn as a six-point star with the topic at the center and one question-type per point. Pereira frames it as a structured way to "cover all bases" before committing to a decision or design — the goal is to surface every important question an idea raises, then answer them deliberately. It works as the inverse of typical brainstorming: ideas die because we answer the wrong questions, so this method makes question-quality the deliverable. The classic recommendation is at least three questions per point, often producing dozens of questions for a single concept.

## Key attributes

- **Six interrogatives** — Who, What, When, Where, Why, How
- **Questions, not answers** — generation phase prohibits answers
- **Star structure** — central topic, six points, multiple questions per point
- **Two-phase** — generate questions first, then answer them
- **Coverage tool** — designed to surface what you haven't thought about
- **Scales with concept complexity** — bigger ideas, more questions per point
- **Pre-commitment discipline** — explicitly NOT an action plan generator

## When to use

- Evaluating a new product or service idea before commitment
- Pre-mortem for projects ("what should we know before we start?")
- Decision-making between multiple options (apply to each option)
- Sales discovery — surface the right questions to ask the prospect
- After a brainstorming session, to stress-test the top ideas
- Onboarding new team members onto a complex project

## When NOT to use

- When you already have clear answers and need to execute
- For root-cause analysis (use Five Whys or Fishbone)
- When the team is question-averse or fatigued
- For pure divergent ideation (this is a focusing/structuring tool)

## Common pitfalls

- Drifting into answer-mode during the generation phase
- Producing one question per point instead of pushing for 3+
- Skipping the "Why" point (often the richest)
- Stopping at the question list and never producing the answers
- Treating answers as decisions rather than as inputs to a decision

## Key metrics

- **Questions per point** (target minimum 3; richer ideas should produce 5-10)
- **Total questions generated** (typical: 20-50)
- **Answered vs unanswered** (gaps highlight where you need research)
- **New questions surfaced** (vs ones the team already had)

## Decision questions

- What is the single central concept we're starbursting?
- Are we in question-mode or answer-mode? (don't mix)
- Which point is producing the fewest questions, and why?
- Which unanswered questions are deal-breakers vs nice-to-knows?

## Canonical examples

- **New product evaluation** — Who's the market? What does it offer? Where will we make it? When to publish? Why will people buy? How will we design production? (page 67)
- **Option selection** — apply the six questions to each candidate option, compare (page 67)
- **Problem-solving prelude** — Who's affected? What's the problem? Where's it occur? When must we fix this? Why's it happening? What's the solution? (page 68)
- **Idea generation extension** — bolted onto a brainstorming session to test the top ideas (page 67)
