# 70% Rule (House Flipping)

## Definition

The 70% rule is a back-of-the-envelope acquisition guardrail used by house flippers and BRRRR investors: **never pay more than 70% of the property's After Repair Value (ARV) minus the cost of repairs**. Formally: `Max Purchase Price = (ARV × 0.70) − Rehab Cost`. The 30% buffer is engineered to absorb holding costs (mortgage, taxes, insurance, utilities, HOA), closing costs on both ends, realtor commissions on resale, contingency for surprises, and the investor's target gross profit. Pereira illustrates: ARV $180k, rehab $35k → max offer = ($180k × 0.70) − $35k = $91k. He's careful to label it a guideline, not a law — in seller's markets investors may stretch to 80–85% to compete; in buyer's markets they may bid below 70% to widen the margin. The rule's power is psychological as much as mathematical: it gives the flipper an objective stop-loss before emotion takes over in negotiation.

## Key attributes

- **Formula** — `Max Purchase Price = (ARV × 0.70) − Rehab Cost`
- **30% buffer absorbs** — holding costs, double closing costs, resale commission, contingency, profit
- **ARV-anchored** — the model is only as good as the ARV estimate
- **Guideline, not law** — bend to 80–85% in seller's markets; tighten below 70% in buyer's markets
- **Pre-negotiation discipline tool** — sets a walk-away price before emotional anchoring
- **Same logic as BRRRR's Buy step** — value-add investors share the heuristic
- **Conservative by design** — protects against rehab overruns and DOM surprises

## When to use

- Initial acquisition screening for fix-and-flip or BRRRR deals
- Quick triage of inbound wholesale assignments
- Auction-bidding limits
- Negotiation anchor with motivated sellers
- New-investor protection against overpaying

## When NOT to use

- Hot seller's market where 70% offers get rejected universally — adjust to 75–85%
- Luxury / unique properties where comp-based ARV is unreliable
- Markets with very low rehab costs where the buffer is excessive
- Pure buy-and-hold deals where rental cash flow drives the buy, not resale margin
- Sub-rehab cosmetic deals (wholetail) where the buffer may be tightened

## Common pitfalls

- **Garbage in / garbage out** — wrong ARV → wrong max price
- **Underestimating rehab cost** — eats the 30% buffer
- **Anchoring on listing price instead of ARV** — defeats the rule's purpose
- **Treating it as a hard floor instead of ceiling** — paying 70% when 60% was available
- **Ignoring local DOM** — slow markets need bigger buffers (consider 65% or 60%)
- **Not stress-testing the ARV** — pulling one comp instead of three to five

## Key metrics

- **ARV** — After Repair Value; the anchor input
- **Rehab cost estimate** — should include 10–20% contingency
- **Max purchase price** — output of the formula
- **Actual gross profit per flip** — should land in the 15–25% of ARV range when rule is followed
- **Holding cost per month** — 2–3% of purchase price typical
- **DOM (Days on Market)** — longer = bigger required buffer

## Decision questions

- How confident am I in the ARV — three comps? five? recent?
- Have I padded the rehab estimate by at least 10–20%?
- Is the local market hot enough that 70% will lose every bid?
- What's my walk-away price, and am I disciplined enough to walk?
- Should I tighten to 65% given my contractor's track record on overruns?

## Canonical examples

- **Pereira's $180k ARV example** — max offer $91k after $35k rehab (page 66, 86)
- **Pereira's $130k ARV example** — max offer ~$76k after $15k rehab
- **BiggerPockets default** — the 70% rule is the most-cited acquisition heuristic in US flipper culture
- **Auction flipping playbooks** — many use the rule to set absolute bid ceilings before walking the property
- **Saudi/MENA**: the rule transfers conceptually but ARV estimation is harder due to thin comparable-sales data; investors typically use a wider 60–65% rule when applied in Riyadh / Jeddah secondary markets
