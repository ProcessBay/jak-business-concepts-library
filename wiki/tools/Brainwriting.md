# Brainwriting

## Definition

Brainwriting is a quiet-brainstorming technique in which group members write down their ideas independently rather than calling them out. Pereira positions it as the canonical alternative to classic verbal brainstorming for groups with mixed personality types — especially when introverts are present, when social anxiety would suppress contribution, or when the goal is "unadulterated" ideas free from interruption and group-think pressure. The general principle is to **separate idea generation from discussion**. Three established variants exist: **6-3-5 brainwriting** (six people write three ideas in five minutes, then pass; six rounds yield 108 ideas), **collaborative brainwriting** (an open board or wall accepts contributions over days/weeks), and **remote brainwriting** (the same on a virtual shared surface like Google Sheets or a blog).

## Key attributes

- **Silent generation** — no verbal interruption during the writing phase
- **Equalizes participation** — quiet members produce as many ideas as loud ones
- **Removes social anxiety** — no hand-raising, no speaking pressure
- **Time-boxed** — short rounds (3-10 min) maintain urgency and creativity
- **Sequential build (in 6-3-5)** — each person extends prior ideas on the same sheet
- **Three variants** — 6-3-5 (structured), collaborative (open board), remote (virtual)
- **Quantity-friendly** — 6-3-5 reliably produces 108 ideas in 30 minutes

## When to use

- Groups with introverted, anxious, or junior members whose ideas would be lost in verbal brainstorming
- When you want unbiased ideas free from groupthink
- When the topic is sensitive and people won't speak openly
- Large groups (6-3-5 scales beautifully)
- Remote or asynchronous teams (collaborative + remote variants)
- When previous verbal sessions produced few ideas from few people

## When NOT to use

- When verbal energy and group dynamics are exactly what you want (e.g. rapid ideation)
- When the team is small and already extroverted
- When the topic requires extended discussion to define (do that first, then brainwrite)
- When immediate action is needed and you have no time for parallel writing

## Common pitfalls

- Skipping the rules briefing — participants write narratives instead of ideas
- Not enforcing the time box — momentum collapses
- Failing to consolidate at the end — ideas die on individual sheets
- Allowing discussion during the write phase (defeats the purpose)
- Over-engineering the rules (keep parameters clear and minimal)

## Key metrics

- **Total ideas generated** (108 is the 6-3-5 benchmark)
- **Ideas per participant** (proxy for participation equality)
- **Distinct categories of ideas** (diversity measure)
- **Adoption rate** (% of ideas that survive to evaluation)

## Decision questions

- Which variant fits the group — 6-3-5 (structured), collaborative (open), or remote (virtual)?
- Is the prompt narrow enough to produce concrete ideas in 5 minutes?
- Who consolidates the sheets, and when?
- Do we follow with a verbal discussion or a voting step?

## Canonical examples

- **6-3-5 Method** — 6 people × 3 ideas × 5 minutes × 6 rounds = 108 ideas (page 87)
- **Collaborative brainwriting** — bulletin board on the wall, team adds ideas over a week (page 87)
- **Remote brainwriting** — shared Google Sheet or blog, contributions on each person's schedule (page 88)
- **Workshop for an introverted team** — replaces verbal brainstorming and surfaces normally-silent voices (page 85)
