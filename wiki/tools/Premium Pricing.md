# Premium Pricing

## Definition

Premium pricing charges a deliberately high price to position a product as high-end, exploiting the consumer association between high price and high quality. Unlike price skimming (which is a staged price decay), premium pricing is a sustained positioning — Rolex doesn't drop the price of a Submariner over time. Pereira's framing: people often buy on price, believing high price implies high quality, and premium brands engineer their pricing to reinforce that belief. The formula is `Premium Price = Cost + (Cost × Markup %)` with markups often 100%+ on cost.

## Key attributes

- **Sustained high price** — not a launch tactic, a permanent position
- **Price signals quality** — exploits the price-quality heuristic
- **Higher gross margin** — price exceeds cost significantly
- **Brand-equity dependent** — requires the brand story to support the price
- **Smaller market, higher value per customer**
- **Recession-vulnerable** — luxury cuts first when budgets tighten
- **Requires constant innovation** — to keep justifying the premium

## When to use

- Luxury, fashion, prestige categories where status signals matter
- Categories where quality cannot easily be objectively verified (price becomes the proxy)
- Products with genuine, defensible differentiation that supports premium claims
- Brands willing to invest in continuous brand building and innovation
- Markets with a viable premium segment willing to pay for status / quality

## When NOT to use

- Commodity categories where price is the primary buying criterion
- Brands without the equity to support a premium claim (price feels unjustified)
- Recession-prone economies where luxury demand is fragile
- Markets where competitors can imitate the product and undercut your premium
- When you don't have the cost structure or operations to maintain premium quality

## Common pitfalls

- Pricing premium without delivering premium experience (consumer feels cheated)
- Failing to invest in brand reinforcement (premium positioning decays without it)
- Discounting your premium product (destroys the premium signal)
- Mistaking expensive for premium (high price alone doesn't make you Rolex)
- Allowing imitators to dilute brand equity through similar-looking products
- Underestimating recession exposure

## Key metrics

- **Gross margin** (premium pricing typically delivers 60%+ gross margin)
- **Brand equity** (NPS, brand value indices, awareness/consideration)
- **ASP** (average selling price) vs. category benchmark
- **Market share within premium segment** (not total category)
- **Innovation cadence** (releases, redesigns)

## Decision questions

- Does our brand have the equity to support a premium price?
- Can customers verify quality independently, or does price serve as the quality signal?
- Are we willing to invest perpetually in brand and innovation to justify the premium?
- How exposed are we to recession-driven luxury cuts?
- What happens if competitors imitate our product at half the price?

## Canonical examples

- **Rolex** — watches priced to signal status, not measured against function (page 128)
- **Apple iPhone, Mac** — premium pricing supported by brand, ecosystem, design (page 129)
- **Tesla** — premium EV positioning via sustainability, tech, performance (page 129)
- **Louis Vuitton** — exclusivity, heritage, handcraft justify extreme premium (page 129)
- **Bentley vs. Honda** — both get you from A to B; Bentley charges 10× for the statement (page 128)
