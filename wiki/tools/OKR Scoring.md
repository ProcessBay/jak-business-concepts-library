# OKR Scoring

## Definition

**OKR Scoring** is the cycle-end practice of evaluating each Key Result on a **0.0 to 1.0 scale**, with the Objective's score typically taken as the average of its KR scores. The convention — popularized by Google and reflected throughout Pereira's text (pages 58-60, 69, 109) — is that **0.7 is "good" for aspirational OKRs** and **1.0 is expected for committed OKRs**. Scores below 0.4 trigger investigation; scores consistently at 1.0 on aspirational OKRs signal sandbagging. Scoring is not the goal of OKRs — *learning* is. The score is a prompt for a retrospective conversation: what worked, what didn't, what to carry forward.

## Key attributes

- **0.0-1.0 continuous scale** — not pass/fail; partial credit reflects partial progress
- **Aspirational target: 0.6-0.7** — anything higher means insufficient stretch
- **Committed target: 1.0** — anything lower is an execution miss to investigate
- **Objective score = avg of KR scores** — simple aggregation; some orgs weight KRs
- **Scored at cycle end** — typically last week of the quarter
- **Decoupled from compensation** at high-functioning OKR cultures (Google, Netflix; pages 105-106)
- **Triggers retrospective** — score is the input to the learning conversation, not the output

## When to use

- At every cycle close — non-negotiable; OKRs without scoring become wish lists
- Mid-cycle informal scoring during check-ins (KR "confidence" 1-10 or 0.0-1.0) — gives early signal
- In retrospectives — comparing predicted vs actual scores reveals planning bias
- When making the case for resource shifts mid-cycle (low-confidence KRs may need help or sunset)

## When NOT to use

- Don't use scores as the primary input to performance reviews — destroys honest scoring
- Don't tie scores to bonuses — produces sandbagging within one cycle
- Don't score in isolation — without the retrospective conversation, the number is meaningless
- Don't grade pass/fail — loses signal in the middle of the distribution

## Common pitfalls

- **Inflated scoring** — scoring 0.9 on a half-done KR to look good
- **Sandbagged targets** — KRs set low enough to guarantee 1.0
- **Score-and-forget** — no retrospective, no carried learning
- **Performance-review weaponization** — managers using OKR scores in evaluations, killing honest scoring
- **Inconsistent measurement** — same KR scored differently by different teams
- **Mid-cycle moving the goalposts** — adjusting KR targets to make the score look better

## Key metrics

- **KR scoring average** — across all KRs in cycle
- **Objective completion rate** — % of Objectives where score ≥ 0.7 (aspirational) or 1.0 (committed)
- **Retrospective completion rate** — % of scored Objectives that ran a retro
- **Score variance vs prediction** — gap between mid-cycle confidence and final score

## Decision questions

- Are we scoring honestly, or telling each other what we want to hear?
- Does anyone's compensation depend on these scores? (If yes, your scores are biased.)
- What's the retrospective conversation that this score should trigger?
- Are aspirational OKRs averaging 0.6-0.7, or 0.95+? (The latter = sandbagging.)
- Are committed OKRs averaging 0.95+, or 0.6? (The latter = scoping failure.)

## Canonical examples

- **Google** — public 0.0-1.0 scoring; aspirational OKRs scoring 0.6-0.7 considered healthy (pages 109-110)
- **Netflix** — scores reviewed for *learning*, not punishment; "failure to achieve 100% of KRs not perceived as personal failure" (pages 105-106)
- **Patagonia** — sustainability KRs scored publicly to drive accountability beyond just internal teams (page 98)
- **Toyota** — scoring tied to kaizen retrospectives; missed KRs become improvement initiatives (pages 102-103)
