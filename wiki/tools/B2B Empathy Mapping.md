# B2B Empathy Mapping

## Definition

B2B empathy mapping adapts the standard empathy map for the reality that a business "customer" is never a single person — it's a constellation of stakeholders across departments, each with their own duties, goals, and decision authority. Pereira's central observation is that empathy maps for B2B segments show **lower uniformity** within a single market segment than B2C maps do, precisely because each target firm contains multiple internal roles. The fix is structural: identify the right stakeholders, identify who influences them, pick one goal at a time, then build a map per stakeholder.

## Key attributes

- **Identify the relevant stakeholders** — don't map "the company" or even "the department". Drill down to the specific team or role you're actually serving. Pereira's example: selling scheduling SaaS to a large firm — your real subject is the scheduling team inside workforce management inside administration, not "the CFO" or "the company" (page 51).
- **Map decision influencers separately** — the stakeholder you're serving may not be the one who buys. Identify the horizontal and vertical relationships that affect their decision-making and the strength of each (pages 51-52).
- **Select one goal at a time** — e.g. "sell new product to company A" or "test marketing a new service to segment X". A single-goal frame prevents the map from collapsing under multi-stakeholder complexity (page 52).
- **One map per stakeholder** — same rule as B2C, but more critical: the same firm may need 3-5 distinct maps for end-user, manager, IT buyer, finance approver, executive sponsor.
- **Track firmographics alongside personas** — business size, industry, customer base, shareholder profile, leadership style all matter as context (page 50).
- **Same canvas, different inputs** — the framework is the same as B2C; what changes is who you map and how you weight decision influence.

## When to use

- Enterprise sales motions where deals involve 3+ stakeholders
- Account-Based Marketing (ABM) where each target account needs a stakeholder map
- B2B SaaS where end-user, admin, and economic buyer have divergent goals
- Channel/partnership deals — map the partner's internal stakeholders the same way
- When a deal is stuck and you don't know why — usually a stakeholder map gap

## When NOT to use

- B2B transactions with a single decision-maker (small business, owner-operator) — the standard empathy map suffices
- When you don't yet have access to the target firm's internal structure — gather firmographic data first
- For broad segment-level marketing where stakeholder specificity isn't actionable

## Common pitfalls

- **Mapping "the company" as one entity** — kills the tool. A company doesn't have feelings; people inside it do (page 51).
- **Confusing user with buyer** — the scheduling team uses the SaaS but the CFO writes the check. Map both, weight differently (page 51).
- **Ignoring informal influencers** — a senior engineer's offhand opinion can block a deal more than the org chart predicts (pages 51-52).
- **Treating the map as static** — stakeholder dynamics shift with reorgs, promotions, and political change inside the target firm. Refresh on each major account event.
- **Letting the framework feel B2B-foreign** — Pereira is explicit that the underlying canvas is the same; only the targeting changes. Don't reinvent.

## Signals of good use

- Sales team can name every stakeholder, their goal, and their influence weight on a live deal
- Marketing can tailor a one-pager per stakeholder type
- Stuck deals get diagnosed via "which stakeholder did we miss?" rather than "lower the price"
- ABM campaigns convert at higher rates because messaging is stakeholder-specific

## Decision questions

- Who is the actual user vs. the actual buyer vs. the actual influencer?
- Which department, team, and role are we mapping — at what depth?
- What is the single goal of this map? (Don't try to cover everything in one map.)
- Who outside this stakeholder's role can block or unblock their decision?
- What firmographic context matters — size, industry, leadership style?

## Canonical examples

- **SaaS scheduling tool sold to a large firm** — Pereira's worked B2B example: the real subject of the empathy map is the scheduling team inside workforce management, even though they don't make the final purchase decision. The empathy map captures their daily pain and gain, then a separate analysis layers in the decision-maker and influencers above them (page 51).
