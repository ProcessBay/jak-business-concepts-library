# Objection Handling

## Definition

Objection handling is the discipline of anticipating, listening to, and responding to a prospect's concerns, pushback, and hesitations during the sales process — most acutely between Pitch and Close. Pereira treats it as both a dedicated sales-process stage (step 5 in the canonical 7-step process) and a continuous skill applied throughout the deal. The core insight: objections are not failures or attacks but information — the prospect is telling you what's actually blocking the decision. The wrong response is to immediately rebut; the right response is to listen sympathetically, understand the underlying concern, and only then respond. Pereira pairs objection handling with closing as the "two halves of the same conversation": deals that get stuck typically have unhandled objections, and deals that close fast have either no objections or excellent handling. Build a documented objection playbook from the patterns reps see most often.

## Key attributes

- **Treat objections as information, not attacks** — surfaces what's actually blocking the deal
- **Listen first, respond second** — sympathetic listening before rebuttal
- **Anticipate, don't react** — preparation reduces surprise; document the top 5-10
- **Dedicated sales-process stage** — formally step 5 of 7 in the canonical process
- **Documented playbook** — concerns + responses + proof points, updated continuously
- **Common objection categories** — price, fit, timing, authority, competition, onboarding
- **Differentiates rep quality** — top reps handle objections; weak reps escalate or stall
- **Pairs with rapport** — strong rapport reduces objection frequency and softens severity

## When to use

- Always — objection handling is continuous throughout the sales process
- After pitch and during proposal stages (objection density peaks here)
- During discovery, to surface objections early before they kill the deal
- In post-close negotiation (price, terms, onboarding)
- When training new reps (top 5 objections per persona is the first enablement asset)
- After losing a deal — capture what objection actually killed it

## When NOT to use

- Do not "handle" objections that are actually disqualifying signals (sometimes "no" means no, and forcing past it wastes time)
- Do not handle every objection in writing — some need a live call
- Do not script responses verbatim — sounds robotic and damages trust
- Do not let objection handling replace listening — over-prepared reps stop hearing the actual concern

## Common pitfalls

- Immediately rebutting instead of listening sympathetically (page 160)
- Treating every objection the same (price ≠ timing ≠ authority)
- No documented playbook — reps rediscover the same objections deal after deal
- Letting one rep's good response stay tribal knowledge instead of enabling the team
- Bulldozing past the objection ("Yes but...") instead of acknowledging it
- Hammering objections with pressure (page 149 anti-pattern — backfires on borderline deals)
- Missing objections that aren't spoken (silence is also an objection)
- Confusing objections with disqualification — sometimes the answer really is no

## Key metrics

- **Win rate after objection** — best reps maintain win rate; weak reps see steep drop
- **Time-to-resolve** — average days an objection delays advancement
- **Escalation rate** — how often reps need to call in manager/specialist
- **Top objections by frequency** — drives the playbook updates
- **Top objections by deal-killing power** — drives the highest-priority responses
- **Loss reason analysis** — which objections most commonly close-lost the deal

## Decision questions

- Do we have a documented objection playbook, or does every rep wing it?
- Are we listening to objections, or rebutting them?
- Have we mapped objections by stage (discovery vs. proposal vs. close)?
- Are top reps' best responses being captured and shared, or staying tribal?
- Do we capture loss-reasons systematically to refresh the playbook?
- Can every rep handle the top 5 objections without escalation? (Pereira's Sales Enablement decision question)

## Canonical examples

- **Pereira's documented-playbook recommendation** (page 172) — keep track of areas with most objections, explore the concerns
- **HubSpot Sales Hub / Salesforce playbooks** — modern CRM-integrated objection libraries
- **Sandler Training** — methodology built heavily on objection management
- **Gong / Chorus call analysis** — modern tools surface objection patterns automatically
- **Pereira's "address uncertainty when you see it"** (page 74) — proactive handling
