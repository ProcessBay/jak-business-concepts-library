# Mind Mapping

## Definition

Mind mapping is a visual ideation and organization technique: a central topic sits in the middle of the surface, with branches radiating outward to subtopics, sub-subtopics, and connections. Pereira describes it as a way to "see how all your ideas fit together and how they fit into the whole" — combining the divergence of brainstorming with the structure of an outline. The visual format does two things at once: it helps generate ideas (by visually inviting you to fill empty branches) and it helps organize them (by showing relationships, hierarchies, and overlaps). It's flexible enough to work on paper, whiteboard, or with dedicated software, and it can serve as the visual layer underneath nearly any other brainstorming technique.

## Key attributes

- **Central node + radial branches** — the canonical structure
- **Hierarchical AND associative** — shows both parent-child and cross-links
- **Visual encoding** — color, symbols, images all reinforce meaning
- **Generative and organizational** — does both ideation and structuring in one artifact
- **Solo or group** — equally useful for one person or a team
- **Tool-agnostic** — pen + paper, whiteboard, or MindManager / Miro / XMind

## When to use

- Opening any creative or strategic session (capture initial structure)
- Note-taking during meetings, lectures, research
- Planning a project, a piece of content, or a presentation
- When the topic is broad and you need to see the whole landscape
- As the capture layer underneath other techniques (SCAMPER, brainstorming)
- For visual thinkers and teams that struggle with linear lists

## When NOT to use

- When you need a decision and a single output (mind maps multiply, not converge)
- For precise quantitative analysis
- When a linear narrative is the deliverable (you'll still need to flatten the map)
- When the team has no visual-thinking experience and the format itself confuses them

## Common pitfalls

- Letting the map sprawl without periodic review and pruning
- Mistaking the map for the deliverable (it's a thinking artifact, not the output)
- Cramming too much on one map — branch out into sub-maps when nodes exceed ~30
- Failing to highlight (color/symbols) — undifferentiated branches lose meaning
- Solo mapping in groups (everyone needs a way to contribute)

## Key metrics

- **Branch count** (proxy for coverage of the topic)
- **Depth** (how many levels you've explored)
- **Cross-links** (lateral connections — markers of integrative thinking)
- **Conversion to action** (which nodes become tasks or decisions)

## Decision questions

- What is the single central topic? (clarity here cascades through the map)
- Are we generating or organizing? (don't try both at once)
- Which branches deserve their own sub-map?
- What's our visual encoding scheme (color = priority, symbol = type)?

## Canonical examples

- **Storyboarding kickoff** — mind map to generate concepts before sketching scenes (page 44)
- **Project planning** — central goal with branches for resources, risks, milestones
- **Research synthesis** — map a literature review's concepts and relationships
- **Brainstorming capture** — visual layer underneath open brainstorming
- **Personal note-taking** — meeting notes that show how points relate
