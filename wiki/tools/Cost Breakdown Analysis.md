# Cost Breakdown Analysis

## Definition

Cost Breakdown Analysis is the discipline of decomposing the full cost of manufacturing, marketing, and selling a product or service into its component dollar values — refusing to take any financial line as a given. Pereira's worked example: a $500 widget might break down to ~$150 (30%) in parts and ~$350 (70%) in labor; the price you charge then becomes meaningfully comparable to the breakdown, and the gap (or inverse gap) tells you whether you're overcharging, underpricing, or operating at a loss. The technique is acknowledged as complicated and tasking, but Pereira treats the effort as a near-certain producer of profitable results, because it surfaces cost-saving opportunities, drives transparency into internal and external operations, and arms the team with the data needed for credible price negotiations.

## Key attributes

- **Dollar value on every line** — refuse to leave anything as "general overhead"
- **Internal and external scope** — covers manufacturing, marketing, sales, distribution
- **Comparative output** — breakdown vs. selling price reveals over/underpricing
- **Negotiation ammunition** — supplier price talks become evidence-led
- **Transparency-forcing** — exposes opaque cost pools where waste hides
- **Tasking but profitable** — high effort, high return; not a casual exercise

## When to use

- Margin compression with no obvious cause
- Preparing for major supplier renegotiations
- New product launches needing a defensible price floor
- Discovery phase of any serious cost-reduction program
- Validating a Design-to-Cost target against actual build economics
- Pricing audits — confirming you're not subsidizing customers

## When NOT to use

- Quick tactical decisions where the breakdown effort exceeds the savings
- Services with highly variable, customer-specific cost structures (use FCA instead)
- Products with no honest cost data to draw on — fix bookkeeping first
- Mature commodity lines where the breakdown is already industry knowledge

## Common pitfalls

- Lumping costs into vague buckets (defeats the entire exercise)
- Stopping at COGS and ignoring marketing, sales, and overhead allocation
- Using outdated breakdowns to justify current decisions
- Treating the breakdown as a one-off — costs drift; refresh quarterly
- Using the breakdown only internally and leaving negotiation leverage on the table
- Confusing breakdown with budgeting — breakdown is descriptive, budget is prescriptive

## Key metrics

- **Cost-component shares** — parts %, labor %, overhead %, distribution %
- **Selling price vs. breakdown delta** — margin or loss signal
- **Variance vs. prior period** for each component
- **Supplier-vs-internal cost spread** per component
- **Cost-per-feature** for value-engineering decisions

## Decision questions

- Have we assigned an honest dollar value to every line, or are we hiding costs in "overhead"?
- Is our selling price meaningfully above or below the breakdown?
- Which components show the largest gap between supplier quote and should-cost estimate?
- Where does the breakdown reveal cost-saving opportunities our budgets missed?
- How fresh is this breakdown — and how often should we refresh it?

## Canonical examples

- **Automotive OEMs** — Toyota / VW supplier negotiations grounded in should-cost models built from cost breakdowns
- **Consumer electronics teardowns** — iFixit / iSuppli / Counterpoint BOM reports as external cost breakdowns competitors use to benchmark
- **Pereira's widget example** — $500 product split 30/70 parts/labor, used to test pricing posture (page 19)
- **Procurement-led cost engineering** — McKinsey-style "design-to-value" engagements that start with a breakdown
