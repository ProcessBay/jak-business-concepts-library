# Concierge MVP

## Definition

A Concierge MVP is a high-fidelity MVP variant where the founder (or team) manually delivers a personalized service to a small number of real customers — and the customers know it's manual. There is no product to build yet; the team *is* the product. Pereira describes it as "manually helping your users accomplish their goals and seeing whether they need what you're offering or not. You don't even have to build a product." Unlike a Wizard of Oz test (which hides the manual operation behind an automated facade), the Concierge approach is transparent — the customer experiences a high-touch service while the team learns exactly what users need before writing any code.

## Key attributes

- **Manual delivery, transparent to the customer** — the user knows you're doing it by hand
- **Personalized one-on-one service** — high-touch, low-volume
- **No product required to start** — distinguishes from single-feature MVP
- **Reveals exactly what users actually need** — you observe real workflow, not stated preferences
- **Tests willingness to pay** — charging for the manual service validates demand for the eventual product
- **Operationally intensive, does not scale** — that's the point; you're learning, not scaling
- **Distinct from Wizard of Oz** — Concierge is transparent; Wizard of Oz hides the humans

## When to use

- Pre-product validation when you don't yet know exactly what to build
- New market entry where you have only a vague understanding of customer needs (page 38)
- Service-shaped products where the workflow itself is the open question
- When you want to charge real money to validate willingness-to-pay alongside demand
- Marketplaces and matching businesses where you can match by hand before building algorithms
- Any context where one-on-one observation will reveal what surveys cannot

## When NOT to use

- High-volume / low-margin businesses where manual unit economics never make sense
- Pure self-serve products where the experience IS the product (e.g., a game)
- When the founder/team lacks the domain expertise to actually deliver the service well
- Regulated services that require credentialed providers
- When you already know what to build (skip Concierge, go to single-feature MVP)

## Common pitfalls

- **Mistaking it for an indefinite operating model** — Concierge is a learning vehicle, not a business
- **Failing to systematize learnings** — running the service without capturing what each customer teaches
- **Falling in love with the service business** — and never automating
- **Charging too little** — defeats the willingness-to-pay test
- **Sampling friends and family** — biased feedback; you need real target-market customers
- **Skipping documentation** — when you finally build the product, you've lost the workflow knowledge

## Key metrics

- Hypothesis validation rate (do customers actually want this output?)
- Customer NPS / repeat usage
- Cost per test customer (your labor cost)
- Willingness to pay (price point that customers accept)
- Workflow time per customer (informs eventual automation requirements)

## Decision questions

- What is the ONE hypothesis we're testing — is it about demand, workflow, or willingness to pay?
- Can we deliver the service well enough that customer feedback reflects the offer, not our operational failures?
- What price reflects real demand without being a "friend discount"?
- At what point — what scale, what cost per customer — do we automate?
- What are we writing down about each engagement so the eventual product captures it?

## Canonical examples

- **Food on the Table** — CEO Manuel Rosso personally provided shoppers with a $10/month in-person service that generated tailored shopping lists, before building any app; later automated into the product (page 35)
- **Rent the Runway** — provided in-person dress rental services to college students before any e-commerce platform, validating that women would rent dresses online (page 73)
- **Airbnb (proto-concierge)** — Chesky and Gebbia hosted guests on air mattresses in their own apartment during a SF conference, personally handling the experience (pages 72, 79)
- **Early Stitch Fix** — manual styling for early customers before algorithmic recommendation
- **Many YC-era startups** — founders personally onboarding the first 10 users (PG's "do things that don't scale")
