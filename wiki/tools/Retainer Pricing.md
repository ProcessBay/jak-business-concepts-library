# Retainer Pricing

## Definition

A retainer is a recurring monthly (or quarterly) fee that buys the client ongoing access to the firm's capacity, expertise, or named deliverables — without the friction of per-project scoping every time. Pereira treats it as the **service-business form of subscription pricing** (pages 90-99) and as one of McKinsey's three named engagement models alongside project-based and performance-based (page 121). The model trades the high-margin spikes of fixed-bid projects for predictable MRR-shaped revenue, deeper client relationships, and the operational advantages that flow from forecastable demand: smoother staffing, less business-development drag, and a cash-flow profile that finances investment in better tooling and IP.

## Key attributes

- **Recurring revenue** — monthly fee paid in advance, regardless of usage in any given month
- **Capacity or deliverable buy** — either "X hours/month" or "this set of recurring deliverables"
- **Deep client relationship** — the firm becomes embedded, not a transactional vendor
- **Predictable cash flow** — same financial advantages SaaS gets from subscriptions (page 91)
- **Compounding context** — month 6 work is faster than month 1 because of accumulated knowledge
- **Use-it-or-lose-it dynamic** — unused hours typically don't roll over (or roll only partially)
- **Lower CAC amortization** — one sale finances many months of revenue (page 98)
- **Quiet scope expansion** — clients ask for "just one more thing," eroding effective rate

## When to use

- Client needs are ongoing, not project-shaped (always-on PR, recurring legal review, fractional CMO)
- The firm wants to smooth revenue and reduce business-development drag
- The work compounds — institutional knowledge of the client creates accelerating value
- Client values guaranteed access and priority response over per-project precision
- Cash-flow predictability matters more than peak per-project margin

## When NOT to use

- Work is genuinely episodic with long quiet periods (client overpays in quiet months, then resents it)
- The firm hasn't built scoping discipline (retainers without guardrails become unprofitable)
- Senior talent is too scarce to commit reserved capacity each month
- Client wants strict line-item accountability for every hour
- Early in a relationship — fixed-bid pilot first, retainer after trust is established

## Common pitfalls

- **Scope-creep compounding** — small additions every month erode the effective hourly rate
- **No usage tracking** — retainer feels like "all-you-can-eat" to the client; track hours internally even if invoicing is fixed
- **Stale renewals** — retainer ladders up in year 1, plateaus, then quietly devalues by year 3 if not re-anchored
- **Wrong staffing match** — senior partner relationship sold, junior delivery executed; client churns
- **Use-it-or-lose-it abuse** — clients save up demands then dump them, breaking capacity planning
- **Failing to formalize scope** — without a written "in scope / out of scope" doc, retainers become open-ended
- **Pricing on capacity, not value** — anchoring on "X hours" leaves the same money on the table as hourly billing

## Key metrics

- **MRR / ARR** — retainer revenue is functionally identical to SaaS recurring revenue
- **Retainer realization** — value delivered ÷ retainer billed (the honest health check)
- **Renewal / retention rate** — % of retainers that renew at the same or higher tier
- **Net Revenue Retention (NRR)** — expansion ÷ churn across the retainer book
- **Scope overrun rate** — % of retainers running above the contracted capacity
- **Client LTV** — average retainer revenue × months retained
- **Time-to-renewal-discussion** — months ahead of expiry that account discussions begin

## Decision questions

- Is the client need genuinely ongoing, or are we forcing a retainer on episodic work?
- What does our retainer realization look like — are we delivering 1.5x what we're charging?
- Do we have the scoping doc that lets us defensibly say no to scope creep?
- Are renewals happening because of inertia, or because we're proving value each quarter?
- Could we re-anchor a tired retainer on outcomes instead of hours/deliverables?

## Canonical examples

- **McKinsey retainer-based engagement model** — explicitly named as one of three engagement options offered alongside project-based and performance-based (page 121)
- **Big Law general counsel retainers** — monthly fee for ongoing legal support
- **Fractional executive services (CFO, CMO)** — monthly fee for part-time C-suite capacity
- **PR agencies** — most major PR firms operate on monthly retainers
- **Boutique strategy advisors** — quarterly board-advisor retainers
- **Compliance and audit subscriptions** — ongoing access to specialist capacity
