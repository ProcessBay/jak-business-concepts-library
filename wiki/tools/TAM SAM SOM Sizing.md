# TAM SAM SOM Sizing

## Definition

TAM/SAM/SOM is a three-layer market sizing tactic used to demonstrate that a market is large enough to support the financial investment required to pursue it. **TAM (Total Addressable Market)** is the entire universe of users with the need (calculated as ARPU × total possible customers, page 45). **SAM (Service Addressable Market)** is the slice of TAM the company can actually serve given geographic, regulatory, or capability constraints (page 32). **SOM (Service Obtainable Market)** is the realistic share of SAM the company can win — "the number of people that you are likely to obtain" (page 32). Multiplying SOM by ARPU yields the projected revenue ceiling (page 33). Pereira positions this as a non-negotiable first step in determining PMF: "you need to demonstrate that the demand is substantial enough to justify financial investment" (page 32).

## Key attributes

- **Three concentric layers** — TAM ⊃ SAM ⊃ SOM
- **TAM = ARPU × total possible customers** (page 45)
- **SAM is geographically/regulatorily bounded** — what you can actually serve (page 32)
- **SOM is the realistically winnable slice** — no single method; requires research and "product math" (page 32)
- **Projected revenue = SOM × ARPU** (page 33)
- **Investor-mandatory** — virtually every Series A deck has this slide
- **Iterative** — refine numbers as you learn about the market
- **Sanity check, not destiny** — large TAM doesn't guarantee success; small TAM may still be venture-scale via expansion

## When to use

- Pre-launch: as part of PMF validation
- Fundraising: investor pitch decks
- New market entry decisions: international expansion, adjacent verticals
- Pricing strategy: ARPU assumptions drive the math
- Resource allocation: which markets deserve focus

## When NOT to use

- As the only PMF check — large market with no fit is still a failure
- With unsourced or fabricated numbers — investors will spot it
- For very early discovery — premature precision is misleading
- In categories where the market is being created (no comparables yet)
- As justification to chase TAM instead of winning a SOM

## Common pitfalls

- **Top-down only** — calculating from industry reports without bottom-up validation
- **Ignoring serviceability constraints** — confusing TAM with SAM
- **Optimistic SOM** — assuming you'll capture 10% when the market leader has 4%
- **Static numbers** — failing to update as the market and your capabilities shift
- **Wrong ARPU** — using a pricing fantasy rather than realized economics
- **TAM theater** — picking a TAM definition designed to look big rather than to be useful

## Key metrics

- **TAM** ($) — total addressable market in revenue terms
- **SAM** ($) — service addressable market
- **SOM** ($) — service obtainable market
- **ARPU** — average revenue per user
- **Market capture %** — SOM / SAM
- **Projected revenue** — SOM × ARPU (page 33)
- **Profit estimate** — projected revenue minus expenses

## Decision questions

- Who are the users with unmet needs we're sizing for? (page 32)
- What geographic and regulatory boundaries cap our SAM?
- What evidence supports our SOM assumption?
- What ARPU is realistic given our pricing and competition?
- Does the math justify the financial investment we're about to make?
- After accounting for all expenses, what's the implied profit?

## Canonical examples

- **Standard investor pitch deck slide** — TAM/SAM/SOM pyramid is near-universal
- **Pre-revenue startup sizing** — when no actuals exist, this is the primary signal
- **Geographic expansion decisions** — SAM grows when you enter a new country
- **SaaS Rule of 40 pairing** — combine TAM/SAM/SOM with Rule of 40 to test growth viability (page 33)
