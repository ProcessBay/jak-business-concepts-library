# Brain-Netting

## Definition

Brain-netting is digital, collective brainstorming — bringing distributed people together virtually through the internet for a group ideation session. Pereira positions it as the canonical method for modern workplaces with remote teams, where the traditional whiteboard-in-a-room format doesn't work. The principle is to use a dedicated collaborative platform so everyone can share ideas (often anonymously) in real time or asynchronously, from phone, tablet, or laptop. It functions like a traditional brainstorming workshop except that participants aren't co-located, and most contributions are made anonymously to encourage participation. The technique has become essential since 2020 — and Pereira catalogues a spectrum of tools from simple (email-to-central-mailbox) to advanced (Miro, Slack, Zoom-based video conferencing with shared screens and online whiteboards).

## Key attributes

- **Distributed and virtual** — participants are not co-located
- **Anonymous-by-default** — encourages contributions from junior/quiet members
- **Sync or async** — can run live or stretch over hours/days
- **Tool-dependent** — needs a shared platform (whiteboard, chat app, video conf)
- **Lower travel/coordination cost** — replaces in-person workshop logistics
- **Time-zone flexible** (when run async)
- **Mediated by facilitator** — manager/facilitator quality matters more, not less

## When to use

- Teleworking employees and fully-remote teams
- Distributed teams across time zones (async is essential)
- Part-time workers who can't attend live sessions
- Large groups split into smaller breakout rooms
- When travel costs would otherwise block an in-person workshop
- Sensitive topics where anonymity will surface honest contributions

## When NOT to use

- When the team is co-located and the in-person dynamic adds value
- When the topic needs rapid back-and-forth that text/async slows down
- When platform familiarity is low — training overhead exceeds value
- When the facilitator lacks remote-meeting skills (high failure mode)
- For decisions requiring strong consensus and rich debate

## Common pitfalls

- Platform/hardware compatibility issues (Mac vs PC; bandwidth)
- Insufficient training on the chosen tool (high overhead for less-technical users)
- Scheduling difficulties across time zones (sync sessions exclude some members)
- Hard to integrate into a quick reflection (async drags)
- Manager/facilitator influence bleeds through even in anonymous mode
- Choosing the wrong tool tier — group calls when an async board would be better

## Key metrics

- **Ideas per participant** (anonymity should equalize this)
- **Participation rate** (% of invited contributors who posted)
- **Async response window** (time-to-quorum for async sessions)
- **Tool friction** (drop-off rate due to platform issues)

## Decision questions

- Sync or async — which fits the team's geography and rhythm?
- Which tool tier — group call, chat app, or full video conferencing with whiteboard?
- How will anonymity be enforced (and is it worth the tradeoff against attribution)?
- Who facilitates, and have they run remote sessions before?
- How will outputs be captured and what's the next-step format?

## Canonical examples

- **Group calls** — voice/video/screen-shared on ConferenceCalling.com or FreeConferenceCall.com (page 75)
- **Chat apps** — Slack, Yammer, Scribblar for topic-channel ideation (page 76)
- **Project boards** — Trello or Basecamp for async card-based contribution (page 76)
- **Video conferencing** — Zoom, GoToMeeting, UberConference with screen-share (page 76)
- **Simplest form** — team emails ideas to a central mailbox for review (page 75)
- **Modern stack** — Miro / Mural / FigJam as the de-facto remote whiteboard
