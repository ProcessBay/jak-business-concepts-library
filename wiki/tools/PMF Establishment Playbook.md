# PMF Establishment Playbook

## Definition

Pereira's six-step playbook for establishing product-market fit from scratch: **(1) determine your ICP**, **(2) research underserved customer needs**, **(3) define your value proposition**, **(4) lock down your MVP**, **(5) build prototypes**, and **(6) test your MVP with customers** (pages 52-55). The playbook is sequenced and gated — each step's output feeds the next. It is paired with Pereira's startup-specific PMF goals (pages 42-45): focus on a single vertical, gather intelligence from sales conversations, specify a sharp value proposition, measure PMF using TAM and validation, and explicitly avoid complacency once initial fit is achieved.

## Key attributes

- **Six-step sequence** — ICP → research → value prop → MVP → prototype → test (pages 52-55)
- **ICP first** — define traits of perfect client and anticipated personas (page 53)
- **Underserved needs focus** — investigate switching reasons from current solutions (page 53)
- **Value prop as "30-second commercial"** — compelling, clear, customer-needs-driven (page 53)
- **MVP discipline** — "It is not the time to make sure that your product is faultless" (page 54)
- **Prototype fidelity choice** — low- or high-fidelity, but always test (page 54)
- **MVP testing via NPS + feedback polls** (page 55)
- **Single-vertical focus** — startups have too few resources to chase all customers; pick a niche (page 43)
- **Cross-functional ICP work** — combine data sources across teams (page 53)
- **Iterate ruthlessly** — be ready to "start completely over and go back to the basics" (page 55)

## When to use

- Pre-launch for a new product or new vertical
- Post-pivot when restarting toward PMF
- New-product introduction within an established company
- Workshop-style PMF planning sessions
- Onboarding a new product team to a consistent PMF process

## When NOT to use

- For mature products with established PMF (use measurement methods instead)
- When team can't actually build prototypes (mid-step gap)
- Without funding/runway for at least one full iteration cycle
- When founder won't accept the iterate-or-restart discipline

## Common pitfalls

- **Skipping ICP definition** — building for "everyone" yields fit with no one
- **Over-engineering the MVP** — Pereira: years of pre-launch perfectionism, only for the feature to be brushed off (page 54)
- **Skipping prototype testing** — going from MVP design straight to build
- **Sending prototypes only to friendly reviewers** — missing critical external feedback
- **Chasing too many verticals** — Pereira: focus on a single vertical and dominate it (page 43)
- **Complacency after first signal** — Pereira's Skype-vs-Zoom warning (page 46)
- **Saying yes to every feature request** — see [[PMF Two-Way Street]]

## Key metrics

- **ICP definition clarity** — can every team member name the ideal customer?
- **Underserved need validation** — % of ICP interviews confirming the unmet need
- **Value prop comprehension** — % of prospects who repeat the value prop accurately
- **MVP scope discipline** — feature count vs target; lower is usually better
- **Prototype feedback velocity** — number of cycles per quarter
- **MVP NPS** — post-launch signal of fit (page 55)

## Decision questions

- Have we written down our ICP and the anticipated adjacent personas? (page 53)
- What are our ICPs currently using, and what makes them want to switch? (page 53)
- Can we state our value prop in 30 seconds? (page 53)
- Have we ruthlessly cut MVP scope to the most critical features only? (page 54)
- Have we tested prototypes (low- or high-fidelity) before building?
- Are we focused on a single vertical, or spread too thin? (page 43)
- Are we ready to restart from basics if testing demands it? (page 55)

## Canonical examples

- **Digit (money management app)** — "Stress less about your money" as a clean value prop example (page 44)
- **Sean Higgins / TechStars four-step ICP process** — assess product, know rivals, define criteria, do research (pages 42-43)
- **Slack's pivot** — built MVP for internal team comms, recognized the pull, restarted toward collaboration software (page 20)
- **Zoom's quick MVP for 2020 remote work** — fulfilled product-market demand before complacent competitors could react (page 46)
