# Osterwalder 7 Questions

## Definition

Alexander Osterwalder's 7-Question business model design assessment scores business model robustness across seven dimensions: (1) switching costs for customers; (2) scalability; (3) recurring revenues; (4) earn-before-spend cycle; (5) others doing work for you; (6) competitive protection; (7) cost structure advantage. Each scored 0-10. The framework is fast (15-30 minutes), diagnostic (reveals model weaknesses), and actionable (each question has a "call to action" for improvement).

## Key attributes

- **Seven scored dimensions** — each 0 (bad) to 10 (excellent)
- **Diagnostic + prescriptive** — each question paired with a "call to action"
- **Speed** — full assessment 15-30 minutes
- **Comparison-ready** — score business models against each other or against competitors
- **No perfect score** — even great business models won't score 10 on all 7

## The seven questions

1. **Switching costs** — How difficult/expensive is it for customers to switch to a competitor?
2. **Scalability** — How rapidly and easily can the business model scale without proportional cost increase?
3. **Recurring revenues** — Does the model produce recurring (vs. one-shot) revenues?
4. **Earn before spend** — Does the firm earn revenue before incurring delivery costs?
5. **Others doing work** — How much do customers or third parties do work (for free) that the firm would otherwise pay for?
6. **Competitive protection** — How much does the business model protect against competition (vs. just product features)?
7. **Cost structure** — Is the cost structure structurally better than competitors'?

## When to use

- Annual strategic review
- Investment decision (your business or as an investor)
- M&A target evaluation
- Competitor analysis (score their model alongside yours)
- Identifying which weakness to address first

## When NOT to use

- For early validation (before product-market fit; questions assume operating model exists)
- As a substitute for financial diligence
- For pure operational decisions

## Common pitfalls

- Self-scoring optimistically — get external perspective
- Treating 10/7 as the goal rather than improving the weakest score
- Confusing product features with business model (the 7Q is at the model level)
- Failing to update scores after strategic changes

## Key metrics

- Score per question (0-10)
- Total score (typically 40-60 for strong models)
- Improvement priority (lowest-scoring question = where to focus)

## Decision questions per dimension

1. Switching costs — Can we increase them without annoying customers? (Apple, Nespresso, printer cartridges examples)
2. Scalability — How can we make the model more scalable? (Facebook, Uber, WhatsApp, franchising)
3. Recurring revenue — How can we increase recurring vs. transactional? (Redhat, Microsoft, Kindle, Amazon Prime, SaaS)
4. Earn before spend — Could we earn more before spending? (Dell, Vistaprint, NikeID, Warby Parker)
5. Others doing work — How can we get others to do more for us? (IKEA assembly, Twitter/Facebook content, Tupperware sales force)
6. Competitive protection — Can we redesign for built-in protection? (Apple iPhone + App Store ecosystem)
7. Cost structure — Can we transform the cost base rather than trim? (Skype vs. telecom, Bharti Airtel, Nike Flyknit)

## Canonical examples

- **Apple** — strong on switching costs (ecosystem) AND competitive protection (App Store moat) (pages 13, 16)
- **Facebook / Uber / WhatsApp** — strong scalability (network-effects platforms) (page 14)
- **Microsoft / Amazon Prime / SaaS** — strong recurring revenue (page 14-15)
- **Dell / Warby Parker** — strong earn-before-spend (made-to-order) (page 15)
- **IKEA / Twitter / Tupperware** — strong "customers do the work" (page 15)
- **Skype / Bharti Airtel** — strong cost structure transformation (page 16)
