# Marketing Budget Definition

## Definition

Pereira defines marketing budgeting as the art of constructing a financial blueprint that brings the organization's marketing vision to fruition. It involves allocating resources toward a range of initiatives — advertising campaigns, events, and everything in between — so that every dollar of the marketing budget is put to optimal use. The process requires informed financial projections: determining the budget, calculating expected ROI, and accounting for cost of implementation, production, cash flow, forecasted sales, and target profit margin. Critically, marketing has many hidden costs (freelancer payments, sponsorship fees, the price of a brand-new marketing hire), and Pereira urges marketers to detail every single expense anticipated. The 80/20 rule is the central allocation principle: focus on the 20% of products/services that contribute 80% of volume and the 20% of customers who account for 80% of revenue.

## Key attributes

- **Financial blueprint** — every initiative tied to a line item
- **Hidden costs accounted for** — freelancers, sponsorships, new hires, tooling, production
- **ROI-projected per initiative** — budget approval depends on demonstrable expected return
- **80/20 allocation rule** — 20% of products = 80% of volume; 20% of customers = 80% of revenue
- **Tied to cash flow and forecasted sales** — budget must align with the financial plan, not exceed it
- **Detailed and itemized** — not "marketing: $X" but "social ads: $A, content: $B, events: $C..."
- **Second-largest expense category** — Pereira: marketing is typically second only to employee salaries
- **Reviewed continuously** — not set-and-forget; budgets re-prioritize as performance data arrives

## When to use

- Annual planning cycle — marketing budget is built in tandem with the marketing plan
- Pitching for budget increase or reallocation to leadership
- Launching a major initiative (new product launch, market entry, rebrand)
- Setting up a new business and projecting marketing spend
- Auditing wasteful spend — apply 80/20 to find the 20% driving results
- Post-mortem on a campaign or quarter — actuals vs. budget feeds the next plan

## When NOT to use

- For a single tactical experiment with discretionary spend — overkill
- When the business has no revenue forecast yet — budget without revenue context is fiction
- As a standalone document detached from the marketing plan
- To justify spend already committed (reverse-engineered budgets fool no one)

## Common pitfalls

- **Forgetting hidden costs** — Pereira: freelancer fees, sponsorship fees, new-hire cost all add up; under-budgeting causes mid-year scrambles
- **No ROI projection** — leadership cannot evaluate without expected return per dollar
- **Ignoring the 80/20 rule** — over-investing in long-tail channels/products that don't drive volume
- **Rigid budgets** — no flexibility to respond to new opportunities or competitive threats
- **Budget without performance feedback loop** — actuals never inform the next allocation
- **Optimizing single-line items** — saving on one channel while ignoring portfolio-level ROI
- **Treating budget as a ceiling rather than a hypothesis** — should be revisited as data accumulates
- **Front-loading without reserves** — no flexibility for late-year opportunities or course correction

## Key metrics

- **Total marketing spend** as % of revenue (benchmark by industry)
- **Marketing ROI** — return per dollar spent
- **Cost per acquisition (CAC)** — by channel
- **Payback period** — time to recover CAC via revenue
- **Channel mix** — % spend across paid, organic, content, events, etc.
- **Budget variance** — actuals vs. plan, % over/under
- **Top-20% contribution** — does the 20% of spend hit the 80/20 mark on revenue?

## Decision questions

- Have we line-itemized every anticipated cost, including hidden ones?
- Does each line item have a projected ROI?
- Which 20% of our marketing spend drives 80% of results — and are we doubling down there?
- What is our marketing spend as % of revenue, and how does that compare to industry benchmark?
- How will we measure marketing ROI in-flight, not just at year-end?
- What is our flexibility budget — what % is reserved for unplanned opportunities?

## Canonical examples

- **80/20 rule application** — Pereira: "By focusing on the 20% of products and services that contribute to 80% of your volume and the 20% of customers who account for 80% of your revenue, you can maximize your impact" (page 22)
