# Decoy Pricing

## Definition

Decoy pricing introduces a strategically priced third option whose only purpose is to make a target option look more attractive by comparison. Pereira's canonical version is the streaming-tier pattern: Basic $8 / Standard $12 / Premium $15, where Premium is intentionally priced so close to Standard that the Standard plan appears to be the obvious value choice. The variant **premium decoy pricing** uses a deliberately overpriced flagship to make the mid-tier feel like a bargain. The mechanism is the cognitive bias known as **asymmetric dominance** — adding a dominated option shifts preference toward the dominator.

## Key attributes

- **Three-option structure** — small, target, decoy
- **Decoy is strategically dominated** — close in price to the target but inferior in value
- **Shifts preference, not preference set** — choice architecture, not new information
- **Premium decoy variant** — overpriced flagship makes mid-tier look reasonable
- **Static, not dynamic** — set once based on tier design, not adjusted in real time
- **Works with bundle and tier structures** — naturally embeds in SaaS pricing pages
- **Vulnerable to consumer wising-up** — overused, it becomes transparent

## When to use

- Subscription and SaaS tier pages where you want customers in the middle tier
- Restaurants and hospitality with sized portions (small/medium/large coffee)
- Consumer electronics with good/better/best model laddering
- When you want to lift average transaction value without raising the target price
- Categories where customers default to "the safe middle choice"

## When NOT to use

- Customers are sophisticated and will see through the decoy
- You have only two real options to sell — forcing a third decoy adds confusion
- Brand is trust-positioned (manipulation backlash risk)
- Categories where price is benchmarked transparently across competitors
- When the target option doesn't actually deliver the value the decoy implies

## Common pitfalls

- Pricing the decoy too far from target — no asymmetric dominance, no effect
- Customers actually buy the decoy — you didn't dominate it enough
- Using the same decoy structure on every tier — customers notice the pattern
- Decoy degrades brand by appearing dishonest if exposed
- Confusing decoy with anchor — anchor lifts perceived value of the lower option; decoy shifts choice toward the middle option

## Key metrics

- **Conversion to target tier** (% choosing the dominant option)
- **AOV** before and after introducing the decoy
- **Tier mix** (% Basic / Target / Premium)
- **Utility per dollar** by tier (the framework Pereira uses on page 124-125)
- **Cancellation/return rate** by tier (decoy buyers may regret)

## Decision questions

- Which tier do we want to push customers toward — is the target genuinely best for them?
- Is our decoy clearly dominated, or could customers reasonably pick it?
- Are we confident the choice architecture is ethical, not manipulative?
- What does the tier mix look like before and after the decoy goes live?
- Will sophisticated B2B buyers see through this?

## Canonical examples

- **Streaming services** — Basic $8 / Standard $12 / Premium $15 nudges to Standard (page 55)
- **Coffee shop sizes** — small / medium / large, where medium is the decoy nudging toward large (page 56)
- **Smartphone tiers** — Basic / Advanced / Premium with Advanced as the target (page 56)
- **Starbucks Tall / Grande / Venti** — Venti as premium decoy makes Grande look reasonable (page 123)
- **Car trim levels** — basic / mid / premium where premium nudges buyers to mid (page 124)
- **Restaurant wine lists** — house / mid / expensive premium nudges to mid (page 124)
