# SCAMPER

## Definition

SCAMPER is a creative-thinking checklist that prompts ideation by applying seven transformation verbs to an existing product, process, or idea. The acronym stands for **Substitute, Combine, Adapt, Modify, Put to another use, Eliminate, Reverse**. Pereira positions it as a way to "break out of typical thought patterns" by forcing teams to interrogate something familiar from seven structured angles. It is especially useful when a team is stuck on an existing offer and needs to surface variants, extensions, or pivots without starting from a blank page. Unlike open brainstorming, SCAMPER provides built-in prompts — making it work well with smaller teams or solo ideation where momentum and structure matter more than free-flowing chaos.

## Key attributes

- **Seven verbs** — Substitute, Combine, Adapt, Modify, Put to another use, Eliminate, Reverse
- **Built-in prompts** — each verb generates its own line of questions
- **Anchored to an existing thing** — works on a product/process/idea, not a blank slate
- **Solo or group friendly** — needs less facilitation than open brainstorming
- **Iterative** — apply each verb in turn; one pass often surfaces 20-50 variants
- **Output is variants, not pure novelty** — best for refinement and extension

## When to use

- Refining or extending an existing product or service
- When brainstorming has gone flat ("we've exhausted ideas")
- Product-development sprints where you need feature variants
- Marketing-positioning workshops (different ways to frame an existing offer)
- Solo ideation when there's no team available
- Educational settings (teaches structured creative thinking)

## When NOT to use

- Greenfield ideation with no existing artifact to transform
- When the goal is divergent vision-setting (SCAMPER is convergent-ish)
- For root-cause analysis (use Fishbone or 5 Whys)
- When stakeholders need to align on direction (use a different format)

## Common pitfalls

- Treating SCAMPER as a checklist to "complete" rather than a generator of options
- Skipping verbs that "don't apply" — every verb often produces surprises
- Stopping at the first reasonable answer per verb (push for 3-5 per prompt)
- Confusing SCAMPER with decision-making — it's still ideation, evaluate later

## Key metrics

- **Variants per verb** (target 3-5 per prompt; total 20-35 ideas)
- **Novel variants** (those genuinely different from the starting artifact)
- **Action conversion** (% that become prototypes or backlog items)

## Decision questions

- What is the specific artifact we're SCAMPER-ing? (be concrete)
- Which verb is producing the most/least output, and why?
- Are we converging too fast on one verb's output?
- Which variants do we want to test or prototype?

## Canonical examples

- **Substitute** — use a viral video in place of a traditional ad campaign (page 48)
- **Combine** — merge online shopping convenience with in-store personalized service (page 48)
- **Adapt** — translate packaging/instructions to enter a new country (page 48)
- **Modify** — add new features or change packaging to refresh a product (page 49)
- **Put to another use** — turn an old T-shirt into a tote bag (page 49)
- **Eliminate** — drop the physical storefront if you only sell online (page 49)
- **Reverse** — have job applicants submit video pitches instead of forms (page 49)
