# Power and Influence Tactics in Negotiation

## Definition

Power and influence in negotiation come from multiple distinct sources, and skilled negotiators consciously deploy them — separately and in combination — to shape outcomes. Pereira catalogs seven power bases plus persuasive communication: (1) **Legitimate power** — formal authority or organizational position; (2) **Reward power** — ability to provide desirable outcomes (incentives, bonuses, discounts); (3) **Coercive power** — ability to impose negative consequences (use cautiously — often crosses into hardball); (4) **Expert power** — specialized knowledge and demonstrated competence; (5) **Information power** — strategic possession and selective disclosure of relevant data; (6) **Referent power** — personal charisma, likability, admiration; (7) **Network power** — influential connections and resource access; plus **coalition building** (alliances with aligned stakeholders) and **persuasive communication** (framing, storytelling, emotional appeals). The taxonomy (originally from French and Raven's social power theory, adapted by Pereira to negotiation) helps negotiators audit their own influence portfolio, recognize what the counterparty is deploying, and choose tactics that fit the context. The integrative use is to lean on legitimate, expert, referent, and reward power; the distributive/hardball use leans more on coercive and information-asymmetry power.

## Key attributes

- **Seven power bases** — legitimate, reward, coercive, expert, information, referent, network
- **Coalition building** — multiplies any single power base by adding aligned stakeholders
- **Persuasive communication** — operational layer that channels power into outcomes
- **Power is portfolio** — multiple bases combine; relying on one is fragile
- **Coercive power is high-risk** — works short-term, damages trust long-term
- **Expert and referent power are durable** — built over years, hard for counterparty to neutralize
- **Information power is perishable** — disclosed information stops being leverage

## When to use

- Always — every negotiation deploys some power base, often unconsciously
- When auditing your position before a negotiation (which bases am I strongest in?)
- When choosing tactics to fit the counterparty's profile and the relationship horizon
- When you need to neutralize a counterparty's power base (counter with your own)
- When building long-term influence (invest in expert and referent power over time)

## When NOT to use

- Coercive power in long-term relationships — relationship cost exceeds deal benefit
- Information asymmetry in deals where the counterparty will eventually discover what you withheld
- Network power in cultures where it reads as nepotism or improper influence
- Reward power as bribery in regulated contexts (legal exposure)
- Any power base used so heavy-handedly it crosses into hardball

## Common pitfalls

- Relying on a single power base when others are available
- Conflating positional authority (legitimate power) with persuasive ability
- Misusing coercive power and burning relationships unnecessarily
- Disclosing information power prematurely (it stops being leverage)
- Failing to recognize the counterparty's power base and being out-leveraged
- Confusing referent power with manipulation — real charisma is earned
- Coalition building that locks you into commitments to the coalition itself

## Key metrics

- **Influence portfolio score** — which power bases you hold, weighted by depth
- **Persuasion success rate** — % of proposals accepted across negotiations
- **Concession yield per tactic** — concessions earned per influence move
- **Coalition strength** — number and depth of aligned stakeholders
- **Counterparty power assessment** — your read of their bases (pre-deal)

## Decision questions

- Which power bases do I hold, and which is strongest in this context?
- Which power bases does the counterparty hold, and how do I neutralize the dangerous ones?
- Where is coercive power necessary, and where will it damage the relationship more than it gains?
- What coalition could I build to multiply my position?
- Which power bases am I investing in for the long term (expert, referent), and which am I just using situationally?

## Canonical examples

- **Legitimate + expert power** — CEO using both title and domain knowledge in M&A talks (page 79)
- **Reward power** — buyer offering volume commitments to a supplier in exchange for pricing (page 79)
- **Information power** — Company B's market analysis as leverage in supplier negotiation (page 118)
- **Referent power** — Starbucks' brand strength shaping partnership terms with Nestlé (pages 123-124)
- **Coalition building** — labor unions aggregating worker demands into one negotiating block (page 117)
- **Network power** — Pereira's "influential connections" as backchannel leverage (pages 80-81)
