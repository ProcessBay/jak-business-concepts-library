# Bundling and Packaging Strategies

## Definition

Bundling combines multiple products or services into a single package sold at a consolidated price; packaging is the broader practice of how offerings are configured, presented, and tiered. Pereira treats them as twin levers for shaping customer perception of value, simplifying decisions, capturing more wallet, and price-discriminating across segments. Two structural types exist: **pure bundling** (only available as a bundle) and **mixed bundling** (available bundled or individually). Packaging adds a third dimension: tiered offerings (good-better-best) that let different customer segments self-select into different price-feature combinations. Done well, bundling and packaging unlock revenue from segments who wouldn't buy individual items at full price and increase wallet share from segments who would.

## Key attributes

- **Two bundling types** — pure (bundle-only) vs mixed (bundle or individual)
- **Three packaging modes** — single-product, multi-product, tiered
- **Five strategic benefits** — enhanced perceived value, revenue optimization, simplified decision-making, market penetration, brand strengthening
- **Price discrimination engine** — tiers let different willingness-to-pay segments self-select
- **Cross-sell amplifier** — bundles introduce customers to items they wouldn't buy standalone
- **Reduces decision fatigue** — fewer choices, clearer trade-offs
- **Requires segmentation discipline** — bundles must map to actual customer groups, not internal product categories
- **Test-and-iterate by design** — A/B test bundle configurations and prices

## When to use

- When customers buy multiple of your products and the cross-product friction is high
- When you have a clear good/better/best segmentation by willingness-to-pay
- When you want to drive trial of underused or new products via inclusion in bundles
- When competitors sell unbundled and you can win by simplifying
- When the buying decision is complex and customers want a "just give me the right package"
- When average transaction value is too low and you need wallet share

## When NOT to use

- When customers value granular control over what they buy (forced bundling causes resentment)
- When bundle math is unfavorable — discount erodes margin without lifting volume
- When your products genuinely don't complement each other (forced cross-sell)
- When tiers proliferate beyond ~3-4 — decision fatigue returns
- When you can't measure attach rates or tier distribution (you're flying blind)

## Common pitfalls

- Bundling underperformers into bundles to clear inventory (customers see through it)
- Over-discounting bundles — destroys reference pricing for standalone items
- Pure bundling when customers want choice — drives them to competitors
- Too many tiers — Pereira's framing notes 3-tier as a sweet spot for decision clarity
- Misaligned tier features — top tier doesn't include the most-wanted feature; middle tier is poorly differentiated
- Bundling without analytics — can't tell which configurations work
- Ignoring the [[Pricing Psychology]] layer — anchoring, decoy effects, and middle-tier preference shape uptake

## Key metrics

- **Average transaction value (ATV)** — should rise with effective bundling
- **Attach rate** — % of buyers who add the bundled item
- **Bundle uptake rate** — % of purchases that include a bundle
- **Tier distribution** — % choosing each tier; healthy = middle dominant
- **Gross margin by bundle** — make sure bundles aren't margin-destroyers
- **Cross-sell rate** — bundle-driven introduction of new products
- **Standalone vs bundle price realization** — ensures bundles complement, not cannibalize

## Decision questions

- Which of our products are naturally complementary in the customer's workflow?
- Can we identify three distinct customer segments by willingness-to-pay?
- Will pure bundling drive cross-sell or just alienate customers who want à la carte?
- What's the right discount on the bundle — enough to incentivize, not enough to anchor low?
- Which tier is our "middle tier" — and is it the one we want most customers to choose?
- How will we test alternative configurations and prices?
- Are bundle margins healthier or worse than standalone — and is the volume lift worth it?

## Canonical examples

- **Microsoft 365** — Word, Excel, PowerPoint, Outlook, OneDrive bundled at a discount vs standalone purchases (page 70)
- **Fast-food combo meals** — burger + fries + drink at a small discount, simplifies ordering, raises ATV (page 70-71)
- **Streaming subscription tiers** — Netflix Basic / Standard / Premium with screen, quality, and resolution variations (page 71)
- **Home entertainment systems** — TV + soundbar + console bundles by electronics retailers (page 71)
- **Telecom triple-play** — internet + TV + phone bundled below individual subscription totals
- **SaaS pricing tiers** — Free / Pro / Business / Enterprise (Three-Tier Pricing pattern)
