# Customer Retention Tactics (Pereira)

## Definition

A catalog of operating tactics for building long-term customer relationships, distilled from Daniel Pereira's "Customer Relationship Strategies" guide. The set spans two layers: (1) **retention-oriented persuasion tactics** — defending a point of view, invoking the ego, reducing pain, offering extras, personalization, maintaining quality, choosing the right channel, leveraging social proof, VIP labeling, etc. (pages 15-19); and (2) **the "18 Ways to Build Strong Customer Relationships" playbook** — communicate, exceed expectations, ask for feedback, connect, show appreciation, understand the customer journey, introduce yourself, implement CRM tooling, market the service perks, match customer habits, respond immediately, set quality controls, follow up, personalize, keep detailed files, prioritize CX, create value, instill etiquette (pages 27-36). Together they form a checklist firms can audit themselves against, not a single methodology.

## Key attributes

- **Catalog, not a methodology** — pick and combine tactics that fit the business
- **Two layers** — emotional/persuasion tactics + operational/process tactics
- **Personalization is the throughline** — most tactics reduce to "treat the customer as a known individual"
- **Quality > speed** — Pereira repeatedly contradicts the "ship fast at any quality" instinct (page 16)
- **Social proof and VIP status exploit identity** — humans want to belong to an in-group above another
- **CRM tooling is enabling, not strategic** — the tools matter only if the behavior changes
- **Service etiquette as differentiator** — 62% of customers won't return after a bad service interaction (page 35); 70% of perceived experience comes from how customers feel they are treated (page 35)
- **Retention math is brutal** — acquiring a new customer can cost up to 5x retaining one (page 27)

## When to use

- When designing a retention or customer-success program from scratch
- When auditing an existing retention program for gaps
- When churn rises and you need a structured set of levers to pull
- When training frontline service staff (the 18-ways list is teachable)
- When building loyalty mechanics (VIP tiers, communities, follow-up cadence)

## When NOT to use

- As a substitute for fixing a broken product — retention tactics don't compensate for low value
- When the underlying segment has no LTV to retain
- As a mechanical checklist applied without judgment about which tactics fit your business
- For pre-acquisition lead-gen (these are retention tactics, not acquisition tactics)

## Common pitfalls

- Cherry-picking flashy tactics (VIP labels, loyalty points) without the operational underpinnings (CRM data, follow-up discipline)
- Asking for feedback and not acting on it — Pereira flags this as a major error (page 29)
- Personalizing email subject lines while delivering generic service — surface personalization, no depth
- Confusing retention with discounting — Pereira notes price cuts can satisfy, but quality is the durable lever (page 17)
- Loyalty programs that don't reduce sign-up friction — momentum matters (page 18)
- Investing in CRM software and never changing the workflow it was meant to enable

## Key metrics

- **Retention rate / churn rate** (cohort, by segment)
- **CLV** — Pereira's headline justification for retention investment (page 27)
- **Repeat purchase rate**
- **Referral rate / word-of-mouth attribution**
- **NPS / CSAT** trended over time
- **First-response time** (page 31's "respond right away" tactic)
- **Loyalty-program enrollment and active-usage rate**

## Decision questions

- Which of the 18 ways are we doing well, which are we faking, and which are we missing?
- Are our retention tactics aligned with what this segment actually values (quality, speed, status, price)?
- Do we ask for feedback AND act on it, or just collect it?
- Is our loyalty program reducing the activation friction, or just adding a card to the wallet?
- Does our CRM tooling change behavior, or is it shelfware?
- Are we training frontline staff on service etiquette, or just hiring and hoping?

## Canonical examples

- **Starbucks** — Pereira's case study: ubiquitous accessibility + consistent atmosphere + loyalty app integrated into customer daily routine (page 38)
- **Google and Facebook** — DIY self-serve advertising; large-enough user base to be "Price Deciders not Price Acceptors" (pages 38-39)
- **Airline status tiers (Gold/Platinum/Diamond)** — VIP labeling and tier-comparison psychology (page 18)
- **Amazon Prime** — quality, speed, and bundled benefits as retention engine
- **Hotel loyalty programs** — points + status + personalized recognition
- **Sephora Beauty Insider** — community + tiered VIP + personalization
