# Price Discrimination

## Definition

Price discrimination charges different customers different prices for the same product, based on each segment's willingness-to-pay. Pereira identifies three textbook degrees: **first-degree** (charge each individual their maximum WTP — the theoretical limit), **second-degree** (volume discounts — large buyers pay less per unit), and **third-degree** (segment by demographic, geography, or use case — student discounts, industrial vs. residential electricity). The strategy captures consumer surplus that a single uniform price would leave on the table, but it carries legal and brand risks when customers compare notes.

## Key attributes

- **Three degrees** — individual, volume-tiered, segment-tiered
- **Captures consumer surplus** — extracts WTP from less-elastic buyers
- **Requires meaningful segments** — geography, time, demographic, usage
- **Segment isolation matters** — arbitrage destroys the model
- **Legal sensitivity** — protected-characteristic discrimination is illegal
- **Brand sensitivity** — customers comparing prices feel cheated
- **Foundational to airlines, software, education** — pervasive once you look

## When to use

- Segments with measurably different willingness-to-pay
- Geographic markets with different price-sensitivity
- B2B with different buyer-size tiers (volume discounts)
- Software/SaaS with academic, nonprofit, or government tiers
- Industries with sophisticated segmentation infrastructure

## When NOT to use

- Segments can easily arbitrage (resell between segments)
- Customer base is small and tight-knit (everyone compares notes)
- Discrimination criteria touch protected characteristics (race, gender, etc.)
- Brand is built on price fairness and transparency
- Regulatory environment prohibits or scrutinizes differential pricing

## Common pitfalls

- Legal exposure for discrimination on protected classes (page 133)
- Customer rage when WTP-based pricing becomes public (Amazon's old experiments)
- Failing to isolate segments — arbitrage erodes the strategy
- Complexity outruns operational capacity to maintain
- Over-discounting one segment cannibalizes higher-paying segments
- Confusing third-degree price discrimination with dynamic pricing (related but distinct)

## Key metrics

- **Consumer surplus captured** (vs. uniform-pricing counterfactual)
- **Revenue per segment** (and segment mix)
- **Price per segment** (and the spread)
- **Arbitrage rate** (cross-segment resale)
- **Customer complaint rate** (the brand watchlist)

## Decision questions

- Do our segments have meaningfully different willingness-to-pay?
- Can we isolate segments so they can't arbitrage (geo, eligibility, contract terms)?
- What's our legal exposure for our specific segmentation criteria?
- How will customers feel if they discover our segment-based pricing?
- Is the operational complexity worth the incremental revenue?

## Canonical examples

- **Airlines** — same seat, different fare class by booking timing, loyalty status (page 134, 606)
- **Movie theaters** — child, senior, matinee, weekend pricing (page 134)
- **Software** — Microsoft charges different prices by feature tier and use case (page 134)
- **Telecom volume discounts** — large internet users pay lower per-GB (page 132, second-degree)
- **Utilities** — industrial customers pay lower per-kWh than residential (page 132, third-degree)
- **Local vs. tourist pricing** — restaurants in tourist areas (page 611)
