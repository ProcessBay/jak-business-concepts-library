# Round Robin Brainstorming

## Definition

Round robin brainstorming is a structured circular method in which every participant contributes one idea in turn before anyone gets a second turn. Pereira frames it as a "balanced method that uses a circular, iterative process that builds on what each person has said before" — designed to neutralize the dominance of loud voices and ensure every member's perspective surfaces. The core rule is **equal time and attention for each contributor**, and no building on any idea until everyone has shared once. It works especially well for medium-to-large teams with quieter members or with a noticeable creativity gap between dominant and subordinate participants. It can also be combined with index cards (write-then-share) for an even quieter variant.

## Key attributes

- **Sequential contribution** — one idea per person per round
- **Equal airtime** — all ideas weighted equally regardless of contributor
- **No early evaluation** — building on ideas comes after everyone has shared
- **"Pass" allowed** — but contributor gets time to think for next round
- **Momentum effect** — pace can pick up as the round goes
- **Combinable with index cards** — write privately, then share aloud
- **Best for 5-15 participants** — too small loses structure; too large drags

## When to use

- Teams with mixed extroversion levels where dominance is a known issue
- When you want every voice on record (inclusion is the goal)
- After a verbal-brainstorming session has been dominated by a few people
- For sensitive topics where junior members hesitate to speak first
- Workshops with senior-junior mix where rank suppresses contribution
- Multi-cultural teams where speaking-up norms differ

## When NOT to use

- Very small teams (3-4 people) — overhead exceeds value
- Very large teams (20+) — each round takes too long; use brainwriting instead
- Time-pressured rapid ideation (the round structure slows pace)
- Highly homogeneous extroverted teams who already self-balance

## Common pitfalls

- Letting people interrupt or build out of turn (kills the structure)
- Not handling "Person X already said my idea" — give them time, don't skip
- Anonymous-attribution myth — even with index cards, ideas often trace back
- Limiting building to the neighbor only (loses cross-group recombination)
- Running too many rounds — momentum dies; cap at 3-5 unless ideas are still flowing

## Key metrics

- **Ideas per participant** (target: minimum 2-3 each)
- **Participation rate** (% who contributed at least once — should be 100%)
- **Ideas building on prior ideas** (snowball measure)
- **Session duration vs ideas generated** (efficiency)

## Decision questions

- Do we need the inclusion guarantee, or is verbal brainstorming faster here?
- How many rounds will we run, and how do we know when to stop?
- Will we use written cards or verbal sharing?
- Who facilitates and how do they enforce turn-taking without being heavy-handed?
- Will building on ideas be "neighbor-only" or "any-prior"?
