# Logistics and Operations Plan Section

## Definition

The logistics and operations plan section of a business plan describes the physical and procedural reality of how the company actually runs. Pereira frames it as "the nuts and bolts of making ideas come to fruition" (p. 44) and decomposes it into six sub-areas: **suppliers** (where raw materials come from), **production** (manufacture, wholesale, drop-ship, or hybrid), **facilities** (physical or virtual), **equipment** (tools and technology), **shipping and fulfillment** (in-house or third party), and **inventory** (how much, stored where, tracked how). It answers the operational reader's question: "Once funded, what actually happens on Monday morning?"

## Key attributes

- **Six sub-areas** — suppliers, production, facilities, equipment, shipping/fulfillment, inventory
- **Make-vs-buy decisions** — explicit choices on what is in-house vs. outsourced
- **Sequencing matters** — order of operations and timelines are spelled out
- **Resource allocation** — names who is involved at each step
- **Equipment list** — major capex (office furniture) through minor (pens, paper clips)
- **Capacity planning** — process must scale with order influx
- **Vendor evaluation** — reliability and quality of suppliers researched, not assumed
- **Inventory math** — quantity, location, tracking method specified

## When to use

- Inside business plans for physical-product, retail, or manufacturing companies
- For e-commerce businesses (especially when choosing drop-ship vs. in-house fulfillment)
- For service businesses where delivery logistics matter (consulting, healthcare, logistics)
- Before scaling — operational gaps surface here first
- For investor diligence — VCs probe operational defensibility
- For bank loans where collateral and physical assets matter

## When NOT to use

- For pure-software SaaS where there is no physical fulfillment (replace with "infrastructure plan")
- As a substitute for actual operational standard operating procedures (SOPs)
- When the team has zero operational experience and is guessing — get operator input first

## Common pitfalls

- **Vague supplier identification** — "we'll source from China" is not a plan
- **No capacity stress test** — what happens when orders 10x in a week?
- **Skipping equipment minutiae** — small items add up; underbudget kills cash flow
- **Confusing in-house with cheaper** — sometimes 3PL is the right answer
- **No backup suppliers** — single-source risk is operational debt
- **Inventory carrying cost ignored** — holding inventory ties up cash
- **No fulfillment SLA** — promising delivery times without operational ability to hit them
- **Facilities mismatch** — leasing space before validating demand

## Key metrics

- **On-time delivery rate** — % of orders shipped on promised date
- **Inventory turnover** — COGS / average inventory; higher = leaner
- **Supplier lead time** — average days from order to receipt
- **Cost per unit** — fully loaded, including fulfillment
- **Fulfillment cost as % of revenue** — operational efficiency indicator
- **Capacity utilization** — current throughput vs. maximum
- **Stockout rate** — % of orders that can't be fulfilled from current inventory

## Decision questions

- Do we manufacture, wholesale, drop-ship, or some combination?
- Where is our single point of failure in the supply chain?
- Can our current process handle a 10x order influx?
- Is our physical space matched to current and 12-month demand?
- Have we identified backup suppliers for every critical input?
- Are we tracking inventory in a way that surfaces stockouts before they happen?
- Have we estimated all equipment costs, including the minor items that add up?
