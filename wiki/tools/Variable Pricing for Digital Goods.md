# Variable Pricing for Digital Goods

## Definition

Variable pricing for digital goods is the practice of pricing each item in a digital catalog at a different price point based on attributes — popularity, length, genre, demand, vintage, author/creator reputation, exclusivity — rather than applying a single flat rate across the catalog. Pereira holds up Amazon Kindle e-books as the canonical example: titles range from free promotional offers to $20+ for premium new releases, with the underlying logic that zero marginal cost means each title's price should reflect *its* willingness-to-pay rather than the platform's cost. Variable pricing differs from dynamic pricing (same item, different prices at different times or to different users) by varying across the catalog dimension instead of the time/user dimension. It's the natural pricing posture for any large digital catalog where customer perceived-value varies dramatically per item.

## Key attributes

- **Per-item differentiation** — each SKU gets its own price based on its attributes
- **Attribute-driven** — popularity, length, genre, recency, exclusivity, creator brand all factor in
- **Long-tail compatible** — pairs with long-tail catalog economics (different prices for hits vs niche)
- **Free-as-funnel** — selective free items act as entry-point to drive catalog discovery
- **Catalog management complexity** — requires tooling to price thousands of items
- **Creator/publisher tension** — creators may want higher floors than the platform's optimization
- **Data-intensive** — needs sales velocity, comparable-item, and demand signals per SKU
- **Different from dynamic** — varies across catalog, not across time or user (though both often layered)

## When to use

- Large digital catalogs (e-book stores, music libraries, course marketplaces, stock-media platforms)
- Marketplaces where individual creators/publishers contribute heterogeneous content
- Platforms with strong long-tail dynamics (most revenue from many obscure items)
- Categories with significant perceived-value variance per item (e.g., bestsellers vs backlist)
- When the platform has rich demand data to optimize per-item pricing
- Indie/creator platforms where the platform sets pricing rules and creators set price within bands

## When NOT to use

- Single-product or small-catalog businesses (overhead exceeds benefit)
- Categories with uniform perceived value (e.g., enterprise SaaS modules priced identically)
- Brand-led products where consistent pricing reinforces brand positioning
- When creators have strong contractual control of their pricing (rules out platform-led variability)
- Without analytics infrastructure to track per-item performance
- When customers expect catalog-wide uniformity (e.g., flat-rate subscription model)

## Common pitfalls

- Pricing every title the same despite massive demand variance (revenue left on the table)
- Pricing too high on long-tail items so they never sell (vs reasonable variable pricing that captures niche demand)
- Pricing too low on hits to "look competitive" (sacrifices margin on highest-value SKUs)
- No regular re-pricing — set-and-forget loses to dynamic market conditions
- Treating free items as pure loss leaders without measuring funnel conversion
- Ignoring creator/publisher relationships when platform pricing decisions hurt them
- Confusion between variable (per-item) and dynamic (per-time/user) pricing in internal language
- Over-using deep discounts on otherwise-strong items, eroding category anchor

## Key metrics

- **Average revenue per title (ARPT)** — catalog-level optimization indicator
- **Revenue distribution curve** — % of revenue from top 10% / 20% / 50% of catalog (long-tail measure)
- **Price elasticity per segment** — how much demand changes per $1 price change, per genre/category
- **Conversion rate by price band** — does $9.99 e-book convert at 2x rate of $14.99?
- **Long-tail revenue share** — % of revenue from items outside top 100
- **Free-item conversion-to-paid** — funnel effectiveness of promotional pricing
- **Per-creator revenue distribution** — equity check on platform-led pricing
- **Refund rate by price band** — high prices may signal misaligned value

## Decision questions

- Does our catalog have enough perceived-value variance to justify per-item pricing complexity?
- Do we have the tooling to manage thousands of price points effectively?
- What's our framework for deciding price per item — popularity, length, genre, recency?
- How often should we re-price the catalog, and what triggers a price change?
- Are our free items earning their keep as funnel entry, or just cannibalizing paid sales?
- Are we balancing platform revenue optimization against creator/publisher fairness?
- What's our anchor protection — keeping reference prices consistent enough to avoid devaluation?

## Canonical examples

- **Amazon Kindle e-books** — variable pricing per title; some free, some $20+; dynamic algorithm using demand, length, genre (page 119-121)
- **Steam game catalog** — per-game pricing varying massively from indie $0.99 to AAA $69.99
- **Udemy course catalog** — courses range $9.99 to $199, with frequent variable discounting
- **Spotify podcasts and audiobooks (paid)** — per-title pricing on individual purchases
- **Stock photo platforms (Shutterstock, Getty)** — per-image pricing varying by license, resolution, exclusivity
- **App Store / Google Play** — apps priced anywhere from free to $50+ based on category and developer choice
- **Kindle Direct Publishing** — authors set per-title prices within platform bands (royalty curves change at price thresholds)
