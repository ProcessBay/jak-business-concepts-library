# Tiered Service Packages

## Definition

Tiered service packages present the buyer with two to four named service tiers (Bronze/Silver/Gold, Starter/Pro/Enterprise, Basic/Standard/Premium) at distinct price points, each with progressively richer scope. Pereira describes the tiered pricing model as the "good, better, best approach... offering multiple service plans with varying levels of features and benefits at different price points" (page 81) and walks through HubSpot's three-tier productized service ladder (pages 87-90) and Salesforce's four-edition structure (page 37) as the canonical templates. For service providers — particularly agencies and consultancies — tiered packaging is the bridge between bespoke pricing (where every deal is negotiated) and pure productization (where every deal is identical). It accommodates buyer diversity without exploding operational complexity.

## Key attributes

- **2-4 named tiers** — fewer than 2 isn't a ladder; more than 4 confuses buyers
- **Each tier is a distinct buyer profile** — not just "more of the same" but a different ideal customer
- **Anchoring effect** — the top tier makes the middle tier look reasonable (decoy pricing)
- **Most buyers cluster in the middle** — design the middle tier as the intended bullseye
- **Upgrade path built in** — Bronze buyers should have a visible reason to ladder up to Silver
- **Each tier is internally deliverable** — junior team can run Bronze; senior team can run Gold
- **Listed pricing on top tiers** — "Enterprise: contact us" is acceptable on the highest tier only
- **Add-on layer** — customization happens via named add-ons, not by negotiating the tier

## When to use

- The firm has 3+ distinct buyer profiles (small / mid / large; basic / strategic / transformational)
- Sales conversations are stuck in custom-proposal mode that drags out close cycles
- Productized offer needs a way to handle clients who need slightly more or slightly less
- Retainer book needs structure — three tiered retainer levels beat fifty bespoke retainers
- Top-of-funnel optimization — listed tiers let prospects self-select before sales engagement

## When NOT to use

- Every engagement is genuinely custom (top-end strategy work)
- The firm only has one ideal customer profile (one tier is honest; three is fake)
- Tiers would be transparently identical except for arbitrary feature gating (buyers see this)
- The firm doesn't have the operational maturity to deliver consistently within each tier
- Procurement-heavy buyer requires a custom proposal regardless of public tiers

## Common pitfalls

- **Tiers without distinct ICPs** — three tiers that all serve the same buyer collapse into negotiation
- **Cheap top tier** — top tier should be uncomfortably premium; that's what makes the middle tier work
- **No bullseye** — middle tier needs to be obviously the best value; otherwise tier collapse
- **Feature-gating that feels punitive** — withholding a small feature to force an upgrade breeds resentment
- **Stale tier definitions** — tier scope needs annual review as market and capabilities shift
- **Misaligned delivery** — selling Gold but staffing it like Silver erodes trust faster than anything else
- **No upgrade path** — Bronze buyers should hit natural ceilings that prompt upgrade, not just churn
- **Hidden tier (custom enterprise) cannibalizing structure** — if 80% of revenue is "custom," the tiers are theater

## Key metrics

- **Tier mix** — % revenue in each tier (healthy mix: ~15% bottom / ~60% middle / ~25% top)
- **ARPU** — average revenue per customer across all tiers
- **Conversion rate by tier** — what % of inquiries land in each tier
- **Upgrade rate** — % of buyers that ladder up within 12 months
- **Downgrade / churn rate** — % that move down or leave
- **Top-tier attach rate** — % of new sales that take the premium tier
- **Add-on attach rate** — % of buyers that purchase add-ons beyond the base tier

## Decision questions

- Do our three tiers serve three different buyer profiles, or just three different budgets for the same buyer?
- Is the middle tier clearly the bullseye, with the top tier as a decoy anchor?
- Where do current bespoke deals fall — could 80% be served by one of these tiers?
- What's the upgrade trigger from each tier to the next, and is it visible to the buyer?
- Are we delivering each tier at the staffing model we promised, or quietly under-staffing the lower tiers?

## Canonical examples

- **HubSpot Starter / Professional / Enterprise** — tiered productized service ladder (pages 87-90); each tier targets a distinct stage of business growth
- **Salesforce Essentials / Professional / Enterprise / Unlimited** — four-tier service edition structure (page 37)
- **TechSolutions Starter / Pro / Enterprise** (Pereira composite) — Small Teams vs Enterprise Clients segmentation drives tier design (pages 33-35)
- **ClassPass Base / Flex / Unlimited** — service tier packages bundled with usage flexibility (pages 77-78)
- **AT&T Unlimited Starter / Extra / Elite** — telecom service tiers (page 52-53)
- **Netflix Basic / Standard / Premium** — streaming service tiers (page 100)
- **Agency retainer ladders (Bronze / Silver / Gold)** — common PR, design, and marketing-agency structure
