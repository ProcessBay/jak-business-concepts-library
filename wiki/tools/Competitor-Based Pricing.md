# Competitor-Based Pricing

## Definition

Competitor-based pricing sets price by reference to competitor prices — at, slightly below, or slightly above the going market rate — rather than by reference to cost or perceived value. Pereira describes it as most effective when many substitutes exist and a product has reached market equilibrium. The simplest implementation: calculate the average competitor price and price at it (parity), under it (undercut), or above it (premium positioning).

## Key attributes

- **External price reference** — competitor prices, not internal cost or value
- **Three positioning options** — at parity, below (undercut), above (premium signal)
- **Simple to implement** — just monitor competitor prices
- **Market-responsive** — moves when competitors move
- **Best for substitute-rich markets** — commodities, basic SaaS, retail staples
- **Risk of price war** — undercut invites response invites further undercut
- **Ignores cost and value** — may trap you in unprofitable parity pricing

## When to use

- Substitute-rich commodity categories where price is the primary buying criterion
- Mature markets at equilibrium with little differentiation
- Retail and e-commerce with transparent competitor pricing
- When you have a non-price differentiator (service, returns, loyalty) to layer on
- When entering a market where you don't yet know your willingness-to-pay

## When NOT to use

- You have genuine differentiation worth premium pricing
- Cost structure is meaningfully different from competitors (may be unprofitable at their price)
- Innovative or unique products with no real comparables
- Premium-brand positioning that requires price separation
- B2B with custom value propositions priced on outcome

## Common pitfalls

- Triggering price wars by undercutting deeper-pocketed competitors
- Anchoring on a weak or struggling competitor as your reference
- Forgetting your own cost structure — matching unprofitable competitor prices
- Letting competitors set your strategy (you become reactive, never proactive)
- Commoditizing yourself by competing only on price
- Misreading competitor prices that include bundles, discounts, or hidden fees

## Key metrics

- **Price index vs. competitors** (your price ÷ competitor avg)
- **Market share** (the supposed payoff)
- **Win rate by price position** (parity vs. undercut vs. premium)
- **Gross margin** (the price you pay for parity)
- **Competitor price-change frequency** (informs monitoring cadence)

## Decision questions

- Do we genuinely have nothing to differentiate on except price?
- What's our reference set — the median, the leader, the cheapest?
- Can we sustain our margin at the competitor price, given our cost structure?
- What happens to share and margin if we price 5% above or below the reference?
- Are we leading or following — and if following, why?
