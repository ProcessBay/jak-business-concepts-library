# Pay-What-You-Want Pricing

## Definition

Pay-What-You-Want (PWYW) is a pricing mechanism where the buyer sets the price they pay for a product or service, with the seller accepting whatever amount is offered (including zero in some implementations). Pereira frames it as a behavioral-economics-driven approach that surfaces willingness-to-pay variance directly — and often performs surprisingly well in practice when social norms, transparency about cost, and customer trust are present. Variants include Name-Your-Own-Price (NYOP), where buyer's offer is accepted only if it meets a hidden reserve (e.g., Priceline).

## Key attributes

- **Buyer-set price** — customer chooses what to pay
- **Behavioral mechanic** — leverages reciprocity, fairness norms, social pressure
- **Variants** — PWYW (any price accepted), NYOP (price must clear hidden reserve), minimum-floor PWYW
- **Performs best with** — visible cost transparency, social context (in-person), trust in seller
- **Revenue surprise** — average price often exceeds expectations when norms are set well
- **Pure PWYW vs. tip-on-top** — some businesses set a base price and accept tips above

## When to use

- Digital goods with zero marginal cost (music albums, e-books)
- Experimental pricing for new products (price discovery)
- Charity-tied offerings (B1G1, social cause)
- Restaurants experimenting with community pricing
- Recovery-stage businesses building customer goodwill

## When NOT to use

- Products with high per-unit cost where customers paying zero would break the business
- Categories with hostile or transactional customer base (people will pay zero)
- B2B procurement contexts (formal RFPs require fixed pricing)
- When social/community context is absent (online anonymous transactions tend toward zero)

## Common pitfalls

- Underestimating freeloaders — some always pay zero; need cost structure to absorb that
- Failing to anchor — without a suggested price, customers undershoot
- Pure PWYW for high-cost goods without minimum floor
- Mistaking PWYW success in one context (music album launch) for general viability
- Not communicating cost transparency — without context customers can't calibrate

## Key metrics

- Average price paid (vs. price you'd have charged)
- Conversion rate (% who paid at all)
- % paying zero (vs. paying something)
- Revenue vs. fixed-pricing estimate
- Repeat purchase rate (does PWYW build relationship?)

## Decision questions

- Is our marginal cost low enough to absorb a meaningful % of zero-payers?
- What anchor (suggested price, cost transparency, social cue) will we provide?
- Is this a one-shot experiment or a sustainable pricing strategy?
- What does the data tell us about variance in willingness to pay?

## Canonical examples

- **Radiohead — In Rainbows (2007)** — album released as PWYW; raised significant revenue + huge PR
- **Humble Bundle** — game/software bundles at PWYW with charity split
- **NYC restaurants** experimenting with PWYW (some sustained, most reverted)
- **Wikipedia donations** — annual fundraising via PWYW model
- **Panera Cares (closed)** — PWYW cafes; piloted social mission
