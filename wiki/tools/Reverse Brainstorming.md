# Reverse Brainstorming

## Definition

Reverse brainstorming inverts a problem before attempting to solve it: instead of asking "how do we solve X?", the team asks "how would we **cause** X?" or "how could we make this problem **worse**?" The ideas generated are then flipped back into solutions. Pereira positions it as a way to break out of solution-fixation — "it's quicker to begin with what not to do than with what to do, because not knowing what to do can leave us feeling stuck." The technique is especially powerful for surfacing hidden flaws (security teams use it as "how would I attack this?") and for breaking through when direct ideation has gone flat. It works because the human brain finds it easier to be specific about failure modes than about success states.

## Key attributes

- **Invert the problem** — ask how to cause it, not how to solve it
- **Two-phase output** — generate bad ideas, then reverse them into good ones
- **Surfaces hidden risks** — failure modes that direct ideation misses
- **Breaks solution-fixation** — useful when the team is stuck
- **Light setup** — same materials as classic brainstorming; only the prompt differs
- **Facilitator-critical** — must enforce "stay in the bad-ideas phase"

## When to use

- When direct brainstorming has produced no useful ideas
- For risk assessment, security review, and red-team exercises
- For QA/UX reviews — "how could a user fail at this?"
- For positioning — "what would the worst possible ad copy look like?" (then reverse)
- For sales — "who would be our worst customer?" (then reverse for ICP)
- Workshop energy boost — the inversion is fun and lowers stakes

## When NOT to use

- When the team is fragile or politically tense (the bad-ideas phase can feel attacking)
- When the problem is genuinely simple and inversion adds overhead
- For root-cause analysis (use 5 Whys or Fishbone instead)
- When you need consensus, not options

## Common pitfalls

- Drifting into solution-mode during the bad-ideas phase (kills the technique)
- Failing to reverse — leaving the team with a depressing list of failure modes
- Inverting the wrong question (start with a clear, narrow problem statement)
- Skipping evaluation after the reverse step (bad ideas don't all map to good ones cleanly)

## Key metrics

- **Bad ideas generated** (richness of the failure space)
- **Reverse-conversion rate** (% of bad ideas that produce viable solutions)
- **Risk factors newly surfaced** (vs. what direct brainstorming missed)

## Decision questions

- Is our problem better stated as a goal or as something to avoid?
- Can we frame the inversion concretely ("how do we lose all customers in 30 days"?)
- Who's the facilitator who can keep the team in failure-mode long enough?
- Do we follow with prioritization or roll outputs into a different framework?

## Canonical examples

- **Security testing** — companies hire hackers to attack their own networks (page 61)
- **Customer ICP** — list your worst possible customer to surface your best leads (page 62)
- **Ad copy** — write the worst possible copy to learn tone and language to use (page 62)
- **Team failure analysis** — define what failure looks like to inform future plans (page 62)
- **Risk discovery** — "how could this fail?" as a planning question (page 61)
