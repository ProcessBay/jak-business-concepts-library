# Franchise Fee Structure

## Definition

The franchise fee structure is the layered set of payments a franchisee makes to a franchisor in exchange for the right to use the franchise system. Pereira documents the standard three-layer structure: (1) initial franchise fee — a one-time payment at signing covering training, site selection support, and the right to use the brand; (2) ongoing royalty fee — typically 4-8% of gross sales paid monthly, funding ongoing franchisor support; (3) advertising/marketing fund contribution — typically 1-4% of gross sales paid into a pooled fund for national or regional campaigns. Beyond these, franchisees face equipment costs, inventory, lease/rent, training fees, renewal fees, technology fees, and potential fines or mandatory supplier purchase markups. The structure shapes unit economics on both sides: franchisor revenue and the franchisee's path to profitability.

## Key attributes

- **Initial franchise fee** — one-time at signing; $10K-$50K typical for small concepts, $1M+ for hotels
- **Royalty fee** — typically 4-8% of gross sales (some fixed-fee or minimum-royalty structures)
- **Advertising/marketing fund** — typically 1-4% of gross sales into pooled fund
- **Equipment and build-out costs** — kitchen equipment, fixtures, signage, technology
- **Inventory and supplies** — initial stocking plus ongoing replenishment from approved suppliers
- **Lease or rental costs** — varies dramatically by format and location
- **Training fees** — sometimes included in initial fee, sometimes separate
- **Renewal fees** — at end of term when extending the franchise agreement
- **Hidden fees** — technology, software updates, mandatory marketing programs, fines for non-compliance, supplier markups

## When to use

- Setting up a new franchise system — calibrating each fee layer to fund operations while leaving franchisees profitable
- Evaluating a franchise opportunity as a prospective franchisee — modeling true cost of ownership
- Benchmarking against competitor franchise systems
- Negotiating modifications (rare but possible for territory size, initial fee, or specific support inclusions)
- Restructuring royalty rates when system economics shift

## When NOT to use

- N/A — fee structures are inherent to any franchise system
- Caution: avoid setting royalty rates too low to fund adequate support, or too high to crush franchisee profitability — both kill the system

## Common pitfalls

- Initial fee priced too high; suppresses prospect conversion
- Royalty rate too low; franchisor can't fund support; system stagnates
- Royalty rate too high; franchisee margins compress; defaults rise
- Advertising fund opaque; franchisees suspect misallocation; trust erodes
- Hidden fees (technology, mandatory supplies) surprise franchisees; relationship damaged
- Failure to account for total initial investment (not just franchise fee) when prospecting
- Renewal fees set high enough to discourage renewal; valuable franchisees exit

## Key metrics

- Initial franchise fee
- Ongoing royalty rate (%)
- Advertising fee rate (%)
- Total initial investment (franchise fee + build-out + working capital)
- Franchisee gross margin after royalties and ad fees
- Franchisee payback period
- Franchisee net profit margin
- Royalty revenue per unit per year

## Decision questions

- Does our royalty rate fund the franchisee support our system actually requires?
- Are we transparent about ALL fees in the FDD, including hidden technology and supply fees?
- What payback period does a typical franchisee experience, and is it attractive vs. alternatives?
- Where do we sit on royalty vs. competitor benchmarks in our category?
- Can our franchisees survive economic downturns at current fee levels?

## Canonical examples

- **McDonald's** — initial fee ~$45K, royalty ~4% of sales, marketing fee ~4%, plus rent (McDonald's typically owns the real estate)
- **Subway** — initial fee ~$15K, royalty 8% of sales, marketing fee 4.5% — among the higher royalty rates in QSR
- **Dunkin'** — initial fee $40K-$90K, royalty 5.9%, advertising fee 5%
- **7-Eleven** — variable initial fee (some convert-existing-store models), royalty as % of gross profit (not sales) — distinctive structure (page 234)
- **Anytime Fitness** — initial fee ~$42K, royalty $699/month (fixed-fee model), marketing fee $499/month
- **Marriott** — hotel franchise initial fee $90K-$120K + ongoing royalty 5-7% of room revenue + marketing fee
