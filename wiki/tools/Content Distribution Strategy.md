# Content Distribution Strategy

## Definition

Content distribution strategy is the deliberate plan for getting created content — articles, videos, e-books, podcasts, infographics, case studies, webinars — in front of the right audiences via the right channels. Pereira organizes the discipline into a three-channel framework: **owned** (your blog, website, social accounts, email list, app — full editorial control), **earned** (PR, guest posts, organic shares, reviews, mentions on Reddit/Quora — won by being shareworthy), and **paid** (PPC, sponsored content, paid influencer deals, paid social ads — bought reach). The strategy answers the founding insight that "it is irrelevant how well-written your content is if no one is going to read it." Pereira reports 2019 market share for content distribution channels: influencer marketing 26%, email marketing 18% (tied), organic social 18%, guest blogging 16%, paid distribution 14%, content distribution platforms 4%. A working strategy researches the audience, audits existing content, selects channels by fit, sets KPIs, builds an editorial calendar, executes, and measures — then iterates.

## Key attributes

- **Three-channel framework** — owned, earned, paid (each with distinct mechanics and economics)
- **Audience-research foundation** — demographics, behaviors, channel preferences before distribution
- **Content audit** — inventory and grade existing assets before producing new ones
- **Channel-content fit** — different content types need different distribution paths
- **Editorial calendar** — coordinates production and distribution across channels
- **Measurement infrastructure** — KPIs per channel, attribution model, iteration loop
- **Paid-organic-earned interplay** — paid amplifies organic; earned validates both
- **Diminishing-return discipline** — over-investment in one channel rarely pays off

## When to use

- Any content-led growth motion (inbound marketing, SEO, thought leadership)
- B2B SaaS where buyer education drives pipeline
- Consumer brands building category authority (DTC, finance, wellness)
- Media companies and publishers (distribution is the business)
- Personal brands and creator economy
- Pre-launch and launch sequences for new products
- Reputation rehabilitation after PR incidents

## When NOT to use

- Pure transactional businesses with no consideration phase (commodity utilities, basic FMCG)
- When you have nothing genuinely useful to say (distribution amplifies whatever exists, including emptiness)
- Categories where audience attention lives entirely outside content channels
- When the firm cannot fund consistent content production (sporadic content underperforms)

## Common pitfalls

- Producing without a plan to distribute — content lands and dies
- Over-reliance on a single channel (algorithm changes wipe out reach)
- Confusing reach with engagement, or engagement with conversion
- Paying for distribution before fixing content quality
- Treating influencer marketing as a list of accounts rather than a relationship strategy
- Ignoring the analytics loop (no measurement → no improvement)
- Hitting "Publish" and calling it distribution
- Cross-posting identical content to every channel rather than adapting per channel
- Underinvesting in evergreen content while chasing trends

## Key metrics

- **Organic reach** (impressions, unique viewers per piece per channel)
- **Paid reach + cost-per-thousand impressions** (CPM)
- **Earned mentions** (backlinks, citations, organic shares)
- **Share rate** (% of viewers who share)
- **Click-through rate** (CTR) per channel
- **Conversion rate** (newsletter signup, demo, purchase) from content
- **Cost per engaged user** (paid channels especially)
- **Content ROI** (revenue attributed to content vs. fully loaded content cost)
- **Time-to-rank** (SEO) and **rank-share** (Share of Voice in target queries)

## Decision questions

- Who is our content actually for, and what channels do they already trust?
- Are we publishing more than we are distributing? (Most B2B firms are.)
- Which of our existing content pieces deserves more distribution rather than new production?
- What's our owned-earned-paid mix today — and does it match our budget and goals?
- Are we measuring conversion or only vanity reach?
- Have we built relationships with influencers, or just sent cold pitches?
