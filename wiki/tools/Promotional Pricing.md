# Promotional Pricing

## Definition

Promotional pricing temporarily reduces a product's price for a short, well-defined window to attract customers, clear inventory, build customer base, or respond to competitive pressure. Unlike penetration pricing (a long-term market entry play) or dynamic pricing (a continuous algorithm), promotional pricing is **time-bound and intentional** — a Black Friday, a happy hour, a flash sale, an end-of-season clearance. Pereira's central warning: it works tactically but, used too often, trains customers to wait for discounts and devalues the brand.

## Key attributes

- **Time-bound** — finite window with a clear start and end
- **Tactical, not strategic** — temporary, not the default price
- **Percentage discounts often outperform flat-amount discounts perceptually** (page 207)
- **Multiple channels** — flash sales, coupons, BOGO, happy hour, seasonal clearance
- **Inventory and traffic driver** — moves slow stock, drives store visits
- **Brand-risk vehicle** — chronic promos devalue brand
- **Best when paired with other tactics** — anchor, bundle, loyalty

## When to use

- Inventory clearance (end-of-season, expiring stock, model refresh)
- New market or customer-base entry (trial driver)
- Competitive response to a rival's promo
- Seasonal demand peaks (Black Friday, Ramadan, back-to-school)
- Loyalty rewards or VIP-segment activations
- Cash flow acceleration in tight quarters

## When NOT to use

- Premium-brand positioning that depends on price stability
- When you've promoted so frequently customers no longer buy at full price
- Categories where the margin can't absorb the discount profitably
- When the promotion will trigger a price war you can't win
- When operational capacity can't handle the promo-driven volume

## Common pitfalls

- Running promos so frequently that "regular price" becomes irrelevant
- Failing to model whether incremental volume offsets margin loss (Pereira's example: revenue up, profit down, page 210)
- Devaluing the brand by chronic discounting
- Triggering competitor counter-promotions and unintentional price war
- Letting promo become permanent (creep)
- Failing to communicate the discount mechanism clearly

## Key metrics

- **Sales lift** during promotional window vs. baseline
- **AOV** during promo (bundle attach often rises)
- **Profit margin change** (volume gain × new margin vs. lost margin × baseline volume)
- **Redemption rate** (% of intended audience that converted)
- **Brand perception drift** (the watchlist for chronic-promo brands)
- **Post-promo demand decay** (does demand drop below baseline after?)

## Decision questions

- What's the specific objective — inventory, trial, response, cash, loyalty?
- Is the projected sales lift enough to make up for the per-unit margin loss?
- How often have we promoted this product recently — are we training waiting behavior?
- What's the competitor's likely response, and can we sustain a counter-move?
- How will we end the promotion cleanly without disappointing customers?

## Canonical examples

- **Black Friday** — Walmart, Best Buy heavy promotional pricing to drive traffic (page 209)
- **Happy hour** — restaurants/bars discount drinks during slow hours (page 209)
- **Apple seasonal discounts** — product launches and holiday promotions to clear older inventory (page 209)
- **Amazon flash sales** — limited-time deep discounts driving urgency (page 209)
- **GlobeTravel personalized flash sales** — 20% more successful for military personnel (page 207)
