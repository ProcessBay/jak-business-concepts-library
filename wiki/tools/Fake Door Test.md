# Fake Door Test

## Definition

A Fake Door test (also called "audience-building MVP" or "MVP before the MVP") is a low-fidelity validation tactic where you advertise a feature or product that doesn't exist yet, then measure how many people try to engage with it — by clicking, signing up, or attempting to purchase. The "door" looks real, but when opened it reveals a message that the functionality is "under construction" or asks the user to enter their email to be notified. Pereira positions it as a way to "gauge interest in a theoretical feature, without having to build that feature or product." The user's click is a real behavioral signal of latent demand.

## Key attributes

- **The offer doesn't exist** — but the user doesn't know that at click-time
- **Behavioral signal, not stated preference** — measures what people DO, not what they say
- **Email capture as the gentle redirect** — turns disappointed clickers into a waitlist
- **Cheap to deploy** — a CTA, a redirect page, and email capture
- **Can be embedded in an existing product** — test a feature within your live UI
- **Ethically delicate** — you're showing something that isn't real; framing matters
- **Distinct from Landing Page MVP** — Fake Door specifically tests a feature against an existing offer or product

## When to use

- Inside an existing product, to test demand for a new feature before building it
- For pricing-tier validation (test if a Premium plan would convert before building it)
- When stakeholders disagree about whether to build feature X
- When engineering cost of the feature is high and demand is uncertain
- For prioritizing among multiple proposed features (run several Fake Doors, compare conversion)

## When NOT to use

- For your overall company offer (use Landing Page MVP instead)
- In contexts where the user reaction to discovering the door is fake would damage trust badly (high-trust B2B, regulated)
- When you can't follow up with the people who clicked (the email list is the whole asset)
- When the feature is small enough to just build and A/B test
- For features that depend on viral/network effects (the test misrepresents the experience)

## Common pitfalls

- **No follow-up on the waitlist** — collected emails go cold, opportunity wasted
- **Negative user experience** — users feel tricked if the "under construction" framing is hostile
- **Confusing click with intent** — many users click out of curiosity, not real demand
- **Low traffic = no signal** — small samples produce noisy conversion data
- **Not pre-committing a conversion threshold** — moving goalposts after seeing data
- **Running too many Fake Doors at once** — feature-test fatigue erodes user trust
- **Misattributing the click** — was it the feature concept, the badge color, or the location?

## Key metrics

- Click-through rate on the fake door
- Email capture rate among those who clicked
- Compared baseline conversion vs the surrounding product
- Cost per validated lead (if running via paid traffic)
- Source-level breakdown if you're testing multiple variants

## Decision questions

- What conversion threshold, set in advance, would justify building this feature?
- Is the click-driving signal coming from real intent — or from misleading positioning?
- What's our promise to people who clicked, and can we honor it?
- Are we testing the feature, the description, or the call-to-action — and have we isolated which?
- Do we have a polite, trust-preserving redirect message ready?

## Canonical examples

- **Premium subscription test (Pereira illustration, page 32-33)** — landing page with full premium-plan description, CTA leads to "under construction" message with email capture; measures willingness to pay for the upgraded tier before building it
- **Buffer feature tests** — historically used Fake Doors inside the app to test which premium features customers would click on
- **Many B2C SaaS** — embedded "Coming Soon" CTAs for new features to gauge demand pre-build
