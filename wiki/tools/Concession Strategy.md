# Concession Strategy

## Definition

Concession strategy is the deliberate planning and sequencing of give-and-take moves that move the negotiation toward agreement without surrendering value unnecessarily. Pereira documents a layered approach: (1) **Prioritize interests over positions** — concede on demands that don't matter to your underlying interest; (2) **Trading and packaging** — exchange concessions across multiple issues, leveraging asymmetric preferences (give what they value more, receive what you value more); (3) **Concession planning** — pre-rank issues by importance so you know which concessions are cheap and which are costly before you enter the room; (4) **Incremental concessions** — make gradual, measured moves to invite reciprocity and signal good faith; (5) **Nibbling** — small additional asks just before close (use sparingly — overuse damages trust); (6) **Flinch** — visible surprise/disappointment that reframes an offer as unreasonable; (7) **Alternative options** — keep optionality alive throughout; (8) **Deadlines and time pressure** — used responsibly to drive movement; (9) **Contingent agreements** — concessions tied to specific conditions being met. The unifying principle: every concession should be planned, sized, and traded — never given unilaterally.

## Key attributes

- **Pre-rank issues** — know cheap vs. costly concessions before the room
- **Trade, don't give** — concessions should be exchanged, not donated
- **Incremental moves invite reciprocity** — large unilateral moves don't
- **Asymmetric value is the key** — your low-cost concession may be their high-value gain
- **Nibbling is risky** — late-stage small asks work occasionally, erode trust if abused
- **Flinch is signaling** — visible reaction shifts the perceived range
- **Contingency-link concessions** — "I'll do X if you do Y" preserves leverage

## When to use

- Multi-issue deals where trades create joint value
- Any negotiation where the counterparty has made an opening move
- Closing-stage when both sides need to find the last bridge
- When relationship preservation matters and unilateral toughness would damage it
- When you have prepared a concession ladder and know your floor

## When NOT to use

- When the counterparty treats your concessions as proof you'll keep moving
- In pure single-issue price negotiations with no other dimensions (use anchoring instead)
- When you have no BATNA and any concession is a step toward a bad deal
- When the cultural norm punishes flinch or nibble tactics as bad faith
- Early in integrative deals before interests are surfaced (premature concessions reduce trade space)

## Common pitfalls

- Conceding unilaterally without asking for anything in return
- Conceding too much too fast — signals weakness and invites more demands
- Treating all concessions as equivalent (failing to rank them by value)
- Nibbling so much that the counterparty walks away at close
- Using flinch every time — loses signaling power
- Failing to document concession patterns mid-negotiation (lose track of your floor)
- Confusing positional concessions with interest-based trades

## Key metrics

- **Concession count** — number of moves you made
- **Concession size** — magnitude of each move
- **Reciprocity ratio** — value received per value given
- **Value per concession** — joint value created by the trade
- **End position vs. target** — how close to your target outcome you closed
- **Time-to-concession** — how quickly you moved (signal management)

## Decision questions

- Have I ranked every issue by my value vs. their value?
- For every concession I plan to make, what am I asking for in return?
- What is my concession ladder — first move, second move, last move?
- Where am I willing to nibble or flinch, and where would those tactics damage trust?
- What is my walk-away point, and am I tracking concessions against it?
