# Agency Pitch and RFP Process

## Definition

The structured workflow a digital marketing agency runs to convert a qualified prospect or RFP into a signed contract — discovery → value proposition → tailored solution → pitch delivery → objection handling → close → onboarding handoff. Pereira lays out the seven-step pitch model (pages 64–65): understand the client's needs deeply, craft a compelling value proposition, showcase expertise via case studies and KPIs, propose tailor-made solutions, emphasize collaboration and rapport, prepare and rehearse exhaustively, then transition into monitoring and evaluation post-win. The process is what separates agencies that close on capability from agencies that close on relationship — both can win, but only one is repeatable.

## Key attributes

- **Discovery before pitching** — research the industry, competitors, and current marketing efforts (page 64)
- **Tailored, not template** — generic decks lose; bespoke solutions win
- **Proof-heavy** — case studies, KPI-driven results, charts and graphs (page 64)
- **Value proposition front-loaded** — why us, articulated in client language
- **Rapport and collaboration story** — clients buy the team as much as the work
- **Rehearsed delivery** — anticipate objections, prepare responses
- **Handoff plan** — win → onboarding without losing momentum

## When to use

- Inbound qualified leads that have asked for a proposal
- RFP / RFI responses from government or enterprise procurement
- Strategic accounts where existing relationship invites a pitch
- Annual re-pitches by enterprise clients (mandatory in many KSA government accounts)
- Competitive bake-offs against 2–4 other shortlisted agencies

## When NOT to use

- Underqualified leads — pitching costs money; qualify ruthlessly first
- "Free strategy" requests dressed as RFPs from prospects with no budget
- RFPs you can't credibly win (wrong vertical, wrong size, locked incumbent)
- Clients whose buying pattern is transactional and won't reward investment in pitching

## Common pitfalls

- Pitching capabilities instead of client outcomes
- Reusing a deck across pitches — clients see the recycled slides
- Underprepared on the client's industry and current marketing
- No anticipation of pricing objections — winging it on negotiation
- Senior team pitches, junior team delivers — bait-and-switch is fatal
- No defined handoff to account management — wins go cold in week 1
- Treating pitching as a cost center, not measuring win rate or cost-per-pitch

## Key metrics

- **Pitch Win Rate** (overall and by segment / channel / deal size)
- **Average Deal Size** of won pitches
- **Pipeline Velocity** — days from qualified lead to signed contract
- **Cost of Pitch** — hours × loaded rate per pitch
- **Pitch-to-Pipeline Ratio** — how many pitches per closed deal
- **Time-to-First-Value** post-win
- **Re-pitch Rate** for retained accounts

## Decision questions

- Are we qualifying hard enough, or pitching everything that walks in?
- Do we know the client's industry better than the next agency on the shortlist?
- Is our value proposition genuinely differentiated, or generic agency-speak?
- Who pitches vs. who delivers — and is the client aware?
- What's the cost-per-pitch, and is win rate justifying it?
- Do we have a written handoff from pitch to delivery?

## Canonical examples

- **Major KSA government RFPs** — MoCIT, MISA, Vision 2030 marketing tenders — multi-round shortlist process, formal scoring matrices, multiple bidders
- **Memac Ogilvy and M&C Saatchi Riyadh** — formal pitch teams for Aramco, STC, Almarai annual mandates
- **Procurement-led RFPs** for enterprise advertisers — fixed templates, blind evaluation
- **Inbound HubSpot-style pitches** — qualified inbound, decked in custom case studies
- **WebFX / Neil Patel Digital pitch decks** — case-study-heavy, KPI-led narratives matching their data-driven brand (pages 113–116)
- **NTDP grant pitches** — Saudi Tech Park grants follow a formal pitch + business-plan format
