# Market Analysis Section

## Definition

The market analysis section of a business plan establishes whether the opportunity is large enough and well-enough understood to justify the venture. Pereira frames it as four sub-tasks: (1) **size the potential market** (TAM and beyond), (2) **define the Ideal Customer Profile** using demographic and behavioral data, (3) **research industry trends and trajectory** (where the market is heading), (4) **make informed guesses** when complete data isn't available. He then layers a **SWOT analysis** to convert the external view into internal strategic implications. "Without proper market analysis, you'll be flying blind, taking risks that could sink your business before it even gets off the ground" (p. 35).

## Key attributes

- **Four sub-components** — market size, ICP, industry trends, informed guesses
- **SWOT layered on top** — converts external research into strategy
- **Data-grounded** — government data, surveys, third-party research are non-negotiable
- **Forward-looking** — must include trajectory, not just current state
- **Cohort-specific** — ICP includes demographic + behavioral characteristics
- **Honest about uncertainty** — informed guesses are acceptable when labeled
- **Sources cited** — investor credibility depends on traceable claims

## When to use

- Inside any business plan (Traditional, Lean, Nonprofit)
- Before entering a new geographic or vertical market
- When pivoting product positioning to a new segment
- For investor decks where market size is a primary credibility test
- During annual strategic planning to re-baseline assumptions
- When evaluating M&A targets or partnership opportunities

## When NOT to use

- As a substitute for actually talking to customers (research ≠ discovery)
- When the team is using analysis to delay launching a testable product
- For one-off tactical decisions where lightweight customer-dev is enough
- When the market is so emergent that all sizing is fiction (admit it and proceed)

## Common pitfalls

- **Top-down sizing only** — "1% of a $10B market" is a fundraising joke without bottom-up validation
- **No ICP** — listing demographics without behavioral psychographics or buying triggers
- **Stale data** — citing 5-year-old industry reports in a fast-moving market
- **Cherry-picked trends** — only citing tailwinds, ignoring headwinds
- **Unsourced claims** — assertions without footnotes destroy investor trust
- **SWOT as box-ticking** — generic SWOT entries with no strategic implication
- **Confusing TAM with revenue opportunity** — TAM is the ceiling, not the forecast

## Key metrics

- **TAM / SAM / SOM** — total / serviceable / obtainable addressable markets
- **ICP clarity** — can the team name the buyer in one sentence?
- **Industry growth rate cited** — with source and recency
- **Number of independent sources** — minimum 3 for credibility
- **Customer interviews referenced** — primary research, not just secondary
- **SWOT items per quadrant** — 3-5 each, with strategic implications

## Decision questions

- Is the market large enough to support our 5-year revenue ambition?
- Who exactly is our ideal customer — demographic, behavioral, and trigger?
- What are the three biggest industry trends, and which way do they cut?
- Where are we guessing, and what would change our mind?
- Does our SWOT lead to specific strategic moves, or is it decorative?
- Have we sourced every quantitative claim?
