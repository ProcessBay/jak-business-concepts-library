# Financial Plan Section

## Definition

The financial plan section translates the business strategy into three core financial statements: the **income statement** (does the business make a profit?), the **cash-flow statement** (can the business pay its bills when due?), and the **balance sheet** (what does the business own and owe at a point in time?). Pereira treats these as the "three cornerstones" of the plan (p. 47) and breaks the cash-flow statement into seventeen specific components — Cash, Cash Sales, Receivables, Other Income, Total Income, Material/Merchandise, Direct Labor, Overhead, Marketing/Sales, R&D, G&A, Taxes, Capital, Loan Payments, Total Expenses, Cash Flow, Cumulative Cash Flow (p. 48-52). The section answers two questions: is the business profitable, and is it solvent.

## Key attributes

- **Three core statements** — income statement, cash-flow statement, balance sheet
- **Seventeen cash-flow components** — Pereira's full enumeration (Cash through Cumulative Cash Flow)
- **Profitability ≠ solvency** — a profitable business can still die from cash mismanagement
- **Forward-looking + historical** — current state + projections (short and long term)
- **Personal financial statement** — Pereira recommends including for investor confidence (p. 48)
- **Brief analysis attached** — the numbers need narrative interpretation
- **Audience-tuned** — VC focus on growth/margin; banks focus on repayment capacity
- **Monthly cadence** — Pereira recommends regular cash-flow monitoring

## When to use

- Inside every business plan, regardless of variant
- For bank loan applications (the bank's primary read)
- For VC pitches (the credibility check)
- For internal annual budgeting and quarterly forecasting
- For nonprofit grant applications (donor due diligence)
- Before any major capital decision (hiring, facility, equipment)

## When NOT to use

- As a substitute for unit economics analysis (financials roll up; unit econ tells the story)
- When the team treats projections as commitments rather than working models
- When the underlying business model isn't yet understood — fix the model first
- For very early ideation where any projection is fiction (Lean Plan handles this)

## Common pitfalls

- **Hockey-stick projections without basis** — exponential curves with no operational backing
- **Confusing profit with cash** — Pereira flags this as the #5 common mistake (p. 55)
- **Missing the 17th component** — forgetting Cumulative Cash Flow obscures runway
- **No sensitivity analysis** — single-scenario projections aren't credible
- **Optimistic receivables** — assuming customers pay on time when they often don't
- **Underestimating G&A and overhead** — founders systematically undercount
- **No personal financial statement** — investors want to see founder skin in the game (p. 48)
- **Static numbers** — financials never revised against actuals are decorative

## Key metrics

- **Revenue projection accuracy** — plan vs. actual variance by quarter
- **Cash runway (months)** — from current cash + burn rate
- **Gross margin** — revenue minus COGS, as % of revenue
- **Burn rate** — monthly net cash outflow
- **Breakeven month** — when cumulative cash flow turns positive
- **LTV:CAC** — for subscription/SaaS contexts
- **Days Sales Outstanding (DSO)** — receivables velocity
- **Plan-vs-actual variance** — by line item, reviewed monthly

## Decision questions

- Are our projections grounded in unit economics or fantasy?
- What is our actual cash runway under realistic assumptions?
- Have we modeled the case where receivables come in 60 days late?
- Does our financial plan include both upside and stress scenarios?
- Are we monitoring cash flow monthly, as Pereira recommends?
- Have we included a personal financial statement for the founders?
- Does the narrative interpretation match what the numbers actually show?
