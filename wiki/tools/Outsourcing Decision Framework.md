# Outsourcing Decision Framework

## Definition

The Outsourcing Decision Framework is the build-vs-buy filter Pereira documents for deciding which parts of a business should be contracted out. A firm could technically outsource almost everything and slap its brand on the result — but profitability comes from striking the right balance between in-house and contracted work. Pereira offers an eight-question diagnostic, then collapses the answers into two outsourceable task classes: (1) **essential-but-not-core** tasks (necessary to deliver the value proposition but not part of it — e.g., last-mile delivery for an e-commerce firm), and (2) **commodity / non-essential** tasks (janitorial, accounting, helpdesk — low specialization, periodic, non-impactful on the value proposition). The framework is then re-applied inside KPO contracting (pages 42–44) as a sequence: define rationale → prioritize domains → identify vendor candidates → set terms.

## Key attributes

- **Eight-question diagnostic** — essential vs non-essential; best use of time; revenue/efficiency generation; weak spots; rationale; effectiveness; permanence; definability (pages 19–22)
- **Two outsourceable task classes** — essential-but-not-core, and commodity/non-essential (pages 22–23)
- **Core value proposition is sacrosanct** — the unit that delivers the differentiator stays in-house by default
- **Definability test** — if the work cannot be clearly specified (scope, timeframe, cost, quality, comms, resources, outcome), it cannot be outsourced cleanly (page 22)
- **Periodicity test** — temporary or cyclical work outsources more cleanly than permanent operations
- **Comparative-advantage lens** — who has the structural advantage in this task, us or a vendor?
- **Capacity-to-govern test** — does the firm have the management bandwidth to run the vendor relationship? (page 21)

## When to use

- Before any new outsourcing commitment
- When reviewing an existing function for possible outsourcing
- Mid-engagement reviews of underperforming vendor relationships
- Capacity planning during rapid growth (KPO-style capacity surges)
- Cost-pressure exercises where leadership asks "what can we cut?"
- Brand-image or quality-control reviews where outsourcing footprint matters

## When NOT to use

- For one-off, low-stakes vendor decisions where full diagnostic overhead is wasted
- As a justification for predetermined outcomes (cherry-picking answers to support a decision already made)
- In isolation from total-cost-of-ownership analysis
- Without considering reversibility — frameworks that ignore exit cost overstate the case for outsourcing

## Common pitfalls

- Confusing "essential to operations" with "core to value proposition" — most operational tasks are essential without being differentiating
- Treating the diagnostic as multiple-choice rather than evidence-gathering
- Failing the definability test mid-engagement (page 22) — scope creep follows poor spec
- Outsourcing tasks you're bad at without first asking why you're bad (the answer may not be outsource — see page 20)
- Ignoring the capacity-to-govern test — outsourcing without the management muscle creates more work, not less
- Picking vendor before defining rationale and prioritized domains (pages 42–44)

## Key metrics

- **Revenue per hour** generated by this task in-house
- **Comparative advantage score** — qualitative assessment of where structural advantage lies
- **Task definability score** — can scope, timeframe, cost, quality, and outcome be specified? (page 22)
- **Periodicity index** — % of year the function is actively needed
- **Total cost of ownership** — vendor fee + transition + governance + exit
- **Reversibility score** — cost and time to bring the function back in-house

## Decision questions (Pereira's eight)

1. **Essential or non-essential?** Most firms avoid outsourcing essentials; if essential, use stricter oversight (page 19)
2. **Best use of your time?** Cost/time analysis — where do you generate the most revenue per unit time? (page 20)
3. **Directly revenue- or efficiency-generating?** Non-revenue tasks outsource more cleanly (page 20)
4. **What are you bad at but forced to do?** Often the highest-ROI outsourcing candidates (pages 20–21)
5. **Why are you outsourcing?** Cost, expertise, downsizing, or temporary capability gap — each implies a different contract shape (page 21)
6. **Can it be outsourced effectively?** Both task suitability *and* your governance capacity (page 21)
7. **Permanent or temporary?** Temporary or periodic work outsources more cleanly (pages 21–22)
8. **Can it be clearly defined?** Specify scope, timeframe, cost, quality, comms, resources, outcome (page 22)

## KPO best-practice sequence (pages 42–44)

1. **Define rationale** — what improvement, current performance, target performance, alternatives, capacity to manage
2. **Prioritize domains** — by cost, value-prop impact, deficiency, expected ROI
3. **Identify candidates** — fit to spec, references, verifiable history, legal framework, brand alignment
4. **Set terms and manage** — contract, data security, comms cadence, reporting, info dissemination

## Canonical examples

- **E-commerce delivery** — third-party logistics is essential to the model but not the value proposition (page 22)
- **Janitorial, accounting, basic IT** — non-essential commodity work, easy to outsource (pages 22–23)
- **GM under Sloan** — core vs additional functions decomposition led to share growth from 12% to 41% (page 15)
- **Kodak 1989** — outsourced IT to focus resources on core photographic capability (page 16)
