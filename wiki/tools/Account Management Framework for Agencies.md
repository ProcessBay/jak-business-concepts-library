# Account Management Framework for Agencies

## Definition

The agency-side operating system that owns the client relationship after the sale — managing communication cadence, expectations, reporting, escalation, renewal, and expansion. Pereira returns to the discipline repeatedly: effective agency management requires "robust and transparent communication with clients" (page 13), long-term relationships are built on "open, transparent, and frequent communication" with "regular client meetings, progress reports, and timely responses" (page 66), and client reporting must include executive summaries, KPI evidence, analysis, recommendations, and a call-to-action (pages 102–108). Account management is the discipline that converts good delivery into renewing revenue.

## Key attributes

- **Single point of contact** — account lead owns the relationship end-to-end
- **Defined cadence** — weekly check-ins, monthly reports, quarterly business reviews
- **Structured reporting** — executive summary, KPIs, analysis, recommendations, CTA (pages 102–108)
- **Expectation management** — proactive on timelines, blockers, scope changes (page 76)
- **Escalation paths** — clear who handles unhappy clients before they churn
- **Renewal motion** — formal pre-renewal QBR, scope review, repricing conversation
- **Expansion motion** — channel attach, new service introductions, referrals

## When to use

- Every retainer or multi-month engagement (project work needs lighter version)
- Strategic accounts above a defined revenue threshold
- Accounts at risk (drop in usage, low engagement scores, missed meetings)
- Pre-renewal windows (90 / 60 / 30 days before contract end)
- Cross-sell moments — case-study quality wins, new agency capability launches

## When NOT to use

- One-off transactional projects (handoff at delivery, no ongoing AM)
- When the client explicitly wants a lighter touch (some sophisticated buyers prefer email-only)
- Below a profitability threshold where AM cost exceeds account margin

## Common pitfalls

- Account leads who become order-takers instead of strategic advisors
- Reports that are data-dumps with no narrative or recommendations
- Surprises in QBRs — issues should never first surface in a quarterly review
- Single point of failure — one account lead leaves, client churns
- Account leads incentivized only on retention, not on expansion
- Sending raw analytics dashboards as "the report" — clients want interpretation, not raw data (page 104)
- Calling weekly when there's nothing to say, killing the value of the call

## Key metrics

- **Client Retention Rate** (logo and revenue)
- **Net Revenue Retention** (NRR) — expansion exceeds churn target >100%
- **Retainer Renewal Rate** by AM
- **Client Satisfaction Score** (CSAT) or **NPS**
- **Average Revenue Per Account** (and growth)
- **Time-to-Issue-Resolution**
- **Expansion Revenue per AM**

## Decision questions

- Does every strategic account have a named AM with renewal accountability?
- Is our reporting cadence aligned to how the client actually consumes information?
- Are our reports narrative-led or data-dumped?
- What's our early-warning system for at-risk accounts?
- Are AMs compensated on retention only, or also on expansion?
- Where would a single-AM departure put us at risk?

## Canonical examples

- **WebFX client extranet** — real-time tracking, analytics, and campaign visibility; transparency as account-management feature (page 115)
- **Neil Patel Digital** — open lines of communication throughout all stages, regular reporting and analysis (page 113)
- **Digital Silk** — frequent updates, client involvement in strategy shaping (page 117)
- **Memac Ogilvy MENA** — formal Account Director roles for Aramco, STC, Almarai; multi-tier client team (Strategic Lead, Account Director, Account Manager, Account Executive)
- **M&C Saatchi Riyadh** — quarterly business reviews with senior KSA clients as renewal anchor
- **NTDP grant management** — monthly progress reporting cadence functions as account management for grant relationship
