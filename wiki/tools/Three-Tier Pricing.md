# Three-Tier Pricing

## Definition

Three-Tier Pricing offers a product or service in three packages (typically Basic / Standard / Premium or Good / Better / Best), creating clear choice architecture and capturing wider willingness-to-pay across customer segments. Pereira's case studies show the pattern is nearly universal in software/subscription businesses: Netflix (Basic / Standard / Premium), Shopify (Basic / Shopify / Advanced + Plus enterprise), Zoom (4 tiers anchored by free), LinkedIn (4 premium tiers), HBO (Channel / App / Max). The middle tier is typically the most-purchased — the "decoy effect" of an expensive top tier makes the middle look reasonable, while the basic tier converts price-sensitive customers.

## Key attributes

- **Three (sometimes four) tiers** — typical Basic / Standard / Premium structure
- **Anchored top tier** — premium tier is often less profitable per buyer but makes Standard look reasonable
- **Feature differentiation, not pure price** — tiers differ on features, limits, support, not just price
- **Modal middle tier** — typically 50-70% of buyers choose the middle
- **Upgrade path** — customers can move up over time as needs grow
- **Decoy effect** — adding an expensive Premium tier raises Standard conversion

## When to use

- SaaS or subscription products with feature variability
- Services with capacity dimensions that can be tiered (users, storage, support level)
- When customer segments have significantly different willingness-to-pay
- After validating product-market fit on a single price point
- When competitors have tiered pricing and customers expect it

## When NOT to use

- Pure commodity products with one core spec
- Very early-stage products still finding price points
- Markets where simplicity matters more than choice (single-price-product brands)
- When tier differences are too small to justify the complexity

## Common pitfalls

- Bad tier design where customers can't tell tiers apart
- Cannibalizing Premium by overpricing it (no one buys; no decoy effect)
- Putting must-have features only in Premium (forces upgrade or churn)
- Too many tiers (analysis paralysis)
- Letting middle tier and top tier converge in value perception

## Key metrics

- Tier mix (% of revenue/users by tier)
- Upgrade rate (% moving from Basic → Standard, Standard → Premium)
- Downgrade rate
- ARPU by tier
- Free-to-paid conversion (if there's a free tier)
- Top-tier purchase rate (decoy effect indicator)

## Decision questions

- Is our middle tier the modal purchase? If not, mispricing somewhere.
- What features are in Premium that should be in Standard (and vice versa)?
- Are we missing a tier that customers want?
- What's the conversion rate at each tier transition?
