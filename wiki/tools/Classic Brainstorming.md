# Classic Brainstorming

## Definition

Classic brainstorming is the canonical group problem-solving and idea-generation strategy: a team gathers (typically around a whiteboard) and generates as many ideas as possible in a short time **without filtering or evaluating them**. Pereira frames it as a fundamentally social practice — its value isn't just the ideas produced but the team morale, communication, and culture of innovation it builds. The classic format runs on four non-negotiable principles (quantity over quality, withhold criticism, welcome the crazy ideas, combine/refine/improve) and three structural variants (analytics brainstorming for data interpretation, quiet/solo brainstorming for sensitive or quick generation, and role-play brainstorming for multi-stakeholder problems). It is the baseline against which every other technique in the catalogue is a modification.

## Key attributes

- **Four principles** — quantity over quality, withhold criticism, welcome crazy ideas, combine/refine/improve
- **Group format** — typically 5-12 participants around a shared surface
- **Time-boxed** — short, focused sessions (typically 30-60 min) for urgency
- **Non-judgmental** — no evaluation during the generation phase
- **Snowball effect** — one idea triggers another in chain reactions
- **Three variants** — analytics, quiet/solo, role-play
- **Separation of phases** — generate first, evaluate later (never finalize during the session)

## When to use

- Developing new products or services
- Solving business problems where the solution space is unclear
- Planning events, campaigns, content
- Kickoff phase of any creative or strategic project
- When team morale needs a boost (genuine side benefit)
- When you have a diverse group whose perspectives you want to surface

## When NOT to use

- When the problem is well-defined and needs analysis, not ideation
- When introverts dominate the team (use brainwriting instead)
- When a few extroverts will dominate (use round robin or quiet methods)
- When the topic is sensitive and people won't speak openly (use anonymous methods)
- When you need a decision, not options (use Lightning Decision Jam or voting)
- When the team has no facilitator and no agenda (will devolve into chaos)

## Common pitfalls

- **Lack of structure** — no agenda, no ground rules; session devolves
- **Lack of participation** — shy members never contribute; ideas are not diverse
- **Lack of diversity** — homogeneous group produces homogeneous ideas
- **Dominance of a few** — loud personalities crowd out the rest
- **Criticism and negativity** — kills the snowball effect and psychological safety
- **Premature pruning** — discarding "crazy" ideas before they spark better ones
- **Finalizing during the session** — collapses ideation into decision-making prematurely

## Key metrics

- **Number of ideas generated** (the headline measure — quantity is the discipline)
- **Participation rate** (% of attendees who contributed at least one idea)
- **Idea diversity** (categories of ideas covered)
- **Conversion to action** (% of ideas that move into prototyping/testing)
- **Session satisfaction** (post-session participant feedback — morale matters)

## Decision questions

- Is the goal of this session to generate options or to choose among them?
- Who in the room is most likely to dominate, and how do I balance airtime?
- Who is missing whose perspective would change the answers?
- Am I (the facilitator) prepared to enforce "no criticism" even from senior people?
- What is our prep — clear problem statement, materials, the right invitees?
