# Pricing Mechanisms

## Definition

Pricing Mechanisms are the structural approaches a firm uses to set prices, organized by Pereira into two systems: Fixed-Price (price is set in advance by the seller) and Dynamic Pricing (price varies based on market conditions, customer, or time). Within Fixed-Price, four sub-mechanisms exist: List Pricing, Product-Feature Dependent, Customer-Segment Dependent, Volume Dependent. Within Dynamic Pricing, four sub-mechanisms: Negotiation/Bargaining, Yield Management, Real-Time Market, Auctioning. Choosing the right mechanism is a strategic decision: it shapes who buys, how much margin you capture, and the predictability of your revenue.

## Key attributes

- **Two systems, eight sub-mechanisms** — Fixed (List, Feature-Dependent, Segment-Dependent, Volume-Dependent) + Dynamic (Negotiation, Yield Management, Real-Time, Auction)
- **Trade-off: predictability vs. margin capture** — fixed pricing is predictable; dynamic pricing captures more value but adds complexity
- **Different fit per industry** — retail tends fixed; airlines/hotels use yield management; B2B enterprise uses negotiation
- **Customer experience implications** — fixed pricing feels fair; dynamic pricing can feel arbitrary if not transparent
- **Tech-enabled dynamic** — modern dynamic pricing (Uber surge, Amazon repricing) only feasible at scale via algorithms
- **Often combined** — list price as anchor, segment-dependent discounts, volume tiers for enterprise

## When to use

- Setting pricing for a new product (start with a hypothesis, test)
- Diagnosing margin gaps — wrong mechanism may be leaving money on the table
- Capacity-constrained services (yield management captures more margin)
- B2B where each deal has different parameters (negotiation fits)
- Auction-suitable markets (used goods, ad inventory, treasury auctions)

## When NOT to use

- Dynamic pricing in markets where customers expect fixed pricing (consumer goods)
- Negotiation in mass-market consumer (slow, expensive, doesn't scale)
- Yield management in zero-variable-cost goods (no scarcity to manage)
- Auction in markets without enough buyers (auctions need depth)

## Common pitfalls

- Defaulting to list pricing when segment-dependent would capture more
- Implementing dynamic pricing without communicating clearly (alienates customers)
- Volume discounts so steep they erode margin without driving real volume
- Negotiating every deal when standardization would scale faster
- Failing to test pricing — leaving prices unchanged for years as market shifts
- Mistaking cost-plus pricing for a strategy (it's an accounting reflex)

## Key metrics

- Average Selling Price (ASP) and trend
- Price elasticity (revenue impact of price change)
- Discount rate (list - actual paid)
- Price realization (actual / list)
- Willingness-to-pay variance across segments
- Gross margin per pricing mechanism

## Decision questions

- For each customer segment: what mechanism captures the most value?
- Where are we using fixed pricing when dynamic would capture more (or vice versa)?
- What's our highest-margin pricing scenario, and can we replicate it more often?
- Are we leaving margin on the table by ignoring volume-dependent or segment-dependent pricing?
- Could yield management work in our capacity-constrained operations?

## Canonical examples

- **List pricing** — manufacturers selling to retailers; consumer packaged goods (page 109)
- **Product-feature dependent pricing** — software tiers (Basic/Pro/Enterprise) (page 110)
- **Customer-segment dependent** — student discounts, enterprise pricing, geographic pricing (page 111)
- **Volume-dependent** — bulk discounts, enterprise tiered pricing (page 111)
- **Negotiation** — B2B enterprise sales, real estate (page 113)
- **Yield management** — airlines, hotels, car rentals — dynamic by demand and time-to-event (page 114)
- **Real-time market** — commodities, stocks, foreign exchange (page 115)
- **Auctioning** — eBay, ad inventory (Google AdWords), bond markets (page 115)
