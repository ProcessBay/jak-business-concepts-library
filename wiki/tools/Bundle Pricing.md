# Bundle Pricing

## Definition

Bundle pricing sells multiple items together for a single price, usually at a discount to the sum of individual prices. Pereira distinguishes two structural variants: **pure bundling** (items sold only together — e.g., cable channel packages) and **mixed bundling** (items sold both bundled and individually, with the bundle priced attractively). Bundling captures consumer surplus, moves slow inventory by pairing it with bestsellers, and increases average transaction value — but at the cost of per-item margin.

## Key attributes

- **Pure vs. mixed bundling** — only-bundled vs. bundle-or-individual
- **Discount vs. à la carte total** — perceived savings is the hook
- **Captures consumer surplus** — extracts value from cross-product willingness-to-pay
- **Moves slow inventory** — pair slow movers with bestsellers
- **Increases AOV** — higher transaction size, often higher total revenue
- **Erodes per-item margin** — total profit math must justify the discount
- **Simplifies decision** — fewer SKUs to evaluate vs. à la carte

## When to use

- Complementary products with natural use together (camera + accessories)
- Slow-moving inventory you want to move alongside hot items
- Subscription tiers where breadth-of-feature is the differentiator
- Categories where customers find pricing complexity overwhelming
- When you want to defend against unbundled competitors

## When NOT to use

- Customers want only one item — forcing the bundle alienates them
- Bundle obscures the perceived value of premium individual items
- Margins are already thin (bundle discount tips you negative)
- Bundle complexity confuses rather than simplifies
- When unbundling is the disruptive opportunity (cable cord-cutting)

## Common pitfalls

- Setting the bundle discount too deep — margin collapses
- Bundling items customers don't want — they refuse the bundle and buy nothing
- Pure bundling when mixed bundling would convert more
- Failing to communicate the savings clearly — perceived value evaporates
- Cannibalizing premium individual sales by bundling premium with low-tier

## Key metrics

- **Average transaction value** (AOV)
- **Attach rate** (% of customers buying the bundle vs. à la carte)
- **Take rate** by bundle tier
- **Bundle margin** (after discount, after mix shift)
- **Units per transaction** (volume amplification)
- **Cannibalization rate** (loss of à la carte premium sales)

## Decision questions

- Are the items in the bundle genuinely complementary, or just lumped?
- What discount level moves the bundle without crashing total margin?
- Should this be pure or mixed bundling for our category?
- Will the bundle cannibalize premium à la carte sales we'd otherwise capture?
- Can customers easily see the savings, or is the comparison obscured?

## Canonical examples

- **McDonald's value meals** — burger + fries + drink at a meal-combo discount (page 198)
- **Adobe Creative Cloud** — multi-app bundle cheaper than individual app purchases (page 198)
- **Consumer electronics** — TV + warranty + accessories at a unified price (page 198)
- **Telecom triple-play** — internet + cable + phone bundled (page 199)
- **Microsoft Office** — Word + Excel + PowerPoint + more in one suite
