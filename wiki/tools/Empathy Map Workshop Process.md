# Empathy Map Workshop Process

## Definition

A six-step facilitation process for building an empathy map (or Empathy Map Canvas) with a cross-functional team. Pereira frames the work as a brainstorm session — not a solo deliverable — because the map's value comes from triangulating perspectives across product, marketing, sales, and customer-facing roles. The process is deliberately lightweight: low-cost supplies, short sessions, and the explicit goal of producing a representative, reliable, repeatable persona rather than a beautiful artefact.

## Key attributes

- **Select your subject** — pick the ideal customer profile for one user persona. If you have multiple segments, you'll run the process once per segment (page 39).
- **Gather your data** — pull from surveys, online forums, social media, product reviews, interviews, beta tests, pre-launches. Sample data from before, during, and after product use (pages 39-40).
- **Gather your supplies** — sticky notes and markers work as well as expensive visualisation software. Don't let tooling become an excuse (page 40).
- **Assemble your team** — product, design, marketing, customer-facing roles, plus any interviewees or external experts who've had repeated contact with the segment (pages 40-41).
- **Draw your map** — facilitate the actual session. For the canvas, start at Goal and work clockwise. Add a fictional name, age, background to give the persona realism (page 41).
- **Summarise the information** — extract salient points, convert into business strategy, flag flaws, then distribute to relevant departments (page 41).
- **Time-box sessions** — 30-60 minutes per session, multiple sessions rather than one marathon. Prevents fatigue and creativity collapse (page 41, 47-48).

## When to use

- Whenever you're standing up a new persona or refreshing a stale one
- Before writing a Value Proposition Canvas, PRD, or marketing campaign brief for a new segment
- When onboarding a new product or CX team into a customer segment they haven't met
- After major market shifts (new competitor, channel change, regulatory event) when prior personas may have drifted

## When NOT to use

- When the team has no real customer data — go gather data first; a workshop on assumptions wastes everyone's time
- When the team is misaligned on what customer to map — resolve segment scope before the session
- For one-off product decisions that don't merit a full persona (use lightweight interviews instead)
- When key voices (sales, support, the actual researcher) can't attend — postpone

## Common pitfalls

- **Marathon sessions** — drawn-out sessions burn out creativity. Multiple short sessions outperform one long one (page 41).
- **Letting biases drive the map** — Pereira's first tip: be aware of biases, double-check every entry, get multiple perspectives, back everything with factual data (page 53).
- **Skipping root-cause analysis** — observed behaviours often trace to deeper beliefs. Investigate why, not just what (page 54).
- **Treating the artefact as the goal** — the map is a means to a sharper persona and changed decisions; pinned on a wall it does nothing (page 41).
- **No follow-up cadence** — empathy maps must evolve. Build a refresh ritual or they decay into decoration (page 38, 54).

## Tips for remote teams

- **Video conferencing** — make real-time discussion possible across distance (page 55).
- **Project management tooling** — track tasks, progress, deadlines from a single terminal (page 55).
- **Shared documents** — enable simultaneous edits, comments, highlights without coordination overhead (page 55).

## Signals of good use

- Each session ends with at least one new insight that wasn't in anyone's head walking in
- The output drives a real product, marketing, or pricing decision within 30 days
- Personas survive scrutiny from the sales team (they're the truth detector)
- Repeated by another team on the same segment, the output is similar — reproducibility test (page 38)

## Decision questions

- Who exactly is the subject — name one persona, not "our users".
- Do we have enough data to fill the map, or are we about to invent it?
- Who must be in the room — and who must be in the room because they'll otherwise reject the output?
- What decision will this map change, and by when?
- How will we refresh it — quarterly, on a market shift, never?

## Canonical examples

- **Jane the scheduling manager** — Pereira's compact end-to-end illustration: subject selected, observation done, post-session interview captured, four quadrants populated with Gains/Pains, decisions surfaced (loading speed, learning curve, pricing) (pages 44-45).
- **Vacuum cleaner brand** — Empathy Map Canvas built around a "middle-to-upper class housewife with little time for chores" persona; the workshop output mapped each customer step to a business lever (pages 30-32).
