# Fixed-Bid Project Pricing

## Definition

Fixed-bid (or fixed-fee) project pricing quotes the client one total price for a defined scope of work, regardless of how many hours the firm spends delivering it. Pereira derives the worked example for consulting on pages 23-25: estimate the underlying cost (direct labor + allocated overhead), add the target margin (e.g. 25%), and that becomes the lump-sum fee — but the client never sees the cost build-up, only the final number. The model **shifts delivery risk from the client to the provider**: overruns eat margin, efficiency gains create margin. McKinsey's "project-based" engagement model (page 121) is the institutional version. Fixed-bid only works when scope can be defined tightly enough that the provider can estimate effort with confidence and the client can verify completion.

## Key attributes

- **Scope-locked price** — fee is set before delivery and does not adjust for actual hours spent
- **Delivery risk on the provider** — overruns reduce margin; efficiency creates margin
- **Margin upside from productivity** — templates, AI, reusable IP all flow directly to bottom line
- **Forces tight scoping** — a clear statement of work is a prerequisite, not an afterthought
- **Client gets budget certainty** — one number for finance, one PO, no surprises
- **Vulnerable to scope creep** — every "small addition" is unbilled unless change-orders are enforced
- **Requires accurate estimation discipline** — bad estimates compound into chronic margin loss

## When to use

- Scope is well-understood (the firm has done similar engagements before)
- Client values budget certainty and a clean PO/contract over flexibility
- The firm has productivity advantages (templates, frameworks, AI) it wants to monetize
- Deliverable is concrete and verifiable (a strategy doc, a built system, a launched campaign)
- Sales cycle benefits from a clean, comparable number against competitors

## When NOT to use

- Scope is genuinely fluid or discovery-driven (use T&M or phased fixed-bids)
- The firm has no historical data to estimate from (you're guessing at margin)
- Client is a serial scope-creeper without a culture of formal change requests
- Project depends heavily on client inputs or decisions outside the firm's control
- The work would be better priced on outcomes (use value-based pricing instead)

## Common pitfalls

- **Underestimating effort** — optimism bias eats the margin; track estimate vs actual ruthlessly
- **Accepting scope creep silently** — the polite "yes" to small additions kills profitability
- **Failing the change-order discipline** — needs a documented, low-friction way to re-price additions
- **Pricing from cost when value justifies more** — fixed-bid still ignores willingness-to-pay unless you anchor on value first
- **Loss-leader bidding** — winning the logo at zero margin rarely recovers in expansion
- **No phasing on uncertain work** — better to fix-bid Phase 1 (diagnostic) and rebid Phase 2 (delivery) than guess at both
- **Ignoring client-side delays** — clauses around client responsiveness are essential

## Key metrics

- **Margin per project** — realized $ minus delivered cost, the only number that matters
- **Estimate accuracy** — actual hours ÷ estimated hours (drift toward 1.0 is the goal)
- **Scope creep rate** — % of projects that hit unplanned scope additions
- **Change-order capture rate** — % of additions that get re-priced vs absorbed
- **Win rate on fixed-bid proposals** — vs. T&M, vs. competitors
- **Realized vs quoted margin** — the gap reveals estimation and scoping discipline

## Decision questions

- Do we have enough historical analogs to estimate this scope with confidence?
- Is our productivity edge real enough that fixed-bid is a strategic moat, not a gamble?
- What's our change-order discipline like — will we actually invoice the additions?
- Should we phase this (small fixed-bid diagnostic, then fixed-bid delivery)?
- Are we anchoring price on cost-plus, or on the value to the client?

## Canonical examples

- **Pereira consulting firm example** — $50,000 cost + 25% margin = $62,500 fixed fee on a strategic advisory project (pages 23-25)
- **McKinsey project-based engagement** — one of three engagement models McKinsey offers alongside retainer and performance-based (page 121)
- **IDEO design sprints** — scoped fixed-fee design engagements
- **Boutique strategy firms** — sell named workshops or playbooks at a list price (productized fixed-bid)
- **Agency website builds** — fixed quote for a defined site spec
