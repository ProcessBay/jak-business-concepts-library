# Drip Campaign

## Definition

A drip campaign is a pre-scheduled series of emails delivered over a defined period to slowly and strategically guide subscribed users through the sales funnel, delivering valuable content or offers at the right time. Pereira frames drip campaigns as one of the most common forms of email automation — different from event-triggered emails because the cadence is time-based and predetermined, not behavior-reactive. A SaaS company, for example, might drip a 14-day onboarding series during a free trial: day 1 welcome, day 3 key features, day 7 success stories, day 12 exclusive discount as the trial ends. Each email "drips" at its scheduled moment regardless of recipient action, though modern drips often blend with behavioral triggers (skip ahead, branch, exit on conversion).

## Key attributes

- **Time-scheduled** — emails fire on a predetermined cadence (day, week, milestone)
- **Sequenced** — designed as a narrative arc, each email building on the prior
- **Segment-targeted** — different drips for different personas, lifecycle stages
- **Goal-anchored** — each drip exists to move recipients toward one specific action
- **Set-and-iterate** — built once, optimized continuously based on performance
- **Branch-capable** — modern drips fork based on opens, clicks, conversions
- **Exit-aware** — recipients leave when they convert, unsubscribe, or hit a guard

## When to use

- Free-trial onboarding (SaaS, premium memberships) where conversion deadline matters
- Welcome series introducing brand, product, community to new subscribers
- Lead nurture over weeks or months for considered B2B purchases
- Re-engagement campaigns for dormant subscribers
- Educational courses or content series delivered over time
- Event-led sequences: webinar follow-up, conference attendees, course completion

## When NOT to use

- When timing depends entirely on recipient behavior (use triggered campaigns instead)
- One-off announcements or transactional messages
- Audiences too small to justify designing a multi-email sequence
- Without enough quality content to fill the sequence with value
- When the goal can be achieved in a single email

## Common pitfalls

- Same drip for everyone — ignoring segment differences kills relevance
- Sequences that never end — recipients fatigue and unsubscribe
- No exit on conversion — sending sales follow-ups to people who already bought
- Too aggressive a cadence — daily emails for products that don't merit it
- Content gap — early emails are strong, later ones thin (because writing 12 emails is hard)
- Treating drip as automated spam — no personalization, no segmentation, no value
- Failing to A/B test subject lines and content blocks within the sequence

## Key metrics

- **Open rate** per email in the sequence (find drop-off points)
- **Click-through rate** per email
- **Conversion rate** (sequence-to-goal)
- **Unsubscribe rate** per email (warning signal)
- **Sequence completion rate** (recipients who receive all emails)
- **Time-to-conversion** (does the drip accelerate buying?)

## Decision questions

- What is the single outcome this drip is designed to drive?
- How many emails, over what period, with what cadence?
- What segment-specific variants do we need?
- What triggers exit (conversion, unsubscribe, score change)?
- What content blocks does each email need, and can we sustain quality across the whole sequence?
- How will we A/B test and iterate after launch?

## Canonical examples

- **SaaS free-trial drip** — introduce platform, demo features, share success stories, exclusive end-of-trial discount (page 78)
- **Welcome series** — new subscriber gets brand intro + popular products + community CTA (page 77)
- **Re-engagement drip** — dormant subscribers receive "we miss you" + incentive + final exit option
- **Webinar follow-up sequence** — replay link + supporting content + sales conversation invite
