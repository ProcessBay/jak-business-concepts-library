# Marketing KPI Selection Process

## Definition

Pereira frames KPI selection as the discipline that makes a marketing plan accountable. Key Performance Indicators are evaluations of various aspects of a marketing strategy that let teams set short-term goals aligned with the overall mission and let higher-ups in the organization see how marketing is performing. He distinguishes KPIs from metrics: KPIs are the metrics that genuinely reflect progress against objectives, while raw metrics may be tracked but never analyzed. Pereira offers a **seven-step process** to land on the right KPIs: (1) identify the tools/software to measure them; (2) narrow down to the final list; (3) create standard reports and reporting cadence; (4) design dashboard visualizations for the most important KPIs; (5) share KPI reports with other teams for quality checks; (6) choose a reporting cadence for stakeholders; (7) set new goals and KPIs based on results — a continuous loop.

## Key attributes

- **Seven-step selection process** — tooling → shortlist → reports → dashboards → cross-team validation → cadence → iteration
- **KPI ≠ metric** — KPIs are the subset of metrics that map to objectives; everything else is noise
- **Tied to objectives** — every KPI must answer "how is the company doing against this specific goal?"
- **Tracked via tools** — integrations, dashboards, custom and standard reports
- **Reporting cadence varies** — weekly, monthly, quarterly, annual; choose based on decision velocity
- **Cross-team validation** — peer review of KPIs catches errors and improves trust in the data
- **Iteration loop** — annual evaluation; KPIs themselves are not permanent
- **Dashboard hygiene** — group by audience; avoid complex graphics; lead with the chart that does justice to the data

## When to use

- When building or refreshing the marketing plan — KPIs are a required element
- When marketing reporting is currently vanity metrics (likes, followers) with no business connection
- Annual KPI review — the seven-step process is the discipline for re-evaluation
- When standing up new dashboards for the executive team
- When a marketing leader inherits an opaque reporting setup
- When sales-marketing alignment requires shared metrics

## When NOT to use

- For a single-campaign post-mortem — use campaign-specific metrics; KPI process is for ongoing measurement
- When the business lacks the data infrastructure to track candidate KPIs (fix that first)
- As a substitute for actually changing behavior based on what KPIs reveal
- When KPI selection becomes a months-long committee exercise rather than a planning input

## Common pitfalls

- **KPI fatigue** — too many metrics, none truly decisional
- **Vanity metrics as KPIs** — likes, followers, impressions without business linkage
- **No tooling decided up front** — picking KPIs the team can't actually measure
- **Reporting too frequently** — Pereira: "Metrics become the main topic of discussion at meetings if they are held too frequently"; weekly reports lead to reader fatigue
- **Reporting too infrequently** — monthly is too slow for problem-solving in some contexts
- **Dashboards designed for analysts, not decision-makers** — complex graphics, no narrative
- **KPIs never re-evaluated** — what mattered last year may not matter now
- **No cross-team quality check** — bad data goes unchallenged

## Key metrics

Pereira's representative KPIs across the marketing plan:

- **Conversion rate** — top-of-funnel to bottom-of-funnel
- **Website traffic** — sessions, organic vs. paid, source attribution
- **Engagement** — likes, shares, comments, time-on-page
- **ROI** — return on marketing investment
- **Sales objectives** — revenue, units, deal count
- **Customer acquisition** — new customers, lead volume, CAC
- **Retention rate** — existing customer retention
- **Brand awareness** — share of voice, mention sentiment, brand recall

## Decision questions

- For each candidate KPI, what decision will it drive?
- Do we have the tooling to measure this KPI reliably and continuously?
- What is the right reporting cadence — weekly, monthly, quarterly?
- Who is the audience for each dashboard, and is it tailored to them?
- Have peers validated our KPI choices, or did we pick them in isolation?
- When did we last re-evaluate our KPIs against current business goals?
- Are we discriminating between KPIs (decisional) and metrics (informational)?

## Canonical examples

- **Step 1 — Identify tools** — Pereira: "KPI measuring begins with your data sources and the data tracking technologies your company uses... Integration, Dashboards, Custom and standard reports" (page 56)
- **Step 2 — Narrow down** — "make sure that key performance indicators are kept apart from metrics... check in with your objectives to ensure the key performance indicators you've chosen accurately reflect your progress" (page 57)
- **Step 3 — Standard reports/cadence** — weekly, monthly, quarterly, annual; "A regular report always follows the same format" (page 57)
- **Step 4 — Dashboard visualizations** — group KPIs for specific people; "avoid complicated graphics" (page 57)
- **Step 5 — Cross-team quality checks** — peer validation strengthens trust in the data (page 58)
- **Step 6 — Reporting cadence for stakeholders** — "More than a monthly frequency may be required for problem-solving. A weekly schedule, though, could lead to reader fatigue" (page 58)
- **Step 7 — Set new goals/KPIs based on results** — annual evaluation is a must (page 58)
