# GTM KPIs and Measurement

## Definition

GTM KPIs are the quantifiable indicators that measure whether a Go-to-Market strategy is achieving its intended outcomes. They span the full commercial funnel—from market awareness through revenue generation to customer retention—and serve as the feedback loop for iterative GTM refinement. Pereira emphasizes that measurement is not an afterthought; it is embedded in the GTM design itself, with clear baselines, targets, and accountability before launch.

## Key attributes

**Financial KPIs:**
- **Revenue and growth** — top-line indicator of GTM effectiveness
- **Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)** — total cost to acquire one customer; should trend down as GTM matures
- **Customer Lifetime Value (LTV/CLTV)** — total revenue expected from a customer; LTV:CAC ratio >3:1 is a common health benchmark
- **Return on Investment (ROI)** — net profit divided by GTM investment; indicates capital efficiency

**Market KPIs:**
- **Market share** — brand presence relative to competitors in target segment
- **Market penetration** — percentage of addressable market captured
- **Time to market** — speed from GTM initiation to first revenue

**Conversion KPIs:**
- **Conversion rates** — stage-by-stage funnel progression (lead → opportunity → customer)
- **Sales cycle length** — average time from first contact to closed deal

**Retention KPIs:**
- **Customer retention rate** — percentage of customers retained over a period
- **Churn rate** — percentage of customers lost; high churn signals GTM-promise misalignment

**Brand KPIs:**
- **Brand awareness** — unaided and aided recall in target segment
- **Brand sentiment** — net positive vs. negative mentions and reviews

## The measurement process

1. **Define objectives** — What does GTM success look like? (SMART goals)
2. **Select KPIs** — Choose metrics that directly reflect objectives
3. **Establish baselines** — Measure current state before GTM launch
4. **Set targets** — Define acceptable, target, and stretch performance levels
5. **Collect data** — Integrate CRM, marketing automation, financial, and product analytics
6. **Analyze and interpret** — Use segmentation, cohort analysis, and correlation study
7. **Iterate** — Adjust strategy based on what the data reveals

## When to use

- Before GTM launch: defining success criteria and securing stakeholder alignment
- During execution: weekly/monthly performance reviews and resource reallocation
- Post-launch: post-mortem analysis and playbook refinement for future launches
- Investor reporting: demonstrating traction and capital efficiency

## When NOT to use

- **Vanity metrics without context** — measuring social media likes when revenue is the goal
- **Static KPIs** — failing to evolve metrics as the business matures
- **Too many KPIs** — diluting focus; teams should optimize for 3–5 core metrics
- **Lagging indicators only** — waiting for revenue to know something is wrong; leading indicators (pipeline, engagement) are essential

## Common pitfalls

- **Metric without owner** — no one is accountable for moving the KPI
- **Data quality issues** — garbage in, garbage out; inconsistent definitions across tools
- **Correlation vs. causation** — assuming a marketing campaign caused a revenue spike when seasonality or other factors were at play
- **Ignoring segment differences** — aggregate metrics hide that one segment is thriving while another is failing
- **Delayed measurement** — waiting for quarterly reviews when weekly dashboards would enable faster correction

## Decision questions

1. What is the one metric that, if it moves, proves our GTM is working?
2. How do we distinguish between GTM execution failure and product-market fit failure?
3. What leading indicators give us early warning before revenue drops?
4. How do our KPIs change as we move from launch phase to scale phase?
5. Are we measuring unit economics per channel, or just in aggregate?
6. What is our feedback loop cadence—daily, weekly, monthly—and who has authority to act on the data?

## Canonical examples

- **Tesla GTM measurement** — tracked direct-to-consumer sales conversion, Supercharger network coverage ratio, and brand evangelist referrals as leading indicators of market disruption (Pereira, pp. 118–121).
- **Peloton niche metrics** — measured subscription attach rate, content engagement hours, and financing application volume to validate niche-market penetration (Pereira, pp. 122–125).
- **Airbnb global expansion** — tracked localized match rates, review sentiment by city, and host acquisition cost per market to guide geographic prioritization (Pereira, pp. 126–129).
- **CAC trends in SaaS** — companies monitor CAC by channel to identify when organic/network-driven acquisition reduces paid dependency (Pereira, pp. 116–117).
