# Competitive Analysis

## Definition

Competitive analysis is the systematic process of identifying, evaluating, and understanding competitors to inform strategic decisions. It goes beyond simply listing competitors — it involves dissecting their products, pricing, marketing, strengths, weaknesses, and market position to find differentiation opportunities and strategic threats. Pereira: "Competitive analysis is not about copying your competitors; it's about understanding the landscape to make informed decisions."

## Key attributes

- **Systematic** — structured framework, not ad-hoc observation
- **Multi-dimensional** — products, pricing, marketing, sales, culture, geography
- **Dynamic** — competitors evolve; analysis must be repeated regularly
- **Action-oriented** — insights must lead to strategic decisions, not just knowledge
- **Honest** — acknowledging competitor strengths, not just weaknesses

## The competitive analysis framework

### Step 1: Identify competitors
| Type | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Direct competitors** | Same product, same market | Slack vs. Teams |
| **Indirect competitors** | Different product, same need | Email vs. Slack |
| **Substitute competitors** | Different solution, same outcome | Phone calls vs. video conferencing |
| **Potential entrants** | Could enter your market | Google entering any tech market |

### Step 2: Gather intelligence
- Products/services and feature sets
- Pricing models and tiers
- Sales tactics and channels
- Marketing messaging and positioning
- Market share and growth rate
- Customer reviews and satisfaction
- Team size and funding
- Partnerships and integrations

### Step 3: Analyze and compare
- **Feature matrix** — your features vs. theirs
- **Pricing comparison** — cost per value unit
- **Positioning map** — price vs. quality or features
- **SWOT** — their strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, threats
- **BCG Matrix** — market share vs. growth rate

### Step 4: Extract insights
- Where are they vulnerable? (pricing, features, service, geography)
- Where are they strong? (brand, technology, distribution)
- What are they missing that customers want?
- Where is the market underserved?

## When to use

- Before launching a new product or entering a new market
- When growth stalls and you need to understand why
- Before pricing decisions
- When considering a pivot
- Annually for strategic planning
- Before fundraising (investors will ask)

## When NOT to use

- As a substitute for customer research (competitors may be wrong too)
- To copy competitors (follower strategy rarely wins)
- As a one-time exercise (markets change)
- When analysis paralysis sets in (must lead to action)
- To justify inaction ("the market is too crowded")

## Common pitfalls

- **Focusing only on direct competitors** — indirect and substitute competitors often matter more
- **Static analysis** — competitors change; analysis becomes stale quickly
- **Confirmation bias** — cherry-picking data to support pre-existing beliefs
- **Feature obsession** — comparing features instead of customer outcomes
- **Ignoring pricing** — assuming you can compete on features alone
- **Internal overestimation** — rating your own strengths higher than objective reality
- **Analysis without action** — producing a document that nobody uses

## Key metrics

| Metric | How to measure | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **Market Share** | Revenue / Total market revenue | Relative position |
| **Feature Parity Score** | # matching features / # competitor features | Gap analysis |
| **Pricing Gap** | Your price - Competitor price | Positioning relative to price |
| **Customer Satisfaction Delta** | Your NPS - Competitor NPS | Quality perception gap |
| **Brand Awareness** | Unaided recall % | Mindshare comparison |

## Decision questions

- Who are our top 3 competitors, and what are they doing better than us?
- What customer need is no competitor adequately serving?
- If we raised our prices 20%, which competitor would customers switch to?
- What is our single most defensible advantage vs. the nearest competitor?
- Where is the competitor most vulnerable (service, geography, segment)?
- Are we competing on the same dimension as everyone else, or a different one?
- What would it cost a competitor to copy our differentiation?
- How often do we update our competitive analysis?

## Canonical examples

- **Netflix vs. Blockbuster** — Netflix analyzed Blockbuster's late-fee pain point and built a subscription model around eliminating it
- **Airbnb vs. Hotels** — Airbnb identified the price/location gap that hotels couldn't fill
- **Tesla vs. Automakers** — Tesla analyzed incumbent EV strategies and chose to compete on performance and brand, not just eco-friendliness
- **Sony vs. Nintendo** — Analyzed in Pereira (page 15) as a case study in competitive dynamics across gaming platforms
