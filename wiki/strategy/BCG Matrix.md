# BCG Matrix

## Definition

The BCG Matrix (Growth-Share Matrix) is a portfolio management framework developed by the Boston Consulting Group that classifies business units or products into four categories based on two dimensions: relative market share (competitive strength) and market growth rate (industry attractiveness). The four quadrants—Stars, Cash Cows, Question Marks, and Dogs—provide a strategic lens for resource allocation, investment decisions, and divestiture planning. Pereira presents the BCG Matrix as a practical tool for understanding where to invest, where to harvest, and where to exit, with clear action implications for each quadrant.

## Key attributes

- **Stars** (high growth, high share) — market leaders in fast-growing industries; require heavy investment to maintain position; future Cash Cows
- **Cash Cows** (low growth, high share) — market leaders in mature industries; generate more cash than they consume; fund Stars and Question Marks
- **Question Marks** (high growth, low share) — operate in attractive markets but lack competitive position; require strategic choice: invest heavily to become Stars, or divest
- **Dogs** (low growth, low share) — weak competitive position in unattractive markets; typically generate low profit or losses; candidates for divestiture or liquidation

## When to use

- Multi-product company deciding where to allocate capital and talent
- Corporate strategy review to evaluate business unit portfolio health
- Venture capital or private equity portfolio review
- M&A target evaluation to understand which assets are stars vs. dogs
- Resource allocation when budget constraints force trade-offs
- Explaining to stakeholders why underperforming products are being sunset

## When NOT to use

- Single-product startups with no portfolio to balance
- When market share is an irrelevant metric (e.g., network-effect platforms where engagement matters more)
- If market boundaries are ill-defined (e.g., "AI" is not a single market)
- When used as the sole decision tool—complement with customer data and operational metrics
- For short-term tactical decisions; BCG is a strategic, not operational, framework

## Common pitfalls

- **Defining markets too broadly** — a "Dog" in one segment may be a "Star" in a niche
- **Static analysis** — a Star can become a Dog if growth slows and share erodes; review annually
- **Ignoring synergies** — Dogs may support Stars through ecosystem effects or brand halo
- **Over-reliance on market share** — in innovation-driven markets, first-mover advantage can be fleeting
- **Harvesting Cash Cows too aggressively** — starves them of maintenance investment, accelerating decline
- **Treating all Question Marks the same** — some have a credible path to Star; others are hopeless
- **Divesting Dogs too quickly** — they may provide strategic cover or serve niche customers profitably

## Key metrics

| Metric | How to measure | Use in BCG |
|---|---|---|
| **Relative Market Share** | Your share / Largest competitor's share | >1.0 = high share (left side of matrix) |
| **Market Growth Rate** | Annual growth % of the served market | >10% typically = high growth (top of matrix) |
| **Revenue Contribution** | Unit revenue / Total portfolio revenue | Identifies Cash Cows that fund the portfolio |
| **Profit Margin by Unit** | Unit profit / Unit revenue | Dogs often have negative or near-zero margins |
| **Investment Requirement** | Capex + R&D + Marketing for unit | Stars and Question Marks require the most |

## Decision questions

- Which of our products/business units are Stars, and are we investing enough to maintain their trajectory?
- Which Cash Cows are we milking too hard, risking their decline?
- For each Question Mark, do we have a credible path to Star status within 2-3 years?
- Which Dogs are we keeping for emotional reasons rather than strategic logic?
- What percentage of our portfolio is in each quadrant, and is that balance sustainable?
- If we had to cut investment by 30%, which units would we protect and which would we sacrifice?
- Are we defining our markets correctly, or are we hiding Stars inside overly broad categories?

## Canonical examples

- **Apple** — iPhone = Cash Cow (mature market, dominant share); Apple Watch = Star (growing market, leading share); Apple TV+ = Question Mark (growing streaming market, low relative share); iPod = Dog (declining market, near-zero share, discontinued). This portfolio balance allows Apple to fund innovation from iPhone profits (Pereira, pp. 89–90)
- **Samsung** — smartphones = Star/Question Mark (growing market, strong but not dominant share); semiconductors = Cash Cow (mature market, dominant share); home appliances = Cash Cow (mature market, high share). Samsung uses semiconductor profits to fund smartphone R&D and marketing (Pereira, pp. 90–91)
- **General Electric (classic BCG case)** — historically used the matrix to manage a diverse industrial portfolio, divesting Dogs (small appliances) and investing in Stars (aircraft engines, medical devices). The framework became synonymous with GE's portfolio management discipline in the 1980s–90s (referenced in Pereira's broader strategy context)
