# Porter's Five Forces

## Definition

Porter's Five Forces is a framework for analyzing the competitive structure of an industry and its overall profitability. Developed by Michael Porter at Harvard Business School, it identifies five forces that determine the intensity of competition and therefore the attractiveness of an industry: rivalry among existing competitors, threat of new entrants, bargaining power of suppliers, bargaining power of buyers, and threat of substitutes. Pereira applies this framework practically: each force is evaluated not as a static fact but as a dynamic pressure that shapes pricing power, margin sustainability, and strategic positioning.

## Key attributes

- **Rivalry among existing competitors** — intensity of competition based on number of players, growth rate, and differentiation
- **Threat of new entrants** — ease of entry determined by barriers like capital requirements, regulation, brand loyalty, and economies of scale
- **Bargaining power of suppliers** — ability of suppliers to raise prices or reduce quality; high when switching costs are high or supplier concentration is low
- **Bargaining power of buyers** — ability of customers to demand lower prices or higher quality; high when buyer concentration is high or switching costs are low
- **Threat of substitutes** — availability of alternative products or services that fulfill the same need; accelerates when technology disrupts legacy solutions

## When to use

- Entering a new market or industry and need to assess structural attractiveness
- Evaluating whether an industry supports sustainable profitability
- Strategic planning to identify which competitive pressures to mitigate
- Investment or M&A due diligence to validate industry thesis
- Explaining to founders why their "great product" may still face margin pressure
- Positioning a new venture by choosing an industry segment with favorable force dynamics

## When NOT to use

- Analyzing a market you are creating from scratch (Blue Ocean) where no industry structure yet exists
- When the analysis becomes a substitute for customer discovery (forces describe structure, not customer needs)
- If treated as a one-time exercise—industry structures evolve with technology and regulation
- When the goal is tactical execution rather than strategic positioning
- In highly fragmented, dynamic digital markets where the "industry" boundary is unclear

## Common pitfalls

- **Treating all five forces as equal** — in some industries, one force dominates (e.g., buyer power in enterprise software)
- **Static analysis** — failing to recognize that forces shift with technology, regulation, or economic cycles
- **Ignoring the sixth force** — complements and platform ecosystems (Porter later acknowledged complementors)
- **Over-aggregating** — analyzing "the SaaS industry" instead of a specific segment (e.g., vertical SaaS for dental practices)
- **Confusing symptoms with forces** — low margins are a symptom; the force causing them must be identified
- **Using it to justify inaction** — "the industry is too competitive" is often an excuse for weak differentiation

## Key metrics

| Metric | How to measure | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| **Market Concentration (CR4/HHI)** | Combined market share of top 4 players / Herfindahl-Hirschman Index | Higher = more oligopolistic; lower = more fragmented |
| **Supplier Concentration** | % of inputs controlled by top 3 suppliers | Higher = more supplier power |
| **Buyer Concentration** | % of revenue from top 5 customers | Higher = more buyer power |
| **Entry Barrier Score** | Capital + regulatory + brand + technology barriers | Higher = harder to enter = more protected incumbents |
| **Substitute Availability Index** | Number of functionally equivalent alternatives | Higher = more price pressure |

## Decision questions

- Which of the five forces is the most intense in our target industry, and can we design around it?
- What would it cost a well-funded competitor to enter our market, and how long would it take?
- How concentrated are our suppliers, and what is our Plan B if they raise prices 30%?
- What percentage of our revenue comes from our top 5 customers, and what happens if one leaves?
- What substitutes exist today, and which emerging technology could make our product obsolete?
- Are we competing in an industry with structurally attractive economics, or are we fighting gravity?
- How will regulatory, technological, or economic shifts change the force dynamics in the next 3-5 years?

## Canonical examples

- **Airline industry** — high rivalry (price wars), high buyer power (online comparison tools), high threat of substitutes (video conferencing replacing business travel), low supplier power (multiple aircraft manufacturers), high barriers to entry (capital, slots, regulation). Result: structurally unprofitable for most players (Pereira, pp. 63–64)
- **Saudi food retail** — high buyer power (price-sensitive consumers, multiple store options), moderate supplier power (local vs. imported goods), moderate rivalry (localized competition), low threat of substitutes (people must eat). Result: margins driven by operational efficiency and loyalty programs (Pereira, pp. 64–65)
- **Enterprise SaaS** — moderate rivalry (many players but high switching costs), low threat of new entrants (integration complexity, sales cycles), low supplier power (cloud infrastructure is commoditized), moderate buyer power (enterprise procurement), low threat of substitutes (custom builds are expensive). Result: structurally attractive if switching costs are maintained (implied in Pereira's SaaS analysis)
