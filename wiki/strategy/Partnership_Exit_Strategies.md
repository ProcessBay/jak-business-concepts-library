# Partnership Exit Strategies

## Definition

A partnership exit strategy is a pre-defined plan for dissolving or restructuring a strategic alliance when objectives are met, circumstances change, or the collaboration no longer delivers mutual value. Well-designed exit strategies protect IP, preserve customer relationships, minimize financial loss, and maintain professional reputations. Pereira: "Exiting a strategic partnership can be intricate, especially when the partnership is legally structured, as in a joint venture. Termination or dissolution may involve intricate negotiations and legal procedures, potentially leading to disputes."

## Key attributes

- **Pre-negotiated exit clauses:** Contractual terms defining termination triggers, notice periods, and wind-down procedures
- **IP and data division:** Clear rules on ownership and transfer of jointly created intellectual property, customer data, and technical assets
- **Customer transition protocol:** Plan for communicating with joint customers and ensuring service continuity
- **Financial settlement framework:** Methodology for dividing remaining assets, liabilities, and ongoing revenue streams
- **Dispute resolution mechanism:** Mediation or arbitration procedures to avoid litigation
- **Professionalism preservation:** Commitment to maintaining market confidence and partner reputation even during dissolution

## When to use

- Drafting initial partnership agreements (exit clauses should be negotiated upfront)
- Partnership performance consistently misses agreed KPIs over multiple review periods
- Strategic priorities of either party fundamentally diverge
- Market conditions change such that the partnership rationale no longer holds
- M&A activity by either party creates competitive conflict or redundancy

## When NOT to use

- As a routine threat during normal partnership negotiations (erodes trust)
- When partnership is performing well and both parties are aligned
- Before exhausting renegotiation or restructuring options
- In public without prior private discussion (surprise exits damage relationships and reputation)

## Common pitfalls

- **No exit clause** — partnerships launched without termination terms become legally and financially entangled
- **Vague IP ownership** — jointly created assets become contentious during dissolution
- **Customer abandonment** — joint customers are left confused or unsupported during transition
- **Public acrimony** — bad exits damage both parties' reputations and future partnership prospects
- **Underestimating exit cost** — wind-down expenses, legal fees, and integration unwinding exceed projections
- **Emotional decision-making** — exiting in anger rather than based on structured performance review

## Key metrics

| Metric | Definition | Target |
|---|---|---|
| **Exit Cost** | Total cost of dissolution including legal, operational, and transition | <10% of partnership value |
| **Customer Transition Rate** | % of joint customers retained by at least one party | >90% |
| **IP Recovery Rate** | % of proprietary IP successfully recovered or licensed | 100% for core IP |
| **Brand Impact Score** | Qualitative assessment of reputational damage | Neutral or positive |
| **Time to Exit** | Months from decision to full dissolution | <6 months for simple; <18 for complex |

## Decision questions

- Does our partnership agreement include clear termination triggers and notice periods?
- Who owns the customer relationships, and how will they be communicated with during transition?
- What happens to jointly developed IP, data, and technology assets?
- Can we structure an exit that preserves the professional relationship for future opportunities?
- What is the total cost of exit, and have we provisioned for it financially?
- Is exit the best option, or would restructuring terms achieve the same outcome?

## Canonical examples

- **Apple + IBM** — Structured enterprise mobility partnership with defined review gates; both parties could exit if milestones were missed; maintained professional relationship despite limited long-term traction (Pereira, p. 97)
- **Sony + Ericsson** — Joint venture dissolution after nearly a decade; Sony acquired Ericsson's share, demonstrating how equity partnerships can transition to full ownership (Pereira, p. 93, analogous case)
- **Spotify + Uber** — lighter co-marketing partnership naturally concluded as both companies evolved; no complex unwind required due to structural simplicity (Pereira, p. 112)
- **Microsoft + LinkedIn** — acquisition served as ultimate "exit" from partnership into full integration; pre-negotiated terms simplified transition (Pereira, p. 107)
