# Partner Due Diligence

## Definition

Partner due diligence is the systematic investigation and verification of a potential partner's financial health, operational capabilities, legal standing, cultural fit, and strategic alignment before entering a formal alliance. It is the primary risk mitigation mechanism in partnership formation, transforming gut feelings and relationship chemistry into evidence-based decisions. Pereira: "Due diligence and risk assessment are critical steps in the partner selection process. They involve a comprehensive evaluation of potential partners to identify risks, validate assumptions, and ensure that the partnership is built on a solid foundation."

## Key attributes

- **Financial due diligence:** Revenue consistency, profit margins, debt levels, cash flow analysis, and diversification of revenue sources
- **Legal due diligence:** Contract review, IP ownership, pending litigation, regulatory compliance, and licensing requirements
- **Operational due diligence:** Production capacity, supply chain robustness, technology infrastructure, and service delivery capabilities
- **Cultural assessment:** Values alignment, decision-making styles, communication norms, and organizational flexibility
- **Reference validation:** Exhaustive review of past partnerships — both successful and unsuccessful — with detailed discussions with previous collaborators
- **Long-term viability evaluation:** Strategic plan review, innovation initiatives, and track record of partnership commitment

## When to use

- Before signing any partnership agreement beyond a simple co-marketing arrangement
- When partnership involves shared IP, joint investment, or operational integration
- Entering a new geographic market where local partner reputation is critical
- Partner will become a significant revenue or supply chain dependency
- Regulatory or compliance requirements mandate third-party verification

## When NOT to use

- For transactional, low-commitment, short-term arrangements
- When speed-to-partnership is critical and the arrangement is easily reversible
- Internal capabilities exist to conduct equivalent verification informally
- Partner is a well-established public entity with transparent disclosure

## Common pitfalls

- **Surface-level checks** — relying on public information without deep verification
- **Confirmation bias** — seeking information that validates the pre-existing desire to partner
- **Neglecting cultural fit** — over-indexing on financials while ignoring organizational compatibility
- **Insufficient reference depth** — contacting only provided references rather than independently sourced ones
- **Static assessment** — treating due diligence as a one-time event rather than continuous monitoring
- **Underestimating integration complexity** — operational due diligence misses technical or process incompatibilities

## Key metrics

| Metric | Definition | Target |
|---|---|---|
| **Financial Stability Score** | Composite rating of revenue, margins, debt, cash flow | Above industry median |
| **Cultural Alignment Score** | Qualitative assessment of values and working style match | >70% alignment on core dimensions |
| **Legal Risk Score** | Count and severity of pending litigation, compliance gaps | Zero critical gaps |
| **Operational Readiness** | Capacity to meet partnership demands within timeline | Confirmed by site visit or audit |
| **Reference Quality** | Depth and candor of feedback from past partners | At least 3 independent references |

## Decision questions

- What is the worst-case scenario if this partnership fails, and can we survive it?
- Does the partner's financial trajectory suggest stability for the partnership's intended duration?
- What do their past partners say about working with them — especially the ones they no longer work with?
- Are there hidden conflicts of interest or competitive tensions that could emerge?
- Does their operational infrastructure match what they claim in presentations?
- What is the partner's true strategic priority — is our partnership central or peripheral to their plans?

## Canonical examples

- **Apple + IBM** — Extensive due diligence on enterprise security, data handling, and cultural compatibility before announcing alliance; both CEOs directly involved (Pereira, p. 94)
- **Starbucks + Nestlé** — Nestlé conducted deep brand and supply chain due diligence on Starbucks' roasting and sourcing capabilities before $7.2B licensing deal (Pereira, p. 98)
- **Microsoft + LinkedIn** — Microsoft's acquisition due diligence included technical architecture review, data governance audit, and talent retention assessment (Pereira, p. 106)
- **McDonald's + Uber Eats** — Pilot programs in select markets served as operational due diligence before global rollout (Pereira, p. 108)
